# Cross-section extraction and model reconstruction of lava tube based on L1-medial skeleton

**Journal:** International Journal of Applied Earth Observation and Geoinformation 132 (2024) 104062  
**Authors:** Qiao Yang, Zhizhong Kang, Teng Hu, Zhen Cao, Chenming Ye, Dongming Liu, Haoxiang Hu, Shuai Shao  
**Affiliation:** China University of Geosciences, Beijing, China

## Abstract
Lunar lava tube caves are prime candidates for lunar base site selection due to their unique geographical structure and potential as shelters. This paper explores a method for extracting lava tube parameters and constructing 3D models using LiDAR point cloud data. The approach uses four key steps: data preprocessing, axis construction based on the L1-medial skeleton, cross-sectional feature extraction, and 3D model reconstruction. The method achieves high accuracy, with 99% of extracted points having an error under 0.12 m and 99% of model positional errors within 0.15 m.

## 1. Introduction
Lava tubes are roofed passages formed by flowing lava. On the Moon and Mars, skylights similar to terrestrial lava tube openings have been observed. These natural shelters offer protection from extreme temperature fluctuations, cosmic radiation, lunar dust, and meteorites, making them suitable for sustained human habitation.

## 4. Methodology
### 4.1. Data Preprocessing
- **Voxelization:** Point clouds are voxelized (0.05 m size) to reduce noise and data volume while preserving internal details.
- **Filtering:** Use of Cloth Simulation Filter (CSF) to segment the ground and manual segmentation to remove artifacts like human shadows.

### 4.2. Lava Tube Axis Extraction
A novel algorithm based on the L1-Medial Skeleton is introduced to simplify 3D point sets into 1D cluster centers. This provides a precise central axis even in irregular natural tunnels.

### 4.3. Extraction of Cross-sections
Sampling occurs every 0.5 m along the axis. Points are projected onto planes to extract geometric parameters like area, length, and width.

### 4.4. Mesh Model Construction
A 3D mesh is built by establishing topological relationships between cross-sectional points, enabling internal volume estimation and structural analysis.
