# 3D-printed Lunar regolith simulant-based geopolymer composites with bio-inspired sandwich architectures

**Journal of Advanced Ceramics**  
2023, 12(3): 510–525  
DOI: [10.26599/JAC.2023.9220700](https://doi.org/10.26599/JAC.2023.9220700)

## Abstract
Over time, natural materials have evolved to be lightweight, high-strength, tough, and damage-tolerant due to their unique biological structures. Therefore, combining biological inspiration and structural design would provide traditional materials with a broader range of performance and applications. Here, the application of an ink-based three-dimensional (3D) printing strategy to the structural design of a Lunar regolith simulant-based geopolymer (HIT-LRS-1 GP) was first reported, and high-precision carbon fiber/quartz sand-reinforced biomimetic patterns inspired by the cellular sandwich structure of plant stems were fabricated. This study demonstrated how different cellular sandwich structures can balance the structure–property relationship and how to achieve unprecedented damage tolerance for a geopolymer composite. The results presented that components based on these biomimetic architectures exhibited stable non-catastrophic fracture characteristics regardless of the compression direction, and each structure possessed effective damage tolerance and anisotropy of mechanical properties.

## 1 Introduction
Human exploration of the Moon necessitates in situ resource utilization (ISRU) for space base construction to minimize transportation costs. Geopolymers are potential alternatives to cement due to low density and the ability to utilize Lunar regolith as a Si and Al source. However, brittleness remains a major obstacle. This study uses biological inspiration (plant cellular sandwich structures) and 3D printing to create toughened geopolymer composites.

## 2 Materials and methods
### 2.1 Preparation of Lunar regolith simulant
The Lunar regolith simulant (HIT-LRS-1) was designed to match Maria geological terrain, similar to JSC-1A. Components include basalt, plagioclase, forsterite, albite, ilmenite, hematite, silica, alumina, and cristobalite.

### 2.2 3D printing of Lunar regolith simulant-based geopolymer inks
Inks were prepared using chopped short carbon fibers (average length 600 μm) and quartz sand as reinforcements. Direct Ink Writing (DIW) was used with a 0.82 mm diameter needle.

### 2.3 Design of biomimetic structures
Four cellular sandwich architectures were constructed: (1) honeycomb, (2) triangular, (3) wave, and (4) rectangular, inspired by *Elytrigia repens*.

## 3 Results and discussion
### 3.1 Rheological properties
The inks exhibited shear-thinning behavior, essential for DIW. The addition of quartz sand and carbon fibers significantly increased the yield stress and viscosity, improving printability.

### 3.2 Mechanical properties
- **Flexural Strength:** 1 vol% carbon fiber reinforcement reached 41.6 MPa (50.7% increase over unreinforced).
- **Compressive Strength (Y-axis):** 11.3 to 20.1 MPa.
- **Compressive Strength (Z-axis):** 23.8 to 46.7 MPa (Honeycomb structure being the highest).

### 3.3 Damage Tolerance
All structures exhibited non-catastrophic fracture behavior, maintaining structural integrity even after initial failure, which is critical for safety in extreme environments.

## 4 Conclusions
The study demonstrated successful DIW of Csf/QS@HIT-LRS-1 based geopolymer composites. The bio-inspired architectures provided anisotropic mechanical properties and improved crashworthiness, making them robust candidates for Lunar base construction.
