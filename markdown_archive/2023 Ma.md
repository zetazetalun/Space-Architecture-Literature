# 3D-printed Lunar regolith simulant-based geopolymer composites with bio-inspired sandwich architectures

**Journal of Advanced Ceramics**  
2023, 12(3): 510–525  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.26599/JAC.2023.9220700

## Abstract
Over time, natural materials have evolved to be lightweight, high-strength, tough, and damage-tolerant due to their unique biological structures. This study reports the application of an ink-based three-dimensional (3D) printing strategy (Direct Ink Writing) to the structural design of a Lunar regolith simulant-based geopolymer (HIT-LRS-1 GP). High-precision carbon fiber/quartz sand-reinforced biomimetic patterns inspired by the cellular sandwich structure of plant stems were fabricated. The results showed that honeycomb, triangular, wave, and rectangular sandwich patterns exhibited stable non-catastrophic fracture characteristics and significant compressive strengths (up to 46.7 MPa in the Z-axis). This research demonstrates that 3D-printed LRS-1 geopolymer with biomimetic patterns are robust candidates for Lunar base construction.

## 1. Introduction
Space exploration, particularly of the Moon, requires In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) to minimize transportation costs. Geopolymers are potential alternatives to cement as they can be synthesized from Lunar regolith with minimal Earth-delivered additives. This study focuses on overcoming the inherent brittleness of geopolymers by combining fiber reinforcement and bio-inspired structural design (honeycomb, triangular, wave, and rectangular patterns) to create damage-tolerant construction materials.

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1 Preparation of Lunar regolith simulant
A Lunar regolith simulant (HIT-LRS-1) was prepared based on the geological terrain of the Lunar Maria, referencing JSC-1A. Raw materials included basalt, plagioclase, forsterite, albite, ilmenite, hematite, silica, alumina, and cristobalite powders.

### 2.2 3D printing of Lunar regolith simulant-based geopolymer inks
Inks were prepared using HIT-LRS-1 powder, an alkaline silicate solution, chopped short carbon fibers (Csf), and quartz sand (QS). Direct Ink Writing (DIW) was used with a 0.82 mm diameter needle at a printing speed of 40 mm/s.

### 2.3 Design of biomimetic structures
Four regular cellular sandwich architectures inspired by plant rhizomes (e.g., *Elytrigia repens*) were constructed: (1) honeycomb, (2) triangular, (3) wave, and (4) rectangular.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### 3.1 Rheological properties
The addition of quartz sand and carbon fibers significantly improved the printability of the HIT-LRS-1 geopolymer inks by increasing the initial viscosity and yield stress, allowing for self-supporting layers.

### 3.2 Mechanical Properties
- **Flexural Strength:** 1.00 vol% carbon fiber reinforcement increased the flexural strength to 41.6 MPa (a 50.7% increase over the unreinforced matrix).
- **Compressive Strength (Y-axis):** Ranged from 11.3 to 20.1 MPa across the different patterns.
- **Compressive Strength (Z-axis):** Significantly higher, ranging from 23.8 to 46.7 MPa.
- **Fracture Behavior:** All bio-inspired structures exhibited non-catastrophic, pseudo-plastic failure modes, maintaining structural integrity even after initial cracking.

## 4. Conclusions
The study demonstrated successful 3D printing of Csf/QS@HIT-LRS-1 based geopolymer composites with plant-inspired architectures. These structures provide high spatial resolution, anisotropic mechanical properties, and excellent crashworthiness. The results suggest these materials are suitable candidates for the robust construction of a Lunar base using ISRU methods.
