# 3D-printed Lunar regolith simulant-based geopolymer composites with bio-inspired sandwich architectures

## Abstract
Over time, natural materials have evolved to be lightweight, high-strength, tough, and damage-tolerant due to their unique biological structures. This study reports an ink-based 3D printing strategy for structural design of a Lunar regolith simulant-based geopolymer (HIT-LRS-1 GP) with high-precision carbon fiber/quartz sand-reinforced biomimetic patterns inspired by plant stem cellular sandwich structures. Results showed stable non-catastrophic fracture characteristics and effective damage tolerance. Compressive strengths for honeycomb, triangular, wave, and rectangular patterns were 15.6, 17.9, 11.3, and 20.1 MPa in the Y-axis and 46.7, 26.5, 23.8, and 34.4 MPa in the Z-axis, respectively.

## 1. Introduction
Space transportation costs necessitate In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) for Moon base construction. Geopolymers are potential alternatives to cement due to low density and thermal conductivity, and can be synthesized using Lunar regolith. To overcome inherent brittleness, structural designs inspired by natural materials (e.g., plant rhizomes) are explored to improve toughness and damage tolerance.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1 Preparation of Lunar regolith simulant
A Lunar regolith simulant (HIT-LRS-1) was designed to simulate the Maria geological terrain, based on the JSC-1A simulant. Raw materials include basalt, plagioclase, forsterite, albite, ilmenite, hematite, silica, and alumina powders. The amorphous phase content is approximately 29.2%.

### 2.2 3D printing of geopolymer inks
Inks were prepared using HIT-LRS-1 GP with short carbon fiber (average length 600 µm) and quartz sand reinforcements. Printing used Direct Ink Writing (DIW) with a 0.82 mm diameter needle, 40 mm/s speed, and 70-80 psi pressure.

### 2.3 Design of biomimetic structures
Four regular cellular sandwich architectures were constructed: (1) honeycomb, (2) triangular, (3) wave, and (4) rectangular, inspired by the rhizome of *Elytrigia repens*.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Rheology**: The addition of quartz sand and carbon fiber increased the initial viscosity and yield stress, improving printability and shape retention.
- **Fiber Alignment**: Extrusion through the nozzle induced unidirectional alignment of carbon fibers parallel to the printing direction, enhancing flexural strength (up to 41.6 MPa at 1.00 vol% CF).
- **Mechanical Properties**: The structures exhibited pattern-dependent mechanical properties and anisotropic behavior. Honeycomb patterns showed the highest fracture strain (10.2-12.1%). Z-axis compressive strength was significantly higher than Y-axis strength for all designs.

| Structure | Y-axis Compressive Strength (MPa) | Z-axis Compressive Strength (MPa) | Max Fracture Strain (Y/Z %) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Honeycomb | 15.6 | 46.7 | 10.2 / 12.1 |
| Triangular | 17.9 | 26.5 | 6.7 / 13.7 |
| Wave | 11.3 | 23.8 | 5.8 / 13.6 |
| Rectangular | 20.1 | 34.4 | 5.9 / 13.9 |

## 4. Conclusions
Successful 3D printing of HIT-LRS-1 based GP composites with bio-inspired patterns was demonstrated. The anisotropic structures provide high damage tolerance and compressive strength, making them robust candidates for Lunar base construction. Future work will address printing under low gravity and durability under freezing cycles.
