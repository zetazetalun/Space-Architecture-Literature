# An assumption of in situ resource utilization for “bio-bricks” in space exploration

**Authors:** Hongyan Zuo, Shuisong Ni, Meiying Xu  
**Publisher:** Frontiers in Materials  
**Published:** 06 April 2023  
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmats.2023.1155643

## Abstract
Microbially induced carbonate precipitation (MICP) mimics natural cementation processes to manufacture “bio-bricks,” which rival regular bricks in strength and durability. This process is a potential energy-saving and cost-effective means of in situ resource utilization (ISRU) for extraterrestrial human settlements on the Moon.

## 1 Introduction
Space exploration faces high costs for transporting materials (approx. $10,000 USD/lb to Earth orbit). ISRU techniques for construction materials include melting/sintering, combustion, and cold pressing, but these are energy-intensive. Bio-cementation (MICP) is a 'green' alternative using microorganisms to precipitate carbonate minerals.

## 2 Microbial carbonate precipitation by different metabolic activities
Metabolic pathways for MICP include:
- **Aerobic:** Photosynthesis, Ureolysis, and Ammonification.
- **Anaerobic:** Sulfate reduction, Denitrification, and Methane oxidation.

### 2.1 Aerobic processes
Ureolysis is the most studied MICP process due to higher calcite precipitation. Common species include *Sporosarcina pasteurii* (formerly *Bacillus pasteurii*).

### 2.2 Anaerobic processes
Given the lack of oxygen on the Moon, anaerobic processes like denitrification are highly relevant. Denitrification is considered a suitable MICP method for soil reinforcement due to substrate solubility and carbonate yield.

## 3 Properties of sand-sized material on the moon
Lunar regolith is fragmental, unconsolidated rock with specific gravity ranging from 2.9–3.5 g/cm³. Geotechnical properties: average mean particle size D50 = 72 μm, coefficient of uniformity Cu = 16, and coefficient of curvature Cc = 1.2.

## 5 Research needs and future directions
- **Micro-gravity effects:** Survival and metabolic activity of microbes in space conditions.
- **Water availability:** Water ice on the lunar surface could support bio-cementation.
- **Urea source:** Potential use of human urine as a urea source for ISRU bio-bricks.
- **Upscaling:** Challenges in obtaining homogeneous treatment at scale.

## Conclusion
MICP is a promising technology for lunar construction. Future work must address low-gravity testing, survival strategies for microorganisms in extreme environments, and the development of cell-free urease systems.
