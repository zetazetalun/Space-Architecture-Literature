## Abstract
Microbially induced carbonate precipitation (MICP) mimics the natural cementation process that occurs in various geological settings by using the bicarbonate minerals resulting from various bacterial metabolic pathways as cementing agents. This bio-technique can be used to manufacture so-called “bio-bricks,” which rival regular bricks in strength and durability. Recently, this process has been shown to have great potential as an energy-saving and cost-effective means of in situ resource utilization (ISRU) to produce construction materials for extraterrestrial human settlements. We review natural cementation, anaerobic and aerobic bacterial metabolic activities leading to calcium carbonate precipitation, the properties of lunar regolith, the production of bio-bricks, and potential research needs.

## 1 Introduction
Space exploration is making efforts to develop extraterrestrial human settlements. Given the high cost of transport from Earth (approx. $10,000 USD per pound), in situ resource utilization (ISRU) is an economic imperative. Main extraterrestrial processing methods currently include melting/sintering, combustion, dry-mix/steam injection, and cold pressing. However, the energy consumption of these methods is daunting. Bio-cementation is being progressively recognized as a green solution, using microorganisms to induce the precipitation of carbonate minerals for construction.

## 2 Microbial carbonate precipitation by different metabolic activities
Metabolic activities inducing carbonate precipitation are classified into aerobic and anaerobic groups. Microorganisms induce precipitation by increasing pH, increasing dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), or both. 

*   **Aerobic processes:** Photosynthesis, ureolysis, and ammonification.
*   **Anaerobic processes:** Sulfate reduction and denitrification.
*   **Methane Oxidation:** Can occur both aerobically and anaerobically.

Ureolysis is the most studied MICP process due to its less complex and higher calcite precipitation. The genus *Bacillus* (e.g., *Sporosarcina pasteurii*) is frequently used for its high levels of secreted urease.

## 3 Properties of sand-sized material on the moon
On the lunar surface, regolith refers to fragmental and unconsolidated rock material. The thickness of this layered regolith is estimated at about 2–15 m with a specific gravity ranging 2.9–3.5 g/cm³. The particle size is consistently distributed, with an average mean particle size D50 = 72 μm. Mineralogy includes plagioclase, pyroxene, ilmenite, and olivine.

## 4 Characteristics of artificial bio-bricks
The grain-size property of the starting materials is the main factor in laboratory experiments. Bio-bricks typically show unconfined compressive strengths ranging from 1 MPa to 23 MPa depending on the saturation and reaction conditions.

## 5 Research needs and future directions
*   **Environment Adaptation:** Microorganisms must be selected/engineered to survive extreme lunar conditions (radiation, vacuum, temperature).
*   **Gravity:** MICP must be tested in low-gravity environments as it affects nucleation.
*   **Resource Sourcing:** Human urine could serve as a source of urea for ureolysis-based MICP.
*   **Cell-free systems:** Urease-based systems might bypass the challenges of culturing live microbes.
