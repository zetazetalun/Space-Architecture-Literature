# An assumption of in situ resource utilization for “bio-bricks” in space exploration

**Authors:** Hongyan Zuo, Shuisong Ni, and Meiying Xu
**Journal:** Frontiers in Materials
**Type:** Mini Review
**Published:** 06 April 2023
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmats.2023.1155643

## Abstract
Microbially induced carbonate precipitation (MICP) mimics the natural cementation process that occurs in various geological settings by using the bicarbonate minerals resulting from various bacterial metabolic pathways as cementing agents. This bio-technique can be used to manufacture so-called “bio-bricks,” which rival regular bricks in strength and durability. This process has also been shown to have great potential as an energy-saving and cost-effective means of in situ resource utilization (ISRU) to produce construction materials for extraterrestrial human settlements such as lunar exploration. We review the metabolic activities leading to calcium carbonate precipitation, properties of lunar regolith, and the production of bio-bricks.

## 1 Introduction
Extraterrestrial settlements require ISRU due to the high cost of transporting material from Earth (~$10,000/lb to orbit). Traditional processing methods like melting/sintering are energy-intensive. Bio-cementation (MICP) is recognized as a green solution, using microorganisms to induce carbonate mineral precipitation for construction.

## 2 Microbial carbonate precipitation by different metabolic activities
Metabolic pathways for MICP include:
- **Aerobic:** Photosynthesis, Ureolysis (most studied), and Ammonification.
- **Anaerobic:** Sulfate reduction, Denitrification, and Methane oxidation.

### 2.1 Aerobic processes
Ureolysis is the enzymatic degradation of urea by ureases, producing carbonic acids and ammonia, which increases pH and dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) to induce precipitation. *Sporosarcina pasteurii* (formerly *Bacillus pasteurii*) is a primary model organism due to its high levels of secreted urease.

### 2.2 Anaerobic processes
Given the lack of oxygen on the moon, anaerobic processes like sulfate reduction and denitrification are critical. Denitrification has been evaluated as a suitable MICP method for soil reinforcement due to substrate solubility and carbonate yield.

## 3 Properties of sand-sized material on the moon
Lunar regolith is fragmental and unconsolidated rock material. The average mean particle size (D50) is approximately 72 μm, with a specific gravity of 2.9–3.5 g/cm³. Major mineral phases include plagioclase, pyroxene, ilmenite, and olivine. Lunar soil contains Ca, which can serve as a source for carbonate precipitation.

## 5 Research needs and future directions
- **Particle Size:** Lunar regolith (D50 = 72 μm) is smaller than the sands typically used in lab bio-brick experiments (0.36–0.73 mm).
- **Survival:** Microbes must be selected to survive extreme lunar conditions (gravity, radiation, temperature).
- **Resources:** Human urine could serve as a source of urea. Water ice in permanently shaded regions could save costs.
- **Cell-free systems:** Urease enzyme systems might bypass the challenges of culturing live microbes in situ.
