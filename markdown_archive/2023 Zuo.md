# An assumption of in situ resource utilization for “bio-bricks” in space exploration

**Authors:** Hongyan Zuo, Shuisong Ni, and Meiying Xu  
**Publication:** Frontiers in Materials (Biomaterials and Bio-Inspired Materials)  
**Date:** April 6, 2023  
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmats.2023.1155643

## Abstract
Microbially induced carbonate precipitation (MICP) mimics natural cementation using bicarbonate minerals from bacterial metabolic pathways. This bio-technique can produce “bio-bricks” that rival regular bricks in strength. This review discusses metabolic activities (aerobic and anaerobic), lunar regolith properties, and the production of bio-bricks for extraterrestrial human settlements.

## 1 Introduction
NASA estimates it costs ~$10,000 USD to launch one pound of material to Earth orbit. In situ resource utilization (ISRU) is an economic imperative for sustainable life support and construction. Traditional lunar construction methods (melting, sintering, combustion) are energy-intensive. Bio-cementation offers a 'green' alternative using microorganisms to precipitate carbonate minerals.

## 2 Microbial carbonate precipitation by different metabolic activities
Since the Moon lacks oxygen, anaerobic processes are particularly relevant. Metabolic pathways inducing precipitation include:
- **Aerobic:** Photosynthesis, Ureolysis (the most studied), and Ammonification.
- **Anaerobic:** Sulfate reduction and Denitrification (considered a highly suitable MICP method).
- **Methane Oxidation:** Both aerobic and anaerobic.

## 3 Properties of sand-sized material on the moon
Lunar regolith is fragmental, unconsolidated rock material. Its thickness is ~2–15 m with a specific gravity of 2.9–3.5 g/cm³. Key minerals include plagioclase, pyroxene, ilmenite, and olivine. The average mean particle size ($D_{50}$) is 72 μm, significantly smaller than the sands typically used in Earth-based MICP experiments (0.42–0.73 mm).

## 4 Characteristics of artificial bio-bricks
Bio-bricks produced in labs show unconfined compressive strengths (UCS) ranging from **1 MPa to 23 MPa**. Performance varies based on saturation conditions, particle size, and the specific microbial species (typically *Sporosarcina pasteurii*).

## 5 Research needs and future directions
- **Microgravity Impact:** Microbial survival and dynamic cementation must be tested in low-gravity.
- **Water Scarcity:** While lunar ice exists, the process requires substantial water.
- **Urea Source:** Human urine could serve as a nitrogen/urea source (Lambert and Randall, 2019).
- **Cell-free Systems:** Cell-free urease systems might bypass challenges of culturing microbes in harsh environments.
