# An assumption of in situ resource utilization for “bio-bricks” in space exploration

**Authors:** Hongyan Zuo, Shuisong Ni, Meiying Xu
**Published:** 06 April 2023
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmats.2023.1155643

## Abstract
Microbially induced carbonate precipitation (MICP) mimics the natural cementation process that occurs in various geological settings by using the bicarbonate minerals resulting from various bacterial metabolic pathways as cementing agents. This bio-technique can be used to manufacture so-called “bio-bricks,” which rival regular bricks in strength and durability. Recently, this process has also been shown to have great potential as an energy-saving and cost-effective means of in situ resource utilization (ISRU) to produce construction materials; these can be utilized for extraterrestrial human settlements for space programs such as lunar exploration.

## 1 Introduction
Space exploration has been making great efforts to develop extraterrestrial human settlements. However, as estimated by NASA, it costs about $10,000 USD to put only one pound of material in Earth orbit. Thus, the in situ resource utilization (ISRU) of raw materials is an economic imperative. ISRU covers construction materials for shelter. Bio-cementation is being progressively recognized as a green solution for the application of bonding in construction materials.

## 2 Microbial carbonate precipitation by different metabolic activities
Microorganisms induce carbonate precipitation either by increasing pH, increasing dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), or both. Metabolic pathways include:
- **Aerobic:** Photosynthesis, ureolysis, and ammonification.
- **Anaerobic:** Sulfate reduction and denitrification.

## 3 Properties of sand-sized material on the moon
On the lunar surface, regolith refers to fragmental and unconsolidated rock material. The mineralogy includes plagioclase, pyroxene, ilmenite, olivine, and impact glass. The particle size distribution ranges from 0.002 to 4 mm, with an average mean particle size D50 = 72 μm.

## 4 Characteristics of artificial bio-bricks
The starting material grain size is the main factor in compressive strength. Bio-bricks produced in laboratories typically use grain sizes in the range of 0.42–0.73 mm.

## 5 Research needs and future directions
- Regolith as a suitable material for MICP based on grain size and mineralogy.
- Challenges include microbial survival in extreme lunar conditions.
- Potential use of human urine as a source of urea for in situ production.
- Need for low-gravity testing and upscaling from laboratory to field applications.
