# An assumption of in situ resource utilization for “bio-bricks” in space exploration

**Authors:** Hongyan Zuo, Shuisong Ni, and Meiying Xu  
**Journal:** Frontiers in Materials  
**Published:** 06 April 2023  
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmats.2023.1155643

## Abstract
Microbially induced carbonate precipitation (MICP) mimics natural cementation by using bacterial metabolic pathways to produce bicarbonate minerals as cementing agents. This technique can manufacture "bio-bricks" which rival regular bricks in strength and durability. This review explores the potential of MICP as an energy-saving and cost-effective means of in situ resource utilization (ISRU) for extraterrestrial human settlements, such as lunar exploration.

## 1 Introduction
Space exploration faces high costs for transporting materials ($10,000 per pound to Earth orbit). ISRU is essential for sustainability. Conventional processing methods like melting, sintering, or cold pressing are energy-intensive. Bio-cementation offers a 'green' alternative using microorganisms to induce mineral precipitation for bonding construction materials.

## 2 Microbial carbonate precipitation by different metabolic activities
Metabolic activities inducing carbonate precipitation are classified into aerobic and anaerobic groups.

### 2.1 Aerobic processes: Photosynthesis, ureolysis, and ammonification
- **Photosynthesis:** Cyanobacteria store Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions, increasing pH to induce precipitation.
- **Ureolysis:** Enzymatic degradation of urea produces carbonic acid and ammonia, increasing both DIC (dissolved inorganic carbon) and pH. The genus *Bacillus* (e.g., *Sporosarcina pasteurii*) is most commonly used due to high urease secretion.
- **Ammonification:** Oxidization of amino acids to form NH₃ and CO₃²⁻.

### 2.2 Anaerobic process: Sulfate reduction and denitrification
- **Sulfate reduction:** Reducing sulfate to sulfide, releasing Ca²⁺ from gypsum and increasing DIC.
- **Denitrification:** Uses nitrate as an electron acceptor. It has been identified as a suitable MICP method for soil reinforcement due to substrate solubility and carbonate yield.

### 2.3 Aerobic and anaerobic methane oxidation
Methane serves as an electron donor, increasing DIC concentration which favors mineral precipitation in alkaline environments.

## 3 Properties of sand-sized material on the moon
Lunar regolith is fragmental, unconsolidated rock material (lunar soil). 
- **Thickness:** 2–15 m.
- **Specific Gravity:** 2.9–3.5 g/cm³.
- **Chemistry:** Rich in Si, Al, Fe, Mg, Ca, Ti, Na, K.
- **Mineralogy:** Plagioclase, pyroxene, ilmenite, olivine, and impact glass.
- **Grain Size:** Average D50 is 72 μm (sandy silt/silty sand), which is smaller than the sands typically used in Earth-based MICP experiments (0.36–0.73 mm).

## 5 Research needs and future directions
- **Microbial Survivability:** Extreme lunar conditions (radiation, temperature, micro-gravity) require robust species or the use of spores/cysts.
- **Resource Sourcing:** Urea could be sourced from human urine; water ice exists in permanently shaded regions.
- **Scaling:** Upscaling from lab to field requires addressing technical difficulties in homogeneous treatment and low-gravity performance.
