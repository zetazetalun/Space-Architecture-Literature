# An assumption of in situ resource utilization for “bio-bricks” in space exploration

**Hongyan Zuo**, **Shuisong Ni**, and **Meiying Xu**

**Published:** 06 April 2023  
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmats.2023.1155643

## Abstract
Microbially induced carbonate precipitation (MICP) mimics the natural cementation process that occurs in various geological settings by using the bicarbonate minerals resulting from various bacterial metabolic pathways as cementing agents. This bio-technique can be used to manufacture so-called “bio-bricks,” which rival regular bricks in strength and durability. Recently, this process has also been shown to have great potential as an energy-saving and cost-effective means of in situ resource utilization (ISRU) to produce construction materials; these can be utilized for extraterrestrial human settlements for space programs such as lunar exploration.

## 1 Introduction
Space exploration requires the production of construction materials for shelter. The energy consumption of traditional processing methods like melting/sintering, combustion, and dry mix/steam injection is daunting. Bio-cementation is being progressively recognized as a green solution. This review focuses specifically on the metabolism of the MICP process, the properties of lunar regolith particles, and the characteristics of artificial bio-bricks.

## 2 Microbial carbonate precipitation by different metabolic activities
Microorganisms induce carbonate precipitation by increasing pH, increasing dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), or both. Metabolic pathways include:
- **Aerobic:** Photosynthesis, ureolysis, and ammonification.
- **Anaerobic:** Sulfate reduction, denitrification, and methane oxidation.

Ureolysis is the most studied MICP process due to its less complex and higher calcite precipitation. The genus *Bacillus* (e.g., *Sporosarcina pasteurii*) is commonly used.

## 3 Properties of sand-sized material on the moon
Lunar regolith is fragmental and unconsolidated rock material. The chemistry shows Si, Al, Fe, Mg, Ca, Ti, Na, K, Mn, and Cr as major elements. Geotechnical particle size parameters ($D_{50}$) for lunar soil are around 72 μm.

## 5 Research needs and future directions
- **Microbial Survival:** Conditions on the Moon (vacuum, radiation, low gravity) are harsh.
- **Water and Urea:** Substantial water and urea (potentially from human urine) are needed for the process.
- **Upscaling:** Transitioning from lab-scale to field application presents technical difficulties.
- **Cell-free systems:** Using active enzymes instead of live cultures to avoid survival issues.
