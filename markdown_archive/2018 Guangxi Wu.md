# Fabrication of Flexible Thermoelectric Energy Harvesting System

**Authors:** Guangxi Wu; Ferin Neff; and Xiong (Bill) Yu  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, pp. 1203-1215  
**Publisher:** © ASCE

## ABSTRACT
Thermoelectric energy harvesting is promising to provide sustainable energy source in the extraterrestrial environment. This paper describes procedures incurred in the fabrication of thermoelectric energy harvester on flexible substrates. An electrically parallel structure is proposed, which features higher energy efficiency than the counterpart electrically series structure based on theoretical analyses. As proof of concept, flexible thermoelectric harvesters in electrically parallel and series structures are prepared with thick film printing procedures. The performance of elements and device with different architectures are characterized. Both analytical and experimental comparison imply a promising improvement of energy conversion efficiency by using the electrically parallel structure.

## INTRODUCTION
Thermoelectric modules (TEMs) when used as generators (TEGs) are capable of providing continuous energy for extraterrestrial explorations, where sun light is not always available or intense enough for solar cells to work. For example, the solar brightness on Mars and Jupiter is as weak as 45% and 4%, respectively. TEGs driven by the decay of suitable radioactive materials (RTGs) have received interest due to reliability. RTGs are solid-state devices, generating no noise, vibration, or torque, making them suitable for autonomous missions in space and on planetary surfaces.

Naturally existing temperature gradients on spacecraft or extraterrestrial vehicle bodies caused by drastic environmental temperature changes (e.g., -153°C to 123°C on the Moon) have not been effectively leveraged. Covering these surfaces with flexible TEGs could significantly boost energy provision.

## ELECTRICALLY PARALLEL TEM EFFICIENCY
This paper proposes an alternative TEM structure where the legs are connected in parallel both thermally and electrically. The legs must be made from the same type of TE materials (p-type or n-type) to keep the same polarity. Analytical results suggest that the potential of TE materials will not be wasted by the mismatch between p-type and n-type legs, increasing efficiency by up to 32% for certain material sets.

## TEM FABRICATION PROCESS USING FILM PRINTING TECHNOLOGY

### Material Preparation
- **Material:** Bismuth Telluride (Bi2Te3), both n-type and p-type.
- **Process:** Nanocomposite bulk processing emulated via ball milling to increase the figure-of-merit (ZT) by limiting phonon transport through grain boundary scattering.
- **Ink:** Ground powder used as filler in an epoxy matrix (EPON Resin 863) with a powder-to-epoxy ratio of 4.5:1.

### Printing Process
- **Substrate:** Polyimide (Kapton), chosen for thermal stability during curing.
- **Equipment:** MPM TF-100 Thick Film Printer in a class 10,000 clean room.
- **Sequence:** 
  1. Gold electrodes printed and cured at 110°C.
  2. TE legs printed using stencils.
  3. TE inks cured at 110°C for 24 hours.
  4. Wires attached using silver epoxy cured at 150°C.

## CONCLUSION
The paper successfully demonstrated the fabrication of flexible TEMs using thick-film printing. Analytical and preliminary experimental results show that electrically parallel structures are promising for improving the output power of TEGs by mitigating material mismatch. The use of a sandwiched multilayered structure is suggested for large-area production to simplify fabrication further.
