# Fabrication of Flexible Thermoelectric Energy Harvesting System

**Authors:** Guangxi Wu, Ferin Neff, and Xiong (Bill) Yu

**Affiliation:** Case Western Reserve University

## Abstract
Thermoelectric energy harvesting is promising to provide sustainable energy source in the extraterrestrial environment. This paper describes procedures incurred in the fabrication of thermoelectric energy harvester on flexible substrates. An electrically parallel structure is proposed, which features higher energy efficiency than the counterpart electrically series structure based on theoretical analyses. As proof of concept, flexible thermoelectric harvesters in electrically parallel and series structures are prepared with thick film printing procedures. The performance of elements and device with different architectures are characterized. Both analytical and experimental comparison imply a promising improvement of energy conversion efficiency by using the electrically parallel structure.

## Introduction
Thermoelectric modules (TEMs) or generators (TEGs) provide continuous energy for extraterrestrial explorations where sunlight is insufficient (e.g., Mars, Jupiter). While Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generators (RTGs) are reliable, they suffer from low conversion efficiency. This research proposes leveraging naturally existing temperature gradients on spacecraft or extraterrestrial vehicle bodies using flexible TEGs that can be printed over large areas. 

## Electrically Series vs. Parallel Efficiency
In conventional series TEMs, mismatch between p-type and n-type material properties (Seebeck coefficient, resistivity, thermal conductivity) impairs the module's figure-of-merit ($ZT_{module}$). The research proposes an electrically parallel structure using the same type of TE material for both legs, allowing the module to achieve the maximum $ZT$ of the superior material.

## Fabrication Process
1. **Material Preparation:** Bi2Te3 coarse powder was ball-milled into nanocomposites to increase $ZT$ via grain boundary scattering.
2. **TE Ink Preparation:** Ground powder mixed with epoxy matrix (EPON Resin 863) at a 4.5:1 ratio.
3. **Screen/Stencil Printing:** Metal electrodes (gold ink) and TE legs were printed on flexible polyimide substrates using mesh screens and stainless steel stencils.
4. **Curing:** Electrodes cured at 110 °C for 10 min; TE inks cured at 110 °C for 24 hours.

## Characterization
Performance was measured using a trans-impedance amplifier circuit under a 50°C temperature gradient (heat source vs. iced water sink). The n-type electrically parallel structure demonstrated higher maximum output power than the p-type parallel or the conventional series structures.

## Conclusion
Electrically parallel TEM structures are a prospective solution for improving energy conversion efficiency and simplifying the fabrication of large-area implementation for space applications.
