# Fabrication of Flexible Thermoelectric Energy Harvesting System

**Authors:** Guangxi Wu, Ferin Neff, and Xiong (Bill) Yu
**Source:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE

## Abstract
Thermoelectric energy harvesting is promising to provide a sustainable energy source in the extraterrestrial environment. This paper describes procedures incurred in the fabrication of thermoelectric energy harvester on flexible substrates. An electrically parallel structure is proposed, which features higher energy efficiency than the counterpart electrically series structure based on theoretical analyses. As proof of concept, flexible thermoelectric harvesters in electrically parallel and series structures are prepared with thick film printing procedures. Both analytical and experimental comparison imply a promising improvement of energy conversion efficiency by using the electrically parallel structure.

## Introduction
Thermoelectric modules (TEMs) when used as generators (TEGs) are capable of providing continuous energy for extraterrestrial explorations, where sunlight is not always available or intense enough for solar cells (e.g., Mars and Jupiter). Radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) have been used for decades due to their reliability, but naturally occurring temperature gradients on spacecraft or extraterrestrial vehicles (ranging from -153°C to 123°C on the Moon) remain under-leveraged. This paper proposes flexible TEGs to cover vehicle or habitat surfaces to boost energy production.

## Electrically Parallel vs. Series Efficiency

### Series Structure
Conventionally, TEMs consist of alternant p-legs and n-legs connected in series. The efficiency is limited by the mismatch of material properties (Seebeck coefficient, electrical resistivity, and thermal conductivity) between p-type and n-type materials. For bismuth telluride ($Bi_2Te_3$), this mismatch leads to an attenuation factor ($A$) of approximately 0.76, wasting 24% of the material's potential.

### Parallel Structure
This paper proposes a structure where legs are connected in parallel both thermally and electrically. By using the same type of material (all p-type or all n-type) for both legs, the figure-of-merit ($ZT_{module}$) can reach the maximum value of the specific material, avoiding mismatch losses. Analytical results suggest a potential efficiency increase of 32% over conventional series structures.

## Fabrication Process

### Material Preparation
- **Material:** $Bi_2Te_3$ (n-type and p-type).
- **Processing:** Ball milling to create nanocomposites, which increases $ZT$ by limiting phonon transport through grain boundary scattering.
- **Ink Formulation:** Ground powder mixed with an epoxy matrix system (EPON Resin 863, MHHPA hardener, and AC-8 catalyst) at a 4.5:1 ratio.

### Printing Technology
- **Method:** Stencil printing on polyimide (thermally stable) substrates.
- **Electrodes:** Printed using commercial gold ink (Ercon E4464).
- **Curing:** Electrodes cured at 110°C for 10 min; TE inks cured at 110°C for 24 hours.
- **Assembly:** After printing, the flexible polyimide sheet is rolled into a compact TEM.

## Performance Characterization
Testing was conducted using a printed platinum resistor on ceramic as the heat source and an aluminum/iced water heat sink (0°C). Measurements were taken using a custom trans-impedance amplifier circuit and a CR1000 data acquisition device. Results confirmed that n-type electrically parallel structures provided higher maximum output power than the p-type counterparts and outperformed the series structure, validating the parallel design's advantage.

## Conclusion
The electrically parallel TEM structure is a prospective solution to improve energy conversion efficiency and simplify the fabrication of large-area energy harvesting systems for space infrastructure. Future work suggests a sandwiched multilayered structure for mass production.
