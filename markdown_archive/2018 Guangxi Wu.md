# Fabrication of Flexible Thermoelectric Energy Harvesting System

**Authors:** Guangxi Wu; Ferin Neff; and Xiong (Bill) Yu
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE

## ABSTRACT
Thermoelectric energy harvesting is promising to provide sustainable energy source in the extraterrestrial environment. This paper describes procedures incurred in the fabrication of thermoelectric energy harvester on flexible substrates. An electrically parallel structure is proposed, which features higher energy efficiency than the counterpart electrically series structure based on theoretical analyses. As proof of concept, flexible thermoelectric harvesters in electrically parallel and series structures are prepared with thick film printing procedures. The performance of elements and device with different architectures are characterized. Both analytical and experimental comparison imply a promising improvement of energy conversion efficiency by using the electrically parallel structure.

## INTRODUCTION
Thermoelectric modules (TEMs) when used as generators (TEGs) are capable of providing continuous energy for extraterrestrial explorations, where sun light is not always available or intense enough for solar cells to work. For example, the solar brightness on Mars and Jupiter is as weak as 45% and 4%, respectively. TEGs driven by the decay of suitable radioactive materials, such as radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs), have received the most interest. In addition, RTGs are compact, rugged, radiation-resistant, and scalable.

TEGs have suffered from low energy conversion efficiency. This paper proposes an electrically parallel TEM structure, which is promising to not only improve the energy conversion efficiency, but also simplify the fabrication process of large area flexible TEGs.

## ELECTRICALLY SERIES TEM EFFICIENCY
Conventionally, a TEM is composed of alternant p-legs and n-legs connected in series both electrically and thermally. The maximum possible energy efficiency is depicted as:

$$\eta_{max} = \frac{T_H - T_C}{T_H} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{1 + Z\bar{T}_{module}} - 1}{\sqrt{1 + Z\bar{T}_{module}} + \frac{T_C}{T_H}}$$

The calculation implies that the overall TEM’s figure-of-merit is highly dependent on whether p-type and n-type material properties are matched. In the example provided, 24% of the potential of the higher-performing material is wasted due to mismatch.

## ELECTRICALLY PARALLEL TEM EFFICIENCY
This paper proposes an alternative TEM structure where the legs are connected in parallel both thermally and electrically. The legs must be made from the same type of TE materials (p-type or n-type) to maintain polarity. Analytical results show that the parallel structure allows the module's figure-of-merit to reach the maximum value of the specific material used, avoiding mismatch losses.

## TEM FABRICATION PROCESS USING FILM PRINTING TECHNOLOGY

### Material Preparation
The TE material used is Bi2Te3 (n-type and p-type). Nanocomposite bulk processing was emulated through ball milling to increase $ZT$ by limiting phonon transport via grain boundary scattering.

### TE Ink Preparation
Ground TE powder was used as filler in an epoxy matrix (EPON Resin 863, MHHPA hardener, and AC-8 catalyst). The powder-to-epoxy ratio was 4.5:1.

### Printing Process
1. Substrate: Polyimide (Kapton).
2. Electrodes: Printed using commercial gold ink (Ercon E4464) via mesh screens.
3. TE Legs: Printed using stencils and the prepared TE inks.
4. Curing: Electrodes cured at 110°C (10 mins); TE inks cured at 110°C (24 hours).
5. Assembly: The printed planar device is rolled up to form the final TEM.

## CONCLUSION
This paper proposes an electrically parallel TEM structure to improve output power. Preliminary results comply with analytical analysis, showing that parallel structures can mitigate efficiency losses from material mismatch. The flexible, rolled-up structure is a prospective solution for large-area implementation on spacecraft bodies or extraterrestrial vehicles.
