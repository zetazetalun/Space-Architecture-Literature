# Fabrication of Flexible Thermoelectric Energy Harvesting System

**Authors:** Guangxi Wu, Ferin Neff, and Xiong (Bill) Yu  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE  

## Abstract
Thermoelectric energy harvesting is promising to provide a sustainable energy source in the extraterrestrial environment. This paper describes procedures incurred in the fabrication of thermoelectric energy harvester on flexible substrates. An electrically parallel structure is proposed, which features higher energy efficiency than the counterpart electrically series structure based on theoretical analyses. As proof of concept, flexible thermoelectric harvesters in electrically parallel and series structures are prepared with thick film printing procedures. The performance of elements and device with different architectures are characterized. Both analytical and experimental comparison imply a promising improvement of energy conversion efficiency by using the electrically parallel structure.

## Introduction
Thermoelectric modules (TEMs) used as generators (TEGs) provide continuous energy for extraterrestrial explorations where sunlight is insufficient (e.g., Mars at 45% solar brightness, Jupiter at 4%). Radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) have been reliable for 50 years but suffer from low efficiency. This research explores leveraging the extreme temperature gradients on spacecraft or planetary surfaces (e.g., Mars: 20°C to -73°C; Moon: 123°C to -153°C) by covering surfaces with flexible TEGs.

## Technical Analysis
- **Electrically Series TEM:** Traditionally composed of alternating p-legs and n-legs connected in series. Efficiency is limited by material property mismatches (Seebeck coefficient, electrical resistivity, thermal conductivity).
- **Electrically Parallel TEM:** The paper proposes connecting legs in parallel (both thermally and electrically) using the same type of material (p-type or n-type) to avoid mismatch losses. Theoretical gains reach up to 32% efficiency improvement.

## Fabrication Process
1. **Material Preparation:** Bismuth Telluride (Bi2Te3) was ball-milled to create nanocomposites, enhancing the Figure-of-Merit (ZT) by scattering phonons at grain boundaries.
2. **TE Ink Preparation:** Ground p-type and n-type powders were mixed with an epoxy matrix (EPON Resin 863) at a 4.5:1 ratio.
3. **Screen/Stencil Printing:** Mesh screens were designed for electrodes (using gold ink), and stainless steel stencils were used for TE legs.
4. **Substrate:** Flexible polyimide (Kapton) was used for its thermal stability.
5. **Assembly:** The printed device is rolled up to form the final TEM configuration.

## Key Findings
- **Efficiency:** Analytical models show that the parallel structure allows the module to reach the maximum figure-of-merit of the best available material leg, rather than being downgraded by the weaker leg in a series pair.
- **Performance:** Experimental I-V and W-V curves confirmed that the n-type electrically parallel structure outperformed the p-type and traditional series structures in output power.
- **Feasibility:** Thick-film printing is a viable method for manufacturing flexible, large-area energy harvesters for space applications.

## Conclusion
Electrically parallel TEMs are a prospective solution to improve energy conversion efficiency and simplify the fabrication process for large-area implementation on space vehicle/habitat surfaces.
