# Mechanical Properties and Additive Manufacturing of Alkali-Activated Lunar Regolith in Artificial Lunar Environments

**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Yi Wei Daniel Tay, Sean Gip Lim, Ming Jen Tan

## Abstract
Lunar base construction has become a cutting-edge and multi-disciplinary research issue worldwide. The development of sustainable lunar infrastructure is essential for future exploration and a major challenge for constructing on the Moon is the availability of suitable building materials. In this regard, the research on alkali-activated lunar regolith simulants (AALRS) is a significant breakthrough. In this paper, the mechanical properties and additive manufacturing of AALRS are investigated with reference to the lunar environments. The study has shown that the type of alkali, curing temperature and vacuum exposure have a prominent effect on the solidification behaviour of AALRS. Furthermore, the acceptable long-term properties of silicate-based AALRS were also revealed after undergoing several lunar diel temperature cycles. The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis have indicated the presence of two solidification mechanisms, geopolymerization and dehydration, within AALRS, which have contributed to its microstructure stability. The development of an innovative 3D printer for AALRS is a crucial step towards the future construction of a lunar base.

## 1 Introduction
The lunar base has been proposed by many countries as a multi-disciplinary, cutting-edge and strategic research issue. However, it is crucial for sustainable construction techniques using in-situ resources to be developed due to the huge launching cost and lunar severe environments. The use of lunar geopolymer, which is an alkali-activated material made up of an aluminosilicate network, has shown promising results in terms of adequate strength, durability, and moderate processing conditions.

## 3 Solidification Properties
The study identifies that sodium silicate (SS) with a modulus ≥ 2 is preferred as the alkali activator. SS2.0-based AALRS established 4.61 MPa of bending strength and 15.5 MPa of compressive strength within 5 hours in vacuum environments.

## 4 Long-Term Properties
Under simulated lunar diel temperature cycles, silicate-based AALRS enables good durability. While bending performance showed a ~20% reduction after one cycle, both bending and compressive strengths sustained with only slight decreases (within 5%) through four cycles.

## 5 Microstructure Characterization
Two hardening patterns were identified:
1. **Geopolymerization:** Needs high temperature and sealing, forming N-A-S-H products.
2. **Dehydration:** More feasible in lunar environments, where a Si-rich film connects particles, promoting early strength in a vacuum.

## 6 Future Prospects on Additive Manufacturing of AALRS
A conceptual 3D printer design is proposed featuring a robotic arm and a specialized printhead. The printer would extrude and compress damp powdery ink through roller systems to allow for dehydration and rapid hardening after deposition.

## 7 Conclusions
The paper concludes that alkali-activated lunar regolith is a viable construction material. The discovery of the dehydration hardening mechanism allows for a prospective way to sustainably construct on the Moon using simplified additive manufacturing techniques.
