# Mechanical Properties and Additive Manufacturing of Alkali-Activated Lunar Regolith in Artificial Lunar Environments

**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Yi Wei Daniel Tay, Sean Gip Lim, and Ming Jen Tan

**Abstract:** Lunar base construction has become a cutting-edge and multi-disciplinary research issue worldwide. The development of sustainable lunar infrastructure is essential for future exploration and a major challenge for constructing on the Moon is the availability of suitable building materials. In this regard, the research on alkali-activated lunar regolith simulants (AALRS) is a significant breakthrough. In this paper, the mechanical properties and additive manufacturing of AALRS are investigated with reference to the lunar environments. The study has shown that the type of alkali, curing temperature and vacuum exposure have a prominent effect on the solidification behaviour of AALRS. Furthermore, the acceptable long-term properties of silicate-based AALRS were also revealed after undergoing several lunar diel temperature cycles. The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis have indicated the presence of two solidification mechanisms, geopolymerization and dehydration, within AALRS, which have contributed to its microstructure stability. The development of an innovative 3D printer for AALRS is a crucial step towards the future construction of a lunar base. Several conceptualized printers that can extrude, compress, and deposit treated lunar regolith will be presented. Additive manufacturing of lunar regolith has the potential to reduce the cost and increase the efficiency of building on the Moon.

## 1 Introduction
Sustainable construction techniques using in-situ resources (ISRU) are crucial due to high launch costs and severe lunar environments. Recent schemes include sintered regolith, sulfur concrete, and lunar geopolymers. Alkali-activated lunar regolith simulant (AALRS) shows promise due to adequate strength and durability under moderate processing conditions. This study focuses on early and long-term mechanical properties under artificial lunar environments and proposes a conceptual 3D printing method.

## 2 Materials and Experiments
The lunar regolith simulant (LRS) used was made from volcanic ash with a mean particle diameter of ~40 μm. Sodium hydroxide (SH) and sodium silicate (SS) were used as alkali activators.

## 3 Solidification Properties
Solidification is significantly influenced by alkali type, temperature, and vacuum exposure. Sodium silicate (SS)-based AALRS (specifically SS2.0) exhibited superior solidification in vacuum compared to sodium hydroxide (SH)-based versions. SS2.0-based AALRS established 4.61 MPa bending strength and 15.5 MPa compressive strength within 5 hours.

## 4 Long-Term Properties
SS-based AALRS showed good durability under artificial lunar environments. After 4 lunar diel temperature cycles, bending and compressive strength decreased by less than 5%, maintaining structural integrity and acceptable performance.

## 5 Microstructure Characterization
Two mechanisms contribute to solidification: geopolymerization (forming N-A-S-H hydrates) and dehydration (forming a Si-rich film). In vacuum, dehydration of SS-based AALRS promotes early strength by creating smooth membranate products connecting particles.

## 6 Future Prospects on Additive Manufacturing of AALRS
A specific 3D printer is conceptualized involving a robotic arm with a transport pipe and a printhead designed to reduce water evaporation. The process involves extruding damp lunar regolith and compressing it through roller systems onto surfaces to allow rapid hardening via dehydration.

## 7 Conclusions
Sodium silicate with a modulus ≥ 2 is preferred for lunar environments due to its stable solidification in vacuum. The proposed innovative 3D printer design for extruding and compressing treated regolith provides a prospective pathway for sustainable lunar construction.
