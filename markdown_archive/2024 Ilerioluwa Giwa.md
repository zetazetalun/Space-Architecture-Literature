# 3D printed sulfur-regolith concrete performance evaluation for waterless extraterrestrial robotic construction

**Journal:** Automation in Construction 165 (2024) 105571  
**Authors:** Ilerioluwa Giwa, Mary Dempsey, Michael Fiske, Ali Kazemian

## Abstract
Sulfur concrete is a waterless construction material that offers great potential to replace Portland cement concrete (PCC) in extraterrestrial construction. The shape stability of 3D printed Martian sulfur-regolith concrete (SRC) was found to benefit from a lower substrate layer temperature. However, this comes at the cost of flexural strength, resulting in up to 53% strength loss. The printed SRC specimens demonstrated a significantly faster strength development rate (gaining about 85% of the ultimate strength after only 12 h) compared to the printed PCC. The printed SRC specimens also outperformed the PCC specimens in vacuum conditions at higher temperatures. Furthermore, modifying the SRC materials with Dicyclopentadiene (DCPD) resulted in up to 44% strength increase and minimized the sublimation rate of the printed specimens in vacuum, especially at an elevated temperature.

## 1. Introduction
To enable long-term presence on the Moon and Mars, infrastructure such as landing pads, habitats, and protective shields are needed. Conventional construction methods cannot be adopted due to the lack of a human workforce and harsh conditions. Extrusion-based Construction 3D Printing (C3DP) holds significant potential for autonomous construction. While Portland cement concrete (PCC) is common on Earth, it requires significant water. Sulfur concrete (SC) is a waterless alternative using elemental sulfur, which can be extracted from Martian or Lunar surface deposits via in-situ resource utilization (ISRU).

## 3. Methodology
### 3.1. Materials and mixture proportions
*   **Binder:** Elemental sulfur powder (99.9% purity).
*   **Aggregate:** Mars Global Simulant (MGS-1).
*   **Modifier:** Dicyclopentadiene (DCPD) at 10% of sulfur weight.
*   **Control:** Type I/II Portland cement concrete (PCC) with limestone powder and silica sand.

### 3.2. Printing Systems
Two custom gantry-type extrusion systems were used: one designed for high-temperature materials (SRC/MSRC) and another for cementitious materials (PCC).

## 4. Key Results and Discussion
### 4.1. Mechanical Performance
*   **Strength Development:** SRC reached ~85% of its 7-day strength in 12 hours, while PCC reached only ~19% in the same timeframe.
*   **DCPD Modification:** MSRC (modified with DCPD) showed a 44% increase in compressive strength (58.5 MPa) and 34% in flexural strength (9.52 MPa) compared to unmodified SRC.
*   **Recyclability:** Printed SRC and MSRC specimens were successfully remelted and reprinted without any significant loss in flexural strength.

### 4.2. Performance in Vacuum (55 °C)
*   **Weight Loss:** SRC weight loss increased at 55 °C due to sublimation (0.92% after 7 days). MSRC significantly reduced this rate (85% lower weight loss than SRC).
*   **PCC Failure:** PCC suffered ~78% flexural strength loss in vacuum at 55 °C due to outgassing of free moisture, which hindered hydration.
*   **Vacuum Resistance:** 4-hour old PCC specimens imploded in vacuum, while SRC specimens showed no visible damage, demonstrating superior early-age resilience.

## 5. Conclusions
1.  SRC is a viable waterless ISRU material for off-world robotic construction.
2.  Substrate temperature is critical: lower temperatures improve shape stability but can reduce interlayer bond strength by up to 53%.
3.  SRC provides significantly higher early strength and vacuum resilience compared to PCC.
4.  DCPD modification effectively mitigates sulfur sublimation and enhances mechanical properties.
5.  SRC materials are fully recyclable through remelting, supporting sustainable long-term habitat construction.
