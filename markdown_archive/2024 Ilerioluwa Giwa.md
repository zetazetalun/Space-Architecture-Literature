# 3D printed sulfur-regolith concrete performance evaluation for waterless extraterrestrial robotic construction

**Journal:** Automation in Construction 165 (2024) 105571  
**Authors:** Ilerioluwa Giwa, Mary Dempsey, Michael Fiske, Ali Kazemian  
**Published:** 18 June 2024

## Abstract
By leveraging the capabilities of construction 3D printing, building structures in harsh extraterrestrial environments is conceivable. Sulfur concrete is a waterless construction material that offers great potential to replace Portland cement concrete (PCC) in extraterrestrial construction. The shape stability of 3D printed Martian sulfur-regolith concrete (SRC) was found to benefit from a lower substrate layer temperature. However, this comes at the cost of flexural strength, resulting in up to 53% strength loss. The printed SRC specimens demonstrated a significantly faster strength development rate (gaining about 85% of the ultimate strength after only 12 h) compared to the printed PCC. The printed SRC specimens also outperformed the PCC specimens in vacuum conditions at higher temperatures. Furthermore, modifying the SRC materials with Dicyclopentadiene resulted in up to 44% strength increase and minimized the sublimation rate of the printed specimens in vacuum, especially at an elevated temperature.

## 1. Introduction
To enable a long-term presence on the Moon and Mars, infrastructure such as landing pads, habitats, and protective shields are needed. Extrusion-based Construction 3D Printing (C3DP) holds significant potential for off-world construction using in-situ resources. Sulfur concrete (SC) is a waterless alternative that utilizes elemental sulfur (potentially extracted from planetary minerals) as a binder.

## 2. Materials and Methodology
### 2.1. Materials
*   **Binder:** Elemental sulfur powder (99.9% purity).
*   **Aggregate:** Mars Global Simulant (MGS-1), a basaltic soil analog.
*   **Modifier:** Dicyclopentadiene (DCPD) at 10% sulfur weight.
*   **Control:** Portland cement concrete (PCC).

### 2.2. Printing Parameters
*   **Printing Temperature (PT):** 130 °C and 150 °C.
*   **Interlayer Time Gap (I):** 45 s, 120 s, and 180 s.

### Mechanical Performance
*   **Strength Development:** Printed SRC gained ~85% of its ultimate strength within 12 hours, whereas PCC gained only ~19% in the same period.
*   **Interlayer Adhesion:** Higher extrusion temperatures (150 °C) significantly improved interlayer bond strength. Lowering substrate temperature to improve shape stability caused up to 53% loss in flexural strength.
*   **Vacuum Performance:** SRC outperformed PCC in vacuum. PCC experienced ~78% strength loss at 55 °C due to moisture evaporation and hydration failure. Modification with DCPD reduced sulfur sublimation rates by an order of magnitude.
*   **Recyclability:** SRC specimens were remelted and reprinted without significant loss of mechanical properties, supporting a circular construction economy.

## 4. Conclusions
Martian SRC is a feasible material for waterless additive manufacturing. The study demonstrates that process parameters must be optimized to balance shape stability and interlayer bonding. The rapid strength gain and recyclability make SRC a superior candidate compared to cement-based systems for early-stage planetary infrastructure.
