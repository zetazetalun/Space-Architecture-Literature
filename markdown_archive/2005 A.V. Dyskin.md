# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10–21  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2004.12.005

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of topological interlocking to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes (usually convex or nearly convex, such that no stress concentration develops) are arranged in such a way that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of extraterrestrial structures are considered: mortar-free structures built from specially engineered interlocking bricks (osteomorphic bricks) and modular extraterrestrial bases or space ships organized in topologically interlocking assemblies. 

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial construction architectures can be categorized into two major classes:
1. Structures built from basic elements such as bricks, manufactured in situ.
2. Modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units (assembled in orbit or on the surface).

Topological interlocking addresses the challenges of in situ manufacturing (e.g., lack of water for mortar, vacuum curing issues, and low precision of robotic assembly).

## 2. Principle of Topological Interlocking
Topological interlocking is achieved by a special choice of geometry and arrangement such that elements are locked by their neighbors. 
- **Tetrahedral Assemblies:** A plate-like assembly where movement is prevented by inclined faces.
- **Osteomorphic Bricks:** Bricks with curved surfaces that allow for self-adjustment and self-positioning, even with inaccurate initial placement.

## 3. Extraterrestrial Construction Based on Osteomorphic Bricks
### 3.1 Mortar-free structures
- **Self-adjustment:** The blocks do not require high-precision positioning, facilitating robotization.
- **Constraint:** These structures require peripheral constraint (compression). This can be provided by planetary topography (craters, pits, caverns) or artificial means (external frames, post-tensioning cables).
- **Roofing:** Standardized planar 'cassettes' or specialized curved blocks can be used for domes.
- **ISRU Production:** Bricks can be produced via sintering regolith in moulds (microwave or external heating) or Selective Laser Sintering (SLS).

### 3.2 Sensitivity to Imperfections
Topological interlocking is insensitive to surface imperfections or dust adherence. Variations in brick height can be compensated for by adjusting cable tension in post-tensioned assemblies.

### 3.3 Radiation Protection
Double-skin walls can be used to prevent radiation leakage through mortar-free joints. The cavity can be filled with bulk regolith.

## 4. Protective Cladding
Osteomorphic tiles can be mapped onto non-planar surfaces (e.g., spacecraft hulls) to provide high fracture resistance. 

## 5. Modular Structures
Modular units shaped as tetrahedra, cubes, or octahedra can form interlocking clusters. 
- **Assembly on Uneven Ground:** Lateral force application can align modular ships/units into a cohesive structure even on unprepared terrain.
- **Serviceability:** Hexagon-tiling based structures allow for the removal and replacement of individual faulty modules via a specific path.

## 6. Conclusion
1. Osteomorphic bricks allow mortar-free, self-adjusting, and fracture-tolerant construction.
2. Peripheral constraints can be supplied by topography or post-tensioning.
3. Interlocking modules facilitate assembly on uneven ground.
4. Interlocking spacecraft modules offer flexibility for long-distance travel and multi-object exploration.
