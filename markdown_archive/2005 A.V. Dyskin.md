# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10–21
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2004.12.005

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of topological interlocking to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes are arranged such that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of extraterrestrial structures are considered: mortar-free structures built from engineered 'osteomorphic bricks' and modular assemblies for extraterrestrial bases or space ships. The self-adjusting property of these bricks permits erecting structures which tolerate low precision of production and assembly, making the method suitable for in situ produced bricks and low-cost assembling machinery.

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial structures are categorized into two major classes:
1. Structures built from basic elements (bricks) manufactured in situ.
2. Modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units.

Interlocking removes the need for mortar (cement and water), which is difficult to produce and cure in vacuum. Traditional interlocking shapes require high precision; however, 'topological interlocking' avoids these requirements.

## 2. Principle of Topological Interlocking
Topological interlocking is achieved by a special choice of geometry such that neither element can be removed without disturbing neighbors. Examples include:
- Assemblies of tetrahedra.
- Osteomorphic bricks with engineered curved surfaces.
- Assemblies of Platonic solids (cubes, octahedra).

## 3. Extraterrestrial Construction Based on Osteomorphic Bricks
### 3.1. Mortar-free Structures
Osteomorphic bricks allow structures without mortar. They are self-adjusting, meaning they do not require high-precision positioning, facilitating robotization.

#### 3.1.1. Constraint by Natural Features
Structures must be constrained at the periphery. This can be provided by planetary topography (e.g., small craters or pits) or cavern roofs (e.g., lava tubes).

#### 3.1.2. Artificial Constraint
Methods include external frames or internal tension cables (post-tensioning) running through the bricks. Post-tensioning provides a low-weight constraint and operational flexibility.

#### 3.1.4. In Situ Production
Production methods include:
- Sintering in a mould (microwave or external heating).
- Selective Laser Sintering (SLS), allowing layer-by-layer manufacturing of pre-positioned bricks.

### 3.3. Radiation Protection
Regolith bricks provide radiation protection. A double-skin wall construction can eliminate radiation leakage through joints by shifting patterns.

## 4. Protective Cladding with Osteomorphic Tiles
Interlocking tiles can protect spacecraft or modules. Experiments demonstrate high fracture resistance: fractures are contained within the loaded block and do not propagate to neighbors.

## 5. Interlocking Modular Structures
Modular stations can be organized as clusters of interlocked tetrahedra, cubes, or octahedra. 
- **Square-tiling/Osteomorphic:** Tolerant to missing blocks.
- **Hexagon-tiling:** Allows easier access/replacement of modules but requires all blocks for integrity.

## 6. Conclusion
1. Osteomorphic bricks relax accuracy requirements for production/assembly and possess high fracture resistance.
2. Peripheral constraints can be provided by topography or post-tensioning.
3. Interlocking modular designs permit assembly on uneven ground using lateral forces.
4. Spaceships can be built as interlocking assemblies of independent modules for flexibility.
