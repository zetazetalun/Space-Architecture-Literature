# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10–21

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of topological interlocking to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes (usually convex or nearly convex, such that no stress concentration develops) are arranged in such a way that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of extraterrestrial structures are considered. The first type represents mortar-free structures built from specially engineered interlocking bricks, called osteomorphic bricks. The self-adjusting property of these bricks permits erecting structures which tolerate low precision of production and assembly, thus making the proposed method suitable for in situ produced bricks and low-cost assembling machinery. The structures of the second type are modular extraterrestrial bases or space ships organised in topologically interlocking assemblies. For an extraterrestrial settlement such an organisation permits easy assembly even if the modules are uploaded on uneven ground. A space ship can be assembled from independent smaller ships interlocked topologically thus becoming a flexible vehicle suitable for both long-distance journeys and simultaneous exploration of extraterrestrial objects clustered in a relative proximity of each other.

## 1. Introduction
With the increasing awareness for the need of utilisation and colonisation of the outer space, the time is ripe to develop methods and techniques of extraterrestrial construction. Currently, architectures can be categorised into: (1) structures built from basic elements like bricks manufactured in situ and (2) modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units. Elements of the first class should satisfy requirements for easy delivery or in situ manufacturing, easy assembly, and multifunctionality. Topological interlocking removes the need for mortar, avoiding the high capital investment and environmental challenges (vacuum curing, water supply) associated with traditional binders.

## 2. Principle of Topological Interlocking
Topological interlocking is achieved by a special choice of geometry and arrangement of building elements such that no element can be removed from the assembly without disturbing neighbors. This can be achieved through:
- Special arrangements of simple shapes (e.g., Platonic solids).
- Specially designed curved surfaces, exemplified by 'osteomorphic bricks'.

## 3. Extraterrestrial construction based on osteomorphic bricks
### 3.1. Mortar-free structures
Osteomorphic bricks allow structures without mortar. They are self-adjusting, meaning assembly does not require high-precision positioning, facilitating robotisation. Construction can rely on in situ mass production of cheap blocks.

#### 3.1.1. Constraint by natural features
Peripheral constraints for plate-like interlocking structures can be provided by lunar/planetary topography such as craters or pits. Gaps between assembly and pit walls can be filled with expanding grout or jacks.

#### 3.1.2. Artificial constraint
Where natural constraints are missing, external frames or internal tension cables (post-tensioning) can be used to hold the assembly together.

### 3.1.4. In situ production of osteomorphic bricks
Production will likely base on sintering bricks from regolith dust. Two major sintering methods are considered: sintering in a mould (external or microwave heating) and selective laser sintering (SLS). SLS allows simultaneous manufacturing of a whole layer of bricks already correctly positioned.

## 5. Interlocking modular structures
Modular units for bases or spacecraft can be shaped as tetrahedra, cubes, or octahedra to form interlocking clusters. This 'self-adjusting' property allows assembly on uneven ground; a lateral force applied to the cluster will force modules into a regular arrangement.

### 5.3. Spaceship based on interlocked modules
Modular spacecraft can be assembled in-orbit from independent modules. Thrust can keep the modules together by inclining jets to generate an in-plane force. This allows for flexible vehicles that can disassemble into separate modules for exploration and reassemble for interplanetary travel.

## 6. Conclusion
1. Mortar-free structures can be built from osteomorphic bricks, which are self-adjusting and tolerant to low precision.
2. Interlocking tiles can be used for protective cladding (thermal/radiation).
3. Modular structures facilitate assembly on uneven ground.
4. Spacecraft can be built as interlocking assemblies of independent units.
