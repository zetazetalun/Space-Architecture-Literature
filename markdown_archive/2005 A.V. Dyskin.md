# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov

**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10 – 21

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of *topological interlocking* to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes (usually convex or nearly convex, such that no stress concentration develops) are arranged in such a way that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of extraterrestrial structures are considered. The first type represents mortar free structures built from specially engineered interlocking bricks, called *osteomorphic bricks*. The self-adjusting property of these bricks permits erecting structures which tolerate low precision of production and assembly, thus making the proposed method suitable for in situ produced bricks and low cost assembling machinery. The structures of the second type are modular extraterrestrial bases or space ships organised in topologically interlocking assemblies.

## 1. Introduction
With the increasing awareness for the need of utilisation and colonisation of outer space, the time is ripe to develop methods and techniques of extraterrestrial construction. Architectures can be categorised into:
1. Structures built from basic elements such as bricks, manufactured in situ.
2. Modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units.

Interlocking removes the need for mortar (cement and water), which is difficult to produce at scale in situ. Traditional interlocking bricks require precise manufacturing, which is difficult in the presence of lunar dust and extreme conditions.

## 2. Principle of topological interlocking
Topological interlocking is achieved by a special choice of geometry and arrangement such that no single element can be removed from the assembly without disturbing neighbors. Examples include plate-like assemblies of tetrahedra or specially designed curved surfaces called *osteomorphic bricks*.

## 3. Extraterrestrial construction based on osteomorphic bricks

### 3.1. Mortar free structures from osteomorphic bricks
Bricks in the shape of osteomorphic blocks allow mortarless assembly. They are self-adjusting, relaxing the need for high-precision positioning, which facilitates robotisation.

#### 3.1.1. Constraint by the natural features of planetary topography
Peripheral constraints for these structures can be provided by craters or pits. Gaps between the interlocking assembly and pit walls can be filled with expanding grout or jacks.

#### 3.1.2. Artificial constraint
Where natural constraints are absent, external frames or internal tension cables (post-tensioning) can be used. Post-tensioning using high-strength cables running through the bricks provides a low-weight constraint system.

#### 3.1.4. In situ production of osteomorphic bricks
Production technology will likely be based on sintering regolith dust in moulds (using microwave or external heating) or selective laser sintering (SLS). The self-adjusting property allows for considerable tolerance in surface quality after sintering.

### 3.3. Radiation protection: Double skin walls
Mortar-free joints can provide passage channels for radiation. A double-skin construction with shifted brick patterns (and regolith infill) is proposed to eliminate radiation permeability.

## 4. Protective cladding with osteomorphic tiles
Osteomorphic tiles can be used as protective cladding for pre-manufactured modules or spacecraft. Experiments show these assemblies have high fracture resistance compared to solid plates; fractures are contained within loaded blocks and do not propagate.

## 5. Interlocking modular structures

### 5.1. Self-adjusting modules for an extraterrestrial base
At a larger scale, pre-fabricated modules can be shaped like tetrahedra, cubes, or octahedrons to form interlocking clusters. This allows for assembly on uneven ground; a lateral force can push the modules into a regular cohesive structure.

### 5.3. Spaceship based on interlocked modules
Spacecraft can be assembled in orbit from independent modules. Thrust from inclined jets can generate the in-plane force required to keep the interlocking modules together. This configuration is flexible and provides shock absorption.

## 6. Conclusion
1. Mortar-free and demountable structures can be built from self-adjusting osteomorphic bricks, ideal for in-situ production.
2. Interlocking tiles provide superior protective cladding with high fracture resistance.
3. Modular structures benefit from self-adjustment, permitting easy assembly on uneven ground.
4. Spacecraft can be built as flexible interlocking assemblies of independent modules.
