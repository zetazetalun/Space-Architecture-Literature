# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10–21
**Publisher:** Elsevier Ltd.

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of *topological interlocking* to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes (usually convex or nearly convex, such that no stress concentration develops) are arranged in such a way that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of extraterrestrial structures are considered. The first type represents mortar free structures built from specially engineered interlocking bricks, called osteomorphic bricks. The self-adjusting property of these bricks permits erecting structures which tolerate low precision of production and assembly, thus making the proposed method suitable for in situ produced bricks and low cost assembling machinery.

## 1. Introduction
With the increasing awareness for the need of utilisation and colonisation of outer space, the time is ripe to develop methods and techniques of extraterrestrial construction. Currently, architectures can be categorised into two major classes: (1) structures built from basic elements such as bricks, possibly manufactured in situ and (2) modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units. The building elements of the first class should satisfy specific requirements including easy delivery or in situ manufacturing, easy assembly, and multifunctionality.

## 2. Principle of topological interlocking
Topological interlocking is achieved by a special choice of geometry and arrangement of the building elements such that neither element can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbour. In an assembly constrained at the periphery, each element will thus be locked. Historically, the first interlocking assembly found was a plate-like assembly of tetrahedra.

## 3. Extraterrestrial construction based on osteomorphic bricks

### 3.1. Mortar free structures from osteomorphic bricks
Bricks made in the shape of the osteomorphic block allow erecting structures without mortar or any other binder. These blocks are self-adjusting, meaning the construction process does not require high precision positioning, facilitating robotisation. Construction can be based on in situ mass production of cheap building blocks via sintering of regolith.

### 3.1.1. Constraint by natural features
Peripheral constraint for plate-like structures can be provided by planetary topography such as small craters or pits. Gaps between the assembly and pit walls can be filled with expanding grout (regolith-based) or equipped with jacks.

### 3.1.2. Artificial constraint
When natural constraint is unavailable, external frames or internal tension cables (post-tensioning) can be used. Post-tensioning provides a low-weight constraint and operational flexibility.

### 3.1.4. In situ production
Production technology will likely be based on sintering bricks from regolith dust. Methods include sintering in a mould (microwave or external heating) or selective laser sintering (SLS). SLS allows for simultaneous manufacturing of a whole layer of bricks already correctly positioned.

### 3.3. Radiation protection
Mortar-free structures may have joints that provide passage for radiation. A double-skin construction is proposed, with shifted brick patterns and regolith filling the cavity to eliminate permeability.

## 5. Interlocking modular structures
Modular units can be shaped to interlock topologically. This permits assembly on uneven ground; a lateral force applied to a collection of modules will force them into a regular planar arrangement due to their self-adjusting properties. Spacecraft could also be assembled from independent modules kept together by thrust-induced in-plane forces.

## 6. Conclusion
1. Mortar-free structures can be built from self-adjusting osteomorphic bricks, ideal for in-situ production and robotised assembly.
2. Interlocking tiles can be used for protective cladding against thermal or radiation hazards.
3. Modular structures benefit from self-adjustment, allowing assembly on uneven terrain.
4. Spacecraft can be built as interlocking assemblies of independent modules for flexibility.
