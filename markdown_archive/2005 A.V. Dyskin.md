# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10 – 21  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2004.12.005

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of *topological interlocking* to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes are arranged such that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of extraterrestrial structures are considered: mortar-free structures built from specially engineered interlocking bricks (osteomorphic bricks) and modular extraterrestrial bases or space ships organised in topologically interlocking assemblies. The self-adjusting property of these bricks permits erecting structures which tolerate low precision of production and assembly, making them suitable for in situ produced materials and low-cost automated machinery.

## 1. Introduction
The development of methods for extraterrestrial construction is essential for space colonization. Current architectures are categorized into: 
1. Structures built from basic elements (bricks) manufactured in situ.  
2. Modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units.

Building elements for the first class must support easy assembly and multifunctionality. Traditional interlocking bricks suffer from high precision requirements and stress concentrations. Topological interlocking offers a solution that minimizes these demands while providing high fracture resistance.

## 2. Principle of Topological Interlocking
Topological interlocking is achieved by geometry and arrangement where no single element can be removed from an assembly constrained at the periphery. Historical examples include plate-like assemblies of tetrahedra. The "osteomorphic brick" uses curved surfaces to prevent displacement along the normal to the assembly in both directions, making them self-adjusting and tolerant of inaccurate initial placement.

## 3. Extraterrestrial Construction Based on Osteomorphic Bricks
### 3.1. Mortar-free structures
These structures do not require binders (cement/water), which are difficult to manage in a vacuum. 

#### 3.1.1. Constraint by natural features
Foundations can be built in small craters or pits where the planetary topography provides the necessary peripheral constraint.

#### 3.1.2. Artificial constraint
Where natural constraint is missing, internal tension cables (post-tensioning) or external frames can be used to maintain structural integrity.

#### 3.1.4. In situ production
Production methods include:
- Sintering in a mould via external or microwave heating.
- Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) for rapid prototyping of entire layers.

### 3.2. Sensitivity and Weathering
Interlocking structures are insensitive to surface imperfections or dust adherence due to the curved faces of osteomorphic bricks. Height variations are managed by adjusting the tension in the cables.

### 3.3. Radiation Protection
Double-skin wall construction with shifted brick patterns can eliminate radiation passage channels inherent in mortar-free joints.

## 4. Protective Cladding
Osteomorphic tiles can be used as cladding for spacecraft or modules to provide high fracture resistance. Experiments show that interlocking assemblies have higher indentation resistance (11.93 kN) compared to solid plates (9.9 kN).

## 5. Interlocking Modular Structures
Large-scale modules can be shaped like tetrahedra, cubes, or octahedra to form interlocking clusters. This allows for "express assembly" on uneven ground using lateral forces, providing a flexible design for stations and modular spacecraft.

## 6. Conclusion
Topological interlocking offers:
1. Mortar-free, demountable structures using self-adjusting bricks.
2. Tolerance for low-precision in situ production and robotisation.
3. Increased fracture resistance.
4. Flexible modular designs for planetary bases and spacecraft.
