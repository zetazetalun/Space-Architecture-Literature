# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10–21  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2004.12.005

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of topological interlocking to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes are arranged such that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of structures are considered: (1) mortar-free structures built from osteomorphic bricks (self-adjusting, tolerant to low precision), and (2) modular extraterrestrial bases or space ships organized in topologically interlocking assemblies.

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial structures are categorized into two major classes:
1. Structures built from basic elements like bricks, manufactured in situ.
2. Modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units (including orbital assembly).

Traditional interlocking bricks require precise manufacturing and are sensitive to lunar dust and stress concentrations. Topological interlocking offers a solution that minimizes manufacturing precision requirements.

## 2. Principle of Topological Interlocking
Topological interlocking is achieved by a special choice of geometry such that elements are locked by their neighbors. 
*   **Platonic Shapes:** Tetrahedra can form plate-like assemblies where each element is locked if peripheral constraint is provided.
*   **Osteomorphic Bricks:** Specially engineered curved surfaces permit construction of layers, corners, and columns. These are self-positioning and self-adjusting.

## 3. Extraterrestrial construction based on osteomorphic bricks
### 3.1. Mortar-free structures
*   **Benefits:** Removes the need for water and cement (difficult to produce/cure in vacuum). Tolerates low-precision robotic assembly.
*   **Constraints:** Requires peripheral constraint (compression). Methods include:
    *   Natural topography (craters, pits, caverns).
    *   Artificial constraints (external frames or internal tension cables/post-tensioning).
*   **Roofing:** Can be achieved with standardized planar cassettes or specialized curved blocks.

### 3.2. In situ production
*   **Sintering:** Microwave or solar sintering in reusable moulds.
*   **Selective Laser Sintering (SLS):** Allows layer-by-layer manufacturing of entire interlocked layers simultaneously.

## 4. Protective cladding and Fracture Resistance
Osteomorphic tiles can be used for radiation/thermal shielding of Earth-made modules. Tests showed high fracture resistance: fractures are contained within loaded blocks and do not propagate. 
*   **Data Point:** Interlocking assemblies failed at an average indentation load of 11.93 kN, compared to 9.9 kN for a solid plate (approx. 10% higher performance).

## 5. Interlocking modular structures
*   **Modular Bases:** Clusters of modules (tetrahedral, cubic) can be self-adjusting during assembly on uneven ground.
*   **Spaceships:** Modular spacecraft assembled in orbit. Thrust can be used to generate the small in-plane force required to keep modules interlocked. This allows for flexible mission profiles (disassembling modules for local exploration).

## 6. Conclusion
1. Osteomorphic bricks relax accuracy requirements for in-situ production and robotization.
2. Topological interlocking provides high fracture resistance and tolerance to missing elements.
3. Peripheral constraint can be provided via planetary topography or internal tensioning.
4. The principle applies both to small-scale masonry and large-scale modular vehicles.
