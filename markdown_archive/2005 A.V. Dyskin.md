# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10–21  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2004.12.005

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of topological interlocking to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes (usually convex or nearly convex, such that no stress concentration develops) are arranged in such a way that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of extraterrestrial structures are considered: mortar-free structures built from osteomorphic bricks and modular extraterrestrial bases or space ships organized in topologically interlocking assemblies. The self-adjusting property of these bricks permits erecting structures which tolerate low precision of production and assembly, making the method suitable for in situ produced bricks and low-cost assembling machinery.

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial structures can be categorized into two classes: (1) structures built from basic elements like bricks manufactured in situ and (2) modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units. In situ manufacturing removes the need for mortar (cement and water), which is costly to produce and difficult to cure in vacuum conditions. Interlocking blocks provide an alternative, though traditional shapes often require high precision and suffer from stress concentrations.

## 2. Principle of Topological Interlocking
Topological interlocking is achieved by a special choice of geometry and arrangement such that elements are locked within the assembly without keys or connectors. Examples include:
- **Platonic shapes:** Tetrahedra, cubes, octahedra arranged in plate-like assemblies.
- **Osteomorphic bricks:** Bricks with engineered curved surfaces that are self-adjusting and allow for both planar and corner structures.

## 3. Extraterrestrial Construction Based on Osteomorphic Bricks
### 3.1. Mortar-free structures
These structures require either peripheral compression or unidirectional constraint to remain stable. Constraints can be provided by:
- **Planetary Topography:** Small craters, pits, or caverns (lava tubes).
- **Artificial Constraints:** External frames or internal post-tensioning cables running through the bricks.

### 3.2. Sintering Methods
Two major sintering methods for regolith are proposed:
1. **Sintering in a mould:** Using external or microwave heating.
2. **Selective Laser Sintering (SLS):** Layer-by-layer manufacturing allows for the simultaneous production of entire interlocking layers correctly positioned.

## 4. Protective Cladding
Osteomorphic tiles can be used as cladding to shield modules or spacecraft from radiation and thermal stress. Their high fracture resistance and tolerance to missing blocks make them superior to solid plates.

## 5. Interlocking Modular Structures
Large-scale modules (e.g., habitable units) can be shaped to interlock topologically. This allows for assembly on uneven ground; a lateral force applied to a collection of modules will force them into a regular planar arrangement due to the self-adjusting geometry.

## 6. Conclusion
1. Osteomorphic bricks allow for mortar-free, self-adjusting structures that relax accuracy requirements for robotics.
2. Interlocking structures possess high resistance to fracture propagation.
3. Modular structures can be easily assembled by applying lateral force to uploaded modules even on uneven ground.
4. Spacecraft can be built as flexible interlocking assemblies of independent modules.
