# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10–21  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2004.12.005

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of *topological interlocking* to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes (usually convex or nearly convex) are arranged such that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of extraterrestrial structures are considered: mortar-free structures built from specially engineered interlocking bricks (osteomorphic bricks) and modular extraterrestrial bases or space ships organised in topologically interlocking assemblies. The self-adjusting property of these bricks permits erecting structures which tolerate low precision of production and assembly, making the method suitable for in-situ produced bricks and low-cost automated machinery.

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial structures can be categorized into two classes: 
1. Structures built from basic elements (bricks) manufactured in situ.
2. Modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units.

In-situ production of bricks removes the need for mortar, which is problematic due to the requirement for cement and water, and the difficulties of curing in a vacuum. However, traditional interlocking bricks require high manufacturing precision, which is difficult to achieve in the presence of lunar dust and extreme thermal/gravitational loading.

## 2. Principle of Topological Interlocking
Topological interlocking is achieved by a special choice of geometry and arrangement such that elements are locked by their neighbors. This requires peripheral constraint (e.g., a frame or internal tensioning) to maintain structural integrity. Examples include plate-like assemblies of tetrahedra or "osteomorphic bricks."

## 3. Extraterrestrial construction based on osteomorphic bricks
### 3.1. Mortar-free structures
Osteomorphic bricks allow for structures without mortar. Their self-adjusting property reduces the need for high-precision positioning, facilitating robotisation. Constraint can be provided by:
- **Natural topography:** Building in pits, craters, or lava tubes.
- **Artificial constraint:** External frames or internal tension cables (post-tensioning).

### 3.2. Sensitivity to manufacturing imperfections
The interlocking mechanism is insensitive to surface imperfections or dust adherence, as the locking is provided by the global curved geometry rather than small-scale keys or pegs.

### 3.3. Radiation protection
Mortar-free joints can provide channels for radiation. A "double skin" construction with shifted brick patterns or concave-convex planar faces can eliminate this permeability.

## 4. Protective cladding
Osteomorphic tiles can be used to clad pre-manufactured modules or spacecraft, providing high fracture resistance and shielding.

## 5. Interlocking modular structures
Modular bases can be designed using interlocking polyhedra (tetrahedra, cubes, octahedra). This allows for assembly on uneven ground; a lateral force can push a collection of loose modules into a cohesive, regular arrangement.

## 6. Conclusion
Topological interlocking offers:
1. Mortar-free, demountable structures tolerant of low-precision assembly.
2. High resistance to fracture propagation.
3. Ability to assemble modular stations on unprepared planetary surfaces.
4. Flexible, shock-absorbing modular spacecraft designs.
