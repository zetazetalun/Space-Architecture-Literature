# The principle of topological interlocking in extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** A.V. Dyskin, Y. Estrin, E. Pasternak, H.C. Khor, A.J. Kanel-Belov
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 57 (2005) 10 – 21
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2004.12.005

## Abstract
Applications of a newly established principle of topological interlocking to different types of extraterrestrial construction are considered. Topological interlocking arises when elements of special shapes (usually convex or nearly convex) are arranged such that neither of them can be removed from the assembly without disturbing the neighbouring elements. Two types of structures are considered: mortar-free structures from osteomorphic bricks and modular extraterrestrial bases or space ships.

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial structures can be categorized into two major classes:
1. Structures built from basic elements like bricks, possibly manufactured in situ.
2. Modular structures based on Earth-manufactured units (including orbital assembly).

The paper focuses on interlocking as it removes the need for mortar (cement and water), which is difficult to produce and cure in vacuum environments.

## 2. Principle of Topological Interlocking
Interlocking is achieved by geometry and arrangement. No keys or connectors are required. Examples include:
- Plate-like assemblies of tetrahedra.
- Osteomorphic bricks with engineered curved surfaces.

## 3. Extraterrestrial Construction Based on Osteomorphic Bricks
### 3.1. Mortar-free structures
- **Self-adjusting:** Low precision in positioning is tolerated, making it ideal for robotized assembly.
- **Constraint:** Requires peripheral constraint (compression) provided by planetary topography (craters, caves) or artificial means (tension cables, external frames).
- **Roofing:** Standardized planar 'cassettes' or special curved blocks for domes.
- **In Situ Production:** Possible via sintering regolith in moulds (microwave) or Selective Laser Sintering (SLS).

### 3.2. Sensitivity and Imperfections
The structures are insensitive to surface imperfections or dust adherence due to the curved contacting surfaces. 

### 3.3. Radiation Protection
Double skin walls can be used to eliminate radiation passage through joints, with shifted patterns or concave-convex faces.

## 4. Protective Cladding
Osteomorphic tiles can be used as cladding for spacecraft to provide high fracture resistance.

## 5. Interlocking Modular Structures
Functional modules (tetrahedron, cube, octahedron shapes) can be interlocked. 
- **Self-adjusting modules:** Lateral loads can force modules into regular planar arrangements on uneven ground.
- **Tolerances:** Square-tiling and osteomorphic assemblies are tolerant to missing blocks; hexagonal-tiling is not but allows easier replacement access.

## 6. Conclusion
Topological interlocking offers:
1. Mortar-free, demountable structures with high fracture resistance.
2. Suitability for in situ production and robotization.
3. Protective cladding applications.
4. Flexible, modular station and spaceship designs.

## Data and Results
- **Fracture Resistance:** Interlocking assemblies of rectangular bricks (280 x 260 x 20 mm³) failed at an average indentation load of 11.93 kN, while a solid reference plate failed at 9.9 kN (approx. 10% lower).
