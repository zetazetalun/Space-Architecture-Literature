# Earthmoving for the extraterrestrial

**Jeffrey S Nesbit**  
Temple University, USA

## Abstract
The United States spaceport, and more importantly, its technical landscape, operate in the background for the technological and political progress in pursuit of the extraterrestrial. Throughout the construction of the launch complexes on the Florida coastline, earthmoving became standard practice to elevate rocket pads above sea level and protect against rocket blasts. However, a more extended history of earthmoving at Cape Canaveral is necessary. From Earth’s early geological formations and indigenous burial mounds to the modernization of rockets, Cape Canaveral presents itself as an evolution of terrestrial form. And still, earthmoving continues today. In 2016, an article advised the greatest threats to NASA’s landscape are rising sea levels and hurricanes, causing substantial erosion to the beach, leaving active and historically significant launch facilities at risk. Cape Canaveral beaches are now preparing for additional dredging, importing new soil, and raising beach and dune elevations. This article reveals a critical history of place-based science on Cape Canaveral through an evolution of earthmoving practices, from cultural commemoration, extraterrestrial imagination, and contemporary environmental crises.

## Introduction
In 1950, the United States Air Force selected the Central Florida eastern coastline to construct its missile operations and rocket launch facilities. Cape Canaveral proved to be a “suitable” decision for military security, proximity to the equator for rocket efficiency entering orbital trajectory, and safety as failed rockets could discharge in the ocean.

The spaceport, and more importantly, its technical landscape dominated by earthmoving practices, operate in the background for the technological and political progress in pursuit of the extraterrestrial. Departing Earth requires the moving of it.

## Technical Landscape and Infrastructure
The earliest of rocket launch sites required the design of earthmoving techniques. The launch pads and facilities at Peenemünde had been under the architectural direction and coordination of Albert Speer. A dense series of mechanical conduit lines organized the complex’s testing facilities. Such lines demonstrate an early example of the application of underground networks and technology management, as well as a merger of systematic operations and ground reconfiguration. Bounded by an “embankment” of built-up earthwork, the launch pad at Peenemünde and its electrically networked systems became the model for Americans at Cape Canaveral.

### Construction and Expansion
- **1950–1961:** 70% of all launch complexes at Cape Canaveral were constructed.
- **1958:** 23 of the total 36 launch complexes were already in place by the time NASA was established.
- **Earthmoving Practice:** Standard practice involved elevating rocket pads above sea level and using earthwork for protection against rocket blasts.

## Apollo and the Saturn V
In 1962, NASA selected locations for Apollo launch complex pads 39A and 39B. Engineers elevated the rocket launch pads to sit above the shallow sea level by dredging hydraulic fill from the Banana River. Two large pyramid-like sandpiles were constructed to elevate the launch pads used to power the Saturn V rocket. 

The technology used to support the new mobile launch complex came from common earthmoving practices. The steam-shovel crawler, originally used for surface mining (coal), was adopted to construct the Missile Crawler Transporter Facility. 

## Current Environmental Challenges
Today, earthmoving persists as a remedy for environmental crises. The 2018 report on “Sea Level Rise: Vulnerability Assessment” indicates that the Merritt Island National Refuge, Kennedy Space Center, and Cape Canaveral Air Force Station are the largest areas in Central Florida at risk of inundation from rising sea levels.
