# Earthmoving for the extraterrestrial\n\n**Jeffrey S Nesbit**\nTemple University, USA\n\n## Abstract\nThe United States spaceport, and more importantly, its technical landscape, operate in the background for the technological and political progress in pursuit of the extraterrestrial. Throughout the construction of the launch complexes on the Florida coastline, earthmoving became standard practice to elevate rocket pads above sea level and protect against rocket blasts. However, a more extended history of earthmoving at Cape Canaveral is necessary. From Earth’s early geological formations and indigenous burial mounds to the modernization of rockets, Cape Canaveral presents itself as an evolution of terrestrial form. And still, earthmoving continues today. In 2016, an article advised the greatest threats to NASA’s landscape are rising sea levels and hurricanes, causing substantial erosion to the beach, leaving active and historically significant launch facilities at risk. Cape Canaveral beaches are now preparing for additional dredging, importing new soil, and raising beach and dune elevations. This article reveals a critical history of place-based science on Cape Canaveral through an evolution of earthmoving practices, from cultural commemoration, extraterrestrial imagination, and contemporary environmental crises.\n\n## Keywords\nInfrastructure, NASA landscapes, technical lands\n\n## Introduction\nIn 1950, the United States Air Force selected the Central Florida eastern coastline to construct its missile operations and rocket launch facilities. Cape Canaveral proved to be a \"suitable\" decision for military security, proximity to the equator for rocket efficiency entering orbital trajectory, and safety as failed rockets could discharge in the ocean.\n\nDeparting Earth requires the moving of it. The mechanical landscape of the launch complex displaces more than just soil; it erases natural and cultural histories. For architects and engineers, the leveling of land becomes fundamental in how we position ourselves in the environment—an attempt to minimize interruptions on the working surface of the earth, and in the spaceport case specifically, driven by an extraterrestrial imaginary.\n\n## Historical Precedents and Earthmoving Techniques\nThe earliest rocket launch sites required the design of earthmoving techniques. The launch pads and facilities at Peenemunde, under Albert Speer, utilized embankments of built-up earthwork. This became the model for Cape Canaveral.\n\nBy 1961, 70% of all launch complexes at Cape Canaveral were constructed. Earthmoving became standard practice for protecting blockhouses against rocket blasts and elevating pads above sea level.\n\n### Case Study: Saturn V Launch Pads\nFor the Apollo program, NASA utilized massive hydraulic fill dredging from the Banana River to elevate rocket launch pads. Two large pyramid-like sandpiles were constructed to elevate the launch pads for the Saturn V. The technology for the mobile launch complex (Crawler Transporter) was adapted from steam-shovel mining equipment used for coal extraction in Kentucky.\n\n## Environmental and Technical Challenges\nThe construction of the crawlerway and the blocking of natural channels have caused hydrological problems. Today, the greatest threat to these technical landscapes is sea-level rise and coastal erosion, necessitating ongoing earthmoving (dredging and dune manipulation) to protect infrastructure.\n\n## Conclusion\nEarthmoving for reaching the extraterrestrial concerns three landform typologies: burial mounds, launch complex infrastructure, and dredging history. These techno-spatial landscapes are produced by the intersection of military necessity, technological ambition, and environmental struggle.
