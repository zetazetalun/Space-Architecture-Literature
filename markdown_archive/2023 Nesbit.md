# Earthmoving for the extraterrestrial

**Jeffrey S Nesbit**
Temple University, USA

## Abstract
The United States spaceport, and more importantly, its technical landscape, operate in the background for the technological and political progress in pursuit of the extraterrestrial. Throughout the construction of the launch complexes on the Florida coastline, earthmoving became standard practice to elevate rocket pads above sea level and protect against rocket blasts. However, a more extended history of earthmoving at Cape Canaveral is necessary. From Earth’s early geological formations and indigenous burial mounds to the modernization of rockets, Cape Canaveral presents itself as an evolution of terrestrial form. And still, earthmoving continues today. In 2016, an article advised the greatest threats to NASA’s landscape are rising sea levels and hurricanes, causing substantial erosion to the beach, leaving active and historically significant launch facilities at risk. Cape Canaveral beaches are now preparing for additional dredging, importing new soil, and raising beach and dune elevations. This article reveals a critical history of place-based science on Cape Canaveral through an evolution of earthmoving practices, from cultural commemoration, extraterrestrial imagination, and contemporary environmental crises.

## Introduction
In 1950, the United States Air Force selected the Central Florida eastern coastline to construct its missile operations and rocket launch facilities. Cape Canaveral proved to be a “suitable” decision for military security, proximity to the equator for rocket efficiency entering orbital trajectory, and safety as failed rockets could discharge in the ocean.

The spaceport, and more importantly, its technical landscape dominated by earthmoving practices, operate in the background for the technological and political progress in pursuit of the extraterrestrial. Departing Earth requires the moving of it. The mechanical landscape of the launch complex displaces more than just soil; it erases natural and cultural histories.

## Technical History of Launch Sites

### Early Earthmoving and Design
The earliest of rocket launch sites required the design of earthmoving techniques. The launch pads and facilities at Peenemunde had been under the architectural direction and coordination of Albert Speers. A dense series of mechanical conduit lines organized the complex’s testing facilities. Bounded by an “embankment” of built-up earthwork, the launch pad at Peenemunde and its electrically networked systems became the model for Americans at Cape Canaveral.

### Construction at Cape Canaveral (1950–1961)
An astonishing 70% of all launch complexes at Cape Canaveral were constructed over a brief seven-year span ranging from 1950 to 1961. During the construction of launch site blockhouses and rocket pads, earthmoving became standard practice for protection against rocket blasts and to elevate pads above sea level.

### Apollo Infrastructure
In 1962, NASA selected the location for Apollo launch complex pads 39A and 39B. By dredging hydraulic fill from the Banana River and pumping it into the construction site, engineers elevated the rocket launch pads to sit above the shallow sea level. Two large pyramid-like sandpiles were constructed to elevate the launch pads used to power the Saturn V rocket to the moon. 

| Facility/Technology | Purpose | Source/Method |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Launch Pads 39A/39B | Saturn V Launch | Hydraulic fill dredging from Banana River |
| Sandpiles | Elevated Pad Foundation | Engineered sand accumulation |
| Crawler Transporter | Rocket Transport | Adopted from surface mining (coal) machinery |
| Crawlerway | Transport Route | Engineered with subterranean culverts |

## Environmental Context and Challenges
The construction of the crawlerway created severe hydrological problems with the lagoon systems, blocking natural water flows. Today, the landscape faces threats from rising sea levels and erosion, requiring ongoing dredging and sand replenishment to protect historic and active launch facilities.
