# Earthmoving for the extraterrestrial\n\n**Jeffrey S Nesbit**\nTemple University, USA\n\n## Abstract\nThe United States spaceport, and more importantly, its technical landscape, operate in the background for the technological and political progress in pursuit of the extraterrestrial. Throughout the construction of the launch complexes on the Florida coastline, earthmoving became standard practice to elevate rocket pads above sea level and protect against rocket blasts. This article reveals a critical history of place-based science on Cape Canaveral through an evolution of earthmoving practices, from cultural commemoration to contemporary environmental crises.\n\n## Introduction\nIn 1950, the United States Air Force selected the Central Florida eastern coastline to construct its missile operations and rocket launch facilities. Departing Earth requires the moving of it. The mechanical landscape of the launch complex displaces more than just soil; it erases natural and cultural histories. For architects and engineers, the leveling of land becomes fundamental in how we position ourselves in the environment—an attempt to minimize interruptions on the working surface of the earth, driven by an extraterrestrial imaginary.\n\n## Earthmoving and Launch Infrastructure\nThe earliest rocket launch sites required the design of earthmoving techniques. Bounded by an 'embankment' of built-up earthwork, the launch pad at Peenemunde became the model for Americans at Cape Canaveral. Between 1950 and 1961, 70% of all launch complexes at Cape Canaveral were constructed. During this construction, earthmoving became standard practice for protection of the blockhouse against rocket blast and to elevate pads above sea level.\n\nNASA initiated massive wharf construction with barge canals and turning basins. Two large pyramid-like sandpiles were constructed to elevate the launch pads for the Saturn V rocket. The technology used to support the mobile launch complex, specifically the steam-shovel crawler, was adopted from coal mining extraction practices in Kentucky to construct the Missile Crawler Transporter Facility.\n\n## Environmental Impact and Hydrology\nThe engineered infrastructure of earthmoving, including the crawlerway and the blocking of channels across rivers and lagoons, disrupts the environmental water flow. Today, the greatest threats to this landscape are rising sea levels and hurricanes, leading to new dredging, importing of soil, and raising of dune elevations to protect historically significant launch facilities.
