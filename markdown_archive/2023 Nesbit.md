# Earthmoving for the extraterrestrial  **Author:** Jeffrey S Nesbit  **Journal:** EPD: Society and Space  **DOI:** 10.1177/02637758231204701  ## Abstract The United States spaceport and its technical landscape operate in the background of technological progress. Earthmoving became standard practice to elevate rocket pads and protect against blasts. This article reveals a history of place-based science through earthmoving practices, from cultural mounds to extraterrestrial imagination.  ## Technical Landscapes and Earthmoving  Departing Earth requires the moving of it. The mechanical landscape of the launch complex displaces soil and erases natural histories. For architects and engineers, the leveling of land is fundamental to positioning infrastructure in the environment, minimizing interruptions on the working surface.  ### Early Site Preparation (Peenemunde) The earliest rocket launch sites required specific earthmoving techniques. At Peenemunde, launch pads were bounded by embankments of built-up earthwork. This became the model for Cape Canaveral, driven by pioneers like Wernher von Braun and Dr. Kurt Debus.  ### Cape Canaveral Infrastructure 70% of all launch complexes at Cape Canaveral were constructed between 1950 and 1961. Earthmoving was used to elevate pads above sea level and protect blockhouses from rocket blasts.  * **Launch Complex 39:** Engineers used hydraulic fill from the Banana River to elevate pads. * **Saturn V Pads:** Two large pyramid-like sandpiles were constructed to elevate pads for the moon missions. * **Crawlerway:** A three-mile-long paved crawler way was engineered with subterranean water channels (culverts). Mining equipment (steam-shovel crawlers) from the coal industry was adapted for this purpose.  ## Findings on Landform Typologies The study identifies three primary typologies: 1. Burial mounds (indigenous earthwork). 2. Launch complex infrastructure (elevated pads and blockhouses). 3. Dredging (hydraulic manipulation of wetland ecologies).  ## Conclusion Contemporary environmental crises, such as rising sea levels and hurricanes, threaten these technical landscapes, requiring ongoing dredging and soil importation to protect historically significant launch facilities.
