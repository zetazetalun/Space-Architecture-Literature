# Biological growth as an alternative approach to on and off-Earth construction

**Authors:** Monika Brandić Lipińska, Chris Maurer, Dave Cadogan, James Head, Martyn Dade-Robertson, Ivan Glaucio Paulino-Lima, Chen Liu, Ruth Morrow, Debbie G. Senesky, Magdalini Theodoridou, Maikel C. Rheinstädter, Meng Zhang, and Lynn J. Rothschild

**Published:** 19 September 2022  
**Journal:** Frontiers in Built Environment  
**DOI:** 10.3389/fbuil.2022.965145

## Abstract
A critical aspect of human space exploration is the ability to construct habitats while minimizing payload mass. We propose fungal bio-composites for growing extra-terrestrial structures directly at the destination. These mycelium-based composites are fire-resistant, insulating, and can be used independently or with regolith. This biological approach facilitates material fabrication, assembly, maintenance, and repair, providing resilient structures for long-duration space missions.

## 1 Introduction
To build on the Moon and Mars, there are significant energy, mass, and volume trade-offs. Using locally available building materials (ISRU) and corresponding technologies is crucial. This paper introduces an alternative, biological approach using Engineered Living Materials (ELMs) for creating living buildings, focusing on mycelium-based bio-composites.

## 2 State of the Art: Construction Approaches
The Moon and Mars lack protective atmospheres and magnetic fields, requiring pressurized, radiation-shielded structures. Traditional approaches include:
- **Class I:** Pre-fabricated rigid modules (e.g., ISS modules).
- **Class II:** Deployable/Inflatable modules (e.g., Bigelow Aerospace).
- **Class III:** ISRU-based (Regolith 3D printing, sintering, ice habitats).

## 4 Mycelium as a Medium
Fungal mycelium consists of branching hyphae that form light composites with excellent mechanical and thermal properties. Mycelium acts as a binder. Exploration of different species, such as *Ganoderma lucidum*, shows potential for varied architectural uses.

## 6 Off-Earth Myco-architecture
Two strategies are presented:
1.  **Standalone Approach (Moon):** Inflatable structures provide a foundational form/vapor barrier where mycelium is grown within cavities in a controlled environment.
2.  **Reinforcement Approach (Mars):** Mycelium acts as a biological binder for Martian regolith, reducing the need for high-mass transport and high-energy sintering.

## 10 Conclusion
Mycelium-based biocomposites offer a sustainable, low-energy, and low-mass alternative for space construction. While still in early stages, the potential for self-healing, adaptive, and regenerative habitats makes bio-fabrication a compelling future technology for off-Earth settlement.
