# Sulfur-based materials for martian construction: Pioneering sustainable extraterrestrial building solutions

**Authors:** Wei Sun, Jiabao Zhao, Handong Yan, Zijin Xiao, Tiejiong Lou
**Journal:** Alexandria Engineering Journal 117 (2025) 27–44
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.aej.2025.01.008

## Abstract
The study aims to devise sustainable construction materials and techniques for Martian infrastructure using local resources like sulfur and Martian soil (MS) simulant. Experiments involved fabricating bricks with varying MS percentages, aggregate types (SiO2, Al2O3, Fe2O3), and bond thicknesses. Key findings show that 20% MS content provides 25.2 MPa compressive strength, 3 mm is the optimal bond thickness, and Fe2O3 aggregates achieve the highest compressive strength (39.2 MPa). The bricks are recyclable and suitable for pyramid-shaped load-distributing structures.

## 1. Introduction
Establishing a sustainable human presence on Mars requires infrastructure capable of sustaining life and utilizing available resources (ISRU). Mars presents challenges such as low atmospheric pressure (636 Pa), extreme temperatures (-143°C to 20°C), and water scarcity. Conventional water-dependent curing processes are unviable; thus, water-free methods like elemental sulfur (ES) are appealing. Sulfur can be extracted from Martian sulfides and used as a binder for regolith aggregates.

## 2. Experiments
### 2.1. Materials
- **Binder:** Elemental sulfur powder (molten at 140°C).
- **Aggregates:** Martian Soil (MS) simulant (analogous to JSC-1m), high-purity SiO2, Al2O3, and Fe2O3.
- **Simulant Composition:** Primary oxides include SiO2 (46.5%), Al2O3 (23.3%), and Fe2O3 (15.6%).

### 2.2. Test Procedures
- **Compressive Tests:** 25.4 mm (1 in.) cube specimens.
- **Bond Strength:** Four-point bending tests on bonded cubic bricks with various bond thicknesses (2 mm to 6 mm).
- **Recycling:** Bricks were melted and repurposed for multiple cycles.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### 3.1. Impact of MS Simulant Content
- **Strength:** Increasing MS content from 0% to 20% improved compressive strength from 16.8 MPa to 25.2 MPa.
- **Microstructure:** SEM images revealed that aggregates help occupy voids in the sulfur matrix, creating a denser structure.

### 3.2. Bond Layer Thickness
- **Optimal Thickness:** 3 mm provided the best balance of strength (1.42 MPa) and workability.
- **Trend:** Strength increased from 2 mm to 3 mm but decreased as thickness moved from 3 mm to 6 mm due to shrinkage and cracking potential.

### 3.3. Aggregate Types
| Aggregate Type | Mean Compressive Strength (MPa) | Bond Strength (MPa) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| MS (20%) | 25.2 | 1.28 |
| Al2O3 (20%) | 27.0 | 1.25 |
| SiO2 (20%) | 33.4 | 1.32 |
| Fe2O3 (20%) | 39.2 | 0.52 |

### 3.4. Recyclability
- First recycling cycle reduced strength from 33.4 MPa to 26.3 MPa.
- Subsequent cycles showed a gradual recovery to 29.9 MPa as sulfur grains refined.

## 4. Structural Design: Pyramid-Shaped Structures
Pyramid designs offer efficient load distribution, stable bases for negative pressure (internal habitat vs. external vacuum), and aerodynamic profiles to deflect Martian wind/dust. A modular construction system using ice blocks as temporary internal supports was proposed, which would later evaporate under controlled heat.

## 5. Conclusions
1. Sulfur-based bricks with 20% MS simulant meet practical strength requirements (MU25 bricks).
2. 3 mm is the optimal bond thickness for masonry assembly.
3. Fe2O3 aggregates offer maximum strength, but SiO2 provides better bonding properties.
4. Sulfur-based materials are recyclable, supporting sustainable long-term missions.
