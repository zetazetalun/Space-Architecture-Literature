# Sulfur-based materials for martian construction: Pioneering sustainable extraterrestrial building solutions

**Authors:** Wei Sun, Jiabao Zhao, Handong Yan, Zijin Xiao, Tiejiong Lou  
**Journal:** Alexandria Engineering Journal 117 (2025) 27–44  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.aej.2025.01.008

## Abstract
This study devises sustainable construction materials and techniques for Martian infrastructure, addressing challenges like water scarcity and extreme temperatures. Martian bricks were fabricated using Martian soil (MS) simulant and sulfur as a bonding agent. Experiments included compressive tests on cube specimens and four-point bending tests for bond strength. Key findings show that MS simulant enhances compressive strength (25.2 MPa at 20% content), 3 mm is the optimal bond thickness (1.42 MPa bond strength), and sulfur-based bricks are recyclable with minimal strength reduction.

## 1. Introduction
The construction of habitats on Mars is crucial for human settlement. Traditional Earth-based construction depends on liquid water, which is unstable under Martian atmospheric pressure (636 Pa). Sulfur, which can be extracted from Martian sulfides, offers a water-free alternative. This research explores elemental sulfur (ES) as a binder for regolith and the use of pyramid-shaped structures for efficient load distribution and resistance to Martian winds/seismic activity.

## 2. Experimental Investigation

### 2.1. Materials
*   **Binder:** Elemental sulfur powder (molten at 140°C).
*   **Aggregates:** Martian Soil (MS) simulant (JSC-1m), SiO2, Al2O3, and Fe2O3.

### 2.2. Manufacturing Process
1. Sulfur powder heated to 140°C for 30 min.
2. Molten sulfur mixed with Martian aggregates for 1-2 min.
3. Re-heated at 140°C for 20 min and stirred.
4. Mixture poured into 25.4 mm (1 in.) cube molds and compacted.
5. Cured for 24 h at 20°C before demolding.

### 2.3. Key Test Results (Table Summary)
| Aggregate Type | Content (%) | Compressive Strength (MPa) | Bond Strength (MPa) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Pure Sulfur | 0 | 16.8 | 1.42 |
| MS Simulant | 20 | 25.2 | 1.28 |
| SiO2 | 20 | 33.4 | 1.35 |
| Al2O3 | 20 | 27.0 | 1.29 |
| Fe2O3 | 20 | 39.2 | 0.72 |

## 3. Findings and Discussion
*   **Strength Enhancement:** Increasing MS simulant from 0% to 20% improved compressive strength from 16.8 MPa to 25.2 MPa.
*   **Aggregate Comparison:** Fe2O3 provided the highest compressive strength (39.2 MPa) but the weakest bond strength. SiO2 offered a balanced performance with 33.4 MPa compressive and 1.35 MPa bond strength.
*   **Bond Thickness:** An optimal thickness of 3 mm was identified. Thicker bonds led to diminished strength due to shrinkage and cracking.
*   **Recyclability:** Bricks can be melted and reformed. While the first recycle cycle reduced strength to 26.3 MPa, subsequent cycles showed recovery towards 29.9 MPa due to microstructural refinement.
*   **Structural Design:** Pyramid-shaped structures are recommended to handle negative pressure (internal Earth-like pressure vs. external vacuum) and to deflect Martian dust storms.

## 4. Conclusions
The study validates sulfur-regolith composites as a robust, water-free building material for Mars. The material is recyclable, utilizes local resources (ISRU), and supports modular construction of resilient pyramid-shaped habitats.
