# Sulfur-based materials for martian construction: Pioneering sustainable extraterrestrial building solutions

**Authors:** Wei Sun, Jiabao Zhao, Handong Yan, Zijin Xiao, Tiejiong Lou  
**Published in:** Alexandria Engineering Journal 117 (2025) 27–44  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.aej.2025.01.008

## Abstract
This study aims to devise sustainable construction materials and techniques for Martian infrastructure, addressing challenges like water scarcity and extreme temperatures using local resources. Martian bricks were fabricated using Martian soil (MS) simulant and sulfur as a binder. Experiments included compressive tests on cube specimens and four-point bending tests for bond strength, alongside a parametric study on simulant percentages and aggregate types. Findings suggest sulfur-based bricks are recyclable, strong (25.2 MPa average with 20% simulant), and suitable for pyramid-shaped structural designs.

## 1. Introduction
The pursuit of Martian habitats necessitates construction methodologies that utilize in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) to minimize Earth-dependence. Sulfur, which can be extracted from Martian sulfides, offers a water-free alternative to traditional cement, which is critical given the absence of stable liquid water on the Martian surface.

## 2. Experimental Investigation
### 2.1 Materials
- **Martian Soil Simulant:** Analogous to JSC-1m.
- **Binder:** Elemental sulfur (ES) heated to 140°C.
- **Aggregates:** High-purity SiO2, Al2O3, and Fe2O3.

### 2.2 Manufacturing Process
1. Sulfur powder is melted at 140°C.
2. Aggregates are added and stirred for 1-2 minutes.
3. Mixture is reheated and stirred again.
4. Molten mixture is poured into 25.4 mm cube molds and cured for 24 hours.

### 2.3 Structural Geometry
The study explores pyramid-shaped structures for their load distribution efficiency and resistance to Martian winds and seismic activity. Martian ice is proposed as a temporary support material during construction.

### 3.1 Compressive Strength
- Bricks with 20% MS simulant achieved an average strength of **25.2 MPa**.
- Pure sulfur bricks averaged **16.8 MPa**.
- Fe2O3 aggregates yielded the highest strength at **39.2 MPa** due to uniform distribution and low porosity.

### 3.2 Bond Strength
- Optimal bond thickness identified as **3 mm**, yielding **1.42 MPa**.
- Excessive thickness (6 mm) reduced strength to **0.91 MPa** due to shrinkage and cracking.

### 3.3 Recyclability
- First recycling cycle reduced strength from 33.4 MPa to 26.3 MPa.
- Subsequent cycles showed a recovery to 29.9 MPa due to refinement of sulfur grains.

## 4. Conclusions
The research validates sulfur-based materials for Martian construction. Pyramid geometries are recommended for their inherent stability against pressure differentials and dust storms. The combination of sulfur and SiO2 aggregates is highlighted as an optimal mixture for its strength and abundance in Martian regolith.
