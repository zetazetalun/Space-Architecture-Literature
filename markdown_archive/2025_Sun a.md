# Sulfur-based materials for martian construction: Pioneering sustainable extraterrestrial building solutions

## Abstract
This study aims to devise sustainable construction materials and techniques for Martian infrastructure using local resources. Martian bricks were fabricated with varying components including Martian soil (MS) simulant, sulfur, and different aggregates (SiO2, Al2O3, Fe2O3). Key findings include the optimization of MS simulant content (20% content yields 25.2 MPa), the identification of 3 mm as the optimal bond thickness (1.42 MPa bond strength), and the validation of recyclability for sulfur-based bricks. Pyramid-shaped structures are proposed for efficient load distribution and resistance to Martian environment factors like low pressure and dust storms.

## 1. Introduction
The construction of habitats on Mars is crucial for providing shelter and protection. Challenges include low atmospheric pressure (636 Pa), extreme temperatures (-143°C to 20°C), and water scarcity. Traditional Earth-based construction depends heavily on liquid water, which is unstable on the Martian surface. Sulfur-based materials offer a water-free alternative, utilizing abundant sulfur resources that can be extracted from Martian sulfides.

## 2. Experimental Methodology
### 2.1 Materials
- **Binder:** Elemental sulfur (heated to 140°C to reach molten state).
- **Aggregates:** Martian Soil (MS) simulant (JSC-1m), SiO2, Al2O3, and Fe2O3.
- **Particle Size:** MS simulant median size ~3.2 μm.

### 2.2 Fabrication and Testing
- **Process:** Sulfur is melted, mixed with aggregates, reheated, poured into molds, and cured for 24h at 20°C.
- **Testing:** Compressive tests on 25.4 mm cubes; four-point bending tests for bond strength (using a 5mm notch).
- **Structural Application:** Assembly of pyramid-shaped structures using a modular brick system with ice supports used as temporary placeholders.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **MS Content:** Increasing MS content from 0% to 20% improves compressive strength significantly (up to 25.2 MPa).
- **Aggregate Types:** Fe2O3 aggregates achieved the highest compressive strength (39.2 MPa), while SiO2 provided the strongest bonds (1.32–1.35 MPa).
- **Bond Thickness:** 3 mm was identified as optimal. Thicknesses beyond 3 mm led to decreased bond strength due to increased shrinkage and potential cracking.
- **Recyclability:** Bricks can be melted and reformed. While the first cycle saw a strength reduction (from 33.4 MPa to 26.3 MPa), subsequent cycles showed recovery up to 29.9 MPa.

## 4. Conclusion
Sulfur-based bricks and molten sulfur bonding provide a viable, water-free construction method for Mars. Pyramid-shaped structures enhance resilience against seismic activity and wind. The study provides empirical equations to predict material behavior under various compositions and reuse cycles.
