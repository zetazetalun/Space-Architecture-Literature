# StarCrete: A starch-based biocomposite for off-world construction ## Abstract Robust and affordable technology capabilities are needed before a sustained human presence on the lunar and Martian surfaces can be established. A key challenge is the production of high-strength structural materials from in situ resources. Here, we demonstrate the use of ordinary starch as a binder for simulated extraterrestrial regolith to produce a high-strength biocomposite material, termed StarCrete. Lunar and Martian StarCrete achieved compressive strengths of 91.7 and 72.0 MPa, respectively, which is well within the domain of high-strength concrete (>42 MPa). ## 1 Introduction A sustained human presence will require habitats with thick walls for radiation and meteoroid shielding. Due to the high cost of transporting mass, bulk construction materials must be produced via in situ resource utilisation (ISRU). Most proposed solutions have drawbacks like high energy/water use. Starch (amylum) is an abundant plant-based carbohydrate produced as food. Its use as a binder could integrate food and construction systems. ## 2 Results and Discussion Several starch sources were screened; potato starch was most effective, yielding an UCS of 17.7 MPa before optimization. Optimization via Design of Experiments (DoE) involved screening additives like MgCl2, urea, and acetic acid. While urea and acetic acid initially showed promise, higher gelatinisation temperatures (up to 120C) without urea proved most optimal. The final optimized StarCrete achieved UCS values of 71.95 MPa (Martian simulant MGS-1) and 91.68 MPa (Lunar simulant LHS-1). Flexural strengths were 8.41 MPa (Martian) and 2.14 MPa (Lunar). ## 3 Conclusions and outlook StarCrete offers significant advantages: 1) Risk reduction (edible binder), 2) Practicality (simple process), 3) System integration (linked to food production), 4) Resourcefulness (low energy, water recovery), 5) Resource locality (on-site production), and 6) Architectural flexibility (high strength).
