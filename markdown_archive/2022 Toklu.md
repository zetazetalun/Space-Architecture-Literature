# Lunar soils, simulants and lunar construction materials: An overview

## Abstract
One of the hot subjects in space civil engineering is the research for establishing outposts on space bodies, starting with the Moon. This paper presents an overview of three aspects: the nature of in-situ soils obtained with lunar missions, the variety of lunar soil simulants produced on Earth, and their use in producing lunar construction materials.

## 1. Introduction
Civil engineering definitions for terrestrial constructions are verified for extraterrestrial constructions, starting with the Moon. Construction materials will be mostly raw materials found on the Moon due to the high cost of transportation. The primary material available is regolith.

## 2. Lunar missions and lunar soil samples
The Luna Program (Soviet Union) and Apollo missions (US) brought back approximately 382 kg of samples. China's Chang'e 5 mission recently added 2 kg. Analysis shows variations between mare (darker, basaltic) and highland (lighter, anorthositic) regions. 

## 3. Lunar soil simulants
Simulants are terrestrial materials mimicking lunar properties. The paper lists 54 simulants developed between 1971 and 2021, including MLS-1, JSC-1, and more recent ones like EAC-1 and TBG-1. 

## 4. Lunar construction materials
### 4.1. Regolith-based ordinary cements and concretes
Research since the 1980s explores using CaO in regolith. Challenges include the lack of liquid water and extreme temperature changes causing evaporation or freezing.

### 4.2. Sulfur-based lunar concretes
A water-less alternative. Sulfur (available in troilite) acts as a binder. It reaches 70-80% strength in 24h but may sublimate in vacuum at high temperatures.

### 4.3. Polymer-based lunar concretes
Uses resins or epoxies (2-10%) as binders. These show ductile behavior and higher flexural strength than typical concrete.

### 4.4. Geopolymerized regolith concretes
Activated with alkaline solutions (e.g., sodium hydroxide). These can yield strengths up to 50 MPa and allow for significant water recycling (up to 98.6%).

### 4.5. Lunar construction materials with 3D printing
Additive Manufacturing (AM) is a key approach. Techniques include:
- **Binder Jetting:** Liquid binder sprayed onto granular simulant (e.g., DNA-1).
- **Sintering-based AM:** Solar, laser, or microwave sintering. 
- **Extrusion:** Direct printing of liquid inks containing regolith and copolymers.

### 4.6. Other sintered or cast regolith blocks
Sintered JSC-1A has shown strengths between 84.6 and 218.8 MPa. Cast basalt can reach 538 MPa compressive strength.

## 5. Conclusive remarks
Construction materials from lunar soil are in their infancy. Future work must find the best materials for the lunar environment using ISRU technologies to enable sustainable colonization.
