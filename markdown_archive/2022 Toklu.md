# Lunar soils, simulants and lunar construction materials: An overview

## Abstract
This paper presents an overview of three aspects of using local materials in lunar construction: the nature of in-situ soils from lunar missions, variety of lunar soil simulants, and their use in producing lunar construction materials like concrete, cement, and bricks.

## 1. Introduction
Civil engineering in space involves establishing outposts, starting with the Moon. Due to high transportation costs, in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) is essential. The primary material available is regolith, which can be processed into cement, concrete, and bricks.

## 2. Lunar missions and lunar soil samples
- **Soviet Luna Program:** Brought samples via Luna 16, 20, and 24.
- **US Apollo Missions:** Six missions returned approximately 382 kg of regolith.
- **China Chang’e 5:** Returned ~2 kg of regolith from a younger basaltic area.

### Characterization
- **Mare areas:** Darker, basaltic plains.
- **Highlands:** Lighter, primarily anorthosites, coarse-grained, lower density.

## 3. Lunar soil simulants
Simulants are terrestrial substitutes used for experiments. The paper lists 54 simulants developed up to August 2021, including MLS-1, JSC-1, FJS-1, NU-LHT series, and the newly developed Turkish simulant TBG-1.

## 4. Lunar construction materials
### 4.1. Regolith-based ordinary cements and concretes
Lunar regolith can serve as aggregates. Challenges include the lack of water and extreme temperatures causing evaporation or freezing of the mix.

### 4.2. Sulfur-based lunar concretes
Water-less alternative using sulfur (available on the Moon as troilite) as a binder. Melting point ~119°C. Reaches 70-80% strength in 24 hours.

### 4.3. Polymer-based lunar concretes
Uses binders like polyethylene or epoxy. Experiments show ductile-like behavior and strengths comparable to steel-reinforced concrete.

### 4.4. Geopolymerized regolith concretes
Formed through dehydration-polycondensation. Can utilize recycled water.

### 4.5. 3D printing and Sintering
- **Binder Jetting:** DNA-1 simulant with liquid binder.
- **Extrusion-based:** Liquid inks with regolith and polymers.
- **Solar/Laser Sintering:** Melting or bonding particles using concentrated energy.
- **Microwave Sintering:** Facilitated by the chemical composition of regolith.

### 4.6. Other sintered or cast blocks
Cast basalt (melted regolith) exhibits high compressive strength but brittle behavior.

## 5. Conclusive remarks
Lunar construction is in its infancy. Future work must focus on standardization of simulants and long-term performance under extreme environmental conditions.
