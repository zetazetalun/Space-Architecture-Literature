# Lunar soils, simulants and lunar construction materials: An overview

**Authors:** Yusuf Cengiz Toklu, Pinar Akpinar
**Journal:** Advances in Space Research 70 (2022) 762–779
**Published:** 20 May 2022

## Abstract
One of the hot subjects in space civil engineering is the research for establishing outposts on space bodies, starting with the Moon. This wide research topic mainly covers learning about ground conditions and soils of these extraterrestrial bodies, looking for means of using the soil in producing construction materials, designing structures appropriate for local conditions, and finally choosing construction techniques to be applied using mainly these materials. In this paper, an overview is presented on three aspects of using local materials in lunar construction activities: nature of in-situ soils obtained with lunar missions, the variety of lunar soil simulants produced on Earth, and their use in producing lunar construction materials.

## 1. Introduction
Civil engineering in space is defined by the necessity of using in-situ materials due to the high cost of transporting materials from Earth. The primary material available is lunar regolith, which can be processed into cement, concrete, bricks, and other structural elements.

## 3. Lunar Soil Simulants
Since real regolith is scarce, over 50 simulants have been developed worldwide (USA, China, Japan, Korea, Germany, India, etc.). Simulants vary in chemical and geotechnical fidelity depending on their intended use (excavation, mobility, or construction).

## 4. Lunar Construction Materials

### 4.1 Regolith-based Cements and Concretes
While hydration of standard cement is difficult due to water scarcity and vacuum conditions, researchers have explored producing cement by treating regolith at 2000°C using solar concentrators.

### 4.2 Sulfur-based Lunar Concretes
Sulfur concrete is a waterless alternative. Sulfur melts at 119°C and acts as a binder. It reaches 70–80% of its strength within 24 hours but is susceptible to sublimation in vacuum at high temperatures.

### 4.3 Polymer-based Lunar Concretes
Uses binders like epoxy or polyethylene. Studies show these can achieve compressive strengths comparable to steel-reinforced concrete (12–16 MPa).

### 4.4 Geopolymerized Regolith Concretes
Formed through dehydration-polycondensation. Some studies achieved strengths of 26–35 MPa with nearly zero water consumption during manufacture due to water recycling.

### 4.5 Additive Manufacturing (3D Printing)
3D printing is considered a key technology for ISRU. Techniques include:
- **D-Shape Technology:** Using liquid binders and granular material.
- **Sintering:** Using lasers, solar energy, or microwaves to bond particles without binders.
- **Extrusion:** Printing liquid inks or polymers mixed with regolith.

## 5. Conclusive Remarks
The research for producing construction materials from lunar soil is in its infancy. Future work must focus on systematical experimental studies to find materials that survive the harsh lunar environment (vacuum, thermal cycles, and radiation).
