# Lunar soils, simulants and lunar construction materials: An overview

**Authors:** Yusuf Cengiz Toklu, Pinar Akpinar
**Journal:** Advances in Space Research 70 (2022) 762–779
**Published:** 20 May 2022 (Available online)

## Abstract
One of the hot subjects in space civil engineering is the research for establishing outposts on space bodies, starting with the Moon. This wide research topic covers learning about ground conditions, using soil for construction materials, designing structures, and choosing construction techniques. This paper presents an overview of in-situ lunar soils, lunar soil simulants, and their use in producing lunar construction materials.

## 1. Introduction
Civil engineering in space requires the use of raw materials found on the Moon due to the high cost of transportation from Earth. The primary material is regolith, which can be used to produce lunar cement, concrete, and bricks. Since real lunar samples are limited (<400 kg), researchers use simulants for terrestrial testing.

## 2. Lunar missions and lunar soil samples
Samples have been returned via the Soviet Luna Program, US Apollo missions, and China's Chang’e 5 mission. Properties vary between mare (darker, basaltic) and highland (lighter, anorthositic) regions. Highland regolith generally shows increased coarse-grained particles and lower density compared to mare samples.

## 3. Lunar soil simulants
Simulants are terrestrial materials engineered to mimic lunar regolith. The paper lists 54 simulants developed globally. Key historical simulants include MLS-1 (1971) and JSC-1 (1993). Recent developments include TBG-1 (Turkey, 2021) and LSS-ISAC-1 (India, 2020).

## 4. Lunar construction materials
Research into ISRU identifies several material categories:

### 4.1. Regolith-based ordinary cements
Requires water, which is scarce. Vacuum conditions on the Moon lead to water evaporation, increasing porosity and reducing strength.

### 4.2. Sulfur-based lunar concretes
Water-less. Sulfur (melted at 119°C) acts as a binder. Can reach 70-80% strength within 24 hours.

### 4.3. Polymer-based lunar concretes
Uses binders like epoxy or polyethylene. High strength is achievable (e.g., 12.9 MPa to 60 MPa flexural strength depending on polymer type).

### 4.4. Geopolymerized regolith concretes
Formed through dehydration-polycondensation using alkaline activators. Can reach strengths over 35 MPa.

### 4.5. 3D Printing
Additive Manufacturing (AM) allows complex geometries with minimal preprocessing. Techniques include binder jetting and sintering.

### 4.6. Sintering/Casting
Regolith is melted or heated to bond particles. Cast basalt can reach compressive strengths of 538 MPa but is brittle.

## 5. Conclusive remarks
Lunar construction is in its infancy. While numerous simulants exist, there is a need for more systematic experimental work to find the best materials and technologies for the harsh lunar environment.
