# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 449 (2024) 138517
**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Jiale Huang, Xiangyu Wang, Ming Jen Tan, Wei She, Hao Zhou, Guoqing Geng

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial exploration, represented by Lunar base construction, has been promoted worldwide. This study proposes a novel extraterrestrial construction material composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and only 4 wt% of silicate adhesive. This silicate-regolith composite (SLRC) can be directly self-hardened in vacuum within 4.5 h without any curing to achieve 29 MPa of hardening strength. It shows comprehensive durability with stable microstructures and >70 % of residual strength against long-term vacuum exposure, extreme temperature differences (-196 °C to 78 °C), and intense γ-ray and proton radiations. The in-situ solidification originates from vacuum dehydration, where silica clusters in solution spontaneously cohere into a solid network. This vacuum self-hardening composite allows for cryogenic preparation even below −40 °C, providing a facile, durable, and versatile solution for in-situ extraterrestrial construction.

## 1. Introduction
Establishing a lunar habitat (NASA's Artemis, ESA's Lunar base 3D printing, CNSA's ILRS) requires materials that can withstand rigorous environments, including temperature differences (-200 °C to 120 °C), high vacuum (10⁻¹⁰-10⁻⁹ Pa), and radiation. Transporting materials from Earth is prohibitively expensive, necessitating the in-situ utilization of lunar resources (ISRU). Current methods like sintering (>1000 °C) or geopolymerization (requiring heat curing at >60 °C) have limitations in efficiency and energy consumption. SLRC addresses these by utilizing vacuum dehydration as a hardening mechanism.

## 2. Materials and Methods
- **Regolith Simulants:** LRS-1 (Apollo-17 target), LRS-2 (Apollo-14), LRS-3 (Apollo-12), and MRS (Martian regolith simulant).
- **Binders:** Sodium silicate (SS) solution with varying moduli (2.0, 2.5, 3.0) and modifying agents (sodium pyrophosphate, sodium dodecyl sulfate, hydrothermal treatment).
- **Preparation:** Damp SLRC powders were cold-pressed (9.5 MPa) into 40x10x10 mm³ green bodies and immediately placed in a vacuum tank (<10 Pa) to mimic the lunar environment.
- **Testing:** Flexural and compressive strength tests, SEM, MIP (porosity), NMR, and FTIR (chemical bonds), plus molecular dynamic (MD) simulations.

## 3. Results and Discussion
### 3.1 Self-hardening
SLRC rapidly loses water and self-hardens in vacuum without curing. A moderate silicate modulus (2.5) produces the toughest material. Modified binders (e.g., SS2.5HT) achieved 6.54 MPa flexural and 20.34 MPa compressive strength within 4.5 hours.

### 3.2 Durability
SLRC maintains >71% of flexural and >88% of compressive strength after 108 days (4 lunar diurnal cycles). The material exhibits exceptional resistance to radiation (γ-ray and protons) and remains stable after 16 thermal shocks.

## 4. Conclusion
SLRC satisfies the essential requirements for in-situ extraterrestrial construction, especially in cryogenic polar regions or sunless lava tubes. It requires minimal terrestrial mass (4 wt%), no heat treatment, and demonstrates long-term stability in extreme environments.
