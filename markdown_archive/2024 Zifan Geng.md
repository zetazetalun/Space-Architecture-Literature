# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 449 (2024) 138517  
**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Jiale Huang, Xiangyu Wang, Ming Jen Tan, Wei She, Hao Zhou, Guoqing Geng

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial exploration, represented by Lunar base construction, has been promoted worldwide as a multi-disciplinary, cutting-edge and strategic issue. Herein we proposed a novel extraterrestrial construction material composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and only 4 wt% of silicate adhesive. This silicate-regolith composite can be directly self-hardened in vacuum within 4.5 h without any curing to achieve 29 MPa of hardening strength. Meanwhile, it shows comprehensive durability with stable microstructures and >70% of residual strength against long-term vacuum exposure, huge temperature difference and intense γ-ray and proton radiations. Compared with lunar geopolymer and other existing extraterrestrial construction materials, this vacuum self-hardening composite allows a cryogenic preparation even below -40 °C using diverse regolith with a wide range of compositions and sizes.

## 1. Introduction
The infrastructure on the Moon will face rigorous environments including huge temperature differences (-200 °C to 120 °C), high vacuum (10⁻¹⁰ to 10⁻⁹ Pa), and radiation. Current materials like lunar geopolymer require sealed thermocuring (above 60 °C) for long periods, which is difficult for in-situ setup. This study investigates Silicate-Lunar Regolith Composite (SLRC) which utilizes vacuum dehydration as a hardening mechanism.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1 Materials
- **Regolith Simulants (LRS):** LRS-1 (Apollo-17 simulant), LRS-2 (Apollo-14), LRS-3 (Apollo-12), and MRS (Martian regolith simulant).
- **Binder:** Sodium silicate (SS) solutions with modulus adjusted between 2.0 and 3.0.

### 2.2 Sample Preparation
Regolith and silicate solution (mass ratio 0.14:1) were mixed in a hermetic environment. The mixture was cold-pressed into 40x10x10 mm³ green bodies under 9.5 MPa and subsequently placed in a vacuum tank (< 10 Pa).

## 4. Conclusion
The SLRC material achieves rapid solidification (80% strength in 0.5h) via vacuum dehydration. It offers a low-energy, low-carried-mass solution that functions in the shadows or polar regions without artificial heating, maintaining structural integrity across repeated thermal cycles and radiation exposure.
