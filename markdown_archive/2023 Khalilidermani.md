# A Survey on Extraterrestrial Habitation Structures with a Focus on Energy-Saving 3D Printing Techniques

**Authors:** Mitra Khalilidermani and Dariusz Knez  
**Publisher:** MDPI (Applied Sciences)  
**Date:** December 2023  

## Abstract
In the past two decades, various space agencies have shown great enthusiasm for constructing habitable structures on lunar and Martian surfaces. This research evaluates different habitat construction techniques from technical, economic, and energy-consumption perspectives. The primary focus is on 3D printing, which has shown significant potential in automated manufacturing. It was found that a combination of 3D-printed components with an internal inflatable module is the most promising technique. Microwave sintering was identified as the most energy-saving and reliable approach for melting on-site regolith.

## 1. Introduction
Large space agencies (NASA, ESA) are tracing the construction of permanent extraterrestrial habitats on the Moon and Mars. Engineering challenges include unfavorable physics (microgravity, radiation), risky regolith characteristics, and uncertain construction techniques.

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1. Physics-Based Challenges
- **Microgravity:** Lower gravitational acceleration (1.62 m/s² on Moon, 3.71 m/s² on Mars) causes stability issues and requires specialized foundations.
- **Cryogenic Temperatures:** Reaching −230 °C at lunar poles and −140 °C at Martian poles, inducing thermal stress and affecting binder liquids.
- **Atmospheric Pressure:** Extremely low pressure (0.6 kPa on Mars) limits fluid applications and increases radiation exposure.
- **Radiation & Micrometeoroids:** High-speed impacts and solar/cosmic rays necessitate protective regolith layers (1.5 m to 2 m thick).

### 2.2. Aggregate Ability of the Regolith
Regolith can be used in raw form (shielding bags/layers) or synthesized (blended with binders or sintered). 
- **Sintering methods:** Laser, Solar, and Microwave sintering.

### 2.4. 3D Printing Technology
Three main techniques for space application:
1. **D-shape (Monolite):** Selective activation of material layers.
2. **Contour Crafting (CC):** Extrusion-based layering of sulfur concrete or regolith paste.
3. **Freeform construction:** Extrusion of concrete-like mixtures.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Microwave Sintering:** Requires 33–44 times less energy than laser sintering. Heat penetration is deeper than solar sintering.
- **Structural Efficiency:** Sulfur concrete using regolith simulants reached a compressive strength of ~55 MPa.
- **Economic Factors:** Transportation cost estimated at ~EUR 200,000 per kg; a 40 m³ outpost would require 5.3 tons of Earth-shipped materials (binders/inflatables), costing over EUR 1,060 billion.

## 4. Conclusions
Cryogenic temperature is the most restricting factor for binders and material durability. The ideal habitat class involves a Class II inflatable module for internal atmosphere containment protected by a Class III 3D-printed regolith shell. Microwave sintering is the preferred sintering method due to energy efficiency.
