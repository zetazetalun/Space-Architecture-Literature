# A Survey on Extraterrestrial Habitation Structures with a Focus on Energy-Saving 3D Printing Techniques

## Abstract
In the past two decades, space agencies have shown interest in constructing habitable structures on the Moon and Mars. This review evaluates habitat construction techniques from technical, economic, and energy-consumption perspectives, focusing on 3D printing. The research identifies the combination of 3D-printed components with internal inflatable modules as the most promising technique and microwave sintering as the most energy-saving regolith melting approach.

## 1. Introduction
Large space agencies (NASA, ESA) are planning permanent extraterrestrial habitats. Key missions include NASA's Mars Exploration Program (Perseverance rover) and the Artemis program for lunar habitation. Engineering challenges include unfavorable physics, risky regolith characteristics, and uncertain construction techniques.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1. Physics-Based Challenges
Primary challenges include:
- **Microgravity**: Leads to habitat instability and requires specialized foundations.
- **Cryogenic Temperatures**: Lunar poles (-230 °C) and Martian poles (-140 °C) induce thermal stresses.
- **Airless Atmosphere**: Low pressure (0.6 kPa on Mars; 4–10 kPa on Moon) curtails fluid application and increases radiation/meteoroid risks.
- **Magnetic Fields**: Absence of magnetospheres exposes surfaces to solar winds and cosmic rays.
- **Surface Radiations**: Requires protective shielding (1.5 m to 2 m regolith thickness).
- **Micrometeoroid Impacts**: High-velocity impacts require resilient structural layers.

### 2.2. Aggregate Ability of the Regolith
Regolith can be used in raw form (shielding bags) or synthesized form (mixed with binders). 
- **Sulfur Concrete**: Mixing regolith with pure sulfur (extracted from troilite) achieved 55.16 MPa compressive strength.
- **Sintering**: Methods include laser, solar, and microwave sintering. Microwave sintering is highlighted for deeper penetration and energy efficiency.

### 2.4. 3D Printing Technologies
Three main techniques are discussed:
1. **Monolite (D-shape)**: Selective activation of material layers using a binder (Binder Jetting style).
2. **Contour Crafting (CC)**: Extrusion-based process using concrete-like mixtures.
3. **Freeform Construction**: Layer-by-layer extrusion with a catalyst.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Microwave Sintering**: Identified as the most reliable option; requires 33–44 times less energy than laser sintering.
- **Hybrid Approach**: The most effective model is a 3D-printed regolith shell providing protection for an internal inflatable module (providing the breathable environment).
- **Transportation Costs**: Estimated at EUR 200,000 per kg; using ISRU can save over 60% of interplanetary transportation costs (specifically by reducing binder transport requirements).

## 4. Conclusions
The research identifies cryogenic temperatures and microgravity as the most restricting environmental factors. Current 3D printing technology is robust enough for early structures, but full ISRU operations with high-power rovers are needed for larger habitats.
