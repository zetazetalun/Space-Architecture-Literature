# A Survey on Extraterrestrial Habitation Structures with a Focus on Energy-Saving 3D Printing Techniques

**Authors:** Mitra Khalilidermani and Dariusz Knez  
**Published in:** *Applied Sciences*, 2023, 13, 12913  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3390/app132312913

## Abstract
In the past two decades, various space agencies have shown great enthusiasm for constructing habitable structures on lunar and Martian surfaces. This research conducts a comprehensive review of proposed extraterrestrial structures, evaluating construction techniques from technical, economic, and energy-consumption perspectives. The primary focus is on 3D printing. It was found that a combination of 3D-printed components with an internal inflatable module is the most promising technique. Microwave sintering was identified as the most energy-saving and reliable approach for melting on-site regolith.

## 1. Introduction
Future space habitation is envisioned for the sustainability of mankind. Agencies like NASA and ESA are tracing the construction of permanent habitats on the Moon and Mars. Three main categories of uncertainties postpone development: unfavorable extraterrestrial physics, risky regolith characteristics, and uncertain construction techniques.

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1. Physics-Based Challenges
Primary challenges include:
*   **Microgravity:** Affects habitat stability and requires specialized foundations.
*   **Cryogenic Temperatures:** Moon poles (-230 °C) and Mars poles (-140 °C) require thermal insulation and materials resistant to thermal stress.
*   **Atmospheric Pressure:** Tenuous atmospheres (Mars: 0.6 kPa; Moon: ~0-10 kPa) limit fluid use and increase radiation/micrometeoroid risks.
*   **Lack of Magnetic Field:** Increases exposure to solar winds and cosmic rays.
*   **Surface Radiation:** Requires protective regolith layers (1.5 to 2 m thick).
*   **Micrometeoroid Impacts:** High-speed impactors necessitate shielding.

### 2.2. Aggregate Ability of the Regolith
Regolith can be used in two forms:
1.  **Raw:** Stored in bags or deposited directly as a shielding layer.
2.  **Synthesized:** Combined with additives (e.g., epoxy) or binders (e.g., sulfur) to create mortar or concrete. Sintering (laser, solar, or microwave) can consolidate regolith into building blocks.

## 3. Results and Discussion
*   **Inflatable Modules:** Most appropriate for providing a breathable environment due to high strength-to-weight ratios and compact transportation size.
*   **3D Printing:** Essential for manufacturing outer protective shells using ISRU.
*   **Sintering Methods:** 
    *   **Microwave Sintering:** 33–44 times less energy required than laser sintering. Greater heat penetration depth.
    *   **Solar Sintering:** Unlimited energy source but dependent on location (not feasible at lunar poles) and sensitive to dust.
    *   **Laser Sintering:** Extremely high energy consumption.

## 4. Conclusions
The most critical environmental challenge for 3D printing is the cryogenic temperature, which affects binder states and induces thermal stress. A safe, reliable outpost is constructible if 3D-printed components are used in conjunction with an internal breathable inflatable module. Over 60% of total interplanetary transportation costs for a habitat are related to binder transport; thus, developing on-site binders is recommended.
