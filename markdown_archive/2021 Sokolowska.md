# Rheological Properties of Lunar Mortars

**Authors:** Joanna J. Sokołowska, Piotr Woyciechowski, Maciej Kalinowski  
**Journal:** Applied Sciences (MDPI)  
**Date:** 28 July 2021  
**DOI:** 10.3390/app11156961

## Abstract
NASA plans to resume manned missions and ensure a permanent presence in habitats on the Moon. These habitats are expected to be built using local resources, such as lunar regolith as aggregate in lunar concrete. This study investigates the rheological properties (consistency) of cement-based 'lunar mortars' using a lunar regolith simulant (LRS). The results support the development of preliminary compositions suitable for 3D concrete printing, evaluating their properties against traditional quartz mortars.

## 1. Introduction
The implementation of extraterrestrial habitats requires the development of material and execution concepts. Given the high cost of transport to the Moon (ca. USD 75,000/kg), using In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) is critical. Lunar concrete can utilize regolith as aggregate, with binders including polymers, sulfur, or ordinary Portland cement (OPC). This research focus on the rheological characterization of mixes with LRS, essential for 3D printing technologies.

## 2. Material Characteristics
### Lunar Regolith Simulant (LRS)
The LRS used was developed by Polish scientists (AGK-2010) to map the grain size distribution and morphology of real lunar regolith. It consists of sharp-edged particles, which affect the water demand and consistency of cementitious mixes.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### 3.1. Rheology of Lunar Mortars
The study found that replacing 20% of standard aggregate with LRS, combined with a 1.0% superplasticizer dosage, maintained a consistency (flow diameter of 245 mm) suitable for 3D printing applications.

### 3.2. Mechanical Properties
The compressive and flexural strengths were tested after 28 days.

| Composition | Compressive Strength (MPa) | Flexural Strength (MPa) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Standard Mortar | 49.9 | 6.9 |
| 20% QP (Quartz Powder) | 59.7 | 9.5 |
| 20% LRS (Lunar Simulant) | 50.6 | 6.8 |

## 6. Conclusions
- LRS particles have a sharp-edged morphology, which increases water demand compared to smooth quartz sand.
- Substitution of up to 20% aggregate with LRS is feasible for 3D printing if appropriate superplasticizers are used.
- The mechanical properties of regolith-based cement composites are sufficient for lunar construction purposes, reaching compressive strengths of approximately 50 MPa.
