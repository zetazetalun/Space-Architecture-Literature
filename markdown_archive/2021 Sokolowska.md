# Rheological Properties of Lunar Mortars

**Authors:** Joanna J. Sokołowska, Piotr Woyciechowski, Maciej Kalinowski
**Journal:** Applied Sciences (MDPI)
**Date:** July 2021

## Abstract
NASA plans to resume manned missions to the Moon by 2024, requiring habitats built using local resources such as lunar regolith as aggregate in lunar concrete. This study investigates cement 'lunar micro-mortars' and 'lunar mortars' using a Polish Lunar Regolith Simulant (LRS). The research focuses on rheological properties, specifically consistency, to determine suitability for applications like 3D concrete printing. Results show that preliminary compositions with a 20% substitution of aggregate with LRS achieve mechanical properties and workability comparable to traditional quartz mortars.

## 1. Introduction
The development of extraterrestrial habitats requires cost-effective material concepts. Transporting cargo to the Moon costs approximately USD 75,000/kg. Using In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU), specifically lunar regolith, is critical. This paper explores ordinary Portland cement (OPC) based mortars, despite the scarcity of water, as a potential early-stage construction solution if water ice is successfully extracted.

## 2. Materials and Methods
- **Binder:** Ordinary Portland Cement (CEM I 42.5 R).
- **Aggregate:** Standard sand (EN 196-1) and Polish Lunar Regolith Simulant (LRS).
- **LRS Characteristics:** Sharp-edged particles, D50 median of 37.0 µm, density of 2.641 g/cm³.
- **Admixtures:** Polycarboxylate ether-based superplasticizer (up to 1.5% dosage).
- **Testing:** Vicat apparatus (standard consistency), flow table test (modified for micro-mortars), compressive and flexural strength tests at 28 days.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Rheology:** LRS particles' sharp-edged morphology increases water demand compared to rounded sand grains. 
- **Substitution:** At w/c = 0.50 and 1.0% superplasticizer, replacing 20% of standard aggregate with LRS resulted in a flow diameter of 245 mm, indicating better plasticity than standard mortar (206 mm).
- **Strength:** Mortars with 20% LRS in the aggregate reached 50.6 MPa compressive strength and 6.8 MPa flexural strength, nearly identical to the reference standard mortar (49.9 MPa and 6.9 MPa respectively).
- **3D Printing Suitability:** The developed micro-mortars achieved flow values within the 180–240 mm range, which is considered the printability window for 3D concrete printing systems.

## 4. Conclusions
The research confirms that lunar regolith simulant can effectively replace fine aggregate fractions in cementitious composites. While the fine granulation increases water demand, the use of superplasticizers allows for the creation of 'lunar mortars' with consistency suitable for automated 3D printing and mechanical strength sufficient for lunar structural purposes.
