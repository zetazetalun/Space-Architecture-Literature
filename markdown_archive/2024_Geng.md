# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 449 (2024) 138517  
**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Jiale Huang, Xiangyu Wang, Ming Jen Tan, Wei She, Hao Zhou, Guoqing Geng

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial exploration, represented by Lunar base construction, has been promoted worldwide as a multi-disciplinary, cutting-edge and strategic issue. Herein we proposed a novel extraterrestrial construction material composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and only 4 wt% of silicate adhesive. This silicate-regolith composite can be directly self-hardened in vacuum within 4.5 h without any curing to achieve 29 MPa of hardening strength. Meanwhile, it shows comprehensive durability with stable microstructures and >70 % of residual strength against long-term vacuum exposure, huge temperature difference and intense γ-ray and proton radiations. Its in-situ solidification and durability originate from vacuum dehydration, where silica clusters in solution spontaneously cohere into a solid network with strong connections of silica tetrahedra and bound water. A moderate silicate modulus around 2.5 and appropriate modifications can disperse finer silica clusters to increase oligomer silica units in solution and enhance hardening strength. Compared with lunar geopolymer and other existing extraterrestrial construction materials, this vacuum self-hardening composite allows a cryogenic preparation even below − 40 °C using diverse regolith with a wide range of compositions and sizes, proposing a facile, durable and versatile solution towards in-situ extraterrestrial constructions.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar construction faces rigorous environments: temperature differences (-200 °C to 120 °C), high vacuum (10⁻⁹ Pa), radiation, and moonquakes. Transportation costs necessitate in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and automated setup. Current methods like sintering (>1000 °C) or binders (polymers, sulfur, alkali) have drawbacks including low efficiency, radiation aging, or complex curing requirements.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1. Materials
*   **Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS):** LRS-1 (resembles Apollo-17), LRS-2, LRS-3.
*   **Martian Regolith Simulant (MRS):** Targeting Spirit-355 samples.
*   **Silicate Solution:** Sodium silicate (SS) with SiO2/Na2O modulus adjusted between 2.0 and 3.0.

### 2.2. Sample Preparation
Regolith and silicate solution (mass ratio 0.14:1) were mixed and cold-pressed at 9.5 MPa to form green bodies. Samples were then exposed to vacuum (<10 Pa) to trigger self-hardening via dehydration. This process was tested under both normal (44 °C) and cryogenic (-40 °C) conditions.

## 3. Results and Discussion
### 3.1. Self-hardening and Durability
*   **Strength:** SS2.5 generated 5.48 MPa flexural and 18.19 MPa compressive strength after 4.5h vacuum exposure. Modified binders (SS2.5HT) reached up to 20.34 MPa compressive strength.
*   **Thermal Cycles:** Samples were exposed to cycles between 78 °C and -196 °C. Strength remained stable after the initial cycle, retaining 71-81% of flexural and 88-98% of compressive strength over the long term (108 days).
*   **Microstructure:** SEM/EDS confirmed stable silicate bridges between regolith particles even after extreme thermal cycling.

### 3.2. Dehydration Mechanism
Solid-state 29Si NMR and FTIR revealed that vacuum dehydration forces silica clusters to cohere into a solid network. Unlike geopolymerization, this process is physical/chemical dehydration-driven and does not require high-temperature curing.

## 5. Conclusion
The SLRC material provides a facile, low-energy solution for extraterrestrial construction. Its ability to self-harden in vacuum and maintain durability at cryogenic temperatures makes it suitable for lunar polar regions and Martian environments.
