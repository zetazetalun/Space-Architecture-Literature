# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial exploration, represented by Lunar base construction, has been promoted worldwide. Herein we proposed a novel extraterrestrial construction material composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and only 4 wt% of silicate adhesive. This silicate-regolith composite can be directly self-hardened in vacuum within 4.5 h without any curing to achieve 29 MPa of hardening strength. Meanwhile, it shows comprehensive durability with stable microstructures and >70% of residual strength against long-term vacuum exposure, huge temperature difference and intense radiation.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar construction has become a multi-disciplinary research issue. Infrastructure on the Moon faces rigorous environments: temperature differences (-200 °C to 120 °C), high vacuum (10^-10 Pa), radiation, and moonquakes. Transportation costs necessitate the use of in-situ resources.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1 Materials
Lunar regolith simulants (LRS-1, LRS-2, LRS-3) and Martian regolith simulant (MRS) were used. The primary binder is a sodium silicate (SS) solution with various moduli (2.0 to 3.0).
### 2.2 Sample Preparation
Regolith and silicate solution (ratio 1:0.14) were mixed and cold-pressed at 9.5 MPa to form green bodies. These were placed in a vacuum tank (<10 Pa) for self-hardening.

## 3. Results and Discussion
### 3.1 Self-hardening and Durability
After 4.5 h of vacuum exposure, SLRC reaches ~18 MPa compressive strength. Water content drops from 8.6 wt% to ~1 wt%. Modified silicates (e.g., SS2.5HT) generated 20.34 MPa compressive strength. Strength elevates during simulated lunar cycles due to cryogenic effects but stabilizes after the second diurnal cycle.
### 3.2 Stability of Microstructures
SEM and MIP analysis show regolith particles bridged by membranous silicate binders. Porosity increases slightly (<3 vol%) during initial dehydration but remains stable under long-term exposure.

## 4. Conclusion
The SLRC demonstrates a facile, durable, and versatile solution for ISRU. It requires minimal energy input, as vacuum dehydration replaces the need for high-temperature sintering or curing, making it suitable for cryogenic polar regions and sunless lava tubes.
