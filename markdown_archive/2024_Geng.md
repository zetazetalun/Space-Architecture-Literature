# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 449 (2024) 138517
**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Jiale Huang, Xiangyu Wang, Ming Jen Tan, Wei She, Hao Zhou, Guoqing Geng

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial exploration, represented by Lunar base construction, has been promoted worldwide as a multi-disciplinary, cutting-edge and strategic issue. Herein we proposed a novel extraterrestrial construction material composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and only 4 wt% of silicate adhesive. This silicate-regolith composite can be directly self-hardened in vacuum within 4.5 h without any curing to achieve 29 MPa of hardening strength. Meanwhile, it shows comprehensive durability with stable microstructures and >70 % of residual strength against long-term vacuum exposure, huge temperature difference and intense \u03b3-ray and proton radiations. Its in-situ solidification and durability originate from vacuum dehydration, where silica clusters in solution spontaneously cohere into a solid network with strong connections of silica tetrahedra and bound water. A moderate silicate modulus around 2.5 and appropriate modifications can disperse finer silica clusters to increase oligomer silica units in solution and enhance hardening strength. Compared with lunar geopolymer and other existing extraterrestrial construction materials, this vacuum self-hardening composite allows a cryogenic preparation even below -40 \u2103 using diverse regolith with a wide range of compositions and sizes, proposing a facile, durable and versatile solution towards in-situ extraterrestrial constructions.

## 1. Introduction
The study focuses on the challenges of constructing lunar habitats, particularly the outer shell for loading and/or shielding, which faces extreme conditions including temperature differences (-200 \u2103 to 120 \u2103), high vacuum (10^-10 to 10^-9 Pa), and radiation. The high cost of transport makes in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) essential.

## 2. Materials and Methods
- **Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS):** LRS-1 (Apollo-17 simulant), LRS-2, LRS-3, and MRS (Martian regolith simulant).
- **Binder:** Sodium silicate solution (SS) with various moduli (2.0 to 3.0) and modifiers (sodium pyrophosphate, sodium dodecyl sulfate).
- **Preparation:** Mixing regolith with 4 wt% silicate binder, cold-pressing at 9.5 MPa, and exposing to a high-performance vacuum tank (<10 Pa).
- **Simulations:** Mimic lunar environment at mid-latitude (cycles between -196 \u2103 and 78 \u2103) and polar regions.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Hardening:** SLRC loses water rapidly and hardens without heat. SS2.5 modulus achieved the best strength (5.48 MPa flexural, 18.19 MPa compressive initially).
- **Durability:** After 4 months of simulated lunar cycles, the material reserved 71\u201381 % of flexural and 88\u201398 % of compressive strength.
- **Microstructure:** SEM and MIP showed stable pores (5-10 \u03bc m) and durable silicate bridges between regolith particles.
- **Martian Simulant:** SMRC achieved 5.31 MPa flexural and 16.20 MPa compressive strength under mimic Martian atmosphere (600 Pa CO2).

## 4. Conclusion
The research proposes a vacuum self-hardening silicate-regolith composite. Key features include:
1. Rapid hardening in vacuum without external heating.
2. Low binder consumption (4 wt%).
3. High durability against thermal shocks, radiation, and vacuum.
4. Flexibility in regolith selection (Moon and Mars).
