# Lunar active seismic profiler for investigating shallow substrates of the Moon and other extraterrestrial environments

**Journal:** Icarus 404 (2023) 115666  
**Published:** 8 June 2023  
**Authors:** Takeshi Tsuji, Taizo Kobayashi, Junji Kinoshita, Tatsunori Ikeda, Tomoki Uchigaki, Yuichiro Nagata, Taichi Kawamura, Kazunori Ogawa, Satoshi Tanaka, Akito Araya

## Abstract
To investigate the structures and properties of shallow material in extraterrestrial environments, we have developed a lunar active seismic profiler (LASP), which integrates active seismic sources and receivers in a short (~1 m) array that can be deployed on a rover. The small active source generates a chirp waveform that sweeps over a wide frequency range (20–250 Hz). The receivers are coupled to the ground by their own weight, but nevertheless deliver acceptable performance. We improve the signal-to-noise ratio of the seismic signal by stacking waveforms, enabling a minimal source to support the exploration of geological substrates. By processing shot gather of the LASP data, we calculated a dispersion curve of surface waves and obtained an accurate S-wave velocity profile from surface to 1 m depth. The results suggest that regions with even small amounts of subsurface ice (~0.5 wt%) can be identified in LASP results by their anomalously high S-wave velocity. Field experiments demonstrate that the LASP can support estimates of 3D S-wave velocity distributions and the identification of a buried geological boundary. The LASP also detected different degrees of compaction at the experimental site.

## 1. Introduction
Active-source seismic surveys can obtain subsurface information in a short time and a well-controlled manner. In the Apollo missions, astronauts deployed active sources and successfully investigated the shallow substrate. As future space missions are conceived, autonomous seismic surveys by rovers could be useful to explore extensive areas. The distribution of regolith and the depth to bedrock are critical information for constructing base camps. Measurements of regolith properties (e.g., degree of compaction) in situ are crucial for developing large human-occupied rovers as well as telescopes and other installations on the Moon.

## 2. Lunar Active Seismic Profiler (LASP)
The LASP is a rover-transportable system designed to investigate the shallow (<1 m) surface material of the Moon and Mars. It uses a short receiver array (~1 m). The seismic approach is useful for ice exploration because S-wave velocities in a porous medium vary with the degree of pore-fluid freezing.

### 2.1. Seismic sources
The portable active seismic source (PASS) uses a motor rotating an eccentric mass to generate a chirp waveform (20–250 Hz). Stacking waveforms improves the signal-to-noise ratio.

### 2.2. Receivers
The LASP typically uses an array of eight geophones. Geophones are coupled to the ground by their own weight (~100 g each). To prevent transmission of vibrations through the frame, geophones are suspended on thin strings and decoupled from the array frame once seated.

## 3. Results and Interpretation

### 3.1. Depth profile of S-wave velocity
Experiments conducted on a sand beach (proxy for lunar regolith) yielded an S-wave velocity profile via inversion of dispersion curves. S-wave velocity was estimated with high accuracy (±5 m/s).

### 3.2. Three-dimensional S-wave velocity structure
To obtain a 3D S-wave velocity structure, several intersecting seismic profiles were obtained in a prepared plot measuring 6.5 m × 9 m containing a conical excavation (1 m deep and ~ 4 m wide). The LASP successfully detected spatial variations in compaction and lithology (e.g., depth of bedrock).

## 4. Discussion and Summary
Our results demonstrate that the LASP enables imaging of the shallow subsurface with high accuracy. The method is accurate enough to identify regions with 0.5 wt% ice saturation as an S-wave velocity anomaly. Furthermore, the LASP can map the degree of consolidation at shallow levels, information that is important for the design of rovers and the construction of base camps.
