# Lunar active seismic profiler for investigating shallow substrates of the Moon and other extraterrestrial environments

**Journal:** Icarus 404 (2023) 115666  
**Authors:** Takeshi Tsuji, Taizo Kobayashi, Junji Kinoshita, Tatsunori Ikeda, Tomoki Uchigaki, Yuichiro Nagata, Taichi Kawamura, Kazunori Ogawa, Satoshi Tanaka, Akito Araya

## Abstract
To investigate the structures and properties of shallow material in extraterrestrial environments, we have developed a lunar active seismic profiler (LASP), which integrates active seismic sources and receivers in a short (~1 m) array that can be deployed on a rover. The small active source generates a chirp waveform that sweeps over a wide frequency range (20–250 Hz). The receivers are coupled to the ground by their own weight. By processing shot gather of the LASP data, we calculated a dispersion curve of surface waves and obtained an accurate S-wave velocity profile from surface to 1 m depth. The results suggest that regions with even small amounts of subsurface ice (~0.5 wt%) can be identified by their anomalously high S-wave velocity. Field experiments demonstrate that the LASP can support estimates of 3D S-wave velocity distributions and the identification of buried geological boundaries, as well as detecting different degrees of compaction.

## 1. Introduction
Seismic studies are vital for understanding planetary interiors. While passive seismic data (Apollo, InSight) require long observation times, active-source surveys provide subsurface information quickly and in a controlled manner. The LASP was designed for shallow (<1 m) investigation to detect ice deposits and determine regolith distribution/bedrock depth, which are critical for constructing base camps.

## 2. Lunar Active Seismic Profiler (LASP)
The LASP is a rover-transportable system (<1 m length) designed for S-velocity profiling. It uses a motor-based Portable Active Seismic Source (PASS) that generates chirp waveforms (20–250 Hz). Geophones are lowered to the ground on strings to decouple them from the frame vibrations.

## 3. Surface Wave Analysis
The system uses Multi-channel Analysis of Surface Waves (MASW) and Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT-MASW) to calculate dispersion curves and estimate S-wave velocity profiles. This approach is robust against noise and scattering dominant on the lunar surface.

## 4. Results and Interpretation
### 4.1 Depth Profile of S-wave Velocity
Field tests at a sand beach (lunar regolith proxy) yielded accurate S-wave profiles. The error was estimated at ±5 m/s.
### 4.2 Compaction and Lithology
Experiments showed the LASP could successfully distinguish between different degrees of soil compaction (Lane A: non-vibrated vs. Lane E: vibrated 8 times). S-wave velocity at 0.1 m depth increased from ~65 m/s to ~100 m/s with increased compaction.

## 5. Discussion and Summary
The LASP enables high-accuracy imaging of the shallow subsurface, identifying ice saturation as low as 0.5 wt%. This data is critical for construction planning, telescope deployment, and the design of crewed rovers.
