# Lunar active seismic profiler for investigating shallow substrates of the Moon and other extraterrestrial environments\n\n**Authors:** Takeshi Tsuji, Taizo Kobayashi, Junji Kinoshita, Tatsunori Ikeda, Tomoki Uchigaki, Yuichiro Nagata, Taichi Kawamura, Kazunori Ogawa, Satoshi Tanaka, Akito Araya\n\n**Journal:** Icarus 404 (2023) 115666\n\n## Abstract\nTo investigate the structures and properties of shallow material in extraterrestrial environments, we have developed a lunar active seismic profiler (LASP), which integrates active seismic sources and receivers in a short (~1 m) array that can be deployed on a rover. The small active source generates a chirp waveform (20–250 Hz). By processing data, we calculated a dispersion curve of surface waves and obtained an accurate S-wave velocity profile from surface to 1 m depth. Results suggest regions with even small amounts of subsurface ice (~0.5 wt%) can be identified. Field experiments demonstrate that the LASP can support estimates of 3D S-wave velocity distributions and the identification of buried geological boundaries, which are crucial for constructing base camps.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nSeismic studies on the Moon and Mars have primarily relied on passive data, which requires long observation times. Active-source surveys can obtain subsurface information in a short time and a well-controlled manner. The distribution of regolith and the depth to bedrock are critical information for constructing base camps and developing large human-occupied rovers.\n\n## 2. Lunar Active Seismic Profiler (LASP)\nThe LASP is a rover-transportable system with active sources and geophones. It was designed to investigate the shallow (<1 m) surface material of the Moon and Mars, mainly using surface wave information. The system targets ice deposits and regolith properties (compaction degree) essential for infrastructure.\n\n### 2.1. Seismic sources\nSources can be motor-based, piezoelectric, or hammer-based. The Portable Active Seismic Source (PASS) uses a motor rotating an eccentric mass to generate chirp waveforms (20–250 Hz). Stacking waveforms improves the signal-to-noise ratio.\n\n### 2.2. Receivers\nThe design typically uses an array of eight geophones. Geophones are coupled to the ground by their own weight (~100 g each) and are suspended from the frame on thin strings to decouple vibrations from the array frame.\n\n## 3. Surface wave analysis\nMultichannel analysis of surface waves (MASW) and Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT) are used to estimate S-wave velocity structures. CWT-MASW improves the stability of phase velocity estimation in short-array systems.\n\n## 4. Results and Interpretation\n### 4.1. Depth profile of S-wave velocity\nField tests on a sand beach (proxy for regolith) produced S-wave velocity profiles with high accuracy (±5 m/s). S-wave velocity offundamental mode Rayleigh waves is sensitive to depths around one-third of the wavelength.\n\n### 4.2. Equipment and analyses to improve dispersion curves\nLaboratory comparisons between buried geophones and geophones resting on the surface showed similar results, suggesting surface deployment is suitable for the Moon where wind noise is absent.\n\n### 4.3. Three-dimensional S-wave velocity structure\nA 3D model was obtained for a 6.5 m x 9 m plot. The S-wave velocity ranged from 60 m/s at the surface to 260 m/s at depth. Compacted zones (heavy roller lanes) showed increased S-wave velocity (from 65 m/s to 100 m/s at 0.1 m depth).\n\n## 5. Discussion and Summary\nThe LASP accurately images shallow subsurface structures. It is sensitive enough to detect 0.5 wt% ice saturation through a ~40 m/s velocity anomaly. This data is critical for site selection for base camps, telescope deployments, and rover design.
