# Lunar active seismic profiler for investigating shallow substrates of the Moon and other extraterrestrial environments

## Abstract
To investigate the structures and properties of shallow material in extraterrestrial environments, we have developed a lunar active seismic profiler (LASP), which integrates active seismic sources and receivers in a short (~1 m) array that can be deployed on a rover. The small active source generates a chirp waveform that sweeps over a wide frequency range (20–250 Hz). The receivers are coupled to the ground by their own weight, but nevertheless deliver acceptable performance. The results suggest that regions with even small amounts of subsurface ice (~0.5 wt%) can be identified in LASP results by their anomalously high S-wave velocity. Field experiments demonstrate that the LASP can support estimates of 3D S-wave velocity distributions and the identification of a buried geological boundary. The LASP also detected different degrees of compaction at the experimental site.

## 1. Introduction
Seismic studies yield unique information about planetary interiors. While previous missions (Apollo, InSight) relied primarily on passive data, active-source surveys provide subsurface information quickly and in a controlled manner. Measurements of regolith properties (e.g., degree of compaction) in situ are crucial for developing large human-occupied rovers as well as telescopes and other installations on the Moon.

## 2. Lunar Active Seismic Profiler (LASP)
The LASP is a rover-transportable system with active sources and multiple receivers on a minimal array <1 m long. 
- **Seismic Sources:** Uses a motor-based Portable Active Seismic Source (PASS) rotating an eccentric mass to generate chirp waveforms (20–250 Hz). A wire cushion transfers weight while insulating the frame from motor vibrations.
- **Receivers:** Typically uses an array of eight geophones (~100 g each) coupled to the ground by their own weight, suspended by strings to prevent frame vibration transmission.

## 3. Surface Wave Analysis
The system uses Multi-channel Analysis of Surface Waves (MASW) and Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT) to estimate S-wave velocity structures. CWT-MASW improves stability in high-noise or short-offset scenarios.

## 4. Results and Interpretation
- **Depth Profile:** Experiments at a sand beach (lunar regolith proxy) accurately estimated S-wave velocity depth profiles. Using CWT-MASW, dispersion curves were clean and stable.
- **Accuracy:** S-wave velocity estimation error was approximately ±5 m/s.
- **3D Structure and Compaction:** A field experiment with varying compaction (using a heavy roller) showed the LASP could detect compaction gradients (65 m/s for soft, ~100 m/s for hard) and buried geological boundaries (concavities).

## 5. Discussion and Summary
LASP enables high-accuracy imaging of the shallow subsurface. Laboratory experiments with regolith simulant show that 0.5 wt% ice saturation increases S-wave velocity by ~40 m/s, which is detectable by LASP. This technology is vital for identifying resources and preparing foundations for lunar base camps.
