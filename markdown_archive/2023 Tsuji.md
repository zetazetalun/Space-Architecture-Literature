# Lunar active seismic profiler for investigating shallow substrates of the Moon and other extraterrestrial environments

**Journal:** Icarus 404 (2023) 115666  
**Authors:** Takeshi Tsuji, Taizo Kobayashi, Junji Kinoshita, Tatsunori Ikeda, Tomoki Uchigaki, Yuichiro Nagata, Taichi Kawamura, Kazunori Ogawa, Satoshi Tanaka, Akito Araya

## Abstract
To investigate the structures and properties of shallow material in extraterrestrial environments, we have developed a lunar active seismic profiler (LASP), which integrates active seismic sources and receivers in a short (~1 m) array that can be deployed on a rover. The small active source generates a chirp waveform that sweeps over a wide frequency range (20–250 Hz). The results suggest that regions with even small amounts of subsurface ice (~0.5 wt%) can be identified in LASP results by their anomalously high S-wave velocity. Field experiments demonstrate that the LASP can support estimates of 3D S-wave velocity distributions and the identification of a buried geological boundary. The LASP also detected different degrees of compaction at the experimental site.

## 1. Introduction
Active-source seismic surveys can obtain subsurface information in a short time and a well-controlled manner. Future space missions involving autonomous seismic surveys by rovers could be useful to explore extensive areas. Distribution of regolith and the depth to bedrock are critical information for constructing base camps. Measurements of regolith properties (e.g., degree of compaction) in situ are crucial for developing large human-occupied rovers as well as telescopes and other installations on the Moon.

## 2. Lunar Active Seismic Profiler (LASP)
### 2.1. Seismic Sources
The LASP uses a Portable Active Seismic Source (PASS). The motor generates a signal by rotating an eccentric mass to generate a chirp waveform between 20 and 250 Hz. Stacking waveforms improves the signal-to-noise ratio.

### 2.2. Receivers
The system typically uses an array of eight geophones. They are coupled to the ground by their own weight (~100 g each). To prevent transmission of vibrations through the frame, geophones are suspended on thin strings and decoupled from the array frame during measurement.

## 3. Surface Wave Analysis
The researchers used Multi-channel Analysis of Surface Wave (MASW) and introduced a Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT-MASW) approach to improve stability in phase velocity estimation from the short-array data.

## 4. Results and Interpretation
### 4.1. Depth Profile of S-wave Velocity
Experiments at a sand beach (lunar regolith proxy) yielded accurate S-wave velocity profiles from the surface to 1 m depth with high accuracy (±5 m/s). 

### 4.2. Compaction Detection
Field tests successfully detected different degrees of compaction as a gradient in S-wave velocities (65 m/s to 100 m/s at 0.1 m depth), corresponding to the number of passes by a heavy roller.

## 5. Discussion and Summary
*   **Ice Detection:** The LASP is sensitive enough to identify 0.5 wt% ice saturation through a ~40 m/s increase in S-wave velocity.
*   **Site Construction:** The ability to map 3D subsurface structures and consolidation levels is essential for the design of rovers and permanent lunar base camps.
*   **Scalability:** Future configurations involving multiple rovers could allow for long-offset data, enabling deeper substrate exploration (refraction/reflection analysis).
