# Lunar active seismic profiler for investigating shallow substrates of the Moon and other extraterrestrial environments

## Abstract
To investigate the structures and properties of shallow material in extraterrestrial environments, we have developed a lunar active seismic profiler (LASP), which integrates active seismic sources and receivers in a short (~1 m) array that can be deployed on a rover. The small active source generates a chirp waveform that sweeps over a wide frequency range (20–250 Hz). By processing data from the LASP, we obtained accurate S-wave velocity profiles from the surface to 1 m depth. Results suggest that even small amounts of subsurface ice (~0.5 wt%) can be identified. Field experiments demonstrate that the LASP can support estimates of 3D S-wave velocity distributions, identify buried geological boundaries, and detect different degrees of compaction, providing critical data for constructing base camps and landing sites.

## 1. Introduction
Active-source seismic surveys can obtain subsurface information in a short time and a well-controlled manner compared to passive seismic methods. Understanding the distribution of regolith and the depth to bedrock is critical information for constructing base camps and developing large human-occupied rovers or telescopes on the Moon and Mars.

## 2. Lunar Active Seismic Profiler (LASP)
The LASP is a rover-transportable system designed to investigate shallow (<1 m) surface material. 

### 2.1 Seismic Sources
The source system uses a motor rotating an eccentric mass to generate chirp waveforms (20–250 Hz). Higher frequencies are appropriate for surface wave analysis at shallow depths (~1 m).

### 2.2 Receivers
The LASP typically uses an array of eight small geophones (~100 g each). They are coupled to the ground by their own weight, but are suspended by thin strings to decouple them from vibrations traveling through the array frame.

## 3. Surface Wave Analysis
The system uses Multi-channel Analysis of Surface Waves (MASW) and Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT-MASW) to estimate phase velocity dispersion curves and invert them for S-wave velocity profiles.

## 4. Results and Interpretation
*   **Depth Profile:** Experiments on a sand beach (lunar regolith proxy) yielded accurate S-wave velocity profiles (±5 m/s accuracy).
*   **Compaction Detection:** The LASP successfully detected varying degrees of soil compaction (ranging from ~65 m/s for loose sand to ~100 m/s for compacted sand).
*   **Subsurface Ice:** Laboratory tests with regolith simulant showed that 0.5 wt% ice saturation increases S-wave velocity by ~40 m/s, making it detectable with this system.

## 5. Discussion and Summary
The LASP enables high-accuracy imaging of the shallow subsurface. This information is valuable for:
1. Identifying ice resources (ISRU).
2. Mapping 3D subsurface structures/boundaries.
3. Determining the degree of regolith consolidation for habitat construction and rover design.
4. Site selection for base camps and telescope deployments.
