# Materials and Design Concepts for Space-Resilient Structures

**Authors:** Mohannad Z. Naser, Alaa I. Chehab  
**Journal:** Progress in Aerospace Sciences 98 (2018) 74–90  
**Keywords:** Space exploration, Lunar and Martian habitats, Space construction materials, Sulfur concrete, Resilient structures.

## Abstract
This paper presents a state-of-the-art literature review on recent developments of “space-native” construction materials and highlights evolutionary design concepts for “space-resilient” structures. It explores the feasibility of in-situ resource utilization (ISRU), harvesting elements from asteroids, and emerging enabling technologies such as “cultured (lab-grown)” construction materials.

## 1. Introduction
Habitats must withstand extreme space environments and should preferably be fabricated using in-situ space resources. Utilizing space-native raw materials is triggered by the high cost of transportation (up to $20,000/kg to the Moon, significantly more for Mars). Composite materials, specifically concrete derivatives, are identified as potentially suitable for space structures.

## 3. Space Resources and ISRU
Analysis of NASA samples shows lunar soil consists of alumina, calcium oxide, and silicate. Comparison of chemical contents (SiO₂, Al₂O₃, FeO, MgO, CaO) between lunar and Martian samples suggests terrestrial cementitious production is possible.

## 4. Properties of Space Construction Materials
### 4.1 Concrete Derivates
*   **Hydraulic Concrete:** Limited by the need for water transport. 
*   **Sulfur Concrete:** Created by melting sulfur (120–150°C) and mixing with regolith. It does not require water, gains strength quickly, but suffers from sublimation in vacuum and thermal cycle degradation.
*   **Geopolymers:** Produced via alkaline solution reaction with aluminosilicate precursors. Strength ranges from 16.6 to 33.1 MPa.

### 4.2 Metals and Alloys
Aluminum (Al), Magnesium (Mg), Iron (Fe), and Titanium (Ti) are available. Aluminum is valued for its low density and low melting point, allowing it to be used as a binder for "waterless-aluminum concrete."

## 5. Processing Methods
*   **Solar/Laser Sintering:** Melting powder layers into solid structures. Limitation: Small treatment area and susceptibility to mirror/lens damage from regolith dust.
*   **Microwave Sintering:** Volumetric heating (2.45 GHz). Can penetrate up to 13.4 mm in lunar stimulants.
*   **Additive Printing:** 3D printing regolith concrete to cast walls protecting against radiation/micrometeoroids.

## 6. Structural Concepts
1.  **Terrestrial-like (Rigid):** Prestressed concrete or sulfur concrete frames. Robust but high mass.
2.  **Inflatable:** High packing efficiency, made of multilayered fabrics (Kevlar). Requires regolith shielding (approx. 3m) for radiation protection.
3.  **Cable Bases:** Using tension-based systems in craters to reduce excavation.
4.  **Underground (Lava Tubes):** Naturally occurring caves (up to 30m diameter) provide radiation shielding and stable thermal environments.

## 7. Future Research Needs
*   Advancements in additive manufacturing for extreme spatial environments.
*   Development of fully autonomous robotic construction equipment.
*   Constitutive modeling for materials under vacuum and radiation.
