# Materials and Design Concepts for Space-Resilient Structures

**Authors:** Mohannad Z. Naser, Alaa I. Chehab  
**Published in:** Progress in Aerospace Sciences 98 (2018) 74–90  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.paerosci.2018.03.004

## Abstract
This paper presents a state-of-the-art literature review on recent developments of "space-native" construction materials and highlights evolutionary design concepts for "space-resilient" structures (habitats). It explores the feasibility of in-situ resource utilization (ISRU), harvesting elements from asteroids, and the emergence of lab-grown materials. Limitations facing Lunar and Martian exploration are identified.

## 1. Introduction
The high cost of transporting materials from Earth ($20,000/kg to the Moon) necessitates the use of in-situ materials. While Lunar and Martian soils contain abundant resources for construction, fabrication under microgravity and vacuum conditions remains challenging.

## 3. Space Resources and ISRU
ISRU is defined as harnessing raw resources to produce products or services for exploration. 
- **Chemical Content:** Analysis of lunar basalt and Martian soil shows high amounts of SiO2 and FeO (Table 2).
- **Regolith Properties:** Lunar regolith possesses significant compressive strength (reaching ~538 MPa when cast).

## 4. Properties of Construction Materials
### 4.1 Concrete Derivates
- **Hydraulic Concrete:** Requires water, which is scarce. NASA has tested lunar soil as aggregate with aluminum cement.
- **Sulfur Concrete:** Created by melting sulfur (120-150°C) and mixing with regolith. It gains strength quickly and does not require water, but is susceptible to sublimation in a vacuum and thermal degradation.
- **Geopolymers:** Produced via reaction between aluminosilicate precursors and alkaline solutions. They offer radiation protection and high compressive strengths (16-33 MPa).

### 4.2 Metals and Alloys
Aluminum (Al), Magnesium (Mg), Iron (Fe), and Titanium (Ti) are available. Al and Mg are attractive due to low density and low melting points for processing.

### 4.3 Advanced Materials
- **Composites:** Organic, metal, and ceramic matrix composites are explored for high strength-to-weight ratios.
- **Shape Memory Materials:** Shape Memory Alloys (SMA) and Polymers (SMP) allow for self-deployable mechanisms and joining structural members.

## 5. Processing Techniques
- **Sintering:** Solar and Laser sintering use concentrated heat to bond regolith particles without binders.
- **Microwave Sintering:** Volumetric heating via 2.45 GHz or higher frequencies to create bricks.
- **Additive Manufacturing:** 3D printing using regolith-based "ink" or foam patterns.

## 6. Structural Concepts
Habitats are categorized by generation:
1. **First Generation:** Pre-assembled, imported from Earth (e.g., Apollo modules).
2. **Second Generation:** Hybrid nature, combining Earth-imported components with indigenous materials.
3. **Third Generation:** Fully self-sufficient colonies built primarily from ISRU materials.

### Structural Types
- **Rigid Bases:** Terrestrial-like framing, often involving precast concrete or sulfur concrete.
- **Inflatable Bases:** High packing efficiency, requiring radiation shielding (regolith cover).
- **Underground Bases:** Utilizing natural lava tubes to provide inherent radiation and meteorite protection.
- **Mobile Bases:** Roving habitats for reconnaissance missions.

## 7. Conclusions
Transitioning to space-native materials is essential. Concrete derivatives (sulfur/geopolymer) are currently the most promising candidates for large-scale construction. Future research must bridge the gap between material science and structural engineering in extreme vacuum and thermal conditions.
