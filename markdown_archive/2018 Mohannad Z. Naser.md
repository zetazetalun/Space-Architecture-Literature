# Materials and design concepts for space-resilient structures

**Authors:** Mohannad Z. Naser, Alaa I. Chehab
**Journal:** Progress in Aerospace Sciences 98 (2018) 74–90
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.paerosci.2018.03.004

## Abstract
This paper presents a state-of-the-art literature review on recent developments of “space-native” construction materials and highlights evolutionary design concepts for “space-resilient” structures (i.e., colonies and habitats). It details the effects of harsh space environments, the feasibility of ISRU, and emerging technologies such as automated construction and lab-grown materials.

## 1. Introduction
Key challenges for space habitats include withstanding extreme environments and the high cost of transporting materials from Earth ($20,000/kg to the Moon). Research focuses on using lunar and Martian regolith, which contain abundance of substances suitable for construction materials.

## 2. Space Environment
- **Lack of atmosphere:** Leads to high temperature fluctuations (-173 to 127 °C on Moon) and high vacuum (outgassing).
- **Radiation:** Solar flares and galactic cosmic radiation.
- **Gravity:** Moon (1/6g) and Mars (1/3g) compared to Earth.
- **Meteoroids:** Impact speeds of 20–70 km/s necessitating shielding.

## 3. Space Resources
### 3.1 In-situ resource utilization (ISRU)
Lunar regolith consists of alumina, calcium oxide, and silicate. Chemical contents are shown to have high SiO2 (37-45%) and FeO (11-21%).

## 4. Properties of Space Construction Materials
- **Concrete Derivates:** Sulfur concrete (non-hydraulic) and geopolymer concrete (alkaline reaction with aluminosilicate) are highlighted as waterless alternatives.
- **Metals and Alloys:** Aluminum, magnesium, iron, and titanium are available for extraction.
- **Advanced Materials:** Organic matrix composites (Gr/E), ceramic matrix composites, and Shape Memory Alloys (SMA) for deployable mechanisms.

## 5. Processing of Space Construction Materials
- **Solar and Laser Sintering:** Heating regolith to its melting point to create solid structures.
- **Microwave Sintering:** Volumetric heating by absorbing microwave fields.
- **Additive Printing:** Use of automated 3D printers (e.g., D-Shape) to build structures from regolith ink.

## 7. Structural Concepts
1. **Class I (Pre-fabricated):** Rigid terrestrial-like bases.
2. **Class II (Deployable/Inflatable):** High packing efficiency, e.g., NASA inflatable lunar base concepts.
3. **Class III (ISRU-based):** Regolith-shielded habitats or 3D-printed structures.
4. **Underground bases:** Utilization of lunar/Martian lava tubes for radiation and thermal protection.

## 9. Summary and Conclusions
- Concrete derivatives (sulfur and geopolymers) are prime candidates for construction.
- Structural systems include stationary (rigid/inflatable) and mobile (roving) bases.
- Automated construction (3D printing and robotics) is essential for realization.
