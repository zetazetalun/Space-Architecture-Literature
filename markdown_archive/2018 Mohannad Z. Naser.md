# Materials and Design Concepts for Space-Resilient Structures

**Authors:** Mohannad Z. Naser, Alaa I. Chehab  
**Journal:** Progress in Aerospace Sciences 98 (2018) 74–90

## Abstract
This paper presents a state-of-the-art literature review on recent developments of “space-native” construction materials and highlights evolutionary design concepts for “space-resilient” structures (i.e., colonies and habitats). It details the effects of harsh space environments on materials and infrastructure, explores the feasibility of in-situ resource utilization (ISRU), and discusses enabling technologies such as lab-grown construction materials and additive manufacturing.

## 1. Introduction
Space colonization requires habitats that can withstand extreme environments. Transport costs (up to $20,000/kg to the Moon) necessitate the use of ISRU. Materials like composite concrete are identified as highly suitable due to their well-documented performance under extreme conditions.

## 2. Background on Extraterrestrial Exploration
### 2.1 Space Environment
Key differences between Earth, Moon, and Mars include:
1. Lack of atmosphere (vacuum, temperature fluctuations, meteorite impacts).
2. Extreme radiation (Solar flares and galactic cosmic radiation).
3. Lower gravity (affecting loading and mechanics).

## 3. Space Resources
### 3.1 In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)
ISRU involves harnessing raw resources to produce products. Lunar soil consists of alumina, calcium oxide, and silicate, similar to terrestrial cementitious materials.

## 4. Properties of Space Construction Materials
### 4.1 Concrete Derivatives
- **Hydraulic Concrete:** Limited by water availability.
- **Sulfur Concrete:** Melts at 120-150°C, does not require water, and gains strength quickly.
- **Geopolymers:** Produced via reaction between aluminosilicate precursors and alkaline solutions; can provide radiation protection.

### 4.2 Metals and Alloys
Aluminum (Al), Magnesium (Mg), Iron (Fe), and Titanium (Ti) are available. Magnesium offers excellent vibration damping and radiation shielding but loses ductility at low temperatures.

### 4.3 Advanced Materials
Includes organic matrix composites (graphite/epoxy), ceramic matrix composites, and Shape Memory Alloys (SMAs) for self-deployable mechanisms.

## 5. Processing of Space Construction Materials
- **Solar and Laser Sintering:** Using concentrated sunlight/lasers to bond granular materials.
- **Microwave Sintering:** Volumetric heating that can penetrate lunar simulant up to 13.4 mm.
- **Additive Printing:** 3D printing of regolith concrete for automated habitat construction.

## 6. Design and Construction Considerations
- **Radiation Shielding:** Often involves covering habitats with thick layers of regolith or burying them underground.
- **Structural Typologies:**
    - Rigid terrestrial-like bases (precast concrete).
    - Inflatable bases (Kevlar/composite envelopes).
    - Cable bases (tension-based systems).
    - Underground bases (lava tubes/caves).
    - Mobile bases (rovers for short-duration missions).

## 7. Summary and Conclusions
Space habitats must account for harsh environments. Concrete and its derivatives (sulfur concrete, geopolymers) show the highest potential for ISRU. Structural solutions range from stationary underground lava tubes to mobile hybrid bases.
