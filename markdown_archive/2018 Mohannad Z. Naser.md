# Materials and Design Concepts for Space-Resilient Structures

**Authors:** Mohannad Z. Naser, Alaa I. Chehab
**Journal:** Progress in Aerospace Sciences
**Year:** 2018

## Abstract
Space exploration and terraforming nearby planets have been fascinating concepts for the longest time. Nowadays, that technological advancements with regard to space exploration are thriving, it is only a matter of time before humans can start colonizing nearby moons and planets. This paper presents a state-of-the-art literature review on recent developments of "space-native" construction materials, and highlights evolutionary design concepts for "space-resilient" structures (i.e., colonies and habitats). This paper also details effects of harsh (and unique) space environments on various terrestrial and extraterrestrial construction materials, as well as on space infrastructure and structural systems. The feasibility of exploiting available space resources in terms of "in-situ resource utilization" and "harvesting of elements and compounds", as well as emergence of enabling technologies such as "cultured (lab-grown)" space construction materials are discussed.

## 1. Introduction
The feasibility of space colonization depends heavily on the ability to produce construction materials using in-situ resources to avoid the high cost of Earth-to-Moon transport (est. $20,000/kg). Research has converged on composite materials like concrete as suitable candidates for space structures due to their familiarity and performance under extreme conditions.

## 2. Space Environment and Constraints
Key challenges include:
1. **Lack of Atmosphere:** Resulting in high-speed micrometeorite impacts (20–70 km/s) and hard vacuum (3 x 10⁻¹³ kPa on the Moon).
2. **Extreme Radiation:** Solar flares and galactic cosmic radiation.
3. **Gravity Differences:** Lower gravity allows for larger structural spans but affects soil confining stress and foundation design.

## 3. Space Resources and ISRU
Analysis of Apollo samples confirms substantial amounts of alumina, calcium oxide, and silicate in lunar regolith. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) is defined as operations harnessing these raw resources for products and services.

## 4. Properties of Space Construction Materials
### 4.1. Concrete Derivates
*   **Hydraulic Concrete:** Requires water, which is scarce. Compressive strengths of ~75.7 MPa have been achieved using lunar simulant.
*   **Sulfur Concrete:** A non-hydraulic alternative created by melting sulfur (120–150°C) and mixing it with regolith. Compressive strengths reach 39–75.7 MPa (Lunar) and 20–63 MPa (Martian).
*   **Geopolymers:** Produced through the reaction between aluminosilicate precursors and alkaline solutions. Can reach strengths of 16.6 to 33.1 MPa.

### 4.2. Metals and Alloys
Aluminum (Al), Magnesium (Mg), Iron (Fe), and Titanium (Ti) are available. Aluminum is particularly attractive due to its low density and low melting point, facilitating thermal liquefaction to bind regolith.

## 5. Processing Techniques
*   **Solar/Laser Sintering:** Focused energy rays bond granular material. Challenges include the need for large mirrors and lens maintenance.
*   **Microwave Sintering:** Volumetric heating via 2.45 GHz or higher frequencies. Penetrates deeper into regolith than surface sintering.
*   **DMSI (Dry-Mix/Steam-Injection):** Uses dry cement particles exposed to high-temperature steam to induce hydration.

## 6. Structural Concepts
Habitats are categorized into generations:
1.  **Class I:** Pre-assembled, imported from Earth (e.g., Apollo modules).
2.  **Class II:** Hybrid, combining Earth-imported components with inflatable or deployable structures.
3.  **Class III:** Built using in-situ resources (ISRU-based), allowing for large-scale, self-sufficient colonies.

Specific designs include:
*   **Rigid/Terrestrial-like:** Concrete shells or frames.
*   **Inflatables:** High packing efficiency, requiring regolith shielding (3m) for radiation protection.
*   **Underground/Lava Tubes:** Natural protection from radiation and meteorites. Diameters can reach several kilometers on the Moon.
*   **Mobile Bases:** Roving habitats that increase exploration range.

## 7. Conclusions
The realization of space habitats requires overcoming limitations in material processing and automated construction. Concrete and its derivatives are the most promising materials, while lava tubes and inflatable systems offer viable architectural solutions for extreme environments.
