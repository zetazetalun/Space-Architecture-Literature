# Designing Biopolymer-Bound Regolith Composites for Maximum Compressive Strength

**Authors:** I. Rosa, A. Coto, M. I. Allende, M. D. Lepech, and D. J. Loftus
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE, pp. 200-214

## ABSTRACT
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Transporting large amounts of construction materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, to take advantage of limited in situ extraterrestrial resources, this work focuses on a novel class of biopolymer-bound soil composite (BSC) materials. These quasi-brittle composites are produced by desiccating a mixture of soil, water, and a biopolymer binder to create a versatile material with uniaxial compressive strength comparable to concrete.

Nineteen BSC mixes using JSC-1A regolith were tested with varying biopolymer solution concentrations and biopolymer to soil ratios. A maximum compressive strength of 25 MPa was obtained at a biopolymer concentration of 48% and a biopolymer to soil ratio of 12.4%. Finally, a design tool is introduced to design regolith BSC for desired compressive strength for future use in the structural design of structures on the Moon, Mars, or other extraterrestrial bodies.

## 1. INTRODUCTION
There is a need for new construction materials that consume less energy to produce, can be recycled, and utilize local resources. Biopolymer-bound Soil Composites (BSC) are a mixture of granular soil aggregate, biopolymer, and water, desiccated to form materials with strengths similar to Ordinary Portland Cement Concrete.

## 2. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
Dried bovine blood plasma proteins (bovine serum albumin - BSA) were used as the biopolymer. Mixes with concentrations of 30%, 38%, 43%, 48%, and 53% were investigated.

## 3. MATERIALS AND METHODS
- **Aggregate:** JSC-1A lunar regolith simulant.
- **Binder:** AP920 Bovine Plasma.
- **Manufacturing:** Dry-mixing of sand and protein, adding water, blending, and compacting in cylindrical molds (25.4 mm diameter) at 5 MPa pressure. Samples were desiccated at 60°C for 24 hours.

## 5. CONCLUSIONS & FUTURE WORK
JSC-1A/AP920 BSC materials exhibited a linear relationship between compressive strength, dry bulk density, and biopolymer-to-soil ratio at saturation ratios ≤ 1. Max density observed was 1.75 g/cm³ for the soil component. This enabling technology is suitable for micrometeoroid shielding or rural airstrips. Future work will explore 3D printing methods.
