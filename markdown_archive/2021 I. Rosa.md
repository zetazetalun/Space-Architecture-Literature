# Designing Biopolymer-Bound Regolith Composites for Maximum Compressive Strength

**Authors:** I. Rosa; A. Coto; M. I. Allende; M. D. Lepech; and D. J. Loftus  
**Affiliations:** Stanford University, Dept. of Civil and Environmental Engineering; NASA Ames Research Center, Space Bioscience Research Division.  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021 (ASCE)

## ABSTRACT
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Transporting large amounts of construction materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, to take advantage of limited in situ extraterrestrial resources, this work focuses on a novel class of biopolymer-bound soil composite (BSC) materials. These quasi-brittle composites are produced by desiccating a mixture of soil, water, and a biopolymer binder to create a versatile material with uniaxial compressive strength comparable to concrete. This paper expands upon a BSC design methodology for regolith soils. Previous work has been successful in reliably designing BSC materials with graded sands that exhibit compressive strengths above 20 MPa. Lunar regolith, JSC-1A simulant presents an additional challenge due to the broad ranges of the soil particle sizes. Nineteen BSC mixes using JSC-1A regolith were tested with varying biopolymer solution concentrations and biopolymer to soil ratios. A maximum compressive strength of 25 MPa was obtained at a biopolymer concentration of 48% and a biopolymer to soil ratio of 12.4%. A brief discussion on the effect of manufacturing variables on compressive strength and bulk density is presented. Finally, a design tool is introduced to design regolith BSC for desired compressive strength for future use in the structural design of structures on the Moon, Mars, or other extraterrestrial bodies.

## 1. INTRODUCTION
NASA’s current efforts to develop closed-loop building material systems for long-duration missions in the resource-constrained and energy-limited environment of space serve as a useful platform for the development of new construction materials that use local resources. Inspired by protein-based binders (e.g., bovine serum albumin), BSC are mixtures of granular soil aggregate, biopolymer, and water. This study translates BSC design methodology to lunar regolith using JSC-1A lunar soil simulant as a proxy.

## 2. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
Maximum compressive strength is a function of:
1. Soil maximum bulk density ($\rho_{bulk\ soil}$)
2. Maximum concentration in solution of the biopolymer ($(\%w/w)_{BP}^{max}$)
3. Biopolymer solution density ($\rho_{BP\ sol}$)

For JSC-1A, literature suggests a max bulk density of 2.01 g/cm³ (vibration) and 1.75 g/cm³ (wet compaction).

## 3. MATERIALS AND EXPERIMENTAL METHODS
*   **Aggregate:** JSC-1A lunar simulant (silicates: plagioclase, pyroxene, olivine).
*   **Binder:** AP920 Bovine Plasma (78% protein).
*   **Manufacturing:** Dry-mixing of soil and protein, adding water, blending, and compacting in a 25.4mm diameter mold at 5 MPa. Specimens were desiccated at 60°C for 24 hours.

## 5. MODELS FOR PREDICTING STRENGTH
A linear model for compressive strength was developed:
$σ_c = a * \rho_{bulk\ soil}^{dry} + b * \%w_{BP/S} + c$
where $a = 62.4, b = 151.2, c = -102.2$ ($R^2 = 0.967$).

## 6. DISCUSSION
Saturation ratios above unity (1.0) result in decreased compressive strength due to biopolymer matrix damage caused by high shrinkage. Maximum bulk density (1.75 g/cm³) occurred at approximately 14% moisture content.

## 7. CONCLUSIONS
JSC-1A/AP920 BSC materials exhibit a linear relationship between compressive strength, dry bulk density, and biopolymer-to-soil ratio when the saturation ratio is ≤ 1. The maximum strength achieved (25.4 MPa) makes this material suitable for shielding or lunar infrastructure like airstrips. Future work includes 3D printing applications.
