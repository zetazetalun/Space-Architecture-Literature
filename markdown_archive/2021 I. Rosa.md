# Designing Biopolymer-Bound Regolith Composites for Maximum Compressive Strength

**Authors:** I. Rosa, A. Coto, M. I. Allende, M. D. Lepech, and D. J. Loftus

**Affiliations:** Dept. of Civil and Environmental Engineering, Stanford Univ.; Space Bioscience Research Division, NASA Ames Research Center.

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial construction requires utilizing in situ resources to minimize costs. This work focuses on Biopolymer-bound Soil Composites (BSC) created by desiccating a mixture of soil, water, and biopolymer binder. This study translates BSC design from sand to JSC-1A lunar simulant. Nineteen mixes were tested, achieving a maximum compressive strength of 25 MPa at 48% biopolymer concentration and 12.4% biopolymer-to-soil ratio. A design tool is introduced for future habitat design on the Moon or Mars.

## 1. Introduction
Ordinary Portland Cement Concrete (OPCC) has high environmental impacts. NASA is exploring closed-loop building materials for space missions. BSCs use natural binders and local aggregates to provide suitable mechanical performance with low energy embodiment.

## 2. Experimental Design
Maximum compressive strength depends on soil bulk density, biopolymer solution concentration, and solution density. For JSC-1A, the max bulk density is 1.75 g/cm³ (wet compaction) to 2.01 g/cm³ (vibration). Mixtures used dried bovine blood plasma proteins (BSA and immunoglobins).

## 3. Materials and Methods
JSC-1A simulant was compacted at 5 MPa. Specimens (25.4 mm diameter, 2:1 height-to-diameter ratio) were desiccated at 60°C for 24 hours.

## 4. Experimental Results
Results showed that a 53% biopolymer solution concentration is likely unachievable due to undissolved material, leading to lower strength. Optimal strength correlates with saturation ratios near unity.

## 7. Conclusions & Future Work
JSC-1A/AP920 BSC materials exhibit a linear relationship between compressive strength, dry bulk density, and biopolymer ratio at saturation ratios ≤ 1. This enables predictive tools for terrestrial and extraterrestrial construction (e.g., micrometeoroid shielding). Future work includes 3D printing applications.
