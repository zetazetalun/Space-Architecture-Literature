# Designing Biopolymer-Bound Regolith Composites for Maximum Compressive Strength

**I. Rosa; A. Coto; M. I. Allende; M. D. Lepech; and D. J. Loftus**

## ABSTRACT
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Transporting large amounts of construction materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, to take advantage of limited in situ extraterrestrial resources, this work focuses on a novel class of biopolymer-bound soil composite (BSC) materials. These quasi-brittle composites are produced by desiccating a mixture of soil, water, and a biopolymer binder to create a versatile material with uniaxial compressive strength comparable to concrete. This paper expands upon a BSC design methodology for regolith soils. Previous work has been successful in reliably designing BSC materials with graded sands that exhibit compressive strengths above 20 MPa. Lunar regolith, JSC-1A simulant presents an additional challenge due to the broad ranges of the soil particle sizes. Nineteen BSC mixes using JSC-1A regolith were tested with varying biopolymer solution concentrations and biopolymer to soil ratios. A maximum compressive strength of 25 MPa was obtained at a biopolymer concentration of 48% and a biopolymer to soil ratio of 12.4%. A brief discussion on the effect of manufacturing variables on compressive strength and bulk density is presented. Finally, a design tool is introduced to design regolith BSC for desired compressive strength for future use in the structural design of structures on the Moon, Mars, or other extraterrestrial bodies.

## 1. INTRODUCTION
The study focuses on NASA’s efforts to develop closed-loop building material systems for long-duration missions in resource-constrained environments. Inspired by protein-based binders, BSC are mixtures of granular soil aggregate, biopolymer, and water, desiccated to form materials with strengths similar to concrete. This research translates BSC design from sand to lunar regolith (JSC-1A).

## 2. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
The maximum compressive strength is a function of maximum soil bulk density ($\rho_{bulk\ soil}^{max}$), maximum biopolymer concentration ($(\%w/w)_{BP}^{max}$), and biopolymer solution density ($\rho_{BP\ sol}$). 
- **Biopolymer:** Dried bovine blood plasma proteins (AP920).
- **Soil:** JSC-1A lunar regolith simulant.
- **Mixes:** Five biopolymer solution concentrations (30%, 38%, 43%, 48%, 53%) were investigated.

## 3. MATERIALS AND EXPERIMENTAL METHODS
### 3.1 Materials
JSC-1A was used as the inorganic aggregate. AP920 Bovine Plasma contains 78% protein, 10% ash, 8% water, and 4% other minerals. 
### 3.2 Sample Manufacturing
Samples were produced via dry-mixing soil and protein, adding water, blending, and compacting at 5 MPa in two lifts. Samples were then desiccated at 60°C for 24 hours.

Linear and mixed-effects models were developed. The linear model for compressive strength ($\sigma_c$) achieved an R-squared of 0.967.

## 7. CONCLUSIONS
- BSC can be successfully manufactured using lunar regolith simulants.
- Compressive strength of 25 MPa is achievable, matching Ordinary Portland Cement Concrete.
- Strength decreases significantly when the saturation ratio exceeds unity due to biopolymer matrix shrinkage damage.
- Future work will explore 3D printing applications of this material.
