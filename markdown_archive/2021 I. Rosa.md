# Designing Biopolymer-Bound Regolith Composites for Maximum Compressive Strength\n\n**Authors:** I. Rosa, A. Coto, M. I. Allende, M. D. Lepech, and D. J. Loftus\n**Affiliations:** Stanford University, Dept. of Civil and Environmental Engineering; NASA Ames Research Center, Space Bioscience Research Division.\n**Conference:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE.\n\n## Abstract\nExtraterrestrial construction requires utilizing limited in situ extraterrestrial resources. This work focuses on Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite (BSC) materials produced by desiccating a mixture of soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (bovine blood plasma) to create materials with uniaxial compressive strength comparable to concrete. Nineteen BSC mixes using JSC-1A regolith simulant were tested. A maximum compressive strength of 25 MPa was obtained at a biopolymer concentration of 48% and a biopolymer-to-soil ratio of 12.4%.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nThere is a need for new construction materials that consume less energy to produce and utilize local resources. NASA's efforts to develop closed-loop building material systems for long-duration missions leverage biopolymer-bound soil composites (BSC), which use granular soil aggregate, biopolymer, and water to form construction materials with compressive strengths similar to Ordinary Portland Cement Concrete (OPCC).\n\n## 2. Experimental Design\nExperiments used JSC-1A lunar soil simulant and bovine blood plasma proteins (AP920). BSC mix design begins by determining the biopolymer solution concentration ($%w/w_{BP}$). Mixes with concentrations of 30%, 38%, 43%, 48%, and 53% were investigated.\n\n## 3. Discussion\nThe design theory for BSC materials made with sand can be successfully applied to JSC-1A regolith. Saturation ratios above unity (oversaturation) result in decreased compressive strength due to biopolymer matrix damage caused by high shrinkage. A linear combination of bulk density and biopolymer-to-soil ratio effectively predicts compressive strength at saturation ratios less than or equal to unity.\n\n## 5. Conclusions\nJSC-1A/AP920 BSC materials exhibit a linear relationship between compressive strength, dry bulk density, and biopolymer-to-soil ratio at saturation ratios ≤ 1. This enables the creation of design tools for future deployment in extraterrestrial construction applications like micrometeoroid shielding or rural airstrips.
