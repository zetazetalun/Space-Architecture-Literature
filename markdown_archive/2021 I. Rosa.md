# Designing Biopolymer-Bound Regolith Composites for Maximum Compressive Strength

**Authors:** I. Rosa; A. Coto; M. I. Allende; M. D. Lepech; and D. J. Loftus
**Source:** Earth and Space 2021 (ASCE)

## ABSTRACT
Extraterrestrial construction presents challenges regarding the cost of transporting materials from Earth. This work focuses on a novel class of biopolymer-bound soil composite (BSC) materials produced by desiccating a mixture of soil, water, and a biopolymer binder. Nineteen BSC mixes using JSC-1A lunar regolith simulant were tested. A maximum compressive strength of 25 MPa was obtained at a biopolymer concentration of 48% and a biopolymer to soil ratio of 12.4%.

## 1. INTRODUCTION
There is a need for new construction materials that consume less energy and utilize local resources for space exploration. NASA is developing closed-loop building material systems using local resources for long-duration missions. BSC materials leverage natural protein-based binders and locally sourced aggregates to achieve mechanical performance comparable to concrete.

## 2. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
Bovine blood plasma proteins (AP920) were used as the biopolymer. JSC-1A was used as the regolith proxy. Mix designs were varied based on biopolymer solution concentration (30% to 53%) and biopolymer to soil mass ratio.

## 3. MATERIALS AND METHODS
- **Inorganic Aggregate:** JSC-1A (major crystalline silicate phases of plagioclase, pyroxene, and olivine).
- **Binder:** AP920 Bovine Plasma (78% protein).
- **Manufacturing:** Dry-mixing of sand and protein, addition of RO water, mixing, compaction in a cylindrical mold at 5 MPa, and desiccation in an oven at 60°C for 24 hours.

## 7. CONCLUSIONS
JSC-1A/AP920 BSC materials exhibit a linear relationship between compressive strength, dry bulk density, and biopolymer-to-soil ratio at saturation ratios less than or equal to unity. These materials are suitable for micrometeoroid shielding or rural airstrips in extraterrestrial environments.
