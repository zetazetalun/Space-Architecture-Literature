# Designing Biopolymer-Bound Regolith Composites for Maximum Compressive Strength

**Authors:** I. Rosa, A. Coto, M. I. Allende, M. D. Lepech, and D. J. Loftus  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, pp. 200–214

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Transporting large amounts of construction materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, to take advantage of limited in situ extraterrestrial resources, this work focuses on a novel class of biopolymer-bound soil composite (BSC) materials. These quasi-brittle composites are produced by desiccating a mixture of soil, water, and a biopolymer binder to create a versatile material with uniaxial compressive strength comparable to concrete. This paper expands upon a BSC design methodology for regolith soils. Nineteen BSC mixes using JSC-1A regolith were tested with varying biopolymer solution concentrations and biopolymer to soil ratios. A maximum compressive strength of 25 MPa was obtained at a biopolymer concentration of 48% and a biopolymer to soil ratio of 12.4%. Finally, a design tool is introduced to design regolith BSC for desired compressive strength for future use in the structural design of structures on the Moon, Mars, or other extraterrestrial bodies.

## 1. Introduction
Consumption of construction materials is a major environmental and economic factor. NASA is investigating closed-loop building material systems for long-duration missions in resource-constrained environments. Inspired by protein-based binders in natural systems, Biopolymer-bound Soil Composites (BSC) use granular soil aggregate, biopolymer, and water to form materials with strengths similar to Ordinary Portland Cement Concrete.

## 2. Experimental Design
Maximum compressive strength of BSC materials is a function of soil maximum bulk density, maximum concentration of the biopolymer in solution, and biopolymer solution density. For JSC-1A, the maximum bulk density from literature is 2.01 g/cm³ (vibration) or 1.75 g/cm³ (wet compaction). Dried bovine blood plasma proteins (BSA and immunoglobins) were used as the biopolymer.

## 3. Materials and Experimental Methods
JSC-1A lunar simulant was used as the inorganic aggregate. AP920 Bovine Plasma was selected as the binder due to its lower environmental footprint and availability. Samples were manufactured using a dry-mixing method, compacted at 5 MPa, and desiccated at 60°C for 24 hours.

## 4. Experimental Results
Results showed that compressive strength and density are highly dependent on the biopolymer concentration and soil ratio.

## 5. Models for Predicting Bulk Density and Compressive Strength
Linear and mixed-effects models were developed to predict bulk density and compressive strength as functions of the mix variables. For compressive strength, the linear model achieved an R² of 0.967.

## 6. Discussion
Saturation ratios above unity result in decreased compressive strength due to biopolymer matrix damage from high shrinkage. The study confirms that BSC design theory for sand can be successfully applied to complex lunar regolith simulants.

## 7. Conclusions & Future Work
JSC-1A/AP920 BSC materials exhibit a linear relationship between compressive strength, dry bulk density, and biopolymer-to-soil ratio for saturation ratios ≤ 1. This enables design tools for lunar and martian construction applications like micrometeoroid shielding. Future work will investigate 3D printing for automated deployment.
