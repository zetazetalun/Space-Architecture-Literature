# Designing Biopolymer-Bound Regolith Composites for Maximum Compressive Strength

**Authors:** I. Rosa; A. Coto; M. I. Allende; M. D. Lepech; and D. J. Loftus
**Affiliations:** Stanford University; NASA Ames Research Center
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Transporting large amounts of construction materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, to take advantage of limited in situ extraterrestrial resources, this work focuses on a novel class of biopolymer-bound soil composite (BSC) materials. These quasi-brittle composites are produced by desiccating a mixture of soil, water, and a biopolymer binder to create a versatile material with uniaxial compressive strength comparable to concrete. This paper expands upon a BSC design methodology for regolith soils. Nineteen BSC mixes using JSC-1A regolith were tested with varying biopolymer solution concentrations and biopolymer to soil ratios. A maximum compressive strength of 25 MPa was obtained at a biopolymer concentration of 48% and a biopolymer to soil ratio of 12.4%. 

## 1. Introduction
While reinforced concrete is a critical material globally, its environmental impact is significant. NASA's efforts to develop closed-loop building material systems for long-duration missions in resource-constrained environments serve as a platform for new construction materials that use local resources. Biopolymer-bound Soil Composites (BSC) leverage natural binders (protein-based glues) and locally sourced aggregates to provide mechanical performance similar to Ordinary Portland Cement Concrete.

## 2. Experimental Design
Maximum compressive strength in BSC is a function of:
1. Soil maximum bulk density ($\rho_{bulk \ soil}$)
2. Maximum concentration in solution of the biopolymer ($\text{\%w/w}_{BP}$)
3. Biopolymer solution density ($\rho_{BP \ sol}$)

Dried bovine blood plasma proteins (primarily BSA and immunoglobins) were used as the biopolymer. Concentrations of 30%, 38%, 43%, 48%, and 53% were investigated.

## 5. Models for Predicting Bulk Density and Compressive Strength
Two models (linear and mixed effects) were fitted. The linear model for compressive strength ($\sigma_c$):
$$\sigma_c = a \cdot \rho_{bulk, soil} + b \cdot \%w_{BP/S} + c$$
With $a = 62.4$, $b = 151.2$, and $c = -102.2$. R-squared = 0.967.

## 7. Conclusions
JSC-1A/AP920 BSC materials exhibited a linear relationship between compressive strength, dry bulk density, and biopolymer-to-soil ratio at saturation ratios $\le 1.0$. This enables the creation of design tools for future deployment of BSCs in terrestrial and extraterrestrial construction, such as micrometeoroid shielding or rural airstrips.
