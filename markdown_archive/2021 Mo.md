# Bearing capacity of surface circular footings on granular material under low gravity fields

**Authors:** Pin-Qiang Mo, Guoqing Zhou, Feng Gao, Ruilin Li  
**Journal:** Journal of Rock Mechanics and Geotechnical Engineering 13 (2021) 612–625  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jrmge.2020.11.009

## Abstract
Low gravity fields have been simulated through magnetic acceleration to conduct experimental study on bearing capacity of circular footings on a type of crushable planetary regolith simulant, which has comparable density and particle size distribution of lunar soil. The load–settlement responses of surface spread footings are obtained by investigating the relative density, footing size and gravity effects. Applying the hyperbolic asymptote method, normalised foundation stiffness and ultimate bearing capacity are obtained by curve fitting and predicted by power functions using multivariate nonlinear regression. The results show that the nonlinear gravity effect is not negligible, related to stress condition, soil dilatancy and mobilised friction angle. A cone penetration test (CPT)-based method for prediction of bearing capacity is proposed with correlations between ultimate bearing capacity of footings and shallow penetration stiffness of CPTs, avoiding the uncertainties of soil property estimations.

## 1. Introduction
Space exploration is entering a new era from the Moon to Mars. The stability of artificially created structures, such as lunar or Martian bases and human habitats, is significantly dependent on the interaction between foundations and planetary regolith under low gravity fields. Gravity on the Moon is about 1.625 m/s² (g/6), and on Mars is around 3.711 m/s² (0.38g). Isolated bearing pads are the typical foundation for spacecraft (e.g., Lunar Module and Mars InSight Lander).

## 2. Methodology
### 2.1 Geotechnical Low Gravity Model Testing (GMMT)
To provide a stable low gravity field, GMMT was proposed by applying a magnetic acceleration in the opposite direction to the Earth gravity. A magnetic-type planetary regolith simulant (CUMT-1c) was produced by mixing cement, iron oxide powder, and water.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Gravity Effects:** The ultimate bearing capacity of surface circular footings on the Moon is approximately 72% of the same footing on Earth (based on the reduction ratio derived from power functions), while on Mars, the reduction is about 84%.
- **Footing Size Effects:** Bearing capacity increases with footing size, described by a power function $q_{ult} \propto B^b$, where $b \approx 0.681$.
- **CPT correlation:** A direct correlation was found between ultimate bearing capacity ($q_{ult}$) and CPT stiffness ($k_{i,CPT}$), allowing for foundation design without precise soil property estimation.

## 4. Conclusions
Gravity field has significant nonlinear relations with foundation stiffness and ultimate bearing capacity. Relative density has a more important effect on the mobilised friction angle and soil compressibility. The CPT-based method provides a quantitative tool for predicting shallow foundation performance in extraterrestrial environments.
