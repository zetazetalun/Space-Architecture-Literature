# Bearing capacity of surface circular footings on granular material under low gravity fields

**Authors:** Pin-Qiang Mo, Guoqing Zhou, Feng Gao, Ruilin Li
**Journal:** Journal of Rock Mechanics and Geotechnical Engineering
**Date:** 2021

## Abstract
Low gravity fields have been simulated through magnetic acceleration to conduct experimental study on bearing capacity of circular footings on a type of crushable planetary regolith simulant, which has comparable density and particle size distribution of lunar soil. The load-settlement responses of surface spread footings are obtained by investigating the relative density, footing size and gravity effects. Applying the hyperbolic asymptote method, normalised foundation stiffness and ultimate bearing capacity are obtained by curve fitting and predicted by power functions using multivariate nonlinear regression. The results show that the nonlinear gravity effect is not negligible, related to stress condition, soil dilatancy and mobilised friction angle. A cone penetration test (CPT)-based method for prediction of bearing capacity is proposed with correlations between ultimate bearing capacity of footings and shallow penetration stiffness of CPTs, avoiding the uncertainties of soil property estimations.

## 1. Introduction
The stability of space architecture (lunar or Martian bases, human habitats) is significantly dependent on the interaction between foundations and planetary regolith under low gravity fields. Gravity on the Moon is about $1.625 \text{ m/s}^2$ ($g/6$), and on Mars it is around $3.711 \text{ m/s}^2$ ($0.38g$). This paper aims to investigate the bearing capacity of circular footings on crushable granular material with interests on the effects of low gravity, footing size, and relative density using the Geotechnical Magnetic-Similitude-Gravity (GMMT) method.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1. Geotechnical Low Gravity Model Testing (GMMT)
GMMT applies magnetic acceleration in the opposite direction to Earth's gravity to provide a stable and adjustable low-gravity field. A reliable gravity field varying from $0g$ to $6g$ can be simulated within a testing space of $\phi 90 \text{ mm} \times 180 \text{ mm}$.

### 2.2. Regolith Simulant: CUMT-1c

### 2.3. Loading Tests
Circular footings of 10 mm, 15 mm, and 20 mm were tested on soil with relative densities ($D_r$) of 59%, 66%, and 70% under gravity levels ($N_g$) of $1/6$, $1/3$, $2/3$, and $1$.

## 3. Results and Analysis

### 3.1. Load-Settlement Responses
The load-settlement curve is described using a hyperbolic relationship:
$$q_b = \frac{s/B}{\frac{1}{k_i} + \frac{s/B}{q_{ult}}}$$
Where $k_i$ is foundation stiffness and $q_{ult}$ is ultimate bearing capacity.

### 3.2. Bearing Capacity Factors
The $N_\gamma$ factor for circular footings is provided as:
$$N_\gamma = \frac{D_1}{0.3} D_r^{D_2} \left(\frac{B}{d_{50}}\right)^{D_3} \left(\frac{\gamma B}{P_{ref}}\right)^{D_4-1} P_{ref}$$
Gravity ($g$) has a more pronounced influence on the bearing capacity factor than footing size ($B$).

## 4. Discussion: Footing size and gravity effect
The ultimate bearing capacity of a surface circular footing on the Moon is approximately 72% ($= N_g^{D_4}$) of that on Earth, while on Mars it is about 84%. Larger footings carry higher pressure but the increase is non-linear (power function with exponent $b = D_3 + D_4 = 0.681$).

## 5. Conclusions
1. Low-gravity fields significantly reduce foundation stiffness and ultimate bearing capacity.
2. Relative density has a more important effect on the mobilised friction angle and soil compressibility than gravity itself.
3. A CPT-based method can effectively predict the bearing capacity and the footing influence zone ($Z_{inf}$) on planetary surfaces without soil property uncertainties.
