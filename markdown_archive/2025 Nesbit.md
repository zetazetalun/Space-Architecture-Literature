# Earthmoving for the extraterrestrial\n\n**Jeffrey S Nesbit**\nTemple University, USA\n\n**Abstract**\nThe United States spaceport, and more importantly, its technical landscape, operate in the background for the technological and political progress in pursuit of the extraterrestrial. Throughout the construction of the launch complexes on the Florida coastline, earthmoving became standard practice to elevate rocket pads above sea level and protect against rocket blasts. However, a more extended history of earthmoving at Cape Canaveral is necessary. From Earth’s early geological formations and indigenous burial mounds to the modernization of rockets, Cape Canaveral presents itself as an evolution of terrestrial form. And still, earthmoving continues today. In 2016, an article advised the greatest threats to NASA’s landscape are rising sea levels and hurricanes, causing substantial erosion to the beach, leaving active and historically significant launch facilities at risk. Cape Canaveral beaches are now preparing for additional dredging, importing new soil, and raising beach and dune elevations. This article reveals a critical history of place-based science on Cape Canaveral through an evolution of earthmoving practices, from cultural commemoration, extraterrestrial imagination, and contemporary environmental crises.\n\n**Keywords**\nInfrastructure, NASA landscapes, technical lands\n\n---\n\n### Introduction\nIn 1950, the United States Air Force selected the Central Florida eastern coastline to construct its missile operations and rocket launch facilities. Cape Canaveral proved to be a \"suitable\" decision for military security, proximity to the equator for rocket efficiency entering orbital trajectory, and safety as failed rockets could discharge in the ocean.\n\n### Earthmoving and the Spaceport Anatomy\nThe earliest of rocket launch sites required the design of earthmoving techniques. Influence came from German pioneers like Wernher von Braun and Dr. Kurt Debus, following models at Peenemunde where launch pads were bounded by embankments of built-up earthwork. During the construction of launch site blockhouses and rocket pads at Cape Canaveral (1950-1961), earthmoving became standard practice for protection against rocket blasts and to elevate pads above sea level.\n\nNASA, under the guidance of the Corps of Engineers, initiated massive wharf construction, barge canals, and turning basins. Two large pyramid-like sandpiles were constructed to elevate the launch pads used for the Saturn V rocket (Apollo missions). The technology used to support the new mobile launch complex—such as the steam-shovel crawler—came from common terrestrial extraction and surface mining practices.\n\n### Environmental and Cultural Impacts\nThe construction of the crawler way and launch complexes disrupted natural hydrological patterns and the movement of parabolic dunes. The article notes that while these technical lands were created to 'depart Earth,' they remain entangled with terrestrial environmental crises, specifically sea-level rise and coastal erosion which now threaten the infrastructure. The history of earthmoving at the Cape includes indigenous burial mounds, colonial dredging, and the massive industrial modifications for the Cold War space race.\n\n### Future Vulnerability\nAccording to the Space Coast Transportation Planning Organization 2018 report, the largest area in Central Florida to be inundated with rising sea is the Merritt Island National Refuge along with Kennedy Space Center and Cape Canaveral Air Force Station. The planetary ocean is now 'taking back' the technical land created through decades of earthmoving.
