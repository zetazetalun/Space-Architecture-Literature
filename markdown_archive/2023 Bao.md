# Preparation and characterization of elevated and cryogenic temperature-resistant regolith-based epoxy resin composites

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 387 (2023) 131560  
**Date:** Available online 5 May 2023  
**Authors:** Chengwei Bao, Yanen Wang, Ray Tahir Mushtaq, Xiaohu Chen, Zhisheng Liu, Xinpei Li, Minyan Liu

## Abstract
The construction of lunar bases requires the integration of multiple manufacturing techniques and in-situ resource utilization (ISRU). This study investigates the feasibility of regolith-based polymer composites using epoxy resin. Specimens were prepared using the Taguchi method to optimize proportions. Results showed a maximum 24 h compressive strength of 156.22 MPa and flexural strength of 103.40 MPa. The composites remained stable (>160 MPa) after ten freeze–thaw cycles (-196°C to 180°C), demonstrating their potential for lunar construction, repair of launch pads, and runways.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar bases are essential for shielding against radiation and meteorite impacts. The prohibitive cost of Earth-to-Moon transportation makes ISRU technologies vital. Polymer forming is promising due to simple manufacturing, excellent mechanical properties, and low energy consumption compared to sintering or sulfur concrete.

## 2. Material and methods
### 2.1. Materials
*   **Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS):** LR150 (D50 ≈ 150 μm) and SC80 (D50 ≈ 80 μm).
*   **Matrix:** Epoxy resins (AG-80 and E-42).
*   **Curing Agents:** Polyetheramines (D400) and m-xylylenediamine (MXDA).
*   **Additives:** Silane coupling agent (KH-560).

### 2.2. Mortar Preparation
LRS was ground in a ball mill. Epoxy resins and LRS were mixed and cured in an ambient atmosphere to simulate a habitat environment. The Taguchi method (L16) was used to determine the optimal formula based on curing agent ratio, filler ratio, and temperature.

## 3. Results and Discussion
### 3.1. Mechanical Properties
*   **Compressive Strength:** Increased with curing temperature up to 60°C. Smaller, irregular particles (SC80) provided higher reinforcement due to larger specific surface area.
*   **Optimal Formulation:** A2B4C3 (40% curing agent ratio, 400% filler ratio, 60°C curing temperature).
*   **Freeze-Thaw Performance:** Compressive strength remained stable around 165 MPa after 10 cycles, showing significant resistance to the extreme lunar thermal environment.

## 4. Conclusion
Regolith-based polymer composites are feasible for lunar construction. Curing temperature is the most critical factor for compressive strength, while the curing agent ratio most impacts flexural strength. The material's ability to maintain strength through extreme temperature cycles suggests it is suitable for rapid repair and structural components in space architecture.
