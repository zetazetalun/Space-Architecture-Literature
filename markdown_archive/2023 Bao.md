# Preparation and characterization of elevated and cryogenic temperature-resistant regolith-based epoxy resin composites

**Authors:** Chengwei Bao, Yanen Wang, Ray Tahir Mushtaq, Xiaohu Chen, Zhisheng Liu, Xinpei Li, Minyan Liu

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 387 (2023) 131560
**Published:** 5 May 2023
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.conbuildmat.2023.131560

## Abstract
The construction of lunar bases requires multiple manufacturing techniques and in-situ resource utilization (ISRU). This study investigates the feasibility of regolith-based polymer composites using the Taguchi method to optimize proportions. Specimens were characterized and tested under elevated (180°C) and cryogenic (-196°C) temperatures, including freeze–thaw cycle tests. Results show maximum 24 h compressive and flexural strengths of 156.22 MPa and 103.40 MPa, respectively. The material remained stable over 160 MPa and 70 MPa after ten freeze–thaw cycles, demonstrating its potential for rapid repair and reinforcement of launch pads and runways on the Moon.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar exploration involves shielding against radiation, impacts, and temperature fluctuations. Due to the high cost of transportation from Earth, ISRU technology is essential. Polymerization is a primary scheme for consolidating lunar soil due to its low energy consumption and excellent mechanical properties compared to sintering or sulfur concrete.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1 Materials
*   **Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS):** LR150 (D50 ≈ 150 μm) and SC80 (D50 ≈ 80 μm).
*   **Resin Matrix:** AG-80 (tetrafunctional epoxy) and E42 (bisphenol A epoxy).
*   **Curing Agents:** Polyetheramine (D400) and m-Xylylenediamine (MXDA).
*   **Additive:** Silane coupling agent (KH-560).

### 2.2 Optimization (Taguchi Method)
Factors optimized included the ratio of curing agent to matrix ($m_{ca}/m_{ep}$), ratio of LRS to matrix ($m_{lrs}/m_{ep}$), and curing temperature.

## 3. Results and Discussion
*   **Mechanical Strength:** Maximum compressive strength (156.22 MPa) and flexural strength (103.40 MPa) were achieved with optimized proportions.
*   **Thermal Resistance:** Samples showed excellent stability through freeze–thaw cycles ($180^\circ C$ to $-196^\circ C$).
*   **Microstructure:** SEM and XRD analysis confirmed that LRS approximates real lunar soil and integrates well into the polymer matrix through a three-dimensional network structure.

## 4. Conclusion
1. LRS (LR150 and SC80) can serve as feedstock for polymer concrete.
2. Optimal formulation (Taguchi) ratio: Curing agent:Matrix:LRS = 0.4:1:4 at $60^\circ C$ curing temperature.
3. The composites are suitable for lunar infrastructure, including landing pads and rapid repair of pavements, due to their short hardening times and resistance to extreme temperatures.
