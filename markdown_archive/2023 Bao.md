# Preparation and characterization of elevated and cryogenic temperature-resistant regolith-based epoxy resin composites

## Abstract
The construction of lunar bases requires the integration of multiple manufacturing techniques. In-situ utilization of lunar regolith for fabrication has been proven feasible, among which polymer forming possesses promising prospects due to its sample manufacturing process, excellent mechanical properties, and low energy consumption. This study investigates the feasibility of regolith-based polymer composites using the Taguchi method. The lunar regolith simulants (LRS) and resulting polymer were characterized by XRD, FTIR, and SEM. Elevated (180°C) and cryogenic (-196°C) temperature resistance properties were investigated by compressive and flexural strength tests after freeze-thaw cycle tests. Results revealed that curing temperatures and the mass ratio of curing agents to the hybrid epoxy matrix significantly affect strength. Maximum 24 h compressive and flexural strength reached 156.22 MPa and 103.40 MPa, respectively, remaining stable after ten freeze-thaw cycles.

## 1. Introduction
NASA, ESA, CNSA, and other agencies aim to build lunar bases for radiation shielding and habitat establishment. ISRU technology provides a solution by utilizing local materials to reduce transportation costs. Sintering, concrete, and polymerization are primary schemes for consolidating lunar soil. Polymerization refers to preparing mortar-type network structures with silicate-based materials and additives like epoxy resin.

## 2. Material and Methods
### 2.1 Materials
Two types of lunar regolith simulant (LRS) powder were used: LR150 (D50 ≈ 150 μm) and SC80 (D50 ≈ 80 μm). Binders included hybrid epoxy resins (AG-80 and E-42) and curing agents (D400 and MXDA).

## 3. Results and Discussion
### 3.2 Mechanical properties
The maximum compressive and flexural strength of SC80 samples was 156.22 MPa and 103.40 MPa, respectively (Experiment No. 8). For LR150, the maximums were 140.01 MPa and 66.69 MPa. Curing temperatures significantly impact performance; strength generally increased with temperature up to 60°C but decreased at 80°C due to rapid reaction defects.

### 3.4 Freeze–thaw cycling results
After 10 freeze-thaw cycles (180°C/1h → 23°C/1h → -196°C/1h → 23°C/1h), compressive strength remained stable around 165 MPa. The structure became denser, though more brittle. This indicates the material can withstand the lunar surface's extreme temperature fluctuations.

## 4. Conclusion
1. LRS LR150 and SC80 are suitable feedstocks for polymer concrete.
2. Optimal formulation (curing agent: resin: LRS = 0.4:1:4 at 60°C).
3. High-strength structural components can be rapidly manufactured or repaired (landing pads, runways).
4. Composites show excellent thermostability up to 348.65°C.
