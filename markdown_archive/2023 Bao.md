# Preparation and Characterization of Elevated and Cryogenic Temperature-Resistant Regolith-Based Epoxy Resin Composites

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 387 (2023) 131560
**Authors:** Chengwei Bao, Yanen Wang, Ray Tahir Mushtaq, Xiaohu Chen, Zhisheng Liu, Xinpei Li, Minyan Liu
**Available online:** 5 May 2023

## Abstract
The construction of lunar bases requires the integration of multiple manufacturing techniques, with in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) of lunar regolith providing a promising solution. This study investigates regolith-based polymer composites using a Taguchi method approach. Results demonstrate the feasibility of preparing high-strength composites that withstand lunar temperature fluctuations (-196°C to 180°C) with low energy consumption, potentially applicable for rapid repair of launch pads and runways.

## 1. Introduction
Establishing permanent habitats on the Moon requires significant raw materials. ISRU technology harvesting lunar regolith for construction materials is essential due to high transportation costs. Sintering, concrete, and polymerization are primary consolidation schemes. This study develops a polymerization process using lunar regolith simulant (LRS) and epoxy resin substrate binders.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1 Materials
- **Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS):** LR150 (D50≈150 μm, SiO2 44.2 wt%) and SC80 (D50≈80 μm, SiO2 51.2 wt%).
- **Epoxy Resin:** Hybrid system of AG-80 (tetrafunctional) and E-42 (bisphenol A modified).
- **Curing Agents:** Polyetheramine (D400) and m-Xylylenediamine (MXDA).

### 2.2 Mortar Preparation
LRS was ball-milled and mixed with epoxy resins and a silane coupling agent. The slurry was manually blended, defoamed under vacuum, and molded for testing.

### 2.3 Taguchi Method Experimental Design
Factors tested at four levels:
1. Factor 1: mca/mep (Curing agent/matrix ratio: 30%, 40%, 50%, 60%)
2. Factor 2: mlrs/mep (LRS/matrix ratio: 100%, 200%, 300%, 400%)
3. Factor 3: Temperature (20°C, 40°C, 60°C, 80°C)

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Mechanical Properties:** Maximum compressive and flexural strengths reached 156.22 MPa and 103.40 MPa, respectively.
- **Freeze-Thaw Stability:** Compressive strength remained stable at ~165 MPa after 10 cycles (180°C to -196°C).
- **Optimal Formula:** A2B4C3 (40% curing agent, 400% LRS ratio, 60°C curing temperature).
- **Microstructure:** SEM analysis confirmed a compact polymer matrix structure with LRS particles acting as reinforcing phases.

## 4. Conclusion
1. LRS is a viable feedstock for regolith-based polymer concrete.
2. Optimal curing at 60°C yields robust properties for extreme environments.
3. Polymer composites significantly shorten hardening times compared to ordinary concrete.
