# Multi-scale modeling of hydrogel-based concrete formed under the ambient environment and the extremely harsh environment of Mars\n\n## Authors\nNing Liu, Tianju Xue, Jishen Qiu\n\n## Abstract\nHydrogel-based concretes (HBCs) are an emerging class of load-bearing composite materials consisting of inert particles joined together by micro-hydrogel joints. As HBCs can harden via sol-gel process and H2O phase changes under a freezing temperature and vacuum, they are suitable for future extraterrestrial constructions. This study presents a new multi-scale model that quantitatively predicts the mechanical properties of HBC formed under different curing environments including Martian atmosphere. On the micro-scale, four representative joint microstructures are studied: tubular, foamy, honeycomb, and tube-cased-foam joints. On the macro-scale, a discrete element method (DEM) model is employed to quantify interparticle relationships. Modeling results predict the combined influence of mix design and curing conditions on mechanical properties.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nHBC is a potential construction material for resource-starved and extremely harsh environments like the surface of the Moon or Mars because minimal non-local material and energy input are required for their production. Authors have demonstrated the feasibility of mixing and curing HBCs in an environment that mimics the temperature and air pressure of Mars (-55 °C, 0.01% atm). Finite element simulation showed that a multi-story HBC building would be structurally adequate for typical Martian loadings (g = 3.7 m/s²).\n\n## 2. Multi-scale Framework\nA multi-scale framework for studying mechanical behaviors of HBC is proposed. On the micro-scale, tensile and shear failure tests are conducted on hydrogel interparticle joints. Summary\nThe multi-scale DEM model successfully predicts the compressive behavior of HBC incorporating environment-dependent porous microstructures. This framework can inform future microstructural design for extreme environments on Mars, where controlling temperature gradients can facilitate the formation of specific oriented structures.
