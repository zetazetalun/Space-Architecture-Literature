# The Research Facility on the Moon

**Author:** Mikolaj S. Trus  
**Affiliation:** Graduate of the Architecture Studies, Cracow Univ. of Technology, Kraków, Poland.  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## ABSTRACT
The research facility on the Moon is an experimental, structural design for space exploration. It was developed as a Student Master Thesis at Cracow University of Technology. The main goal was to introduce innovative extraterrestrial architecture focusing on modularity, multifunctionality, and simplicity. The facility consists of seven modules formed from repetitive, easy-to-transport components, designed to host twelve astronauts with future expansion capabilities.

## INTRODUCTION
### Concept
The crewed base design was initiated in February 2019. The Moon's hostile environment—low gravity, lack of atmosphere, meteoroid threats, solar radiation, and extreme temperature amplitudes—defined strict requirements for mass, dimensions, and form. The project sought to find an innovative structural solution that prioritized modularity and ease of transport while protecting against external damage.

## METHODS
1.  **Hand drawing:** Used to visualize experimental ideas, leading to the conclusion that a spherical shape is most efficient.
2.  **Virtual model:** Created in Blender 3D to refine geometry. The design utilized truncated icosahedrons connected in structural systems resembling crystal patterns.
3.  **3D printing:** Used to verify the virtual design through physical scale models (1:200).
4.  **Technical drawings:** Developed using CAD programs to produce detailed sections and floorplans.

## RESULTS
### Construction
The design opted for rigid assembly elements over inflatable structures to ensure predictability in altered gravity. The chosen form is a **truncated icosahedron** (12 regular pentagons and 20 regular hexagons), further triangulated for structural efficiency. 
- **Exoskeleton:** A light rod construction system reinforced with internal support bars.
- **Modularity:** The spherical modules (diameter 10.02 m, volume 520 m³) connect via corridor modules (elongated antiprisms) at pentagonal faces.

### Protection
A multilayer shell was designed to mitigate environmental threats:
- **Internal layer:** 20 cm thick thermal insulation.
- **External layer:** 5 cm thick thin layer to absorb radiation.
- **Vacuum Insulation:** A 5 cm gap filled with vacuum between the internal and external layers acts as a thermal insulator.
- **Regolith Cover:** The finished structure is intended to be covered with lunar regolith for radiation absorption and meteoroid protection.

### Function
The base consists of seven modules:
- **Top modules:** Living and sleeping areas (housing four astronauts each).
- **Bottom modules:** Dining/kitchen, laboratory sector, and a space gym.
- **Central module:** Common space and communication node.
The entire structure stands on light posts to minimize ground contact.

## DISCUSSION
The concept is compared to the Foster + Partners Lunar Habitation project. While the Foster + Partners design uses an inflatable dome with 3D-printed regolith foam, this project uses repetitive rigid components (regular polyhedrons) that provide additive growth capabilities.

## CONCLUSION
The research demonstrates the potential of regular polyhedrons in extraterrestrial architecture. The design resolves transport issues through modularity and addresses lunar environmental challenges through multi-layered protection and flexible functional planning.
