## Abstract
The research facility on the Moon is an experimental, structural design for space exploration developed as a Master Thesis at Cracow University of Technology. The main goal was to introduce innovative extraterrestrial architecture focusing on modularity, multifunctionality, and simplicity. The facility consists of seven modules formed from repetitive, easy-to-transport components, designed to host twelve astronauts with future expansion capabilities. This structural concept is intended to be universal for various extraterrestrial environments.

## Introduction
### Historical background
Space exploration history, from the V2 rocket in 1944 to the Apollo missions ending in 1972, sets the stage for current lunar research. Recent interest has turned toward Moon facilities, transport possibilities, 3D printing, and new materials.

### Concept
The crewed base concept was developed at the Department of Construction and Building Techniques. Key constraints included mass and transport dimensions. The hostile Moon environment (low gravity, lack of atmosphere, radiation, meteors, and high temperature amplitudes) defined the design requirements.

## Methods
1.  **Hand drawing:** Used for initial visualization to avoid technical limitations in the early creative process, leading to the identification of spherical shapes as efficient.
2.  **Virtual model:** Created using Blender 3D to establish precise control over shape and modularity. Truncated icosahedrons were identified as the most useful modular unit.
3.  **3D printing:** Physical models were produced at a 1:200 scale to verify virtual geometry and output.
4.  **Technical drawings:** Detailed sections and floor plans were developed using CAD programs to determine functional interiors.

## Results

### Construction
The design opted for rigid assembly elements over inflatable structures to ensure predictability in altered gravity. The core geometry is a **truncated icosahedron** (12 regular pentagons and 20 regular hexagons). 

**Technical Specifications:**
*   **Module Diameter:** 10.02 m
*   **Accessible Volume:** 520 m³
*   **Construction System:** Light rod exoskeleton reinforced with internal support bars.
*   **Component Modularity:** The sphere is divisible into twenty repetitive triangular elements, each consisting of nine flat triangles.
*   **Expansion:** Corridor modules (elongated antiprisms) connect spheres at pentagonal or hexagonal faces, allowing for crystal-like growth patterns.

### Protection
The design utilizes a multi-layered shell:
1.  **Internal Thermal Insulation:** 20 cm thick layer to retain heat.
2.  **Vacuum Layer:** 5 cm gap acting as a thermal insulator.
3.  **External Layer:** 5 cm thick to absorb temperature differences and radiation.
4.  **Regolith Cover:** A final stage involves covering the structure with lunar regolith to absorb radiation and protect against meteor showers.

### Function
The base consists of seven modules:
*   **Three Upper Modules:** Living/sleeping areas for four astronauts each (private rooms, bathroom, common room).
*   **Three Lower Modules:** Diverse functions including a kitchen/dining area, a laboratory sector (two floors), and a gym for physical activity.
*   **One Central Module:** Acts as a common space and communication node.
*   **Foundation:** The structure stands on lean posts to minimize ground contact.

### Energy, Supplies, and Materials
*   **Power:** Solar energy is the primary source.
*   **Logistics:** Food is expected to be resupplied from Earth.
*   **Technical Areas:** Located in the top/bottom of modules and central shafts for air generation and ventilation.

## Discussion
The concept differs from Foster + Partners' inflatable design by using repetitive rigid components. While both address transport and protection, this project emphasizes additive growth through regular polyhedrons.

## Conclusion
The Research Facility on the Moon demonstrates that regular polyhedrons provide a flexible and adjustable structural solution for extraterrestrial architecture. It solves transport issues through modularity and addresses lunar hazards through innovative multi-layer shielding and regolith application.
