# The Research Facility on the Moon

**Author:** Mikolaj S. Trus  
**Affiliation:** Graduate of Architecture Studies, Cracow Univ. of Technology, Kraków, Poland.

## ABSTRACT
The research facility on the Moon is an experimental, structural design for space exploration. It was developed as a Student Master Thesis. The main goal was to introduce innovative extraterrestrial architecture focusing on modularity, multifunctionality, and simplicity. The facility is formed from seven modules with repetitive, easy-to-transport components. It is designed to host twelve astronauts and is expandable.

## INTRODUCTION
### Concept
The crewed base design was initiated in February 2019. The Moon's environment—characterized by low gravity (1/6th of Earth), lack of atmosphere, meteoroid threats, lethal solar radiation, and extreme temperature amplitudes—defined strict requirements for mass, dimensions, and protection.

## METHODS
- **Hand drawing:** Used for initial visualization and defining spherical shapes as efficient.
- **Virtual model:** Developed in Blender 3D using platonic solids, specifically truncated icosahedrons connected in crystal-like patterns.
- **3d printing:** Scaled physical models were printed to verify virtual geometry.
- **Technical drawings:** Created CAD floorplans and sections to determine functional interiors.

## RESULTS
### Construction
The design transitioned from inflatable concepts to rigid assembly elements for better predictability in altered gravity. The chosen form is a **truncated icosahedron** (12 regular pentagons and 20 regular hexagons). 
- **Module Dimensions:** 10.02 m diameter; 520 m³ accessible volume.
- **Structural System:** A light rod exoskeleton reinforced with internal support bars. It is divisible into 20 repetitive elements.
- **Expansion:** Corridor modules (elongated antiprisms) connect spherical modules at pentagonal or hexagonal faces, allowing for vertical, horizontal, or spherical expansion.

### Protection
A multilayer shell protection system was designed:
1.  **Internal layer:** 20 cm thick thermal insulation.
2.  **Vacuum gap:** 5 cm space acting as a thermal insulator.
3.  **External layer:** 5 cm thin layer to absorb temperature differences and radiation.
4.  **Regolith Cover:** The final stage involves covering the structure with lunar regolith for radiation and meteoroid shielding.

### Function
The base consists of seven modules:
- **Top modules:** Living/sleeping areas (4 astronauts per module).
- **Bottom modules:** Diverse functions including a dining/kitchen area, laboratory sector (two floors), and a gym for physical activity.
- **Central module:** Common space and communication node.
- **Support:** The structure stands on light posts to minimize ground contact.

## DISCUSSION
The concept differs from Foster + Partners' inflatable design by using repetitive rigid components. While Foster + Partners use a robot to print regolith over an inflatable dome, this concept uses regular polyhedrons that can be covered with regolith in a later stage.

## CONCLUSION
The design provides an innovative proposal using regular polyhedrons. The structural concept is "multipotential," meaning it can be adjusted to various environmental conditions and allows for almost unlimited additive expansion.
