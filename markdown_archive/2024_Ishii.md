# Low-velocity impact response of lunar and Martian regolith simulants: Implications for lunar and Martian surface explorations

**Published:** November 05, 2024  
**Journal:** Physics of Fluids 36, 113318 (2024)  
**DOI:** [10.1063/5.0233884](https://doi.org/10.1063/5.0233884)

## Abstract
The granular mechanics of lunar and Martian regolith remain inadequately understood, impeding progress in successful exploration, landing, drilling, sampling, and construction activities on extraterrestrial surfaces. This study investigates the granular behavior of lunar and Martian regolith under impact conditions. Impact cratering experiments were conducted using lunar highlands (LHS-1), lunar mare (LMS-1), and Martian regolith (MGS-1) simulants, comparing them against terrestrial silica sand (T-8). The results demonstrate that crater depths in lunar highlands and Martian simulants are significantly greater than those in terrestrial sand, highlighting less resistance to impact. These findings provide valuable geomechanical insights for future exploration, drilling, and resource utilization endeavors.

## I. Introduction
Understanding the geomechanical characteristics of regolith—including strength, stiffness, and cohesion—is indispensable for designing successful landing systems and infrastructure. The lunar surface is covered by regolith (5–10 m thick), while Mars features fragmental unconsolidated rock material across its surface. This study uses high-fidelity simulants to close the gap in understanding regolith behavior under low-velocity impact, which simulates landing and shallow surface operations.

## II. Material and Methodology

### A. Target Granular Materials
Four materials were tested: LHS-1 (Lunar Highlands), LMS-1 (Lunar Mare), MGS-1 (Martian Global Simulant), and T-8 (Terrestrial Silica Sand).

### B. Experimental Setup
- **Projectile:** Alumina sphere (10 mm diameter).
- **Drop Heights:** 10.0, 15.0, 20.0, and 25.0 mm.
- **Imaging:** 3D topographic mapping using a CR-Scan Lizard scanner and X-ray Computed Tomography (X-CT).

## III. Results and Discussion
- **Crater Depth:** Crater depths in LHS-1 and MGS-1 were nearly identical and significantly deeper than those in T-8. For example, at impact velocities of 40–70 cm/s, LHS-1 crater depth was approximately double that of T-8.
- **Scaling Laws:** The study established power-law scaling relationships ($d \sim E_k^a$) where $d$ is crater depth and $E_k$ is kinetic energy. The exponents for regolith simulants (0.417 to 0.553) were distinct from terrestrial sand (0.672).
- **Influencing Factors:** High porosity and specific grain morphology (angular shapes) in regolith simulants contribute to lower impact resistance compared to rounded terrestrial grains.

## IV. Conclusions
The resistance to crater impact in lunar and Martian regolith layers is significantly lower than in terrestrial sand. This reduced resistance is attributed to the higher porosity and unique granular properties of the extraterrestrial materials. These findings are crucial for future infrastructure development, such as constructing landing pads or bases, as they allow engineers to better plan for foundation stability and address mechanical challenges during construction.
