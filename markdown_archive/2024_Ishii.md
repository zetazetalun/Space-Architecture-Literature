# Low-velocity impact response of lunar and Martian regolith simulants: Implications for lunar and Martian surface explorations

**Journal:** Physics of Fluids 36, 113318 (2024)
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1063/5.0233884
**Authors:** Takuma Ishii, Arata Kioka, Jyh-Jaan Steven Huang, Yoshiki Tabuchi, Yasuhiro Yamada

## Abstract
The granular mechanics of lunar and Martian regolith remain inadequately understood, impeding progress in successful exploration, landing, drilling, sampling, and construction activities on extraterrestrial surfaces. This study investigates the granular behavior of lunar and Martian regolith under impact conditions. Impact cratering experiments were conducted for lunar highlands (LHS-1), lunar mare (LMS-1), and Martian regolith (MGS-1) simulants, compared against terrestrial silica sand (T-8). Findings demonstrate that crater depths in lunar highland and Martian simulants are significantly greater than those in lunar mare and terrestrial sand, highlighting a lower resistance to impact that must be considered for future infrastructure development.

### Experimental Setup
- **Projectile:** Spherical alumina projectile (10 mm diameter).
- **Heights:** 10.0, 15.0, 20.0, and 25.0 mm.
- **Velocity Range:** 0.44 m/s to 0.70 m/s.
- **Analysis:** 3D topographic mapping (CR-Scan Lizard) and X-ray computed tomography (X-CT).

## Results and Discussion
- **Crater Depths:** Crater depths in LHS-1 and MGS-1 were nearly identical and significantly deeper than those in LMS-1 and T-8. For instance, at a 25 mm fall height, LHS-1 produced a crater depth of 8.3 ± 0.5 mm compared to T-8's 3.8 ± 0.1 mm.
- **Resistance:** The power-law scaling analysis indicated that the lunar highland and Martian regolith layers offer less resistance to impact than terrestrial sand or lunar mare layers.
- **Geomechanical Factors:** Higher porosity and cohesion in the simulants contribute to the increased variability and depth of the craters compared to the uniform behavior of silica sand.

## Conclusions
The study highlights significant differences in granular behavior between Earth-based sand and extraterrestrial regolith. These insights are essential for optimizing the design of landing gear, drilling systems, and foundation stability for lunar and Martian infrastructure.
