# Effect of grain size distribution on stress-strain behavior of lunar soil simulants\n\n## Abstract\nGeotechnical behavior of the lunar soils is important for engineering analyses regarding various aspects of the future extraterrestrial settlement plans including lunar exploration and construction. Many lunar soil simulants had been produced so far, in order to resemble lunar soils and conduct such analyses. The goal of this study is to investigate how and to what extent the variations in the grain size distribution of different lunar soil simulants affect their shear strength and volume change behaviors, both of which are quite important for constitutive modeling and geotechnical design. Static simple shear tests were conducted on four lunar soil simulants that were reproduced in terms of original gradation characteristics. The results indicate that various gradational parameters, such as mean grain size, coefficient of uniformity and fines content influence the shear strength, the amount of volumetric dilatancy, and the rate of dilatancy of simulant specimens in different levels when they were compared at the same density or void ratio.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nEstablishing permanent lunar bases requires interaction with local regolith. This interaction is critical for the design of structures such as laboratories, telescopes, storage, accommodation facilities, site preparation, and radiation shielding. Understanding the geotechnical properties of lunar soils is essential for the sustainability of lunar bases.\n\n## 2. Materials and Methods\nCarrier (2003) noted that lunar soil is generally classified as silty sand (SM) to sandy silt (ML). The study analyzed various simulants including JSC-1, JSC-1A, MLS-1, GRC-1, TJ-1, and others. Four reconstituted simulants (Y-GRC-1, Y-TJ-1, Y-BP-1, Y-QH-E) were tested using a static simple shear device under drained conditions at normal stresses of 10, 20, and 40 kPa.\n\n### Table 1: Gradational parameters of lunar soil simulants\n| Simulant name | Fines content (%) | D50 (mm) | CU (=D60/D10) | USCS symbol |\n| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |\n| JSC-1 | 36 | 0.12 | 7.5 | SM |\n| JSC-1A | 47 | 0.086 | 6.3 | SM |\n| MLS-1 | 43 | 0.1 | 7.9 | SM |\n| GRC-1 | 5 | 0.28 | 4.2 | SP |\n| TJ-1 | 23 | 0.26 | 21.5 | SM |\n| BP-1 | 40 | 0.098 | 11.8 | SM |\n| QH-E | 52 | 0.071 | 12.1 | ML |\n\n## 3. Results and Discussion\n### 3.1. Shear Strength\nPeak secant friction angles (phi) decrease with increasing normal stress (sigma_n). For Y-GRC-1, phi reduced from 58.6 degrees at 10 kPa to 38.8 degrees at 40 kPa. For Y-TJ-1, it reduced from 56.9 degrees to 37.7 degrees. \n\n### 3.2. Volumetric Dilation\nSimulants with sand-dominated fabrics (e.g., Y-GRC-1 with FC=5%) showed significantly more dilation than those with higher fines content (e.g., Y-TJ-1 with FC=23%) at the same density. The intergranular void ratio (es) is a key parameter; Y-TJ-1 had higher es values (0.977-0.982) than Y-GRC-1 (0.626-0.629), indicating a looser sand grain matrix configuration due to silt interference.\n\n## 4. Conclusions\nGradational characteristics influence shear strength and volume change. Effects of fines content (FC) are most pronounced in sand-dominated fabrics (FC < 25%), where increasing FC reduces the rate of dilation significantly. For silt-dominated fabrics (FC > 40%), changes in FC have a negligible effect on the rate of dilation.
