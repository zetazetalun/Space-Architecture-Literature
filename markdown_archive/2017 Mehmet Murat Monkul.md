# Effect of grain size distribution on stress-strain behavior of lunar soil simulants

**Mehmet Murat Monkul, Amina Dacic**
*Department of Civil Engineering, Yeditepe University, 34755 Atasehir, Istanbul, Turkey*
Received 24 February 2017; accepted 29 April 2017. Available online 11 May 2017
Advances in Space Research 60 (2017) 636–651

## Abstract
Geotechnical behavior of the lunar soils is important for engineering analyses regarding various aspects of the future extraterrestrial settlement plans including lunar exploration and construction. Many lunar soil simulants had been produced so far, in order to resemble lunar soils and conduct such analyses. The goal of this study is to investigate how and to what extent the variations in the grain size distribution of different lunar soil simulants affect their shear strength and volume change behaviors, both of which are quite important for constitutive modeling and geotechnical design. Static simple shear tests were conducted on four lunar soil simulants that were reproduced in terms of original gradation characteristics. The results indicate that various gradational parameters, such as mean grain size, coefficient of uniformity and fines content influence the shear strength, the amount of volumetric dilatancy, and the rate of dilatancy of simulant specimens in different levels when they were compared at the same density or void ratio. The possible reasons behind such different levels of influence were also discussed by focusing on the initial fabric of specimens achieved before shearing and the interaction between silt and sand matrices in the simulants.

*Keywords:* Lunar soil simulants; Simple shear test; Shear strength; Internal friction angle; Dilatancy; Soil fabric

## 1. Introduction
It is not difficult to imagine that developed countries will establish permanent lunar bases in the following few decades in order to explore deeper space. Lunar bases would also be a good trial to extend towards relatively distant planets such as Mars. In fact, countries such as the United States (Benaroya and Bernold, 2008) and Japan (Matsumoto et al., 2006) are already planning to return or access to the moon in order to construct bases. Recently, on December 2013, China successfully landed a lunar rover on the moon as a part of their lunar exploration program (CNSA, 2014).

Likewise on earth, virtually every structure that is necessary for the sustainability of a land base needs to interact with the local regolith or the soil on that particular planet. For lunar exploration, such an interaction is not only important for the design of lunar structures such as laboratories, telescopes, storage and accommodation facilities, power plants, transportation network etc. at the bases, but also important for soft landing on the moon, terramechanics of the lunar rovers, site preparation and radiation shielding. All of the mentioned examples imply the significance of understanding the geotechnical properties of lunar soils for the sustainability of lunar bases and the lunar exploration.

## 2. Grain size distributions of different lunar soil simulants in literature and soils tested in this study

Several countries have produced various lunar soil simulants for their space research programs. Carrier (2003) compiled the grain size distribution curves of over 300 lunar soil samples taken from the vicinity of seven landing sites and mentioned that the grain size distribution of the lunar soil is quite consistent. Accordingly, average mean grain size (D50) of the lunar soil is 0.072 mm, average coefficient of uniformity (CU) is 16 and average coefficient of curvature (CC) is 1.2. Based on the Unified Soil Classification System (USCS) used for terrestrial soils, lunar soil can be classified as silty sand (SM) to sandy silt (ML).

## 3. Experimental program
### 3.1. Static simple shear testing
In this study, an NGI type static simple shear device at Yeditepe University was used and the tests were done in drained condition (with dry specimens). The typical specimens have a diameter of 64 mm and a height of 20 mm. The lateral confinement was provided by aligning several teflon coated rings around a conventional latex membrane.

Assuming that gravity on the moon is about one sixth that of the earth (i.e. g = 1.62 m/s2), relatively lower normal stresses of 10 kPa, 20 kPa and 40 kPa were selected for the static simple shear tests in this study. These stress values correspond to depths of 3.6 m, 7.3 m and 14.6 m below the lunar surface respectively, when no surface loading was considered.

## 6. Summary and conclusions
Drained shear strength characteristics of the tested proxy simulants were compared based on the Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion at the peak and residual states. When the first pair of simulants is considered; drained shear strength and peak secant friction angles (φ_sec-peak) of Y-GRC-1 was found to be slightly greater than those of Y-TJ-1. When volume change behaviors of the first pair of the proxy simulants were compared, there was a clear trend of Y-GRC-1 specimens being considerably more dilative than the Y-TJ-1 specimens both at the same density and at the same void ratio. The findings and conclusions in this study show that gradational characteristics of the lunar soil simulants influence their shear strength and volume change behaviors. Therefore, such effects should be carefully evaluated when simulants are produced and utilized for experimenting several engineering purposes including lunar exploration.
