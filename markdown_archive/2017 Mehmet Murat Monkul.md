# Effect of grain size distribution on stress-strain behavior of lunar soil simulants

## Metadata
- **Journal:** Advances in Space Research 60 (2017) 636–651
- **Authors:** Mehmet Murat Monkul, Amina Dacic
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.asr.2017.04.029
- **Received:** 24 February 2017; **Accepted:** 29 April 2017

## Abstract
Geotechnical behavior of the lunar soils is important for engineering analyses regarding various aspects of the future extraterrestrial settlement plans including lunar exploration and construction. The goal of this study is to investigate how and to what extent the variations in the grain size distribution of different lunar soil simulants affect their shear strength and volume change behaviors. Static simple shear tests were conducted on four lunar soil simulants. The results indicate that various gradational parameters, such as mean grain size, coefficient of uniformity and fines content influence the shear strength, the amount of volumetric dilatancy, and the rate of dilatancy.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar bases are planned by countries like the US, Japan, and China. Every structure interacts with the local regolith. Understanding geotechnical properties is significant for the sustainability of lunar bases, soft landing, terramechanics, site preparation, and radiation shielding.

## 3. Experimental Program
Static simple shear tests were performed under drained conditions using an NGI type device. Reconstituted simulants (Y-GRC-1, Y-TJ-1, Y-BP-1, Y-QH-E) were tested at target densities around 1.68 g/cm³, matching typical lunar surface density.

## 4. Results
### 4.1. Drained Shear Strength
Shear strength increased with normal stress. Failure surfaces for the first pair (Y-GRC-1 vs. Y-TJ-1) were nearly parallel with peak friction angles of 27.2° vs 27.1°. Secant friction angles (φ_sec-peak) decreased as normal stress increased from 10 to 40 kPa.

### 4.2. Volume Change
Specimens showed initial contraction followed by dilation. Y-GRC-1 was more dilative than Y-TJ-1 despite Y-TJ-1 being better graded (higher CU). This is attributed to the initial fabric and the role of silt grains between sand grain contacts.

## 6. Summary and Conclusions
1. Gradational parameters significantly influence shear strength and volume change.
2. In sand-dominated fabrics (FC < 25%), increasing fines content reduces the rate of dilation.
3. In silt-dominated fabrics (FC > 40%), fines content has negligible effect on the rate of dilation.
4. These findings are applicable to Martian soil simulants under similar soil mechanics principles.
