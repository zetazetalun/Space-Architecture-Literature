# Effect of grain size distribution on stress-strain behavior of lunar soil simulants

**Authors:** Mehmet Murat Monkul, Amina Dacic  
**Journal:** Advances in Space Research 60 (2017) 636–651  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.asr.2017.04.029

## Abstract
Geotechnical behavior of the lunar soils is important for engineering analyses regarding various aspects of the future extraterrestrial settlement plans including lunar exploration and construction. Many lunar soil simulants had been produced so far, in order to resemble lunar soils and conduct such analyses. The goal of this study is to investigate how and to what extent the variations in the grain size distribution of different lunar soil simulants affect their shear strength and volume change behaviors, both of which are quite important for constitutive modeling and geotechnical design. Static simple shear tests were conducted on four lunar soil simulants that were reproduced in terms of original gradation characteristics. The results indicate that various gradational parameters, such as mean grain size, coefficient of uniformity and fines content influence the shear strength, the amount of volumetric dilatancy, and the rate of dilatancy of simulant specimens in different levels when they were compared at the same density or void ratio. The possible reasons behind such different levels of influence were also discussed by focusing on the initial fabric of specimens achieved before shearing and the interaction between silt and sand matrices in the simulants.

## 1. Introduction
Establishing permanent lunar bases is critical for deep space exploration. Every structure necessary for the sustainability of a land base must interact with local regolith. Understanding geotechnical properties is significant for soft landing, rover terramechanics, site preparation, and radiation shielding.

## 4. Stress-strain behavior of tested lunar soil simulants

### 4.1. Drained shear strength characteristics
Shear strength increased with normal stress. Failure surfaces were analyzed using the Mohr-Coulomb criterion. The first pair (Y-GRC-1 vs Y-TJ-1) showed peak friction angles between 27.1° and 27.2°. The second pair (Y-BP-1 vs Y-QH-E) showed friction angles between 34.5° and 35.3°. 

### 4.2. Volume change characteristics
Initial contraction followed by dilation was observed. Y-GRC-1 specimens were more dilative than Y-TJ-1 due to differences in intergranular void ratio. Increasing fines content reduces the rate of dilation in sand-dominated fabrics but has a negligible effect in silt-dominated fabrics.

## 5. Practical implications for planetary exploration
Gradational characteristics influence shear strength and volume change. These must be evaluated for landing, rover mobility, and lunar construction. Similar principles are likely applicable to Martian soil behavior.

## 6. Summary and conclusions
1. Grain size distribution affects shear strength and dilatancy in lunar simulants.
2. In sand-dominated fabrics, increasing fines content reduces dilation significantly.
3. Peak secant friction angles decrease with increasing normal stress, reflecting failure envelope curvature.
4. Simulant fidelity must consider application-specific geotechnical parameters (c, φ, ψ).
