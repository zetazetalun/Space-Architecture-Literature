# Effect of grain size distribution on stress-strain behavior of lunar soil simulants

**Authors:** Mehmet Murat Monkul, Amina Dacic
**Journal:** Advances in Space Research 60 (2017) 636–651

## Abstract
Geotechnical behavior of the lunar soils is important for engineering analyses regarding various aspects of the future extraterrestrial settlement plans including lunar exploration and construction. Many lunar soil simulants had been produced so far, in order to resemble lunar soils and conduct such analyses. The goal of this study is to investigate how and to what extent the variations in the grain size distribution of different lunar soil simulants affect their shear strength and volume change behaviors, both of which are quite important for constitutive modeling and geotechnical design. Static simple shear tests were conducted on four lunar soil simulants that were reproduced in terms of original gradation characteristics. The results indicate that various gradational parameters, such as mean grain size, coefficient of uniformity and fines content influence the shear strength, the amount of volumetric dilatancy, and the rate of dilatancy of simulant specimens in different levels when they were compared at the same density or void ratio. The possible reasons behind such different levels of influence were also discussed by focusing on the initial fabric of specimens achieved before shearing and the interaction between silt and sand matrices in the simulants.

## 1. Introduction
It is not difficult to imagine that developed countries will establish permanent lunar bases in the following few decades in order to explore deeper space. Lunar bases would also be a good trial to extend towards relatively distant planets such as Mars. In fact, countries such as the United States (Benaroya and Bernold, 2008) and Japan (Matsumoto et al., 2006) are already planning to return or access to the moon in order to construct bases. Recently, on December 2013, China successfully landed a lunar rover on the moon as a part of their lunar exploration program (CNSA, 2014).

Likewise on earth, virtually every structure that is necessary for the sustainability of a land base needs to interact with the local regolith or the soil on that particular planet. For lunar exploration, such an interaction is not only important for the design of lunar structures such as laboratories, telescopes, storage and accommodation facilities, power plants, transportation network etc. at the bases, but also important for soft landing on the moon, terramechanics of the lunar rovers, site preparation and radiation shielding. All of the mentioned examples imply the significance of understanding the geotechnical properties of lunar soils for the sustainability of lunar bases and the lunar exploration.

According to Arslan et al. (2010a) US Apollo missions brought approximately 115 kg of lunar soil, while Carrier et al. (1973) and Martel (2009) mentioned an approximate amount of 382 kg of lunar rock and soil brought by several missions. In fact, China is planning to bring about 2 kg of lunar regolith to the earth in 2017 by Chang'e 5 mission (CNSA, 2014). Nevertheless, such amounts mentioned are clearly insufficient for conducting extensive experimental programs on various engineering aspects. Therefore, several countries have produced various lunar soil simulants for their space research programs.

Carrier (2003) compiled the grain size distribution curves of over 300 lunar soil samples taken from the vicinity of seven landing sites and mentioned that the grain size distribution of the lunar soil is quite consistent. Accordingly, average mean grain size (D50) of the lunar soil is 0.072 mm, average coefficient of uniformity (CU) is 16 and average coefficient of curvature (CC) is 1.2. Based on the Unified Soil Classification System (USCS) used for terrestrial soils, lunar soil can be classified as silty sand (SM) to sandy silt (ML).

Several researchers have carried out shear strength tests and other experiments in order to characterize the geotechnical properties of the different lunar soil simulants that were intended to mimic the lunar soil... Even though all of the mentioned simulants were intended to be produced with high fidelity considering the lunar soil, it is inevitable to have differences among different lunar soil simulants as they are artificially produced. These differences could be in different aspects such as mineralogical composition, toxicity, abrasiveness, geotechnical properties etc. This study focuses on the geotechnical aspect only, as 'high fidelity' is a relative concept for different science and engineering disciplines. Yet, geotechnical engineering is especially important for the development and the sustainability of lunar bases (Monkul, 2013a).

## 2. Grain size distributions of different lunar soil simulants in literature and soils tested in this study
Grain size distribution shows the range and values of the size of individual particles in a soil matrix in terms of percent by dry weight. It is an important characterization tool for soils as it influences many geotechnical properties including, but not limited to shear strength, compressibility, compaction, and permeability in different levels. Grain size distribution of the soils is conventionally determined either by sieve analysis and/or hydrometer tests (ASTM D422, 2005), though other methods are also emerging such as techniques based on laser diffraction (Li et al., 2009; Yang and Wei, 2012), and X-ray micro-computed tomography (Fonseca et al., 2012).

## 3. Experimental program
### 3.1. Static simple shear testing
Direct shear and triaxial compression tests are perhaps the most common high strain element tests used for determination of the shear strength of soils. In this study, another alternative: static simple shear testing was preferred to investigate the stress-strain behavior of the reconstituted lunar soil simulants... Assuming that gravity on the moon is about one sixth that of the earth (i.e. g = 1.62 m/s²), relatively lower normal stresses of 10 kPa, 20 kPa and 40 kPa were selected for the static simple shear tests in this study. These stress values correspond to depths of 3.6 m, 7.3 m and 14.6 m below the lunar surface respectively, when no surface loading was considered.

### 3.2. Specimen preparation
Another important parameter for comparing the stress-strain behavior of lunar soil simulants is the specimen density... The dry funnel deposition technique, explained in Monkul et al. (2015), is used in order to prepare simple shear specimens. A bottle shaped funnel with a curved tip was placed on the porous stone at the base platen, and it was filled with dry soil.

## 5. Consideration of limitations, practical implications of findings for planetary exploration and future research
Terrestrial soils are formed by physical and chemical weathering of various parent rocks and their fragments... Meanwhile, weathering of lunar rocks has different processes such as impacts of macro and micro meteoroids, and bombardment by charged atomic particles from the Sun. These 'space weathering' processes result in the formation of extraterrestrial components in the lunar soil such as agglutinates.

Experimental results in this study clearly demonstrated that gradational characteristics of the lunar soil simulants influence their shear strength and volume change behaviors. Therefore, such effects should be carefully evaluated when the future simulants will be produced and utilized for experimenting several engineering purposes including lunar exploration (landing, terramechanics of rovers) and lunar construction activities (e.g. site preparation, lunar bases, transportation networks etc.).

## 6. Summary and conclusions
In this study the influence of grain size distribution on the stress-strain behavior of lunar soil simulants were investigated... Drained shear strength characteristics of the tested proxy simulants were compared based on the Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion at the peak and residual states. When the first pair of simulants is considered; drained shear strength and peak secant friction angles of Y-GRC-1 was found to be slightly greater than those of Y-TJ-1.

The findings and conclusions in this study show that gradational characteristics of the lunar soil simulants influence their shear strength and volume change behaviors. Therefore, such effects should be carefully evaluated when simulants are produced and utilized for experimenting several engineering purposes including lunar exploration and lunar construction activities.
