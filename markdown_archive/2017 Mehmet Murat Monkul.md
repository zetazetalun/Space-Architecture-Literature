# Effect of grain size distribution on stress-strain behavior of lunar soil simulants

**Authors:** Mehmet Murat Monkul, Amina Dacic  
**Publication:** Advances in Space Research 60 (2017) 636–651  
**Date:** Received 24 February 2017; accepted 29 April 2017  

## Abstract
Geotechnical behavior of the lunar soils is important for engineering analyses regarding various aspects of the future extraterrestrial settlement plans including lunar exploration and construction. The goal of this study is to investigate how and to what extent the variations in the grain size distribution of different lunar soil simulants affect their shear strength and volume change behaviors. Static simple shear tests were conducted on four lunar soil simulants reproduced in terms of original gradation characteristics. The results indicate that various gradational parameters, such as mean grain size, coefficient of uniformity, and fines content influence the shear strength, the amount of volumetric dilatancy, and the rate of dilatancy.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar bases would be a good trial to extend towards relatively distant planets such as Mars. Virtually every structure necessary for the sustainability of a land base needs to interact with the local regolith. Understanding geotechnical properties is significant for the design of lunar structures, site preparation, and radiation shielding.

## 3. Experimental Program
Static simple shear testing was preferred to investigate the stress-strain behavior of reconstituted lunar soil simulants. Specimens had a diameter of 64 mm and a height of 20 mm. Tests were conducted in drained conditions with dry specimens under normal stresses of 10 kPa, 20 kPa, and 40 kPa, corresponding to lunar surface depths of approximately 3.6m to 14.6m.

## 4. Key Findings and Conclusions
- **Shear Strength:** Drained shear strength and peak secant friction angles increased with decreasing mean grain size (D50) in specific pairs.
- **Dilatancy:** Volume change behavior is highly sensitive to the initial fabric. For sand-dominated simulants (fines < 25%), increasing fines content significantly reduces the rate of dilation. For silt-dominated simulants (fines > 40%), fines have a negligible effect on the rate of dilation.
- **Gradation Impact:** The coefficient of uniformity (CU) and fines content (FC) are critical in predicting the mechanical response of the regolith, which must be accounted for in lunar construction planning and simulation.
