# A Framework for Extraterrestrial Environmental Assessment

**Author:** William R. Kramer
**Affiliation:** Outer Space Consulting, Frederick, MD, 21701, USA
**Journal:** Space Policy 53 (2020) 101385
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.spacepol.2020.101385

## Abstract
Many of the proposed and foreseeable activities on the Moon, Mars, and elsewhere in our solar system will require construction and similar actions. Most will, by intent, alter those natural environments in a variety of ways. Some impacts may foreclose future options for use of the area, hinder sustained access to resources, impede long-term human occupation, interfere with scientific or other purposes, harm cultural assets, create unsafe conditions, or cause other detriments. This article proposes a simple framework for an appropriate assessment and review process, drawing upon but distinct from the US National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA).

## 1. A rapidly evolving problem in space development
Nearly all foreseeable physical activities on the Moon, Mars, and elsewhere—such as construction, mining, energy production, and human habitation—may adversely affect extraterrestrial environments. We build roads here, and we will build roads there. We construct launch facilities here, and we will construct them there. Alterations to alien landscapes could have longer-lasting implications than anthropogenic changes on Earth due to the lack of tectonic movements and biological resilience.

## 2. The neglected need for extraterrestrial environmental assessment
Regardless of space industries or scientific missions, the need for extraterrestrial environmental assessments (EEAs) is critical. Impacts include:
- **Resource Depletion:** Inefficient mining techniques could waste water ice or minerals.
- **Physical Damage:** Blasting rocks could damage nearby infrastructure.
- **Interference:** Dust from construction/mining could coat solar panels or interfere with telescopes.
- **Contamination:** Lubricants and compounds could leak into subsurface water ice.
- **Exogeoconservation:** The need to protect geological features of scientific or cultural value.

## 4. Framework for EEA
The proposed EEA framework for space actions includes the following structure:

### 1.0 Introduction
### 2.0 Purpose and Need
Why is the action being undertaken?

### 3.0 Proposed Action
- **3.1 Timing:** Launch dates, construction duration.
- **3.2 Location:** Specific coordinates, terrain alteration, road construction areas.

### 4.0 Affected Environment
- **4.1 Physical Resources:** 
    - 4.1.1 Geology, Regolith, and Seismicity (vibration impacts, structural stability).
    - 4.1.2 Atmospheric Composition (dust generation).
    - 4.1.3 Climate.
    - 4.1.4 Solar Radiation.
    - 4.1.5 Water Resources (ice location, chemistry, and water use).
- **4.2 Cultural Resources:** Historic or cultural significance.
- **4.3 Hazardous Material and Pollutants:** Disposition of wastes.

### 5.0 Human Health and Safety
Mission characterization and exposure to radiation/extremes.

### 6.0 Cumulative Impacts
Later-in-time mining or actions encouraged by the current activity.

### 7.0 Summary
Mitigation strategies to reduce or eliminate adverse impacts.

## 5. Final thoughts
EEAs would document pre-action baseline conditions and create a permanent record for future reference. A repository of such assessments would assist in avoiding similar problems in subsequent actions and foster a culture of environmental awareness in space development.
