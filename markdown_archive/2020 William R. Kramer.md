# A Framework for Extraterrestrial Environmental Assessment

**Author:** William R. Kramer
**Affiliation:** Outer Space Consulting, Frederick, MD, 21701, USA
**Journal:** Space Policy 53 (2020) 101385
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.spacepol.2020.101385

## Abstract
Many of the proposed and foreseeable activities on the Moon, Mars, and elsewhere in our solar system will require construction and similar actions. Most will, by intent, alter those natural environments in a variety of ways. Some impacts may foreclose future options for use of the area, hinder sustained access to resources, impede long-term human occupation, interfere with scientific or other purposes, harm cultural assets, create unsafe conditions, or cause other detriments. This article proposes a simple framework for extraterrestrial environmental assessment (EEA) as a starting point for discussion, drawing upon but not directly applying the US National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA).

## 1. A rapidly evolving problem in space development
Nearly all physical activities beyond Earth, such as construction, mining, and human habitation, may adversely affect extraterrestrial environments. Unlike Earth, alien landscapes lack tectonic movements, water, and vegetation that facilitate resilience, making alterations potentially permanent. While current focus remains on LEO orbital debris, there is a lack of progress regarding environmental management for the Moon, Mars, and asteroids.

## 2. The neglected need for extraterrestrial environmental assessment
There is an economic incentive for "first in time" ventures to target resources (ISRU). However, unplanned exploitation could waste resources or impede access. Impacts include:
- **Blasting/Mining:** Ejected rocks damaging nearby infrastructure; vibrations invalidating seismic studies.
- **Dust:** Coating solar panels, interfering with telescopes, or eroding gaskets.
- **Chemical Leaks:** Lubricants/compounds seeping into subsurface water ice.
- **Topography:** Disturbing geological features of scientific or cultural value ("exogeoconservation").

## 3. NEPA and potential sources of reticence to EEAs
Industry and agencies may fear that acknowledging environmental impacts will lead to stifling regulations similar to NEPA. However, NEPA currently does not apply to extraterrestrial actions and is viewed as too cumbersome (lengthy, litigated) for space applications. The ESA uses life cycle assessments (LCA), but these are currently limited to Earth and LEO impacts.

## 4. Framework for EEA
The proposed EEA is designed to be practical and efficient, facilitating a pre-launch review process.

### Simplified Outline for an EEA:
1. **Introduction:** Purpose and need for the action.
2. **Proposed Action:** Detailed narrative of what is to be built, equipment used, and outcomes if abandoned.
3. **Timing and Location:** Coordinates, terrain alteration maps, and seasonal topography changes.
4. **Affected Environment:**
    - **Physical Resources:** Geology, regolith (vibration, structural stability), and seismicity.
    - **Atmospheric Composition:** Generation of dust and its persistence.
    - **Climate and Solar Radiation:** Local changes and exposure intensity.
    - **Water Resources:** Location, state, chemistry, and use (extraction volume, reinjection).
5. **Cultural Resources:** Indicators of previous human actions or aesthetic impacts.
6. **Hazardous Material and Pollutants:** Disposition of wastes and chemical degradation.
7. **Human Health and Safety:** Exposure risks and mission characterization.
8. **Cumulative Impacts:** Long-term effects of repeated mining or actions.
9. **Summary and Mitigation:** Tables of direct/indirect impacts and proposed mitigation actions.

## 5. Final Thoughts and Next Actions
EEAs should be digitally filed in a library for public reference, creating a record of baseline conditions. A consortium of nongovernmental space actors could monitor the process to ensure transparency and accuracy.
