# A Framework for Extraterrestrial Environmental Assessment

**Author:** William R. Kramer
**Journal:** Space Policy 53 (2020) 101385
**Publisher:** Elsevier Ltd.

## Abstract
Many of the proposed and foreseeable activities on the Moon, Mars, and elsewhere in our solar system will require construction and similar actions. Most will, by intent, alter those natural environments in a variety of ways. This article proposes a simple framework for an Extraterrestrial Environmental Assessment (EEA) as a starting point for discussion. While it draws upon procedures like the US National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA), it suggests that space industries and actors should develop their own appropriately structured assessment process rather than applying terrestrial laws directly.

## 1. A rapidly evolving problem in space development
Physical activities such as construction, mining, energy production, and human habitation may adversely affect extraterrestrial environments. Unlike Earth, alterations to alien landscapes could be permanent due to the lack of tectonic movements, water flow, and vegetation that facilitate resilience. There is a critical lack of a sustainable and legally binding framework for the exploitation of celestial bodies.

## 2. The neglected need for extraterrestrial environmental assessment
Space actors have largely failed to acknowledge potential impacts. Concerns include:
*   **Resource Management:** Ensuring sustained access to water and avoiding waste.
*   **Operational Interference:** Dust from mining coating solar panels or interfering with scientific equipment (telescopes/seismic studies).
*   **Exogeoconservation:** Protection and preservation of geological features of scientific or cultural value.

## 3. NEPA and potential sources of reticence to EEAs
While NEPA is a model for terrestrial environmental regulation, its direct application to space is viewed as impractical and counterproductive due to its lengthy, highly litigated processes and lack of international authority. The European Space Agency (ESA) uses life cycle assessments, but these are currently limited to Earth and LEO impacts.

## 4. Framework for EEA
The proposed EEA framework is designed to support the Outer Space Treaty and build investor confidence. 

### Simplified EEA Structure Outline:
1.  **Introduction**
2.  **Purpose and Need:** Why is the action being undertaken?
3.  **Proposed Action:** Scope, what is to be built, equipment, and timing (launch/construction dates).
4.  **Affected Environment:**
    *   *Physical Resources:* Geology, regolith, seismicity, and atmospheric composition.
    *   *Water Resources:* Location, physical state, and chemical composition.
5.  **Cultural Resources:** Indicators of human actions with historical significance.
6.  **Hazardous Materials:** Disposition of wastes and pollutants.
7.  **Human Health and Safety:** Personnel involvement and mission characterization.
8.  **Cumulative Impacts:** Effects of later-in-time mining or other actions.
9.  **Summary of Impacts:** Mitigations and unavoidable adverse effects.

## 5. Final thoughts
EEAs will provide a permanent record of pre-action baseline conditions and help solve unanticipated engineering problems as they arise. Creating a repository of incremental findings is critical for future missions.

## 6. Next actions
Space actors should take the initiative to develop industry standards for EEAs before strict regulations are imposed by necessity following environmental degradation.
