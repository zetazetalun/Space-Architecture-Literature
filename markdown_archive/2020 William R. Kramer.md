# A Framework for Extraterrestrial Environmental Assessment

**Author:** William R. Kramer
**Affiliation:** Outer Space Consulting, Frederick, MD, 21701, USA
**Journal:** Space Policy 53 (2020) 101385

## Abstract
Many of the proposed and foreseeable activities on the Moon, Mars, and elsewhere in our solar system will require construction and similar actions. Most will, by intent, alter those natural environments in a variety of ways. Some impacts may foreclose future options for use of the area, hinder sustained access to resources, impede long-term human occupation, interfere with scientific or other purposes, harm cultural assets, create unsafe conditions, or cause other detriments. This article proposes a simple framework as a starting point for an appropriate assessment and review process, drawing upon but not directly applying the US National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA).

## 1. A rapidly evolving problem in space development
Physical activities on the Moon and Mars—such as construction, mining, energy production, and human habitation—may adversely affect extraterrestrial environments. Alterations to alien landscapes could be permanent due to the lack of Earth-like resilient processes (tectonics, water flow, vegetation). There is currently a lack of a sustainable and legally binding framework for the exploitation of celestial bodies.

## 2. The neglected need for extraterrestrial environmental assessment
Space actors (government and commercial) have largely ignored the critical need for extraterrestrial environmental assessments (EEAs). Potential adverse impacts include:
- Competition for resource-rich areas (e.g., water ice).
- Damage to nearby infrastructure from blasting/vibrations.
- Dust coating solar panels or interfering with scientific instruments.
- Chemical leaks (lubricants, compounds) into subsurface water ice.
- Destruction of geologic features with scientific or cultural value.

## 3. National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) and potential sources of reticence to EEAs
While NEPA provides a model for environmental regulation, its direct application to space is deemed impractical and counterproductive. NEPA is limited to actions with a US federal nexus and lacks international authority. Furthermore, recent US policy has shifted toward easing restrictions on the commercial recovery of space resources.

## 4. Framework for EEA
An EEA is envisioned as a planning tool to identify direct, indirect, and cumulative impacts. The proposed framework includes:

### Proposed Outline for an EEA (Structure)
- **1.0 Introduction**
- **2.0 Purpose and Need:** Why is the action being undertaken?
- **3.0 Proposed Action:** Detailed narrative of what is to be built, equipment used, and foreseeable outcomes.
  - **3.1 Timing:** Launch, transit, construction dates.
  - **3.2 Location:** Coordinates, terrain alteration, road construction, storage areas.
- **4.0 Affected Environment:**
  - **4.1 Physical Resources:** Geology, regolith, seismicity (vibrations from blasting), atmospheric composition, climate, solar radiation, and water resources (location, chemistry, use).
  - **4.2 Cultural Resources:** Indicators of previous human actions with historical significance.
  - **4.3 Hazardous Material and Pollutants:** Materials taken to or generated on site.
- **5.0 Human Health and Safety:** Exposure to radiation, extreme temperatures/pressures.
- **6.0 Cumulative Impacts:** Later-in-time mining or actions encouraged by the project.
- **7.0 Summary:** Foreseeable impacts and mitigation actions.

## 5. Final thoughts
Even the simplest EEA would document specific actions at specific locations, creating a permanent record of baseline conditions. This repository of findings would assist in solving unanticipated engineering and environmental problems as space exploration evolves toward long-term habitation.

## 6. Next actions
Space industries and governments should take the initiative to develop industry standards for EEAs to achieve environmental goals while remaining practical for commercial and scientific objectives.
