## Abstract
Many of the proposed and foreseeable activities on the Moon, Mars, and elsewhere in our solar system will require construction and similar actions. Most will, by intent, alter those natural environments in a variety of ways. Some impacts may foreclose future options for use of the area, hinder sustained access to resources, impede long-term human occupation, interfere with scientific or other purposes, harm cultural assets, create unsafe conditions, or cause other detriments. This article proposes a simple framework for an appropriately structured assessment process for space industries and other space actors.

## 1. A rapidly evolving problem in space development
Nearly all reasonably foreseeable physical activities on the Moon, Mars, and elsewhere, such as construction, mining, energy production, and human habitation, may adversely affect those extraterrestrial environments. We build roads here, and we will build roads there. We construct launch facilities here, and we will construct them there. Alterations to alien landscapes could have longer-lasting implications than anthropogenic changes on Earth because alien landscapes lack the resilient effects of tectonic movements, water, ice flow, and vegetation.

## 2. The neglected need for extraterrestrial environmental assessment
Regardless of space industries, government agencies, and other space actors, such as scientific missions, not recognizing the critical need for extraterrestrial environmental assessments (EEAs), once proposed actions on celestial bodies are underway, it is predictable that interest groups and perhaps governments will demand them.

### Potential Impacts of Mining and Construction:
- **Resource Squandering:** Inefficient extraction of "lowest hanging fruit" like water ice.
- **Infrastructure Damage:** Rocks ejected by blasting could damage nearby structures.
- **Scientific Interference:** Vibrations could invalidate seismic studies; dust could coat solar panels or interfere with telescopes.
- **Contamination:** Lubricants and compounds could leak from vehicles and seep into subsurface water ice.
- **Topographical Disturbance:** Undocumented prospecting may confound future researchers and destroy geologic features (exogeoconservation).

## 3. National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) and EEAs
While the US National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) provides a model for terrestrial assessment, it does not currently apply to extraterrestrial actions. NEPA is limited to actions with a US federal nexus. Applying NEPA directly to space is deemed impractical and potentially counterproductive due to its cumbersome nature and lack of international authority.

## 4. Framework for EEA
The proposed EEA framework is designed to be a practical, efficient, and effective pre-launch review process. 

### 4.1 Framework Categories:
1. **Introduction**
2. **Purpose and Need**
3. **Proposed Action:** Scope, narrative of construction, equipment used, and decommissioning plans.
   - *3.1 Timing:* Launch dates, transit, and construction duration.
   - *3.2 Location:* Coordinates, terrain alteration, and road construction.
4. **Affected Environment:**
   - *4.1 Physical Resources:* Geology, Regolith, Seismicity, Atmospheric Composition, Climate, Solar Radiation.
   - *4.2 Water Resources:* Location, physical state, chemistry, and use (volume extracted/injected).
   - *4.3 Cultural Resources:* Historic or cultural significance.
   - *4.4 Hazardous Material and Pollutants:* Disposition of wastes and hazardous materials brought to or generated on site.
5. **Human Health and Safety:** Number of personnel, mission characterization, and welfare (radiation/pressure extremes).
6. **Cumulative Impacts:** Later-in-time mining or actions encouraged by the project.
7. **Summary and Mitigation:** Foreseeable impacts and actions proposed to reduce or eliminate them.

## 5. Final thoughts
Assessments should be digitally filed and made available for public reference, creating a permanent record of pre-action baseline conditions. Over time, an indexed catalog of such determinations would assist in anticipating and avoiding similar problems in future missions.

## 6. Next actions
Space industries and private actors should take the initiative to develop industry standards for EEAs before strict regulation is imposed by national or international governments in response to public concern.
