# A Framework for Extraterrestrial Environmental Assessment

**Author:** William R. Kramer  
**Affiliation:** Outer Space Consulting, Frederick, MD, 21701, USA  
**Journal:** Space Policy 53 (2020) 101385  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.spacepol.2020.101385

## Abstract
Many of the proposed and foreseeable activities on the Moon, Mars, and elsewhere in our solar system will require construction and similar actions. Most will, by intent, alter those natural environments in a variety of ways. Some impacts may foreclose future options for use of the area, hinder sustained access to resources, impede long-term human occupation, interfere with scientific or other purposes, harm cultural assets, create unsafe conditions, or cause other detriments. This article proposes a simple framework for an extraterrestrial environmental assessment (EEA) process, drawing upon but not strictly applying the US National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) model.

## 1. A Rapidly Evolving Problem in Space Development
Nearly all physical activities—construction, mining, energy production, and human habitation—on the Moon and Mars may adversely affect those environments. Alterations to alien landscapes could be permanent compared to Earth's resilient ecosystems. The paper highlights that current space development plans often lack a structured environmental management framework beyond orbital debris and planetary protection (forward/backward contamination).

## 2. The Neglected Need for Extraterrestrial Environmental Assessment
- **Resource Management:** Ensures sustained access to resources like water and avoids "first in time" ventures wasting resources.
- **Landscape Protection:** Mining can eject rocks that damage nearby infrastructure, vibrations can invalidate seismic data, and dust can coat solar panels or interfere with telescopes.
- **Exogeoconservation:** The protection and preservation of geological features of scientific or cultural value.
- **Political Considerations:** Potential for public protest if commercial ventures disregard the "common heritage of mankind" principle.

## 3. NEPA and Potential Sources of Reticence
While NEPA provides a model, it is limited to US federal actions and is often viewed as cumbersome or politically sensitive. The author argues that applying NEPA directly is impractical due to lack of international authority and the specific nature of extraterrestrial environments.

## 4. Framework for EEA
The proposed framework serves as a planning tool for space industries and science missions. It includes:

### Outline of a Proposed EEA Structure
- **1.0 Introduction**
- **2.0 Purpose and Need:** Why is the action being undertaken?
- **3.0 Proposed Action:** Narrative description of construction, equipment, and foreseeable outcomes.
  - **3.1 Timing:** Launch, transit, landing, and construction dates.
  - **3.2 Location:** Specific coordinates, maps of terrain alteration, road construction, etc.
- **4.0 Affected Environment:**
  - **4.1 Physical Resources:** Geology, regolith, seismicity, atmospheric composition, climate, solar radiation, and water resources.
  - **4.2 Cultural Resources:** Impact on historical or cultural significance.
  - **4.3 Hazardous Materials and Pollutants:** Disposition of waste and chemicals.
- **5.0 Human Health and Safety:** Mission characterization and welfare.
- **6.0 Cumulative Impacts:** Long-term effects of repeated or concurrent actions.
- **7.0 Summary:** Foreseeable direct, indirect, and cumulative impacts; mitigation strategies.

## 5. Final Thoughts
EEAs would document pre-action baseline conditions and become a permanent record. This repository of planetary data is critical for solving unanticipated problems as exploration progresses.

## 6. Next Actions
Space ventures and governments should begin building a workable and efficient framework for environmental stewardship, learning from Earth's history of environmental degradation to avoid repeating it on the Moon and Mars.
