# Mechanical behavior of the metal parts welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator in ISRU & ISRF application

**Authors:** Wei Zheng, Guofu Qiao  
**Published in:** Advances in Space Research 65 (2020) 2303–2314  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.asr.2020.02.008

## Abstract
The use of in-situ resources plays an important role on future extraterrestrial human activities for the facility repair and habitat construction, especially in sustainable space exploration of Moon and Mars. A method of the metal welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant using solar processing under ambient conditions is presented. Metal parts are made of Q235B ferroalloy and TA2 titanium alloy into standard tensile members according to the ASTM code. They are disconnected from the middle in advance, and then welded together with lunar and Martian regolith simulant under ambient conditions, respectively. The results show that it is possible to weld metal parts together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator. The average ultimate tensile strength of ferroalloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 2.94 MPa and 1.66 MPa; The average ultimate tensile strength of titanium alloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 4.95 MPa and 2.59 MPa.

## 1. Introduction
The long-term explorations of the Moon and Mars have been goals of human space programs for decades. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and in-situ fabrication and repair (ISFR) technologies can significantly reduce the required launch mass and cost. This paper proposes a method for welding metal together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by solar concentrator at ambient conditions.

### 2.2. Welding process
A Fresnel lens (450mm diameter) was used to concentrate solar energy. The heat flux density of the focal spot was approximately 56.5 MW/m². Regolith (50 mg) was placed between the metal parts as a "sandwich" and melted using the concentrated solar spot to create the weld.

## 4. Conclusions
The study demonstrates that extraterrestrial regolith simulant can successfully fuse into base metals (ferroalloy and titanium alloy) using solar energy. While the tensile strength is currently low (less than 2% of the base metal), the research provides a proof-of-concept for in-situ repair and fabrication on the Moon and Mars. Failure modes were consistently brittle.
