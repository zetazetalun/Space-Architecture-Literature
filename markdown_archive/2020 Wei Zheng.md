# Mechanical behavior of the metal parts welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator in ISRU & ISRF application

**Authors:** Wei Zheng, Guofu Qiao  
**Journal:** Advances in Space Research 65 (2020) 2303–2314  
**Received:** 29 October 2019; **Accepted:** 9 February 2020  

## Abstract
The use of in-situ resources plays an important role on future extraterrestrial human activities for the facility repair and habitat construction, especially in sustainable space exploration of Moon and Mars. A method of the metal welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant using solar processing under ambient conditions is presented. Metal parts are made of Q235B ferroalloy and TA2 titanium alloy. They are welded together with lunar and Martian regolith simulant under ambient conditions. The results show that it is possible to weld metal parts together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator. The average ultimate tensile strength of ferroalloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 2.94 MPa and 1.66 MPa; The average ultimate tensile strength of titanium alloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 4.95 MPa and 2.59 MPa.

## 1. Introduction
Long-term explorations of the Moon and Mars require sustainable solutions such as in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and in-situ fabrication and repair (ISFR) technologies to reduce launch mass and cost. The surface of these planets is covered with basaltic regolith. Previous researchers have explored geopolymer cement, selective laser sintering (SLS), and microwave methods, but these often require high-power electricity or binders from Earth. This study explores using direct solar energy to weld metal components using regolith as a solder.

### 2.2 Welding Process
A Fresnel lens (diameter 450mm) was used to concentrate solar energy. The heat flux density at the focal spot was approximately 56.5 MW/m². Metal parts were arranged in a sandwich configuration with a 50mg layer of regolith between them. The solar spot was moved along the interface to fuse the metal and regolith.

## 3. Results and Discussion
Four welding forms were tested (Fe-L, Fe-M, Ti-L, Ti-M) with five parallels each. Stress-displacement curves indicated brittle failure.

Microstructural analysis (SEM/EDS/XRD) confirmed that the regolith successfully fused into the metal base. In ferroalloys, wustite and silicate melts formed a uniform phase. In titanium alloys, an obvious boundary existed between regions with different microstructures.

## 4. Conclusions
The study demonstrates the feasibility of using solar concentrators to weld metal parts with extraterrestrial regolith simulant. While the joints currently show brittle failure and low strength relative to the base metal, they provide a new path for astronaut assistance in repair and fabrication missions on the Moon and Mars.
