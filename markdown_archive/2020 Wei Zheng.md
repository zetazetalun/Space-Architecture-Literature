# Mechanical behavior of the metal parts welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator in ISRU & ISRF application

**Authors:** Wei Zheng, Guofu Qiao
**Journal:** Advances in Space Research
**Year:** 2020

## Abstract
The use of in-situ resources plays an important role on future extraterrestrial human activities for the facility repair and habitat construction, especially in sustainable space exploration of Moon and Mars. A method of the metal welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant using solar processing under ambient conditions is presented. Metal parts are made of Q235B ferroalloy and TA2 titanium alloy into standard tensile members according to the ASTM code. They are disconnected from the middle in advance, and then welded together with lunar and Martian regolith simulant under ambient conditions, respectively. The results show that it is possible to weld metal parts together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator. The average ultimate tensile strength of ferroalloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 2.94 MPa and 1.66 MPa; The average ultimate tensile strength of titanium alloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 4.95 MPa and 2.59 MPa.

## 1. Introduction
Sustainable space exploration requires in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and in-situ fabrication and repair (ISFR) technologies to reduce launch mass. Current research focuses on geopolymer cements, thermite reactions, and sintering methods (SLS, microwave, and solar) to produce building elements like interlocking bricks.

## 2. Experimental

### 2.1. Materials
*   **Regolith Simulants:** HIT-L-1 (Lunar) and HIT-M-1 (Martian), derived from volcanic material in China.
*   **Metal Parts:** Q235B carbon steel and TA2 titanium alloy.
*   **Solar Setup:** A 450 mm diameter Fresnel lens used to concentrate solar radiance (approx. 1200 W/m²) into a focal spot with a heat flux density of ~56.5 MW/m².

### 2.2. Welding Process
A sandwich-like assembly was created where regolith powder (approx. 50 mg) was placed between two metal parts. The solar concentrator was used to melt the metal surface and the regolith, forming a weld pool. The specimens were then cooled slowly under a powder bed.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### Tensile Strength Results
| Specimen Type | Lunar Simulant (MPa) | Martian Simulant (MPa) |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| Q235B Ferroalloy (Fe) | 2.94 | 1.66 |
| TA2 Titanium Alloy (Ti) | 4.95 | 2.59 |

*   **Failure Mode:** All specimens exhibited brittle failure.
*   **Microstructure:** SEM/EDS analysis showed that regolith fused into the base metal. For ferroalloys, a homogeneous fusion was observed with the presence of pearlite and wustite. For titanium alloys, an inhomogeneous fusion was observed with a distinct interface boundary between the fusion zone and the base metal.

## 4. Conclusions
It is feasible to weld metal parts using extraterrestrial regolith as a solder via solar energy. While current tensile strengths are low (<2% of base metal strength), the study provides a proof-of-concept for repairing and fabricating structures on the Moon and Mars using in-situ resources.
