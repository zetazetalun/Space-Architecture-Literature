# Mechanical behavior of the metal parts welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator in ISRU & ISRF application

**Authors:** Wei Zheng, Guofu Qiao
**Journal:** Advances in Space Research
**Year:** 2020
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.asr.2020.02.008

## Abstract
The use of in-situ resources plays an important role on future extraterrestrial human activities for the facility repair and habitat construction, especially in sustainable space exploration of Moon and Mars. A method of the metal welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant using solar processing under ambient conditions is presented. Metal parts are made of Q235B ferroalloy and TA2 titanium alloy into standard tensile members according to the ASTM code. They are disconnected from the middle in advance, and then welded together with lunar and Martian regolith simulant under ambient conditions, respectively. The results show that it is possible to weld metal parts together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator. 

## Materials and Methods
### Regolith Simulants
Two simulants were used: HIT-L-1 (Lunar) and HIT-M-1 (Martian).

| Oxide | HIT-L-1 (wt%) | HIT-M-1 (wt%) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| SiO2 | 39.16 | 43.20 |
| Al2O3 | 13.41 | 15.29 |
| CaO | 17.10 | 9.87 |
| FeOt | 17.81 | 18.50 |
| MgO | 2.45 | 2.82 |
| TiO2 | 3.25 | 3.17 |
| Na2O | 1.88 | 2.49 |

### Welding Process
- **Base Materials:** Q235B ferroalloy (carbon steel) and TA2 titanium alloy.
- **Heat Source:** Fresnel lens (diameter 450 mm, focal length 240 mm) concentrating solar radiance to a 2 mm spot.
- **Energy Density:** Average heat flux density of ~56.5 MW/m².

## Results and Discussion
### Tensile Strength
The ultimate tensile strength (UTS) of the weldments was measured using an Instron model 2519-106.

| Specimen Type | Lunar Simulant (Avg UTS) | Martian Simulant (Avg UTS) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Ferroalloy (Fe) | 2.94 MPa | 1.66 MPa |
| Titanium (Ti) | 4.95 MPa | 2.59 MPa |

### Characterization
- **Failure Mode:** All specimens exhibited brittle failure.
- **Microstructure:** In ferroalloys, pearlite and wustite phases were observed. In titanium alloys, high concentrations of Ti were observed in the fusion zone with a clear boundary between regions.
- **Mechanism:** Welding joint strength is derived from phases where the regolith acts as a solder, fusing into the metal alloys.

## Conclusions
It is possible to join metal components using regolith and solar energy. While current tensile strengths are low (less than 2% of the base metal strength), the method provides a feasible pathway for astronaut-assisted repair and fabrication in subsequent lunar and Martian missions.
