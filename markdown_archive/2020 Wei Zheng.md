# Mechanical behavior of the metal parts welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator in ISRU & ISRF application

**Authors:** Wei Zheng, Guofu Qiao  
**Publication:** Advances in Space Research 65 (2020) 2303–2314  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.asr.2020.02.008

## Abstract
The use of *in-situ* resources plays an important role on future extraterrestrial human activities for the facility repair and habitat construction, especially in sustainable space exploration of Moon and Mars. A method of the metal welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant using solar processing under ambient conditions is presented. Metal parts are made of Q235B ferroalloy and TA2 titanium alloy into standard tensile members according to the ASTM code. They are disconnected from the middle in advance, and then welded together with lunar and Martian regolith simulant under ambient conditions, respectively. The entire welding process and precautions are detailed. Additionally, the mechanical behavior of weldments is characterized regarding their tensile strength. Furthermore, the fusion zone of weldments is studied by Scanning electron microscopy (SEM), energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) and X-ray diffraction (XRD) measurements. The results show that it is possible to weld metal parts together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator. 

## 1. Introduction
Sustainable solutions like in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and in-situ fabrication and repair (ISFR) technologies can significantly reduce launch mass and cost. The surface of planets like the Moon and Mars are covered with basaltic material (regolith), which can be utilized for construction. This study proposes using solar energy directly to weld metal components using these regoliths as the welding medium.

## 2. Experimental
### 2.1 Materials
- **Regolith Simulants:** HIT-L-1 (Lunar) and HIT-M-1 (Martian) prepared from volcanic materials in Jilin Province, China.
- **Metal Parts:** Q235B carbon steel and TA2 titanium alloy.
- **Solar Setup:** Fresnel lens (450mm diameter) with a solar irradiance of approximately 1200 W/m² yielding a heat flow of 177.5 W.

## 3. Results and Discussion
### 3.1 Tensile Strength
The tensile strength of the weldments was measured using an Instron model 2519–106. All specimens showed brittle failure.

| Specimen Type | Average Tensile Strength (MPa) | Ratio to Base Metal (%) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Fe-L (Ferroalloy + Lunar) | 2.94 | 1.25 |
| Fe-M (Ferroalloy + Martian) | 1.66 | 0.71 |
| Ti-L (Titanium + Lunar) | 4.95 | 1.33 |
| Ti-M (Titanium + Martian) | 2.59 | 0.69 |

### 3.2 Microstructure
- **Ferroalloy Joints:** Microstructure showed pearlite in the pit region and wustite in the plate welding area. Wustite and silicate melts uniformly fused into a solid solution.
- **Titanium Joints:** Showed an obvious boundary at the fusion zone, with Ti and Si elements showing a gradient characteristic.

## 4. Conclusions
- It is feasible to weld metal parts using extraterrestrial regolith simulant and solar concentrators.
- Titanium alloys welded with lunar simulant achieved the highest strength (4.95 MPa).
- The strength is derived from regolith phases (wustite and silicates) fusing into the base metal at high temperatures.
- Future work will explore a wider range of aerospace materials and vacuum/simulated space environments.
