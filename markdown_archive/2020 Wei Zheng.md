# Mechanical behavior of the metal parts welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator in ISRU & ISRF application

**Authors:** Wei Zheng, Guofu Qiao
**Journal:** Advances in Space Research 65 (2020) 2303–2314
**Received:** 29 October 2019; **Accepted:** 9 February 2020

## Abstract
The use of in-situ resources plays an important role on future extraterrestrial human activities for the facility repair and habitat construction, especially in sustainable space exploration of Moon and Mars. A method of the metal welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant using solar processing under ambient conditions is presented. Metal parts are made of Q235B ferroalloy and TA2 titanium alloy into standard tensile members according to the ASTM code. They are disconnected from the middle in advance, and then welded together with lunar and Martian regolith simulant under ambient conditions, respectively. The results show that it is possible to weld metal parts together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator. The average ultimate tensile strength of ferroalloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 2.94 MPa and 1.66 MPa; The average ultimate tensile strength of titanium alloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 4.95 MPa and 2.59 MPa. Moreover, the failure mode of all weldments was brittle failure.

## 1. Introduction
Sustainable solutions for space exploration include in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and in-situ fabrication and repair (ISFR) technologies to reduce required launch mass and cost. The surface of the planets is covered with basaltic regolith. Previous studies explored geopolymer cements, thermite reactions (SHS), and selective laser sintering (SLS). This paper proposes a method for welding metal together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by solar concentrator at ambient conditions.

### 2.2. Welding process
1. Regolith (50 mg) was placed between two metal parts in a sandwich configuration.
2. A Fresnel lens (450 mm diameter) concentrated solar energy into a focal spot.
3. The metal was preheated for 5s, then the spot was moved along the interface to fuse the metal and regolith jointly.
4. Specimens were buried in powder to ensure slow cooling.

## 3. Results and discussion
Four welding combinations were tested: Fe-L, Fe-M, Ti-L, and Ti-M. 

| Group | Average UTS (MPa) | Ratio to Base Metal (%) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Fe-L | 2.94 | 1.25 |
| Fe-M | 1.66 | 0.71 |
| Ti-L | 4.95 | 1.33 |
| Ti-M | 2.59 | 0.69 |

Failure was characterized as brittle. The ratio of average strength of weldments to base metal was less than 2%, indicating that the strength of welded joints needs improvement. SEM/EDS analysis showed that wustite and silicate melts fused into the phase like a solid solution, contributing to the joint strength.

## 4. Conclusions
The study demonstrates the feasibility of welding metal parts using regolith simulant as solder via solar concentration. Titanium alloys showed higher strength than ferroalloys when welded with regolith. The presence of oxygenated compounds and brittle failure modes are the primary current limitations. Future work will explore a wider range of materials and simulated space environments.
