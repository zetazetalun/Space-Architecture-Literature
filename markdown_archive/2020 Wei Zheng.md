# Mechanical behavior of the metal parts welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator in ISRU & ISRF application

**Wei Zheng, Guofu Qiao**
*Advances in Space Research 65 (2020) 2303–2314*

## Abstract
The use of in-situ resources plays an important role on future extraterrestrial human activities for the facility repair and habitat construction, especially in sustainable space exploration of Moon and Mars. A method of the metal welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant using solar processing under ambient conditions is presented. Metal parts are made of Q235B ferroalloy and TA2 titanium alloy into standard tensile members according to the ASTM code. They are disconnected from the middle in advance, and then welded together with lunar and Martian regolith simulant under ambient conditions, respectively. The results show that it is possible to weld metal parts together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator. 

## 1. Introduction
Launching all material resources from Earth hinders space exploration due to payload mass, cost, and risks. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and in-situ fabrication and repair (ISFR) technologies can significantly reduce required launch mass. This paper proposes a method for welding metal together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by solar concentrator at ambient conditions.

## 2. Experimental
### 2.1 Materials
*   **Lunar Regolith Simulant:** HIT-L-1 (Harbin Institute of Technology Lunar Simulant 1).
*   **Martian Regolith Simulant:** HIT-M-1 (Harbin Institute of Technology Martian Simulant 1).
*   **Metals:** Q235B ferroalloy and TA2 titanium alloy.

### 2.2 Solar Irradiance & Lens
A Fresnel lens (450mm diameter) was used to concentrate solar energy. The heat flow reached approximately 177.5 W, with an average heat flux density at the focal spot of ~56.5 MW/m².

### Key Observations
- The failure mode for all weldments was **brittle failure**.
- Ti-L (Titanium welded with lunar regolith) achieved the highest average ultimate tensile strength at 4.95 MPa.
- The strength of the joints derives from phases where the regolith acts as a solder, fusing into the ferroalloys homogeneously and titanium alloys inhomogeneously.

## 4. Conclusions
The study demonstrates the feasibility of utilizing a solar concentrator to weld metal parts using extraterrestrial regolith as solder. While the joint strengths are currently less than 2% of the base metal's strength, the method offers a potential solution for repairing and fabricating structures during future Moon and Mars missions.
