# Mechanical behavior of the metal parts welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator in ISRU & ISRF application

**Wei Zheng, Guofu Qiao**

*School of Civil Engineering, Harbin Institute of Technology, Harbin 150090, China*

**Published:** 19 February 2020, *Advances in Space Research 65 (2020) 2303–2314*

## Abstract
The use of in-situ resources plays an important role on future extraterrestrial human activities for the facility repair and habitat construction, especially in sustainable space exploration of Moon and Mars. A method of the metal welded with extraterrestrial regolith simulant using solar processing under ambient conditions is presented. Metal parts are made of Q235B ferroalloy and TA2 titanium alloy into standard tensile members according to the ASTM code. They are disconnected from the middle in advance, and then welded together with lunar and Martian regolith simulant under ambient conditions, respectively. The results show that it is possible to weld metal parts together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by the solar concentrator. The average ultimate tensile strength of ferroalloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 2.94 MPa and 1.66 MPa; The average ultimate tensile strength of titanium alloy specimens welded with lunar and Martian regolith simulant is 4.95 MPa and 2.59 MPa.

## 1. Introduction
Launching all material resources from Earth hinders space exploration in terms of payload mass and cost. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and in-situ fabrication and repair (ISFR) technologies can significantly reduce the required launch mass. This paper proposes a method for welding metal together with extraterrestrial regolith simulant by solar concentrator at ambient conditions.

## 2. Experimental

### 2.1. Materials
*   **Lunar Simulant (HIT-L-1):** Developed by Harbin Institute of Technology.
*   **Martian Simulant (HIT-M-1):** Developed by Harbin Institute of Technology.
*   **Metal Parts:** Q235B carbon steel (ferroalloy) and TA2 titanium alloy.

### 2.2. Welding Process
1.  A layer of regolith (approx. 50 mg) was placed between two metal parts.
2.  A Fresnel lens (diameter 450 mm) was used to concentrate solar energy.
3.  Solar irradiance intensity was 1200 W/m².
4.  Heat flow was calculated at approximately 177.5 W, with a focal spot heat flux density of 56.5 MW/m².
5.  Metal surfaces were melted into droplets to form a weld pool, incorporating the regolith as solder.

## 3. Results and Discussion

| Specimen Type | Lunar Simulant UTS (MPa) | Martian Simulant UTS (MPa) |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| **Ferroalloy (Fe)** | 2.94 | 1.66 |
| **Titanium Alloy (Ti)** | 4.95 | 2.59 |

*   **Failure Mode:** All weldments exhibited brittle failure.
*   **Microstructure:** In ferroalloys, regolith fused homogeneously. In titanium alloys, it fused inhomogeneously with an obvious boundary at the fusion zone.
*   **Composition:** XRD patterns of the fusion zone showed oxygenated compounds like Wustite (FeO) and silicate melts, contributing to joint strength.

## 4. Conclusions
The study confirms it is possible to weld metal parts using solar concentrators and regolith simulants. While the current tensile strength is relatively low (less than 2% of the base metal's strength), the method provides a new thought for astronaut assistance in repairing and fabricating during Moon and Mars missions.
