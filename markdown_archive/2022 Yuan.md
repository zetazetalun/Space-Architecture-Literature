# Robotic 3D printed lunar bionic architecture based on lunar regolith selective laser sintering technology

**Abstract:** 
The lunar base is not only an experimental station for extraterrestrial space exploration but also a dwelling for humans performing this exploration. Building a lunar base presents numerous obstacles and requires environmental perception, feedback design, and construction methods. An integrated fabrication process that incorporates design, 3D printing workflow, and construction details to build a bionic, reconfigurable and high-performance lunar base prototype is presented in this paper. The research comprises the study of the lunar regolith 3D printing mechanism, the real-time control of powder laying and compaction procedure, and the development of a 3D printing tool end system. In this paper, many scientific questions regarding in situ fabrication on the lunar surface are raised and addressed with the proposal of a progressive optimization design method, the molding principle, and gradation strategy of lunar soil-polyaryletherketone (PAEK) hybrid powder, and the principle of dual-light field 3D laser printing. The feasibility of the technical strategy proposed in this paper is verified by the presented empirical samples.

### 2.2 State-of-the-art lunar soil in situ 3D printing technology
There are five primary types of additive manufacturing processes for lunar regolith:
1. Material extrusion/jetting
2. Powder bed fusion (PBF)
3. Laser Engineered Net Shaping (LENS)
4. Selective Microwave Sintering (SMWS)
5. Stereolithography/Digital Light Processing (SLA/DLP)

## 3 Architectural conceptual design for lunar habitats
The shells are inspired by mollusk shell structures adapted for low gravity environments. Steps include:
1. Environmental study (vacuum, microgravity, radiation).
2. Geometric prototype based on microgravity state analysis of marine shellfish.
3. Robotic simulation and motion planning.

## 4 Lunar base 3D laser-printing construction material and technology
Research focuses on the interaction between lunar soil-PAEK hybrid powder and laser beams. PAEK is used as a binder to lower sintering temperature and improve efficiency.

## 5 Experiments
### 5.1 Optimal temperature range for hybrid powder printing
Experiments used JSC-1A synthetic regolith. It was found that resin powder had low absorption at 2μm wavelength, while lunar soil had high absorption, creating an energy transfer route. Optimal printing temperature: **360°C to 450°C**.

### 5.2 The ratio of lunar soil-PAEK and the rate of soil compaction
Compressive strength increases with resin content (4% to 20%). A **20% resin content** was selected for cost-effectiveness and structural performance. Tapping and compaction are essential during powder distribution.

### 5.3 Large-scale sample experiments
Verified feasibility with decimeter-scale samples including a sectional wall interior and a three-layer cross-dislocation structure.

## 6 Conclusion
In situ construction is the most cost-effective method for lunar bases. 3D printing technology offers flexibility and adaptability. Success depends on integrated systems capable of handling lunar environmental challenges like dust and radiation.
