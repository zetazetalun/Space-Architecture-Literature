# Robotic 3D printed lunar bionic architecture based on lunar regolith selective laser sintering technology

## Abstract
An integrated fabrication process incorporating design, 3D printing workflow, and construction details for a bionic lunar base is presented. Key elements include the study of lunar regolith-polyaryletherketone (PAEK) hybrid powder molding, dual-light field 3D laser printing, and real-time control of powder laying. The feasibility is verified through empirical samples.

## 1 Introduction
The construction of a lunar base encompasses four stages: preliminary preparation, infrastructure construction, base construction, and post-operation. Current global initiatives like NASA's Artemis and China's Chang'e 8 focus on field research for in situ construction with lunar regolith.

### 2.2 Regolith Printing Techniques
*   **Material extrusion/jetting**: Premixed pastes and liquid binders.
*   **Powder bed fusion (PBF)**: Selective solar/laser sintering (SLS/SLM).
*   **Laser Engineered Net Shaping (LENS)**: Molten powder slurry.
*   **Stereolithography (SLA/DLP)**: Slurry with organic binder and UV curing.

## 3 Architectural Conceptual Design
The concept is based on a **mollusk shell structure** (bionic), providing lightweight and high-strength protection. Design involves:
1.  Environmental load simulation (vacuum, microgravity, radiation).
2.  Geometric prototype design using thrust network analysis and BESO optimization.
3.  Robot simulation (FURobot) for real-time obstacle avoidance.

## 4 Materials and Technology
### 4.1 Hybrid Powder
PAEK is chosen as the binder for its material strength and extreme environment adaptability. It lowers sintering temperature and improves efficiency.
### 4.2 Dual-Light Field Laser Printing
A low-power density large-size spot preheats the powder, while a high-power density small-size focused spot performs the scan-sintering. This creates a uniform temperature field and high-efficiency forming.

## 5 Experiments
*   **Synthetic Regolith**: JSC-1A simulant used.
*   **Laser Wavelength**: 2-μm band selected due to low PAEK absorption and high regolith absorption.
*   **Thermal Range**: Melting starts at 310°C; decomposition at 510°C. Printing range: 360-450°C.
*   **Optimal Ratio**: A 20% PAEK resin content with 20MPa processing pressure provided optimal compressive strength and material density.

## 6 Conclusion
In situ construction is the most cost-effective method for lunar base development. 3D printing technology offers the flexibility needed for various challenging lunar scenarios.
