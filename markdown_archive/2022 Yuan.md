# Robotic 3D printed lunar bionic architecture based on lunar regolith selective laser sintering technology

**Authors:** Philip F. Yuan, Xinjie Zhou, Hao Wu, Liming Zhang, Lijie Guo, Yun Shi, Zhe Lin, Jinyu Bai, Youhai Yu, and Shanglu Yang
**Journal:** Architectural Intelligence (2022) 1:14
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s44223-022-00014-9

## Abstract
The lunar base is not only an experimental station for extraterrestrial space exploration but also a dwelling for humans performing this exploration. Building a lunar base presents numerous obstacles and requires environmental perception, feedback design, and construction methods. An integrated fabrication process that incorporates design, 3D printing workflow, and construction details to build a bionic, reconfigurable and high-performance lunar base prototype is presented in this paper. The research comprises the study of the lunar regolith 3D printing mechanism, the real-time control of powder laying and compaction procedure, and the development of a 3D printing tool end system.

## 1 Introduction
Construction of a lunar base encompasses four stages: preliminary preparation, infrastructure construction, base construction, and post-operation. The lunar environment poses challenges such as vacuum, microgravity, radiation, extreme temperatures, and micrometeorite bombardment.

## 3 Architectural Conceptual Design
The concept focuses on "Moon-base buildings" based on mollusk shell structures, utilizing bionic features for lightweight and high-strength performance. Optimization steps include structural analysis of marine shellfish, geometry prototype iteration, and printing feasibility research.

## 4 3D Laser-Printing Construction Material and Technology
Research focuses on the use of Polyaryletherketone (PAEK) as a binder for lunar regolith. PAEK is selected due to its material strength, adhesion, and extreme environmental adaptability. A dual-light field coupling laser-printing technology is proposed to optimize temperature distribution.

## 5 Experiments
### 5.1 Optimal temperature range
Tests using JSC-1A simulant and PAEK determined:
- Hybrid powder starts to melt at 310°C and decomposes at 510°C.
- Optimal printing temperature: 360°C to 450°C.
- Wavelength: 2 μm laser band selected due to high absorption in regolith and stability.

### 5.2 Ratio and Compaction
- Optimal resin content: 20% by weight (based on compressive strength vs. transport cost).
- Compressive strength increases with resin content (4% to 20%) and processing pressure (5 MPa to 20 MPa).

## 6 Conclusion
In situ construction is determined to be the most cost-effective method for lunar development. The study successfully demonstrated the feasibility of printing complex bionic shapes in both horizontal and vertical directions using the regolith-PAEK hybrid powder strategy.
