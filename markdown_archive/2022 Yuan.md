# Robotic 3D printed lunar bionic architecture based on lunar regolith selective laser sintering technology ## Abstract The lunar base is not only an experimental station for extraterrestrial space exploration but also a dwelling for humans performing this exploration. Building a lunar base presents numerous obstacles and requires environmental perception, feedback design, and construction methods. An integrated fabrication process that incorporates design, 3D printing workflow, and construction details to build a bionic, reconfigurable and high-performance lunar base prototype is presented in this paper. The research comprises the study of the lunar regolith 3D printing mechanism, the real-time control of powder laying and compaction procedure, and the development of a 3D printing tool end system. In this paper, many scientific questions regarding in situ fabrication on the lunar surface are raised and addressed with the proposal of a progressive optimization design method, the molding principle, and gradation strategy of lunar soil-polyaryletherketone (PAEK) hybrid powder, and the principle of dual-light field 3D laser printing. The feasibility of the technical strategy proposed in this paper is verified by the presented empirical samples. ## 1 Introduction Human exploration of the moon began in the 1960s. Since the beginning of the twenty-first century, nations including China (Chang'e 8) and the USA (Artemis) have focused on in situ construction with lunar regolith. A lunar base serves as an experimental station and outpost for settlements, requiring preliminary preparation, infrastructure, and base construction phases. ## 2 State-of-the-art Lunar Base construction exploration 2.1 Lunar base design based on the in situ construction method This paper evaluates the Sinterhub scheme (NASA), Lunar Habitation (ESA/Foster), and China Super Mason (HZUST). Table 1 shows that Sinterhub and Lunar Habitation use microwave sintering, while Super Mason uses autoclaved bricks. 2.2 State-of-the-art lunar soil in situ 3D printing technology ISRU involves turning regolith into AM raw materials. Key processes include Material extrusion/jetting, Powder bed fusion (PBF), Laser Engineered Net Shaping (LENS), Selective Microwave Sintering (SMWS), and Stereolithography (SLA/DLP). This research focuses on SLS (a PBF variant) using a regolith-PAEK hybrid powder. ## 3 Architectural conceptual design for lunar habitats The design proposes Moon-base buildings based on mollusk shell structures (nacre), providing a prototype for lightweight, high-strength structures suitable for microgravity. The workflow includes environmental analysis, bionic prototype design based on marine shellfish force simulation, and robotic simulation using the FURobot platform. ## 4 Lunar base 3D laser-printing construction material and technology Research focuses on: 1. Interaction between regolith-PAEK and laser beams. 2. Dual-light field coupling laser-printing to improve molding efficiency. 3. Strategy optimization for transportation, laying, and compaction. ## 5 Experiments Experiments used JSC-1A simulant and a 2um mid-infrared laser. Findings: 1. Hybrid powder starts melting at 310°C and decomposes at 510°C; optimal printing range is 360-450°C. 2. 20% resin content and specific processing pressures significantly improve compressive strength (up to 20MPa). 3. Successful horizontal and vertical printing of complex decimeter-scale samples verified the feasibility of the process. ## 6 Conclusion In situ construction using 3D printing is identified as the most cost-effective method. The research validates a comprehensive system from material ratio optimization to robotic tool development for building reconfigurable bionic lunar bases.
