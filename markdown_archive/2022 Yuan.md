# Robotic 3D printed lunar bionic architecture based on lunar regolith selective laser sintering technology

**Authors:** Philip F. Yuan, Xinjie Zhou, Hao Wu, Liming Zhang, Lijie Guo, Yun Shi, Zhe Lin, Jinyu Bai, Youhai Yu, Shanglu Yang
**Journal:** Architectural Intelligence (2022) 1:14
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s44223-022-00014-9

## Abstract
The lunar base is presented as both an experimental station and a human dwelling. The research proposes an integrated fabrication process incorporating design, 3D printing workflow, and construction details for a bionic, reconfigurable lunar base prototype. Key innovations include the study of lunar regolith 3D printing mechanisms, real-time control of powder laying, and a dual-light field 3D laser printing system using a lunar soil-polyaryletherketone (PAEK) hybrid powder.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar exploration has progressed from early missions to current plans like the NASA Artemis program and China's "Chang'e 8" mission, which aims for field research in in situ construction. The construction process is categorized into four stages: preliminary preparation, infrastructure, base construction, and post-operation.

### 2.2 3D Printing Technologies
Five primary types of additive manufacturing for regolith are identified:
- Material extrusion/jetting
- Powder bed fusion (PBF)
- Laser Engineered Net Shaping (LENS)
- Selective Microwave Sintering (SMWS)
- Stereolithography/Digital Light Processing (SLA/DLP)

## 3. Architectural Conceptual Design
The paper proposes a "Moon-base" concept based on mollusk shell structures, providing lightweight and high-strength performance adapted to lunar gravity. 

### 3.1 Design Prototype
- **Analysis:** Extreme vacuum, radiation, and temperature differences (95K–387K).
- **Geometry:** Optimized via BESO (Bi-directional Evolutionary Structural Optimization) and thrust network analysis.
- **Path Generation:** Integrated control systems for real-time obstacle avoidance of robotic arms.

## 4. Materials and Technology
### 4.1 Hybrid Powder
Polyaryletherketone (PAEK) is chosen as a binder due to its strength and adaptability. It lowers sintering temperatures and improves printing efficiency.
- **Binding Mechanism:** Thermodynamic models examine energy transfer between regolith and PAEK.
- **Dual-light field:** Uses a low-power density large light spot for pre-heating and a high-power density small spot for focused sintering.

## 5. Experiments
### 5.1 Optimal Temperature
Experiments using JSC-1A simulant determined that the hybrid powder starts to melt at 310°C and decomposes at 510°C. The ideal printing window is between 360°C and 450°C.
### 5.2 Compression Tests
- Compressive strength increases as resin content rises from 4% to 20%.
- A 20% resin content was selected as the optimal balance for building materials.
- Compaction rate significantly impacts structural performance; tapping/compaction is required after powder distribution.

## 6. Conclusion
In situ construction is the most cost-effective method for lunar base development. The 3D laser-printing tool end and the bionic design approach provide a flexible, adaptable solution for extraterrestrial habitation.
