# Review of space resources processing for Mars missions: Martian simulants, regolith bonding concepts and additive manufacturing

**Authors:** David Karl, Kevin M. Cannon, Aleksander Gurlo  
**Journal:** Open Ceramics 9 (2022) 100216  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.oceram.2021.100216  
**Received:** 3 December 2021; **Accepted:** 27 December 2021; **Available online:** 30 December 2021

## Abstract
Scientific exploration of extraterrestrial planets has gripped human imagination since the advent of space travel. Human missions to Mars would only be feasible using local space resources materials (ISRU). In the absence of organic materials, regolith is the only viable resource for large-scale construction of habitats, greenhouses, and landing pads. This review provides the first comprehensive literature review of ISRU materials research employing Martian simulants, introduces a generational concept for simulant development, and categorizes processing literature into seven regolith bonding concepts.

## 1. Martian Simulant Generations
The authors propose categorizing simulants into three generations:
1.  **First-generation:** Single-source materials (e.g., JSC Mars-1 from Hawaii tephra).
2.  **Second-generation:** Mixtures of carefully curated minerals to mimic mineralogical distribution (e.g., P-MRS, S-MRS).
3.  **Third-generation:** Enhanced physical properties (texture/grain distribution) using binders to fuse grains into multi-mineral granules (e.g., MGS-1).

## 2. Regolith Bonding Concepts
The paper identifies seven categories of bonding concepts for ISRU:
*   **Sintering:** Radiant furnace, microwave, cold sintering, dynamic/static compaction.
*   **Melting:** Producing glass or cast basalt.
*   **Powder Pressing:** Dry pressing, clay pressing, Duricrete.
*   **Powder Agglomeration:** Dry vibration, Lunacrete, wet processing/fusion drying.
*   **Aggregate Bonding:** Concrete (cement/MgO), thermal liquefaction, sulfur concrete (Marscrete), geopolymers, chitosan/bio-binders, polymers (PE).
*   **Chemical Fusion:** Thermite reactions (SHS), reduction of metal oxides (FFC-Cambridge, MRE).
*   **Freezing:** Ice House concepts, water freezing.

## 3. Additive Manufacturing (AM) in ISRU
Robotic manufacturing is essential for pre-deployment of structures. The paper assesses seven ISO/ASTM 52900 AM categories for space application:

| Technology | Suitability Assessment for ISRU |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Material Extrusion** | Judged most suited for direct regolith ISRU (Contour Crafting, Robocasting). |
| **Powder Bed Fusion** | Feasible but induces thermal stress/cracking; requires better powder flow in low-g. |
| **Binder Jetting** | Feasible (D-shape); requires shipping liquid binders (e.g., Mg salts). |
| **Vat Photopolymerization** | Less feasible due to high requirements for shipped polymers. |
| **Directed Energy Deposition** | Feasible once refined metals are available via regolith reduction. |
| **Sheet Lamination** | Not suitable for unrefined regolith. |

## 4. Mechanical Performance Data
*   **Sintering (MGS-1):** 1150–1200 °C for 30 min yielded compressive strengths of 27–77 MPa.
*   **Sulfur Concrete:** Compressive strengths of 5–60 MPa.
*   **Binder Jetting (D-shape):** Reached 20.35 MPa (comparable to Earth concrete).
*   **Compacted Montmorillonite:** High pressure (150 MPa) gave flexural strengths of 2.29–8.93 MPa; dynamic compaction up to 50 MPa.
*   **Biopolymer (Chitosan):** Compressive strength of 2.6–3.6 MPa.

## 5. Summary of Challenges
*   **Preprocessing:** Feedstocks should ideally require minimal processing (milling/sorting).
*   **Energy:** Sintering and melting are energy-intensive; aggregate bonding requires shipped chemicals.
*   **Environment:** Processing under Mars-simulated vacuum and temperature remains largely unresearched.
