# Review of space resources processing for Mars missions: Martian simulants, regolith bonding concepts and additive manufacturing

**Authors:** David Karl, Kevin M. Cannon, Aleksander Gurlo
**Journal:** Open Ceramics 9 (2022) 100216
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.oceram.2021.100216

## Abstract
This review provides the first comprehensive literature review of ISRU materials research employing Martian simulants. It categorizes available Mars simulant processing literature into seven regolith bonding concepts and discusses the state-of-the-art on additive manufacturing (AM) in ISRU research.

## 1. Martian Simulants
The authors propose a generational concept for simulants:
- **First-generation:** Single source materials (e.g., JSC Mars-1).
- **Second-generation:** Mixed minerals mimicking distribution and elemental abundance (e.g., P-MRS, MMS-2).
- **Third-generation:** Enhanced physical properties using binders to fuse grains into multi-mineral granules (e.g., MGS-1).

## 2. Regolith Bonding Concepts
The authors identify seven categories for bonding regolith:
1. **Sintering:** Radiant furnace, microwave, cold sintering, dynamic/static compaction.
2. **Melting:** Glass and cast basalt production.
3. **Powder pressing:** Dry pressing and clay pressing (duricrete).
4. **Powder agglomeration:** Dry vibration (Lunacrete) and wet-processing.
5. **Aggregate bonding:** Concrete, geopolymers, sulfur concrete, phosphoric acid binders, and polymer composites.
6. **Chemical fusion:** Thermite reactions and reduction of metal oxides.
7. **Freezing:** Using ice or brine as a structural material (e.g., Mars Ice House).

## 3. Additive Manufacturing in ISRU
- **Material Extrusion:** Judged as the most suited AM technology for direct regolith ISRU due to scalability and suitability for vacuum/low gravity.
- **Powder Bed Fusion:** Requires energy but no additives; however, induces thermal stresses and unfavorable microstructures.
- **Binder Jetting:** Feasible for large structures; current research includes D-shape technology with magnesium salt binders.
- **Vat Photopolymerization:** Generally unfeasible for large-scale ISRU due to high polymer requirements.

## 4. Feasibility and Comparison
- **Mechanical Strength:** Sintering produces the highest strength values (27–77 MPa compressive strength).
- **Feedstock Availability:** Aggregate bonding typically requires 20–50 wt% of Earth-imported or processed binders. Non-aggregate concepts (sintering, melting) use 90–100% in-situ materials.
