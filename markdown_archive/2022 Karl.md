# Review of space resources processing for Mars missions: Martian simulants, regolith bonding concepts and additive manufacturing

**Authors:** David Karl, Kevin M. Cannon, Aleksander Gurlo
**Journal:** Open Ceramics 9 (2022) 100216
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.oceram.2021.100216

## Abstract
Scientific exploration of extraterrestrial planets has gripped human imagination since the advent of space travel. Human missions to Mars could produce insight into the essential questions of how, when and where life began on Earth. Such missions would only be feasible using local space resources materials, a concept called in situ resource utilization (ISRU). In the absence of organic materials from plants, the globally available oxidic surface minerals (regolith) are the only viable resource for large-scale construction efforts such as habitats, greenhouses, landing pads and equipment building. This review provides the first comprehensive literature review of ISRU materials research employing Martian simulants. It gives a detailed overview of all Mars simulants, their history, properties, and challenges, introducing a generational concept for simulants development. The available Mars simulant processing literature (including selected work on lunar simulants) is categorized into seven regolith bonding concepts. The state-of-the-art on additive manufacturing (AM) in ISRU research is discussed. Detailed feasibility assessments for all processing approaches are given, including overview graphs comparing the mechanical performance of each fusion concept with feedstock availability on the surface of Mars. Finally, major open questions and future challenges of materials processing for early Mars missions is examined.

## 1. Introduction to ISRU
In situ resource utilization (ISRU) is the collection, processing, storing and use of materials encountered in the course of human or robotic space exploration that replace materials that would otherwise be brought from Earth. Launching mass from Earth is expensive (99 units of mass required to launch 1 unit of mass into space). ISRU is essential for long-residence missions to Mars, which may involve stays of ~500 Earth days.

## 2. Mars Simulants
The authors identify ~30 Mars simulants and categorize them into three generations:
1.  **First-generation:** Single source materials (e.g., JSC Mars-1).
2.  **Second-generation:** Compilations of different minerals to mimic mineralogical distribution.
3.  **Third-generation:** Enhanced physical properties using binders to fuse grains into multi-mineral granules (e.g., MGS-1).

## 3. Regolith Bonding Concepts
The review categorizes bonding concepts into seven categories:

| Bonding Concept | Description | Mechanical Performance Highlights |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sintering** | Heating below melting point to fuse particles. | Highest strength (~27–77 MPa compressive). |
| **Melting** | Producing cast basalt or glass. | High energy requirement; produces durable, gas-tight products. |
| **Powder Pressing** | Dry or cold pressing of regolith. | Low energy; flexural strengths ~23–45 MPa at high pressures. |
| **Powder Agglomeration** | Using vibration, moisture, or colloidal wet processing. | Simple process; compressive strengths 0.23–0.7 MPa. |
| **Aggregate Bonding** | Using binders like cement, sulfur, or geopolymers. | Compressive strengths up to 75.6 MPa (for concrete). |
| **Chemical Fusion** | Thermite reactions or reduction of metal oxides. | Produces oxygen and metals; requires metal fuel. |
| **Freezing** | Using water ice as a structural material. | Promising for radiation shielding (Mars Ice House concept). |

## 4. Additive Manufacturing in ISRU
Robotic manufacturing is essential for building infrastructure before human arrival. The ISO categories of AM are mapped to ISRU:
*   **Material Extrusion:** Judged the most suited technology for direct regolith ISRU (robocasting, contour crafting).
*   **Powder Bed Fusion:** Uses lasers or solar sintering; can lead to unfavorable microstructure/cracking.
*   **Binder Jetting:** Selective deposition of liquid bonding agents (e.g., D-shape technology using Sorel cement).
*   **Vat Photopolymerization:** High precision but requires large amounts of terrestrial polymers.

## 5. Summary of Findings
*   Clay minerals (smectites) are excellent primary feedstocks for low-energy construction approaches (fusion drying/adobe).
*   Sintering is the 'gold standard' for mechanical strength if high energy is available.
*   Most aggregate bonding concepts require 20-50 wt% of materials not directly available on Mars (like sulfur or magnesium).
*   Material extrusion AM is the most scalable and adaptable technique for Martian environments.
