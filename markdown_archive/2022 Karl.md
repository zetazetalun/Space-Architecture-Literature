# Review of space resources processing for Mars missions: Martian simulants, regolith bonding concepts and additive manufacturing

**Journal:** Open Ceramics 9 (2022) 100216  
**Authors:** David Karl, Kevin M. Cannon, Aleksander Gurlo  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.oceram.2021.100216

## Abstract
This review provides the first comprehensive literature review of ISRU materials research employing Martian simulants. It introduces a generational concept for simulant development and categorizes regolith bonding into seven concepts: sintering, melting, powder pressing, powder agglomeration, aggregate bonding, chemical fusion, and freezing. The state-of-the-art in additive manufacturing (AM) for ISRU is discussed, including feasibility assessments comparing mechanical performance with feedstock availability.

## 1. Motivation for Mars Missions
Human missions to Mars require In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) to be feasible. Oxidic surface minerals (regolith) are the only viable resource for large-scale construction efforts such as habitats, greenhouses, and landing pads.

## 2. In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)
ISRU is defined as the collection, processing, and use of local materials to replace those brought from Earth. For Mars, the residence time (~500 days) and distance make ISRU essential for cost and risk reduction.

## 3. Mars Simulants
The authors propose three generations of simulants:
1.  **First-generation:** Single-source materials (e.g., JSC Mars-1).
2.  **Second-generation:** Mixtures of minerals mimicking mineralogical distribution (e.g., P-MRS, S-MRS).
3.  **Third-generation:** Multi-source mixtures with grain fusion to enhance physical properties (e.g., MGS-1).

## 4. ISRU Regolith Bonding Concepts
The review identifies seven categories of bonding:

1.  **Sintering:** Radiant furnace or microwave. Sintering MGS-1 at 1150-1200°C yields compressive strengths of 27–77 MPa.
2.  **Melting:** Producing cast basalt or glass fibers. Melting occurs around 1320-1350°C.
3.  **Powder Pressing:** Dry or clay pressing. Cold pressing can yield ~1.8-3.97 MPa.
4.  **Powder Agglomeration:** Vibration or fusion drying (colloidal wet processing).
5.  **Aggregate Bonding:** Concrete, geopolymers, or sulfur concrete. Sulfur concrete (Marscrete) can reach 5–60 MPa.
6.  **Chemical Fusion:** Thermite reactions or metal oxide reduction (FFC-Cambridge process).
7.  **Freezing:** Using subsurface ice as structural material (e.g., Mars Ice House).

## 5. Additive Manufacturing in ISRU
### AM Categories for Space
- **Material Extrusion:** (Robocasting/Contour Crafting). Judged as the most suited AM technology for direct regolith ISRU due to scalability and material flexibility.
- **Powder Bed Fusion:** Laser or solar sintering. Challenges include low packing density in vacuum and microgravity.
- **Binder Jetting:** Selectively depositing liquid bonding agents into a powder bed.
- **Vat Photopolymerization:** High precision but requires high volumes of Earth-imported polymers.

## 6. Feasibility Evaluation
- **Sintering:** "Gold standard" for strength but high energy demand.
- **Aggregate Bonding:** Most studied (concrete-like) but requires 20-50 wt% of additives often not readily available (e.g., specific salts or polymers).
- **Clay/Wet Processing:** High potential as it can use 90-100% Martian materials with low energy input.

## 7. Conclusions
Feedstocks should ideally require minimal preprocessing. For the future, research must shift from terrestrial conditions to simulated Martian environments (low pressure, extreme temperatures, and 0.38g gravity).
