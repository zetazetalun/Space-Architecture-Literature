# Review of space resources processing for Mars missions: Martian simulants, regolith bonding concepts and additive manufacturing

**Authors:** David Karl, Kevin M. Cannon, Aleksander Gurlo
**Journal:** Open Ceramics 9 (2022) 100216
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.oceram.2021.100216

## Abstract
Scientific exploration of extraterrestrial planets has gripped human imagination since the advent of space travel. Human missions to Mars could produce insight into the essential questions of how, when and where life began on Earth. Such missions would only be feasible using local space resources materials, a concept called in situ resource utilization (ISRU). In the absence of organic materials from plants, the globally available oxidic surface minerals (regolith) are the only viable resource for large-scale construction efforts such as habitats, greenhouses, landing pads and equipment building. This review provides the first comprehensive literature review of ISRU materials research employing Martian simulants. It gives a detailed overview of all Mars simulants, their history, properties, and challenges, introducing a generational concept for simulants development. The available Mars simulant processing literature (including selected work on lunar simulants) is categorized into seven regolith bonding concepts. The state-of-the-art on additive manufacturing (AM) in ISRU research is discussed. Detailed feasibility assessments for all processing approaches are given, including overview graphs comparing the mechanical performance of each fusion concept with feedstock availability on the surface of Mars.

## 1. Motivation for Mars missions
Human exploration of Mars is viewed as the next logical step after the Apollo program. The Mars Exploration Program (MEP) aims to prepare for human exploration as its fourth central goal. Long residence times (approx. 500 Earth days) in a surface habitat are inevitable due to orbital dynamics.

## 2. Introduction to ISRU
In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) involves the collection, processing, storing, and use of materials encountered during space exploration to replace materials otherwise brought from Earth. Launching mass from Earth is extremely expensive (99 units of mass required to launch 1 unit).

### 2.1 Focus on construction/manufacturing
Mars regolith (basaltic) is ubiquitous. Direct utilization of regolith is preferable over elemental separation due to lower power requirements and complexity.

## 3. Mars simulants
Real Martian regolith is unavailable, so terrestrial simulants are used. The authors propose a generational concept:
- **First-generation:** Single source materials (e.g., JSC Mars-1).
- **Second-generation:** Compilations of different materials mixed to mimic mineralogy.
- **Third-generation:** Mixtures that enhance physical properties using binders to fuse grains into multi-mineral granules (e.g., MGS-1).

## 4. ISRU regolith bonding concepts
The authors categorize bonding concepts into seven categories:
1. **Sintering:** Compacted solids formed by heat or pressure (radiant furnace or microwave).
2. **Melting:** Production of cast basalt or glass fibers.
3. **Powder pressing:** Dry or cold pressing into bricks (e.g., duricrete).
4. **Powder agglomeration:** Use of moisture or vibration to compact regolith (e.g., Lunarcrete).
5. **Aggregate bonding:** Use of binders like sulfur, MgO-silica cement, or geopolymers (e.g., Marscrete).
6. **Chemical fusion:** Reduction of metal oxides (thermite reactions or electrolysis).
7. **Freezing:** Using subsurface ice or depositing water to build structures (e.g., Mars Ice House).

## 5. Additive manufacturing in ISRU
Robotic AM technologies enable layer-by-layer construction:
- **Material extrusion:** Robocasting/contour crafting. Judged the most suited for direct regolith ISRU due to scalability and low requirements.
- **Powder bed fusion (PBF):** Selective laser sintering/melting using lasers or solar energy.
- **Binder jetting:** Selective deposition of liquid bonding agents (e.g., D-shape technology).
- **Directed energy deposition:** Laser-engineered net shaping (LENS).

## 6. Feasibility evaluation
- **Mechanical Strength:** Sintering produces the highest strength (gold standard) but requires high energy. Powder pressing and aggregate bonding follow.
- **Feedstock Availability:** Aggregate bonding typically requires 20–50 wt% additives not available on Mars. Sintering and powder-based methods use 90–100% in-situ materials.
- **Sustainability:** Clay minerals are excellent feedstocks for low-energy processing (adobe-like structures) which can later be sintered into high-strength ceramics.

## 7. Outstanding Research Questions
- Need to verify processing under simulated Martian conditions (low pressure, 0.38g gravity).
- Investigation of properties beyond mechanical strength, such as radiation protection, thermal conductivity, and long-term stability.
