# Review of space resources processing for Mars missions: Martian simulants, regolith bonding concepts and additive manufacturing

**Journal:** Open Ceramics 9 (2022) 100216  
**Authors:** David Karl, Kevin M. Cannon, Aleksander Gurlo

## Abstract
Scientific exploration of extraterrestrial planets has gripped human imagination since the advent of space travel. Human missions to Mars could produce insight into the essential questions of how, when and where life began on Earth. Such missions would only be feasible using local space resources materials, a concept called in situ resource utilization (ISRU). In the absence of organic materials from plants, the globally available oxidic surface minerals (regolith) are the only viable resource for large-scale construction efforts such as habitats, greenhouses, landing pads and equipment building. This review provides the first comprehensive literature review of ISRU materials research employing Martian simulants. It gives a detailed overview of all Mars simulants, their history, properties, and challenges, introducing a generational concept for simulants development. The available Mars simulant processing literature (including selected work on lunar simulants) is categorized into seven regolith bonding concepts. The state-of-the-art on additive manufacturing (AM) in ISRU research is discussed.

## 1. Introduction to ISRU
In situ resource utilization (ISRU) is defined as the collection, processing, storing, and use of materials encountered in the course of human or robotic space exploration that replace materials that would otherwise be brought from Earth. Mass reduction is critical as 99 units of mass are currently required to launch one unit of mass into space.

## 2. Regolith Bonding Concepts
The review categorizes regolith bonding into seven concepts:
1. **Sintering:** Radiant furnace, microwave, and cold sintering.
2. **Melting:** Casting basalt or creating glass fibers.
3. **Powder pressing:** Dry pressing, clay pressing, and dynamic compaction.
4. **Powder agglomeration:** Dry vibration, wet processing, and fusion drying.
5. **Aggregate bonding:** Concrete, geopolymers, sulfur concrete, and polymer composites.
6. **Chemical fusion:** Thermite reactions and reduction of metal oxides.
7. **Freezing:** Using water ice or brine as a structural material (e.g., Mars Ice House).

## 3. Additive Manufacturing in ISRU
The paper analyzes several AM categories for space application:
- **Material Extrusion:** Judged as the most suited AM technology for direct regolith ISRU due to scalability and material range.
- **Powder Bed Fusion (PBF):** Uses lasers or solar energy to fuse layers. Challenges include low flowability in microgravity.
- **Binder Jetting:** Selectively depositing liquid bonding agents. Examples include the D-Shape technology using magnesium salt binders.
- **Vat Photopolymerization:** High precision but requires large amounts of sensitive polymers not available via ISRU.

## 4. Feasibility Evaluation
Sintering is considered the "gold standard" for high-strength building materials if sufficient energy is available. However, for early missions, non-aggregate bonding concepts (90-100% ISRU material) are preferred over aggregate bonding which often requires 20-50 wt% of shipped additives.
