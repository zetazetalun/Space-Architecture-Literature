# Review of space resources processing for Mars missions: Martian simulants, regolith bonding concepts and additive manufacturing

**Authors:** David Karl, Kevin M. Cannon, Aleksander Gurlo
**Journal:** Open Ceramics 9 (2022) 100216
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.oceram.2021.100216

## Abstract
Scientific exploration of extraterrestrial planets has gripped human imagination since the advent of space travel. Human missions to Mars could produce insight into the essential questions of how, when and where life began on Earth. Such missions would only be feasible using local space resources materials, a concept called in situ resource utilization (ISRU). In the absence of organic materials from plants, the globally available oxidic surface minerals (regolith) are the only viable resource for large-scale construction efforts such as habitats, greenhouses, landing pads and equipment building.

## 1. Regolith Bonding Concepts
The review categorizes available literature into seven regolith bonding concepts:
1. **Sintering:** Radiant furnace, microwave, cold sintering, dynamic/static compaction.
2. **Melting:** Glass and cast basalt production.
3. **Powder Pressing:** Dry pressing and clay pressing.
4. **Powder Agglomeration:** Dry vibration compaction, wet-processing, and fusion drying.
5. **Aggregate Bonding:** Concrete, sulfur concrete, geopolymers, phosphoric acid, and polymer composites.
6. **Chemical Fusion:** Geothermite/SHS reactions and reduction of metal oxides.
7. **Freezing:** Using water/ice as a structural binder.

## 2. Additive Manufacturing (AM) in ISRU
The paper evaluates seven AM categories according to ISO/ASTM 52900 for their suitability in Martian environments:

| Technology | Suitability Remarks |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Material Extrusion** | Judged as the most suited AM technology for direct regolith ISRU. |
| **Powder Bed Fusion** | Produces unfavorable microstructures (cracks) due to fast heating rates. |
| **Binder Jetting** | Feasible for large structures; requires transport of binders or ISRU production of brines. |
| **Vat Photopolymerization** | Not feasible for direct regolith due to high polymer requirements. |
| **Directed Energy Deposition** | Feasible for metals once refined from regolith. |

## 3. Mechanical Performance Overview
| Bonding Concept | Additional Materials Required (wt%) | Typical Compressive Strength (MPa) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Sintering | 0% | 20 - 100 |
| Freezing (Ice) | 0% (in-situ water) | 5 - 20 |
| Sulfur Concrete | 20 - 50% | 5 - 60 |
| Magnesium Cements | 30 - 50% | 12 - 35 |
| Phosphoric Acid | 30 - 40% | ~20 |
| Sorel Cement (D-Shape) | ~10% | ~20 |

## 4. Conclusions and Future Challenges
- **Sintering** is the "gold standard" for strength but energy-intensive.
- **Clay minerals** are excellent feedstocks for low-energy processing.
- Transferring processing from Earth conditions to **simulated Martian conditions** (low pressure, extreme temperature) is a critical research gap.
