# Overview of the Lunar In Situ Resource Utilization Techniques for Future Lunar Missions

**Authors:** Peng Zhang, Wei Dai, Ran Niu, Guang Zhang, Guanghui Liu, Xin Liu, Zheng Bo, Zhi Wang, Haibo Zheng, Chengbao Liu, Hanzhe Yang, Yifan Bai, Yang Zhang, Dong Yan, Kefa Zhou, and Ming Gao.
**Journal:** Space: Science & Technology (A Science Partner Journal)
**Date:** Published June 1, 2023
**DOI:** 10.34133/space.0037

## Abstract
Building a lunar base by in situ resource utilization (ISRU) will drastically reduce the reliance on supplies from Earth. This article provides a comprehensive review of the latest progress of several ISRU technologies, including in situ water access, oxygen production, in situ construction and manufacture, energy utilization, and in situ life support on the Moon. A 3-step development plan for lunar ISRU technologies in the next decade is proposed.

## Introduction
Future lunar exploration will shift from understanding the Moon to making use of the Moon, transitioning from short-term exploration to long-term residence. ISRU includes the mining, treatment, and utilization of natural resources including lunar minerals, water/ice, volatiles, and solar energy.

### Major Purposes of Lunar ISRU:
1. In situ supply of life-supporting matters (water, oxygen, food, energy).
2. Construction and manufacturing of key facilities (manned habitats, landing pads, transport tools).
3. In situ supply of lunar-based rocket propellant (H2, O2, CH4).
4. Matter and facility support for scientific research.
5. Transit/supply stations for Mars missions.

## Natural Resources on the Moon
- **Mineral Resources:** Silicate minerals (pyroxene, plagioclase, olivine) and oxide minerals (ilmenite, spinels). Native iron grains are also present.
- **Water/Ice:** Approximately 2.9 × 10¹² kg of water is estimated in the uppermost meter of permanently shadowed regions (PSRs). Chang’E-5 samples estimated water content in soil at ~170 ppm.
- **Solar Energy:** Average radiant energy flux is ~1.1 × 10¹³ ergs/cm² per year.

## In Situ Construction Techniques

### Additive Manufacturing (AM) Techniques
- **Contour Crafting:** A gantry-based 3D printing technique using liquid-state binders to crystallize regolith. NASA explored this for quick infrastructure, though pumping binder in a vacuum remains a challenge.
- **D-Shape Method:** Suitable for large-scale construction in a single process. Developed by ESA for protective shells. Drawbacks include fluid injection issues in low gravity and the mass of liquid ink required from Earth.

### Sintering Techniques
- **Microwave Sintering:** Alternative to radiant furnaces. Proved effective with Apollo 17 soil and simulants like JSC-1A. Provides high efficiency and even heating.
- **Solar Sintering:** Uses concentrated sunlight (e.g., Fresnel lenses) to sinter regolith for dust mitigation or interlocking brick production.

### Extrusion Printing
- **Regolith Inks:** Uses premixed pastes of lunar soil simulant and liquid binders (e.g., poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) or photocurable resins). Samples have shown compressive strengths up to 428.1 MPa.

## Materials and Refining
- **Basalt Fibers:** Can be fabricated from lunar soil for use in composites. Continuous fibers from simulants have reached tensile strengths of ~1,400 MPa.
- **Oxygen/Metal Extraction:** Methods include Molten Regolith Electrolysis (MRE), Molten Salt Electrolysis (MSE), Hydrogen Reduction (H2 + FeTiO3 → Fe + TiO2 + H2O), and Vacuum Thermal Decomposition.

## Roadmaps
- **Phase 1 (By 2027):** Mineral resource exploration and assessment.
- **Phase 2 (By 2030):** In situ experiments via payload experiments.
- **Phase 3 (By 2035):** Pilot-scale replenishment for medium-term residence.
- **Phase 4 (Around 2040):** Large-scale replenishment for long-term lunar and Martian missions.
