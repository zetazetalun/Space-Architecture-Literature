## ABSTRACT
Space exploration and terraforming nearby planets have been fascinating concepts for the longest time. Nowadays, technological advancements in manufacturing, robotics, and propellants are thriving, it is only a matter of time before humans can start colonizing nearby moons and planets. In recognition of the 50th anniversary of the first manned lunar landing, NASA, together with the European Space Agency (ESA), revealed plans to establish a permanent human presence (habitats) on the Moon and Mars by 2040. In order to facilitate feasible and sustainable space exploration, such habitats are envisioned to be primarily built from lunar and Martian in-situ resources. To date, our understanding of indigenous resources continues to be lacking and in order to bridge this knowledge gap, this paper explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, along with terrestrial derivatives, for interplanetary construction. This paper also identifies key processing techniques suitable to produce extraterrestrial construction materials under alien environments (i.e., vacuum, low gravity, etc.) and showcases prominent design concepts for "space-resilient" habitats and colonies.

## INTRODUCTION
Whether we pursue lunar or Martian colonization first, space explorers are to be housed in habitats that not only need to withstand extreme environments, but also preferably fabricated from in-situ resources. Transporting one pound to the Moon can cost up to $10,000, a cost that scales exponentially for Mars. While utilizing in-situ resources promotes the development of independent and sustainable space habitats, characterization and processing such materials under microgravity as well as radiation and vacuum is challenging.

## LUNAR AND MARTIAN ENVIRONMENTS
Lunar and Martian surfaces hold harsh environments different from Earth, including weak/lack of atmosphere, vacuum, micrometeorites, high radiation, and rapid temperature fluctuations. Gravity on the Moon and Mars is approximately 17% and 38% of Earth's gravity, respectively. The Moon lacks an atmosphere (3x10⁻¹³ kPa), while the Martian atmosphere is ~0.7 kPa.

## EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS
- **Concrete-like derivatives:** Includes sulfur concrete, geopolymer concrete, polymer concrete, and waterless concrete. These leverage the abundance of regolith and aim to eliminate the need for water/curing.
- **Metals:** Regolith can be processed to produce Al, Mg, Fe, and Ti, which are beneficial for radiation and vibration resistance.
- **Advanced materials:** Includes composites, shape memory materials, and self-sensing/healing materials.

## PROCESSING OF EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS
- **Sintering:** Heating powder below melting point using solar, microwave (2.45 GHz), or laser energy (reported at 2.12 J/mm²).
- **Cold-pressing:** Leverages adhesive forces and smectite-like clay minerals to transform pressed regolith into solids.
- **Biocementation:** Microbially induced calcite precipitation (MICP) using microorganisms like *Haematococcus pluvialis* and *Chlorella protothecoids* with Martian CO₂ and organic waste (urea).

## DESIGN AND CONSTRUCTION CONSIDERATIONS FOR HABITATS
Structural systems in space would be similar to Earth's but modified for easier installation and extreme environments. Due to lower gravity, structures can resist dead loads 3 to 6 times smaller than on Earth, potentially leading to larger spans and slender members.

### Concepts for Habitats
1.  **Rigid habitats:** High redundancy but high mass and complex transportation.
2.  **Inflatable habitats:** Low transportation volume; preassembled/folded and inflated via automated processes.
3.  **Underground habitats:** Utilizing lava tubes for protection; light inflatable systems could be installed within them.
4.  **Mobile habitats:** Roaming units to improve exploration range.

## CONCLUSIONS
- Lunar and Martian environments adversely affect materials and structural systems.
- Emphasis should be placed on improving the efficiency of space-based processing techniques.
- Space habitats are categorized into rigid, inflatable, underground, and mobile types.
