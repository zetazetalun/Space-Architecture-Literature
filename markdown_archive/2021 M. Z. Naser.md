# Extraterrestrial Construction in Lunar and Martian Environments

**Authors:** M. Z. Naser and Qiushi Chen  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE (pp. 1200-1207)

## Abstract
Space exploration and terraforming nearby planets have been fascinating concepts for the longest time. Nowadays, technological advancements in manufacturing, robotics, and propellants are thriving, it is only a matter of time before humans can start colonizing nearby moons and planets. NASA and ESA have revealed plans to establish a permanent human presence (habitats) on the Moon and Mars by 2040. To facilitate feasible and sustainable space exploration, such habitats are envisioned to be primarily built from lunar and Martian in-situ resources. This paper explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, along with terrestrial derivatives, for interplanetary construction. It also identifies key processing techniques suitable to produce extraterrestrial construction materials under alien environments and showcases prominent design concepts for "space-resilient" habitats.

## Introduction
Transporting materials from Earth is prohibitively expensive ($10,000 per pound to the Moon). Utilizing in-situ resources (ISRU) promotes the development of independent and sustainable space habitats. However, characterizing and processing these materials under microgravity, radiation, and vacuum is challenging.

## Extraterrestrial Construction Materials
*   **Concrete-like derivatives:** Includes sulfur concrete, geopolymer concrete, and polymer concrete. Efforts aim to eliminate the need for water/curing.
*   **Metals:** Regolith can be processed to produce Al, Mg, Fe, and Ti for radiation and impact resistance.
*   **Advanced materials:** Composites, shape-memory materials, and self-healing/self-sensing capabilities.

## Processing Techniques
1.  **Sintering:** Heating powder below its melting point using solar, microwave (2.45 GHz), or laser energy.
2.  **Cold-pressing:** Leverages adhesive forces within regolith particles (e.g., smectite-like clay) and moisture to transform pressed material into a solid.
3.  **Biocementation (MICP):** Using microorganisms (e.g., *Chlorella protothecoids*) to precipitate CaCO3 as a binding agent using Martian CO2 and organic waste (urea).

## Habitat Concepts
*   **Rigid habitats:** High redundancy, complex construction, high mass.
*   **Inflatables:** High packing coefficient, shipped from Earth and deployed via mechanical/automated processes.
*   **Underground habitats:** Utilizing lava tubes to provide natural shielding against radiation and micrometeorites.
*   **Mobile habitats:** Roaming bases (e.g., independent rovers) to improve exploration range.

## Conclusions
Lunar and Martian environments adversely affect materials and structures. Efficiency in processing techniques must be improved to minimize energy and equipment needs. Habitats are categorized into rigid, inflatable, underground, and mobile types.
