## ABSTRACT

Space exploration and terraforming nearby planets have been fascinating concepts for the longest time. Nowadays, technological advancements in manufacturing, robotics, and propellants are thriving, it is only a matter of time before humans can start colonizing nearby moons and planets. In recognition of the 50th anniversary of the first manned lunar landing, NASA, together with the European Space Agency (ESA), revealed plans to establish a permanent human presence (habitats) on the Moon and Mars by 2040. In order to facilitate feasible and sustainable space exploration, such habitats are envisioned to be primarily built from lunar and Martian in-situ resources. To date, our understanding of indigenous resources continues to be lacking and in order to bridge this knowledge gap, this paper explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, along with terrestrial derivatives, for interplanetary construction. This paper also identifies key processing techniques suitable to produce extraterrestrial construction materials under alien environments (i.e., vacuum, low gravity, etc.) and showcases prominent design concepts for “space-resilient” habitats and colonies.

**Keywords:** Space exploration; Lunar and Martian habitats; Space construction materials.

## INTRODUCTION

We are explorers by nature. Our curiosity continues to grow towards finding an Earth-like destination that would be suitable for colonization. This pursuit has allowed us to land manned missions to the Moon in 1969 and inspired current efforts to launch manned missions to Mars within the next 10 years. While utilizing in-situ resources promotes the development of independent and sustainable space habitats, one should note that characterization and processing such materials under microgravity as well as radiation and vacuum is challenging (Naser 2019b; Toutanji and Grugel 2008).

## LUNAR AND MARTIAN ENVIRONMENTS

Both lunar and Martian surfaces hold a multitude of harsh environments including lack of atmosphere, vacuum, space debris, high radiation, and rapid temperature fluctuation. The gravity on the Moon and Mars is about 17% and 38%, respectively, of that on Earth.

## EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS

### Concrete-like derivatives
Suitability of sulfur concrete, geopolymer concrete, polymer concrete, and waterless concrete has been examined for lunar and Martian environments.

### Metals
Regolith can be processed to produce aluminum (Al), magnesium (Mg), iron (Fe), and titanium (Ti).

### Advanced materials
Includes composites, shape memory materials, and self-sensing/self-healing materials.

## PROCESSING OF EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS

### Sintering
Sintering involves heating granular material below its melting point using solar, microwave, or laser energy. Successful low-energy laser sintering has been reported at 2.12 J/mm².

### Cold-pressing
Leverages adhesive forces and moisture within regolith (especially smectite-like clay) to create solid components by applying pressure.

### Biocementation
Also known as MICP, this uses microorganisms (e.g., Chlorella protothecoids) to precipitate calcium carbonate as a binder, potentially using CO2 from the Martian atmosphere.

## CONCEPTS FOR HABITATS

1. **Rigid habitats:** High redundancy but high mass and complex construction.
2. **Inflatables habitats:** High packing coefficient; unfolded and inflated via automated processes.
3. **Underground habitats:** Utilization of natural lava tubes to provide radiation shielding.
4. **Mobile habitats:** Roaming units for short-to-medium duration reconnaissance.

## CONCLUSIONS

Space habitats can be grouped under four main types: rigid, inflatables, underground, and mobile. These habitats must be designed to accommodate the unique effects of low gravity and radiation on material performance.
