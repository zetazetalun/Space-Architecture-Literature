## ABSTRACT

Space exploration and terraforming nearby planets have been fascinating concepts for the longest time. Nowadays, technological advancements in manufacturing, robotics, and propellants are thriving, it is only a matter of time before humans can start colonizing nearby moons and planets. In recognition of the 50th anniversary of the first manned lunar landing, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA), together with the European Space Agency (ESA), revealed plans to establish a permanent human presence (habitats) on the Moon and Mars by 2040. In order to facilitate feasible and sustainable space exploration, such habitats are envisioned to be primarily built from lunar and Martian in-situ resources. To date, our understanding of indigenous resources continues to be lacking and in order to bridge this knowledge gap, this paper explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, along with terrestrial derivatives, for interplanetary construction. This paper also identifies key processing techniques suitable to produce extraterrestrial construction materials under alien environments (i.e., vacuum, low gravity, etc.) and showcases prominent design concepts for “space-resilient” habitats and colonies.

**Keywords:** Space exploration; Lunar and Martian habitats; Space construction materials.

## INTRODUCTION

We are explorers by nature... Whether we pursue lunar or Martian colonization first, space explorers are to be housed in habitats that not only need to withstand extreme environments, but also preferably fabricated from in-situ resources. This emphasis is triggered by the fact that transporting one pound to the Moon can cost up to $10,000, a cost that can exponentially scale in the case of Mars (Johnson-Freese 2017).

## LUNAR AND MARTIAN ENVIRONMENTS

Both lunar and Martian surfaces hold a multitude of harsh environments that are quite different from those on Earth. These include weak/lack of atmosphere, vacuum, space debris (micrometeorites), high radiation and rapid temperature fluctuation. The gravity on the Moon and Mars is about 17 and 38%, respectively, of that on Earth.

## LUNAR AND MARTIAN IN-SITU RESOURCES

A comprehensive analysis led by NASA on collected lunar soil and rocks showed the presence of substantial amounts of alumina, calcium oxide and silicate (Morris et al. 1983). Remote sensing has confirmed similar compounds on Mars.

## EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS

### Concrete-like derivatives
Suitability of sulfur concrete, geopolymer concrete, polymer concrete, and waterless concrete was examined. Current efforts aim at optimizing mixture design and possibly eliminating the need for water/curing.

### Metals
Regolith can be processed to produce aluminum (Al), magnesium (Mg), iron (Fe), and titanium (Ti). Metals are beneficial for radiation and vibration/impact resistance.

### Advanced materials
Includes composites, shape memory alloys, and self-sensing/self-healing materials. A challenge is the lack of verification tests in space environments.

## PROCESSING OF EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS

### Sintering
Heating powder below its melting point using solar, microwave, or laser energy. Microwave sintering (2.45 GHz) and low-energy laser sintering (2.12 J/mm²) are noted methods.

### Cold-pressing
Leverages adhesive forces (smectite-like clay and moisture) within regolith to bind particles into a solid under pressure.

### Biocementation
Microbially induced calcite precipitation (MICP) using microorganisms like *Haematococcus pluvialis* and *Chlorella protothecoids* to precipitate CaCO₃ from regolith and atmospheric CO₂.

## DESIGN AND CONSTRUCTION CONSIDERATIONS FOR HABITATS

Space habitats must provide higher redundancy and resiliency. Lower gravity allows for larger spans and more slender members. Structural systems may utilize self-healing/sensing materials to monitor structural "health."

## CONCEPTS FOR HABITATS

1.  **Rigid habitats:** High redundancy but higher mass and transport volume.
2.  **Inflatables habitats:** Folded for shipping, unfolded and inflated on-site. Made of high-strength composite fabrics.
3.  **Underground habitats:** Utilizing lava tubes (several km long) to provide natural shielding. inflatable systems can be used inside.
4.  **Mobile habitats:** Independent rover units forming a base to improve exploration range and redundancy.

## CONCLUSIONS

- Lunar/Martian environments adversely affect construction materials and structural systems.
- Processing and fabrication techniques must prioritize efficiency and minimize energy needs.
- Habitat types include rigid, inflatable, underground, and mobile configurations.
