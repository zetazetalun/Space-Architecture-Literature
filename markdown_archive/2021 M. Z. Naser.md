## ABSTRACT
Space exploration and terraforming nearby planets have been fascinating concepts for the longest time. Nowadays, technological advancements in manufacturing, robotics, and propellants are thriving, it is only a matter of time before humans can start colonizing nearby moons and planets. In recognition of the 50th anniversary of the first manned lunar landing, NASA, together with the ESA, revealed plans to establish a permanent human presence (habitats) on the Moon and Mars by 2040. In order to facilitate feasible and sustainable space exploration, such habitats are envisioned to be primarily built from lunar and Martian in-situ resources. This paper explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, identifies key processing techniques (e.g., vacuum, low gravity), and showcases design concepts for “space-resilient” habitats.

## LUNAR AND MARTIAN ENVIRONMENTS
Both lunar and Martian surfaces hold harsh environments including lack of atmosphere, vacuum, micrometeorites, high radiation, and rapid temperature fluctuations. Gravity on the Moon and Mars is approximately 17% and 38% of Earth's, respectively.

## EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS
- **Concrete-like derivatives:** Includes sulfur concrete, geopolymer concrete, and waterless concrete.
- **Metals:** Regolith can be processed to extract Aluminum (Al), Magnesium (Mg), Iron (Fe), and Titanium (Ti).
- **Advanced materials:** Includes composites, shape memory materials, and self-healing systems.

## PROCESSING TECHNIQUES
- **Sintering:** Heating powder below melting point using solar, microwave (2.45 GHz), or laser energy.
- **Cold-pressing:** Leveraging adhesive forces and smectite-like clay minerals in regolith.
- **Biocementation:** Microbially induced calcite precipitation (MICP) using microorganisms like *Haematococcus pluvialis* and *Chlorella protothecoids* to generate CaCO₃ binders.

## HABITAT CONCEPTS
1. **Rigid habitats:** High redundancy but high mass/volume.
2. **Inflatable habitats:** High packing coefficient, unfolded/inflated on-site.
3. **Underground habitats:** Utilizing lava tubes for radiation shielding.
4. **Mobile habitats:** ROV-based habitats for increased exploration range.

## CONCLUSIONS
- Environmental factors adversely affect materials and must be integrated into structural design.
- Processing techniques must be optimized for energy efficiency.
- Habitat designs are categorized into rigid, inflatable, underground, and mobile systems.
