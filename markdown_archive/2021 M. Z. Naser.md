## ABSTRACT

Space exploration and terraforming nearby planets have been fascinating concepts for the longest time. Nowadays, technological advancements in manufacturing, robotics, and propellants are thriving, it is only a matter of time before humans can start colonizing nearby moons and planets. In recognition of the 50th anniversary of the first manned lunar landing, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA), together with the European Space Agency (ESA), revealed plans to establish a permanent human presence (habitats) on the Moon and Mars by 2040. In order to facilitate feasible and sustainable space exploration, such habitats are envisioned to be primarily built from lunar and Martian in-situ resources. To date, our understanding of indigenous resources continues to be lacking and in order to bridge this knowledge gap, this paper explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, along with terrestrial derivatives, for interplanetary construction. This paper also identifies key processing techniques suitable to produce extraterrestrial construction materials under alien environments (i.e., vacuum, low gravity, etc.) and showcases prominent design concepts for “space-resilient” habitats and colonies.

**Keywords:** Space exploration; Lunar and Martian habitats; Space construction materials.

## INTRODUCTION

We are explorers by nature... Whether we pursue lunar or Martian colonization first, space explorers are to be housed in habitats that not only need to withstand extreme environments, but also preferably fabricated from in-situ resources. This emphasis is triggered by the fact that transporting one pound to the Moon can cost up to $10,000, a cost that can exponentially scale in the case of Mars (Johnson-Freese 2017). Fortunately, remote sensing missions have reported the abundant substances and elements that could potentially be used to produce lunar and Martian construction materials...

## LUNAR AND MARTIAN ENVIRONMENTS

Both lunar and Martian surfaces hold a multitude of harsh environments... Some of these include weak/lack of atmosphere, vacuum, space debris (micrometeorites), high radiation and rapid temperature fluctuation... The gravity on the Moon and Mars is about 17 and 38%, respectively, of that on Earth...

## EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS

- **Concrete-like derivatives:** Sulfur concrete, geopolymer concrete, polymer concrete, and waterless concrete.
- **Metals:** Aluminum (Al), magnesium (Mg), iron (Fe), and titanium (Ti) extracted from regolith.
- **Advanced materials:** Composites, shape-memory materials, and self-sensing/self-healing materials.

## PROCESSING OF EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS

### Sintering
Sintering can be carried out by focusing solar, microwave or laser energy onto bedded granular material... Typical heat sintering methods available allow sintering 0.1 mm successive layers at 1000°C. Application of microwave sintering enables electromagnetic energy using ultra-high frequency microwaves (2.45 GHz) and/or extra-high frequency microwaves (100-500 GHz).

### Cold-pressing
Leverages adhesive forces within regolith particles, especially smectite-like clay minerals... transforming the pressed material into a solid.

### Biocementation
Also known as microbially induced calcite precipitation (MICP)... photosynthetic microalgae (e.g., Haematococcus pluvialis and Chlorella protothecoids) were used... Preliminary results confirmed the formation of biocement and significant improvement in mechanical strength.

## DESIGN AND CONSTRUCTION CONSIDERATIONS FOR HABITATS

Space habitats must implement higher levels of intelligence, redundancy, and resiliency. Lower gravity on the Moon (1/6) and Mars (1/3) allows for structures to resist dead loads 3-6 times smaller than on Earth, potentially enabling larger spans and slender members.

## CONCEPTS FOR HABITATS

- **Rigid habitats:** High redundancy, complex construction, high mass.
- **Inflatable habitats:** Practical solution, low packing coefficient, shipped preassembled/folded.
- **Underground habitats:** Utilization of lava tubes to reduce radiation shielding needs.
- **Mobile habitats:** Habitats formed by independent rovers to increase exploration range.

## CONCLUSIONS

1. Harsh environments adversely affect materials and systems.
2. Space-based processing needs to improve efficiency and minimize energy.
3. Habitats can be categorized as rigid, inflatables, underground, and mobile.
