# Extraterrestrial Construction in Lunar and Martian Environments

**Authors:** M. Z. Naser and Qiushi Chen  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021 (ASCE), pp. 1200-1207

## Abstract
Space exploration and terraforming nearby planets have been fascinating concepts for the longest time. This paper explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, identifies key processing techniques (sintering, cold-pressing, biocementation), and showcases prominent design concepts for “space-resilient” habitats.

## Introduction
Transporting materials to the Moon costs approximately $10,000 per pound, and even more for Mars. Consequently, habitats must be fabricated from in-situ resources. Utilizing ISRU promotes independent and sustainable space habitats, though material processing under microgravity and vacuum remains challenging.

## Lunar and Martian Environments
Key environmental factors include:
- **Gravity:** Moon (17% of Earth), Mars (38% of Earth).
- **Atmosphere:** Moon has a hard vacuum; Mars has a thin atmosphere (~0.7 kPa).
- **Hazards:** Micrometeorites, high radiation, and rapid temperature fluctuations.

## Lunar and Martian In-Situ Resources
NASA analyses show substantial amounts of alumina, calcium oxide, and silicate in lunar soil. Similar compounds are confirmed on Mars via remote sensing.

## Extraterrestrial Construction Materials
- **Concrete-like derivatives:** Sulfur concrete, geopolymer concrete, and waterless concrete.
- **Metals:** Extraction of Al, Mg, Fe, and Ti from regolith.
- **Advanced materials:** Multi-functional composites with self-sensing or self-healing capabilities.

## Processing of Extraterrestrial Construction Materials
1. **Sintering:** Heating material below its melting point using solar, microwave, or laser energy. Successful sintering of 0.1mm layers at 1000°C has been reported.
2. **Cold-pressing:** Leveraging adhesive forces and moisture in regolith (especially smectite-like clay) to bond particles into solids.
3. **Biocementation:** Microbially induced calcite precipitation (MICP). Photosynthetic microalgae (*Haematococcus pluvialis*) can utilize CO2 and urea to create bioconcrete.

## Design and Construction Concepts
Habitats are categorized into:
- **Rigid habitats:** High redundancy but high transportation volume.
- **Inflatable habitats:** Shipped folded; unfolded and inflated via mechanical/automated processes.
- **Underground habitats:** Utilizing natural lava tubes for radiation and micrometeorite shielding.
- **Mobile habitats:** Rovers acting as independent habitat units to increase exploration range.

## Conclusions
Lunar and Martian environments adversely affect materials and structures. Future efforts must focus on improving the efficiency of space-based fabrication techniques and minimizing energy requirements.
