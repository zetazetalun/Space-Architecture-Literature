## Abstract
This paper explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, along with terrestrial derivatives, for interplanetary construction. It identifies key processing techniques suitable for extraterrestrial environments (vacuum, low gravity) and showcases prominent design concepts for "space-resilient" habitats including rigid, inflatable, underground, and mobile structures.

## Introduction
To facilitate sustainable space exploration, habitats are envisioned to be primarily built from in-situ resources due to the high cost of transportation (approx. $10,000 per pound to the Moon). Utilizing indigenous resources promotes independent and sustainable space habitats, though processing materials under microgravity, radiation, and vacuum remains a challenge.

## Lunar and Martian In-Situ Resources
NASA analyses of lunar soil show substantial amounts of alumina, calcium oxide, and silicate. Remote sensing on Mars has confirmed similar compounds.

## Extraterrestrial Construction Materials
- **Concrete-like derivatives:** Includes sulfur concrete, geopolymer concrete, and waterless concrete. These materials offer high potential due to regolith abundance and the potential for in-situ repair.
- **Metals:** Regolith can be processed to extract Aluminum (Al), Magnesium (Mg), Iron (Fe), and Titanium (Ti).
- **Advanced materials:** Focuses on composites, shape-memory materials, and self-sensing/self-healing capabilities.

## Processing of Extraterrestrial Construction Materials
1. **Sintering:** Heating powder below its melting point using solar, microwave, or laser energy. Microwave sintering (2.45 GHz) and laser sintering (2.12 J/mm²) are noted methods.
2. **Cold-pressing:** Leverages adhesive forces in regolith particles (like smectite-like clay) and moisture to transform pressed material into a solid.
3. **Biocementation:** Microbially induced calcite precipitation (MICP) using photosynthetic microalgae (*Haematococcus pluvialis* and *Chlorella protothecoids*) to generate CaCO₃ binders from Martian atmosphere (CO₂) and organic waste (urea).

## Design and Habitat Concepts
- **Rigid Habitats:** High redundancy but high mass and complex transportation.
- **Inflatable Habitats:** High packing coefficient; shipped from Earth and unfolded/inflated on-site.
- **Underground Habitats:** Utilization of natural lava tubes to provide radiation shielding and protection from seismic activity.
- **Mobile Habitats:** Rover-based independent units that increase exploration range and redundancy.
