# Additive manufacturing evolution in construction: From individual terrestrial to collective, aerial, and extraterrestrial applications\n\n## Abstract\nThis study reviews the advancement of Additive Manufacturing (AM) in the construction industry, tracing its evolution from individual terrestrial to collective, aerial, and extraterrestrial construction. Key topics include bio-inspired technology, topology optimization, material characteristics for lunar and martian surfaces, and automated robotic swarms for extreme environments.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nInnovative construction technologies like AM can expedite construction, enhance safety, and contribute to economic efficiency. AM facilitates sustainability by reducing waste and eliminating unnecessary molding processes. The trajectory of AM is moving from off-site to on-site construction through collective robotic work and remote lunar/martian construction.\n\n## 3. Fundamental AM Techniques\n*   **Contour Crafting (CC):** Developed by Khoshnevis (2004), uses XYZ gantry systems for large-scale building. Supports curved structures like domes/vaults.\n*   **Binder Jetting (D-Shape):** Powder-based method injecting liquid binder on granular material. Useful for using coarse aggregates.\n*   **Concrete Printing (CP):** Arm-based printing equipment depositing mortar composites.\n\n## 4. Advanced AM Techniques (Non-Terrestrial)\n### 4.1 Collective Robotic Construction (CRC)\nInvolves coordinated autonomous robots operating simultaneously to enhance scale and speed, inspired by social insects (ants/termites).\n\n### 4.2 Aerial AM (UAV AM)\nUses drones for surveying and depositing materials in inaccessible environments. Challenges include limited payload capacity and battery life.\n\n### 4.3 Lunar and Martian AM\nNASA and ESA introduced the concept of 'Advanced 3D Printing Construction Systems for the Moon and Mars'. The goal is habitats before 2040. \n*   **ISRU Materials:** Regolith is used to create 'Marscrete' (sulfur and regolith) or geopolymer-based lunar concrete.\n*   **Mars Ice House:** Uses H2O as a resource for AM ice structures in low Martian temperatures.\n*   **Environmental Comparison:**\n\n| Planet | Temperature (°C) | Oxygen (%) | Relative humidity (%) | Gravity (m/s²) |\n| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |\n| Moon | -171–127 | 45 | ~0 | 1.625 |\n| Mars | -143–17 | 0.13 | 80-100 (night); ~0 (day) | 3.721 |\n| Earth | -89.3–56.7 | 21 | 0–100 | 9.807 |\n\n## 5. Construction Considerations\nSuccessful implementation requires managing rheology (static/dynamic yield stress) and shrinkage. Using fibers (steel, carbon, glass) and local resources like lunar soil is critical to address logistical costs of transporting materials from Earth.\n\n## 6. Novel Approaches\n*   **Bio-inspired AM:** Leveraging biological architectures (honeycomb, Bouligand, nacre) to improve structural integrity and energy dissipation.\n*   **Topology Optimization:** Mathematical approaches like BESO to minimize material use while maximizing strength.\n*   **Magnetorheological Binders:** Smart materials controlled by magnetic fields to regulate flow on-demand during printing.
