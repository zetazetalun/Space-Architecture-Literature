# Thermal and Mechanical Properties of Cementitious Composites for Additive Construction of Energy-Saving Habitats

**Authors:** Hongyu Zhou; Adam L. Brooks; Dominic Hanna; and Babak Salarieh

**Published in:** Earth and Space 2018

## Abstract
Lightweight cementitious composites (LWCC) are cement based material combined with lightweight fillers. Because of its advantages in reducing both structural weight and thermal conductivity, it has the potential to be used in the construction of terrestrial and extraterrestrial habits. Furthermore, since it has relatively small and spherical shaped aggregates, LWCC is also suitable for extrusion based 3-D printing. In this research, a comprehensive and thorough study was carried out to investigate the effects of different lightweight fillers on both the mechanical and thermal properties of LWCC. Various lightweight fillers including three types of hollow glass microspheres (HGM) and two fly ash cenospheres (FACs) are studied in conjunction with various particle size, shell wall thickness, and volume fractions. Both mechanical and thermophysical properties were tested. The results indicated that the thermal property of LWCC is mostly governed by the volume fraction of LWFs whereas the mechanical properties are heavily affected by the type and property of LWF particles included.

## Introduction
Provoked by recent advancements in additive manufacturing, 3D printable concrete has been explored as an alternative method to construct concrete structures. 3-D printing of cementitious materials has the potential to address several major challenges: (i) it eliminates the use of formwork, (ii) the highly automated construction process reduces material waste and human error, and (iii) it allows for parametric material design.

## Summary of Findings
- **Thermal Properties:** The thermal conductivity of LWCC decreases notably as the LWF volume fraction increases. When volume fraction (vf) is 7%, conductivity reduces by ~30%; at 28%, it decreases to 0.65-0.8 W/m K.
- **Mechanical Properties:** Compressive strength is a function of LWF particle size and shell property. Smaller HGMs with thicker shells yield higher mechanical strength.
- **3D Printing Suitability:** LWCC with small, spherical fillers improves the rheological properties of fresh mortar, reducing air content and making it highly suitable for extrusion-based additive construction.
- **Dynamic Performance:** LWCC is strain-rate sensitive; dynamic compressive strength at a strain rate of 400 s⁻¹ was ~40% higher than quasi-static strength.

## Conclusions
LWCC incorporating HGMs and FACs can be designed for structural load-bearing while providing significant insulation. These materials are suitable for extrusion-based 3D printing of habitats, provided LWF volume fractions and particle sizes are carefully controlled.
