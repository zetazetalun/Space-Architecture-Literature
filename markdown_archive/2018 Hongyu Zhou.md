# Thermal and Mechanical Properties of Cementitious Composites for Additive Construction of Energy-Saving Habitats

**Authors:** Hongyu Zhou, Adam L. Brooks, Dominic Hanna, Babak Salarieh
**Affiliation:** Dept. of Civil and Environmental Engineering, Univ. of Alabama in Huntsville, USA
**Published in:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE, pp. 600-611

## Abstract
Lightweight cementitious composites (LWCC) are cement based material combined with lightweight fillers. Because of its advantages in reducing both structural weight and thermal conductivity, it has the potential to be used in the construction of terrestrial and extraterrestrial habits. Furthermore, since it has relatively small and spherical shaped aggregates, LWCC is also suitable for extrusion based 3-D printing. In this research, a comprehensive and thorough study was carried out to investigate the effects of different lightweight fillers on both the mechanical and thermal properties of LWCC. Various lightweight fillers including three types of hollow glass microspheres (HGM) and two fly ash cenospheres (FACs) are studied in conjunction with various particle size, shell wall thickness, and volume fractions. Both mechanical and thermophysical properties were tested. The results indicated that the thermal property of LWCC is mostly governed by the volume fraction of LWFs whereas the mechanical properties are heavily affected by the type and property of LWF particles included. In addition, to understand the materials’ mechanical performance under high strain rate, split Hopkinson pressure bar (SHPB) tests were conducted for strain rate ranging from 400 s-1 to 1000 s-1.

## Introduction
### Cementitious Materials for Additive Construction
Provoked by recent advancements in additive manufacturing (Wu et al. 2016), 3D printable concrete has been explored as an alternative method to construct concrete structures. 3-D printing of cementitious materials has the potential to address several major challenges: (i) it eliminates the use of formwork, (ii) the highly automated construction process reduces material waste and human error, and (iii) it allows for parametric material design.

## Experimental Program
The constituent materials include ASTM Type I-II Portland cement, silica sand, water, superplasticizer, and lightweight fillers (LWF). Water to cement ratio (w/c) was kept at 0.43. Four LWF volume fractions (7%, 14%, 21%, and 28%) were tested.

### Mechanical and Thermal Testing
- **Mechanical:** Tested using a MTS-810 servo-hydraulic system on 50x100mm cylinders.
- **Thermal:** Tested using the Transient Plane Source (TPS) method for thermal conductivity and volumetric heat capacity.
- **Dynamic:** High strain rate behavior tested using a Split Hopkinson Pressure Bar (SHPB).

## Conclusions
1. Thermal properties are primarily dictated by the volume fraction of LWF and are insensitive to filler type.
2. Mechanical properties are greatly affected by LWF particle size, shell thickness, and crushing strength.
3. Smaller LWF particles (HGM S60, FAC E106) allow for better distribution and higher strength, suitable for structural load bearing.
4. LWCC is strain-rate sensitive; dynamic strength increases with strain rate.
5. Spherical LWFs improve rheology, making the material suitable for extrusion-based 3D printing.
