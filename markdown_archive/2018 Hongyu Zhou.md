## ABSTRACT

Lightweight cementitious composites (LWCC) are cement based material combined with lightweight fillers. Because of its advantages in reducing both structural weight and thermal conductivity, it has the potential to be used in the construction of terrestrial and extraterrestrial habits. Furthermore, since it has relatively small and spherical shaped aggregates, LWCC is also suitable for extrusion based 3-D printing. In this research, a comprehensive and thorough study was carried out to investigate the effects of different lightweight fillers on both the mechanical and thermal properties of LWCC. Various lightweight fillers including three types of hollow glass microspheres (HGM) and two fly ash cenospheres (FACs) are studied in conjunction with various particle size, shell wall thickness, and volume fractions. Both mechanical and thermophysical properties were tested. The results indicated that the thermal property of LWCC is mostly governed by the volume fraction of LWFs whereas the mechanical properties are heavily affected by the type and property of LWF particles included. In addition, to understand the materials’ mechanical performance under high strain rate, split Hopkinson pressure bar (SHPB) tests were conducted for strain rate ranging from 400 s-1 to 1000 s-1.

## INTRODUCTION

### Cementitious Materials for Additive Construction

Provoked by recent advancements in additive manufacturing (Wu et al. 2016), 3D printable concrete has been explored as an alternative method to construct concrete structures. 3-D printing of cementitious materials has the potential to address several major challenges faced by the current concrete industry: (i) it eliminates the use of formwork (mold) and vibration that are typically necessary for consolidating wet concrete, which can significantly reduce material and labor costs involved in the formwork construction, particularly for in situ cast concrete; (ii) the highly automated construction process can reduce the material waste, cut down construction time, and reduce human error (Buswell et al. 2006; Bos et al. 2016); in addition, (iii) it allows a new approach for concrete design – the composition and property of printed material can be parametrically varied from one location to another, tuning to the optimal structural and energetic performances.

### Lightweight Cementitious Composites

Lightweight cementitious composites (or LWCC) are produced by incorporating millimeter and micrometer size lightweight functional fillers (LWFs) including expanded polystyrene beads, expandable thermoplastic microspheres (Aglan et al. 2009), hollow glass microspheres (Zhang et al. 2015; Oreshkin et al. 2016), and fly-ash cenospheres (Huang et al. 2013; Hanif et al. 2017) into cementitious binder matrix. Some of the emerging LWFs are introduced herein. The use of LWCC in buildings and habitat structure brings two-fold advantages: first, it reduces structural weight and the dead load acting on the structures which would lead to smaller structural members and foundation size (Yasar et al. 2003); secondly, the materials have lower thermal conductivity than normal weight concrete which will in turn reduce building energy consumption and provide better fire resistance (Clarke 1993). Unlike most lightweight coarse aggregates with irregular shape and porous surfaces (e.g., expanded shale clay), the lightweight particulate aggregates/fillers have much smaller particle size and are mostly spherical in shape (or have a core-shell configuration), leading to much more tightly controlled rheological properties for advanced construction process such as 3-D printing and long-distance pumping. It also allows the fine-tuning of material properties by varying material parameters such as particle size, shell thickness (stiffness), and volume fraction. Thus, an accurate knowledge of relationships between the composition, aggregate property, and the equivalent properties of the LWCC is desired for material design.

In this research, a comparative study is conducted on the properties of lightweight cementitious composites (LWCC) mixed with two types of lightweight filler particles – i.e., hollow glass microspheres (HGM) and fly ash cenospheres (FAC). The material thermophysical and mechanical properties are investigated with respect to the lightweight filler particle type, size distribution, as well as their volume concentration. Key parameters governing the material thermal and mechanical properties are discussed, and the composition-property relationships are deduced from both experimental results and predictive models. In addition, to understand the strain-rate dependent behavior of LWCC, a set of compressive tests were conducted using the split Hopkinson pressure bar (SHPB) on LWCC containing fly-ash cenospheres. The dynamic strain-strass behavior and damage mode were investigated as functions of the strain rate.

## EXPERIMENTAL PROGRAM

### Material Preparation and Mixture Design

The constituent materials used for preparing the LWCC in this study include ASTM Type I-II Portland cement, silica sand, water, superplasticizer, and the lightweight fillers (LWF). The water to cement ratio (w/c) was selected at 0.43 for all mixtures. The mass of cement, sand, and water used for each cubic meter of the reference mortar (without LWF) are 531 kg/m³, 1368 kg/m³, and 228 kg/m³, respectively. The LWF used in this research include three types of hollow glass microspheres (HGM) and two types of fly ash cenospheres (FAC). For each type of LWF, different particle properties (i.e., size, density, crushing strength, and volume fraction etc.) were studied with respect to their influences on the mechanical and thermophysical properties of LWCC. For each group of LWF tested herein, four LWF volume fractions (vf) – i.e., namely 7%, 14%, 21%, and 28% of the total volume were tested and for each volume fraction the equivalent volume of regular fine aggregate (i.e., silica sand) was deducted from the mix (i.e., the amount of silica sand used for vf = 7%, 14%, 21%, and 28% are 1138 kg/m³, 937 kg/m³, 716 kg/m³, and 534 kg/m³, respectively). The air content of fresh cement mortar was measured at 2-5% according to ASTM C185 – 15a, which does not account for the air contained within the closed pores of LWF.

Hollow glass microsphere (HGM) bubbles with density ranging from 0.25 g/cm³ to 0.6 g/cm³ were used for the mixes. The average particle densities tested by gas pycnometry for HGM K25, S38HS, and S60 are 0.25 g/cm³, 0.38 g/cm³, and 0.60 g/cm³, respectively.

Two types of cenospheres with particle sizes up to 106 μm (E106) and 600 μm (E200/600) are studied in this research. X-ray Diffraction (XRD) analysis performed on the FACs indicated that the cenospheres are comprised primarily of SiO2 and Al2O3, and there is a large amount of amorphous materials with small amounts of mullite and calcite.

### Mechanical and Thermal Testing

Mechanical properties of the LWCC mortar mixtures were tested using a MTS-810 servo-hydraulic universal testing system. The loading was carried out in a displacement-control mode at the loading rate of 0.01 mm/min. Five 50 mm diameter by 100 mm tall cylinders were tested for each mixture group.

The thermal properties (i.e., thermal conductivity and volumetric heat capacity) of LWCC were tested using the Transient Plant Source (TPS) method (ISO 2008).

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### Compressive Strength
It was observed that the compressive strength of LWCC mortars containing HGM and FAC is a function of LWF particle size and shell property (e.g., thickness) of the LWF included. When vf is small (<15%), the compressive strength of LWCC mortars increases with higher HGM concentrations... 

### Thermal Properties
In general, the thermal conductivity of LWCC mortar decreases with the increase in LWF volume fraction vf. For the control mortar samples (without LWF), the thermal conductivity was tested between 2.1-2.2 W/m K. The thermal conductivities of LWCCs are significantly lower than that of normal weight cement mortar and concrete due to the incorporation of hollow lightweight particulate fillers.

## DYNAMIC TESTING USING SPLIT PRESSURE HOPKINSON BAR

To obtain the dynamic behavior of LWCC under high strain rate, a set of impact dynamic tests were performed using Split Hopkinson Pressure Bar (SHPB). The dynamic stress-strain curves of specimens tested under different strain rates are similar while the compressive strength of LWCCs is strain-rate sensitive.

## CONCLUSIONS

A comprehensive study on lightweight cementitious composites (LWCC) incorporating various types of lightweight filler (LWF) materials (i.e., hollow glass microspheres (HGM) and fly ash cenospheres (FAC)) was conducted... 

*   The thermal properties (i.e., thermal conductivity and specific heat capacity) of LWCCs are primarily dictated by the volume fraction of LWF and they are insensitive to the type of LWF used; on the other hand, the mechanical properties of LWCC are greatly affected by the LWF particle properties including the size, shell thickness (stiffness), and crushing strength of the particles.
*   Because of the high strength-to-unit weight ratio, most fly ash cenospheres (FAC) and hollow glass microspheres (HGM) with higher density (e.g., S60) are suitable for producing lightweight cementitious composite (LWCC) materials that may be used for structural load bearing, as long as the LWF volume fraction is controlled within a reasonable range.
*   SEM imaging performed on LWCC samples indicated that smaller size LWF particles are well distributed in the cementitious matrix during mixing, despite their very low densities. Thus, these lightweight fillers may be suitable for extrusion based additive construction.
*   The Split Hopkinson Pressure Bar (SHPB) tests performed on LWCC indicate that the compressive behavior LWCC is strain rate sensitive. More systematic study is desired to understand the material behavior under high strain-rate loading.
