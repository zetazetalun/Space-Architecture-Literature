# In-depth analysis of sintering, exposure time, and layer height (um) in LRS 3D printed devices with DLP\n\n**Authors:** Shenggui Chen, Sadaf Bashir Khan, Nan Li, Chuang Xiao\n**Journal:** Journal of Manufacturing Processes 133 (2025) 576–591\n**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jmapro.2024.11.060\n\n## Abstract\nThe technology of 3D printing, referred to as additive manufacturing, is widely acknowledged as a transformative innovation for numerous domains. This study showcases a quantitative assessment of the mechanical properties of moon dust, also known as Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS), printed through vat polymerization. The investigation explores the effects of layer height (LH), exposure time (ET), and sintering temperature on mechanical properties. A 55% volume suspension of LRS was used to print configurations via digital light printing (DLP) and consolidated through sintering. Results show that a 30 μm LH with 11,000 ms ET at 1085°C exhibited maximum compressive and flexural strength of 330 MPa and 100 MPa, respectively.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nSpace agencies are looking into In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) to create new components through additive manufacturing (AM) using raw materials from planetary surfaces. The challenge is constructing habitats and infrastructure on the Moon under extreme temperature variations and radiation. This research explores robotics and 3D printing to reduce the cost of lunar exploration and pave the way for long-term settlements.\n\n## 2. Experimental\n\n### 2.1. Photocurable slurry formulation\nThe slurry is composed of acrylic resin, photoinitiator, and CLRS-2 (a basaltic lunar simulant developed by the Institute of Geochemistry, CAS). A 55% volumetric ratio of LRS to resin was used after ball milling the LRS to reduce particle size to approximately 7.62 μm (d50).\n\n### 2.2. 3D-printing technique\nPrinting was performed using a Ceramatrix printer (DLP) with a 405 nm UV light source. Samples were produced with varying layer heights (10–60 μm) and exposure times (3000–11,000 ms).\n\n### 2.3. Sintering phenomenon\nSamples were sintered in two stages: debinding (pyrolysis) up to 600°C and sintering at 1085°C for 3 hours. A range of temperatures (1050–1090°C) was tested to determine the optimal mechanical performance.\n\n| Parameter | Specification (SQFL-1700C Furnace) |\n| :--- | :--- |\n| Max Temp | 1700°C |\n| Heating Rate | 0–20°C/min |\n| Elements | 1800 grade MoSi2 |\n| Controller | PID control, 30 segments |\n\n## 3. Results and Discussion\n\n### 3.1. Rheological properties\nThe 55% solid volume fraction slurry exhibited shear-thinning behavior, which is essential for seamless printing with a scraper. Wetting agents were used to manage viscosity.\n\n### 3.2. Mechanical strength analysis\n- **Exposure Time (ET):** Strength improved linearly with increasing ET. Maximum strength was observed at 11,000 ms.\n- **Layer Height (LH):** A height of 30 μm was found to be optimal. Increasing height above 30 μm led to decreased interlayer adhesion and increased porosity.\n- **Sintering Rate:** Fast sintering rates (3°C/min) were found to be more favorable for mechanical strength than slow rates, resulting in reduced grain growth and enhanced densification.\n\n### 3.3. Phase Analysis\nXRD patterns confirmed the presence of hematite, magnetite, pyroxene, plagioclase, and olivine. Sintering above 1080°C increased the presence of iron oxides, enhancing the red color of the specimens.\n\n## 4. Conclusion\nThe study concludes that a combination of 30 μm layer height, 11,000 ms exposure time, and a 1085°C sintering temperature provides the highest mechanical resilience for lunar regolith structures. This approach offers a viable solution for constructing lunar bases without Earth-shipped binding agents.
