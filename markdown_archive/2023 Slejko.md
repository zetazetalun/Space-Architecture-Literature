# Vacuum 3D printing of highly filled polymeric matrix composites

**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 204 (2023) 25–33
**Authors:** Emanuele Alberto Slejko, Nicholas Sesto Gorella, Advenit Makaya, Paolo Gallina, Nicola Scuor, Stefano Seriani

## Abstract
In this contribution, we have investigated how polymeric matrix composites for aerospace applications are affected, when fabricated at low pressure, by the additive manufacturing process. Commercial filaments have been selected based on their representativeness of materials derived from lunar resources. Standard samples of thermoplastic polymers reinforced by organic fibers and inorganic fillers have been printed inside a vacuum chamber, and their mechanical and thermal properties have been characterized and discussed based on the printing conditions. The reported study represents a preliminary investigation of the potential applicability of the 3D printing technology on highly filled polymers for extraterrestrial applications.

## 1. Introduction
Vacuum 3D printing represents an evolution of the typical additive manufacturing process, usually conducted in atmosphere. It is a hot topic for the space sector because future exploration missions will require the capability to produce structures directly in-orbit or on-planet. Key advantages include forgoing pressurized environments and enabling the printing of very large structures. Challenges involve the integration of In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU), specifically using raw materials present in the target environment (Moon, Mars).

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1. Materials
Three commercial filaments were used: PLA, PLA filled with 40% organic fibers (pine wood), and PLA filled with 50% inorganic clays. These were selected as representatives of plant-based materials and lunar regolith simulants.

### 2.3. Adaptation for Vacuum
To operate at 10⁻⁴ bar, the printer (Monoprice Select Mini V2) was modified: 
- Extruder motor relocated outside the chamber or to a different position.
- Cooling fan replaced with copper thermal braids for conductive cooling.
- Control board moved outside the chamber.
- Polymeric bed sheets replaced with Kapton® tape.

## 3. Results and Discussion
Mechanical testing (tensile tests) and thermal analysis (DSC, TGA, XRD) showed that mechanical and thermal properties are not significantly affected by the vacuum environment. However, dimensional accuracy was impacted. Samples printed in vacuum were thinner but wider than those printed in 1 bar. This is attributed to increased surface tension at low pressure, promoting better wettability of the molten strand on the substrate.

## 4. Conclusions
3D printing in a vacuum (10⁻⁴ bar) is feasible with minor modifications to commercial hardware. While mechanical properties remain consistent, designers must account for dimensional deviations caused by surface energy changes. This technology is a fundamental asset for in-orbit manufacturing and ISRU-based construction.
