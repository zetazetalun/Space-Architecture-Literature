# Vacuum 3D printing of highly filled polymeric matrix composites

**Authors:** Emanuele Alberto Slejko, Nicholas Sesto Gorella, Advenit Makaya, Paolo Gallina, Nicola Scuor, Stefano Seriani
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 204 (2023) 25–33
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.actaastro.2022.12.033

## Abstract
In this contribution, we have investigated how polymeric matrix composites for aerospace applications are affected, when fabricated at low pressure, by the additive manufacturing process. Commercial filaments have been selected based on their representativeness of materials derived from lunar resources. Standard samples of thermoplastic polymers reinforced by organic fibers and inorganic fillers have been printed inside a vacuum chamber, and their mechanical and thermal properties have been characterized and discussed based on the printing conditions. The reported study represents a preliminary investigation of the potential applicability of the 3D printing technology on highly filled polymers for extraterrestrial applications.

## 1. Introduction
Vacuum 3D printing represents an evolution of the typical additive manufacturing process. Future exploration and colonization missions will require the capabilities to produce structures and mechanisms directly in-orbit or on-planet. Being able to print directly in vacuum forgoes the requirement of having a pressurized environment and enables very large structures to be printed. The challenge relies not only in being able to print under vacuum but doing so recurring to a high portion of raw material already present in the target environment (the Moon, Mars, etc.).

## 3. Results and discussion
Mechanical properties (ultimate strength, modulus, and elongation) showed no major differences between atmospheric and low-pressure (10⁻⁴ bar) printed samples. However, dimensional accuracy was influenced; samples printed under vacuum were thinner but wider. This is attributed to higher surface tension of the molten strand in vacuum, increasing wettability on the substrate.

## 4. Conclusions
3D printing can be successfully conducted in a vacuum chamber with appropriate thermal control (e.g., using copper thermal braids). While material properties remain stable, dimensional deviations must be accounted for in the design phase due to changed surface energy interactions in vacuum environments. This process is a fundamental asset for in-orbit manufacturing and lunar exploration.
