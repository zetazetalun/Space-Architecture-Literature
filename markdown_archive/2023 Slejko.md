# Vacuum 3D printing of highly filled polymeric matrix composites

**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 204 (2023) 25–33  
**Authors:** Emanuele Alberto Slejko, Nicholas Sesto Gorella, Advenit Makaya, Paolo Gallina, Nicola Scuor, Stefano Seriani

## Abstract
In this contribution, we have investigated how polymeric matrix composites for aerospace applications are affected, when fabricated at low pressure, by the additive manufacturing process. Commercial filaments have been selected based on their representativeness of materials derived from lunar resources. Standard samples of thermoplastic polymers reinforced by organic fibers and inorganic fillers have been printed inside a vacuum chamber, and their mechanical and thermal properties have been characterized and discussed based on the printing conditions. The reported study represents a preliminary investigation of the potential applicability of the 3D printing technology on highly filled polymers for extraterrestrial applications.

## 1. Introduction
Vacuum 3D printing represents an evolution of the typical additive manufacturing process. Future exploration and colonization missions will require the capabilities to produce structures directly in-orbit or on-planet. Printing directly in vacuum forgoes the requirement of having a pressurized environment and enables very large structures to be printed. Regolith-based concrete is one of the most investigated materials, but 3D printed composites based on polymeric materials present the advantage of being easily processable into complex geometries.

## 2. Materials and Methods

2. PLA filled with 40% organic fibers (pine wood fibers)
3. PLA filled with 50% inorganic clays (CaSiO3)

## 3. Results and discussion
- **Mechanical Properties:** No major differences were observed between samples printed at atmospheric pressure (1 bar) and low pressure (10^-4 bar) regarding ultimate strength and modulus.
- **Dimensional Accuracy:** Low pressure printed samples showed thinner but wider strand cross-sections. This is attributed to increased surface tension and enhanced wettability of the molten strand on the substrate in vacuum.
- **Thermal Stability:** Thermal degradation profiles (TGA) and crystallinity (DSC) remained largely consistent across pressure conditions.

## 4. Conclusions
3D printing can be effectively conducted in a vacuum chamber with appropriate thermal control (e.g., copper thermal straps). While material quality is not significantly affected by pressure, dimensional deviations must be accounted for in design to ensure accuracy.
