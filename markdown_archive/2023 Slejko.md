# Vacuum 3D printing of highly filled polymeric matrix composites

**Authors:** Emanuele Alberto Slejko, Nicholas Sesto Gorella, Advenit Makaya, Paolo Gallina, Nicola Scuor, Stefano Seriani
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 204 (2023) 25–33
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2022.12.033

## Abstract
In this contribution, we have investigated how polymeric matrix composites for aerospace applications are affected, when fabricated at low pressure, by the additive manufacturing process. Commercial filaments have been selected based on their representativeness of materials derived from lunar resources. Standard samples of thermoplastic polymers reinforced by organic fibers and inorganic fillers have been printed inside a vacuum chamber, and their mechanical and thermal properties have been characterized and discussed based on the printing conditions.

## 1. Introduction
Vacuum 3D printing represents an evolution of typical additive manufacturing for the space sector. It enables in-orbit or on-planet production of structures and mechanisms without requiring pressurized environments. The study focuses on highly filled polymers representing ISRU-based materials (using raw materials like lunar regolith or organic fibers from agricultural activities).

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1 Materials
Three PLA-based filaments were used:
- PLA (unfilled)
- PLA filled with 40% organic fibers (pine wood fibers)
- PLA filled with 50% inorganic clays (mainly CaSiO3)

### 2.2 Printer Specifications & Adaptation
- **Printer:** Monoprice Select Mini V2.
- **Modifications:** Extruder motor relocated; cooling fan replaced with copper thermal braids for conductive cooling; control board moved outside the vacuum chamber.
- **Vacuum Level:** Pressure as low as 10^-4 bar.

| Parameter | Value |
| :--- | :--- |
| Layer Height | 0.175 mm |
| Printing Temperature | 215.0 °C |
| Build Plate Temperature | 45.0 °C |
| Print Speed | 50 mm/s |

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Mechanical Properties:** No major differences were observed between atmospheric and vacuum-printed samples in terms of ultimate strength or modulus. However, standard deviation increased at 10^-4 bar due to heat accumulation.
- **Chemical/Thermal Stability:** FTIR and DSC analyses showed that the chemical composition and crystallinity were preserved regardless of the environment pressure.
- **Dimensional Accuracy:** Vacuum-printed samples showed a thinner but wider cross-section (strand). This is attributed to higher surface tension of the molten strand in vacuum, increasing substrate wettability.

## 4. Conclusions
3D printing can be effectively conducted in vacuum with appropriate thermal control (conductive cooling). While material properties remain stable, dimensional deviations (thinner/wider strands) must be accounted for in design to maintain accuracy. This study validates vacuum FDM as a viable asset for space exploration.
