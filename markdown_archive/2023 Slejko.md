# Vacuum 3D printing of highly filled polymeric matrix composites

**Authors:** Emanuele Alberto Slejko, Nicholas Sesto Gorella, Advenit Makaya, Paolo Gallina, Nicola Scuor, Stefano Seriani
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 204 (2023) 25–33
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2022.12.033

## Abstract
In this contribution, we have investigated how polymeric matrix composites for aerospace applications are affected, when fabricated at low pressure, by the additive manufacturing process. Commercial filaments have been selected based on their representativeness of materials derived from lunar resources. Standard samples of thermoplastic polymers reinforced by organic fibers and inorganic fillers have been printed inside a vacuum chamber, and their mechanical and thermal properties have been characterized and discussed based on the printing conditions.

## 1. Introduction
Vacuum 3D printing represents an evolution of typical additive manufacturing for the space sector. It enables the production of structures directly in-orbit or on-planet without requiring pressurized environments. The study focuses on In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) by testing polymers with high filler content (organic fibers and inorganic clays) representing lunar soil.

## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Materials
Three commercial filaments based on PLA were used:
1. Neat PLA
2. PLA filled with 40% organic fibers (pine wood fibers)
3. PLA filled with 50% inorganic clays (CaSiO3)

## 3. Results and discussion
Mechanical properties (ultimate strength, modulus, and deformation) showed no significant differences between atmospheric and vacuum-printed (10^-4 bar) samples. Thermal characterization via DSC and TGA confirmed that the chemical composition and crystallinity were preserved regardless of the pressure. However, dimensional analysis revealed that vacuum-printed samples were thinner and wider than atmospheric ones due to increased surface tension of the molten strand in low-pressure environments.

## 4. Conclusions
3D printing in a vacuum is feasible with minor modifications to commercial hardware (e.g., thermal braids for cooling). While material properties remain stable, the higher surface tension in vacuum causes measurable dimensional deviations that must be accounted for in design.
