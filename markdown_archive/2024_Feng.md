# A comprehensive review of lunar lava tube base construction and field research on a potential Earth test site

## Abstract
The Moon plays a pivotal role in deep space exploration. Lunar lava tubes are special underground caves considered as ideal natural shelters for lunar base construction. This paper provides an in-depth overview of geological origins, exploration history, and distribution. It evaluates advantages such as stable temperatures, radiation protection, and structural stability. Field studies on the 'Seventy-Two Caves' in Haikou, China, identify it as a promising analog site due to basalt composition similarities with Apollo missions. Challenges in geotechnical engineering, excavation, reinforcement, and simulated Earth-based testing are discussed.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar lava tubes offer unique environmental advantages: stable temperatures, low radiation levels, and large internal volumes. Recent discoveries of skylights and gravity gradients have fueled interest in these for human habitation. Inflatable modules and ISRU-based construction are being proposed for these environments.

## 2. Lunar Lava Tubes
### 2.1 Geological Origins
Lava tubes (pyroducts) form from volcanic eruptions where surface lava cools and solidifies while internal lava continues to flow, leaving a hollow conduit. Lunar gravity (1/6th of Earth) allows for much larger tubes, spanning hundreds to thousands of meters.
### 2.2 Exploration
Detection methods include imagery (SELENE, LRO), gravity measurements (GRAIL), and ground-penetrating radar (GPR).

## 3. Lunar Lava Tube Base Advantages
- **Stable Temperature:** Maintains approx. -20°C (subsurface) compared to surface fluctuations of -170°C to 110°C.
- **Radiation Protection:** Regolith layers between 1-6m thick make radiation effects negligible.
- **Protection:** Shielding from micrometeorites and lunar dust.

## 4. Habitat Concepts
- **Swarm Habitat:** 100 truncated octahedron modules (Mizuguchi and Ikeda).
- **ISRU & 3D Printing:** Conceptual plans by Harbin Institute of Technology involve scanning, reinforcement of skylights, and using lunar soil for 3D printed bricks.

## 5. Earth Analog Site: Seventy-Two Caves (Haikou)
Field surveys and lab tests on basalt samples show:
- **Density:** 2.152 to 2.680 g/cm³.
- **Compressive Strength:** 76.40 MPa (dense basalt), 63.25 MPa (porous basalt).
- **Composition:** High similarity to Apollo 14 regolith (SiO2, Al2O3, FeO, CaO).

## 6. Geotechnical Challenges
- Cave exploration robotics and 3D laser scanning.
- In-situ drilling and sampling in microgravity.
- Excavation and reinforcement technology (mechanical, melting, or laser-based).
