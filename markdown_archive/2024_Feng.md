# A comprehensive review of lunar lava tube base construction and field research on a potential Earth test site

**Authors:** Yujie Feng, Peng-Zhi Pan, Xuhai Tang, Zhaofeng Wang, Yuxin Li, Altaf Hussain

**Journal:** International Journal of Mining Science and Technology 34 (2024) 1201–1216

## Abstract
Lunar lava tubes are special underground caves formed by volcanic eruptions and are considered as ideal natural shelters for lunar base construction. This paper provides an overview of their geological origins, exploration history, and distribution. It delves into four distinctive advantages and concepts for constructing bases within these tubes, summarizing ground-based attempts and field studies on a lava tube in Hainan as an Earth analog site. Challenges in geotechnical engineering, including in-situ testing, geomechanical properties, and excavation techniques, are discussed.

## 1. Introduction
The Moon serves as a primary site for resource exploration and deep space exploration. Lunar lava tubes offer unique environmental advantages: stable temperature, low radiation, and large internal volumes. They are considered optimal locations for human construction of research bases.

## 2. Lunar Lava Tubes
### 2.1 Geological Origins
Lava tubes (pyroducts) form during volcanic eruptions as subterranean cavities created by the flow and solidification of lava. On the Moon, gravity (1/6th of Earth) allows for much larger tubes, spanning hundreds to thousands of meters in width.

### 2.3 Distribution Locations
Candidate locations include:
- **Marius Hills Region:** Minimum width 370m; depth 200-300m.
- **Rima Sharp:** Width 2km; length 75km; depth 600m.
- **Aristarchus Plateau:** Width 3.75km; length 60km.

## 3. Lunar Lava Tube Base
### 3.1 Advantages
1. **Stable Temperature:** While surface temperatures range from -170°C to 110°C, internal tube environments are estimated to maintain a constant -20°C to 17°C.
2. **Radiation Protection:** Regolith thicknesses between 1-6m make radiation effects negligible; lava tube roofs are significantly thicker.
3. **Dust & Meteorite Shielding:** Shielded from solar wind and micrometeorite impacts.

### 3.2 Conceptual Designs
- **'Green' Habitat (Grandl):** Inflatable modules inside the Mare Tranquillitatis Pit for 100 residents.
- **Swarm Habitat (Mizuguchi & Ikeda):** 100 truncated octahedron modules designed for irregular terrain.
- **3D Printed Base (Harbin Institute of Technology):** A 5-step plan using radar exploration, drilling projectiles for entrance enlargement, and 3D printing robots utilizing lunar soil mixtures to create skeletal structures and living chambers.

## 4. Analog Sites
The paper identifies the "Seventy-Two Caves" in Hainan, China, as a high-fidelity analog. Laboratory tests on the basalt showed compositions highly similar to Apollo 14 samples, with an average compressive strength of 76.40 MPa for dense basalt.

## 5. Geotechnical Challenges
- **Exploration:** Localization in pitch-black caves.
- **In-situ Testing:** Drilling and sampling under microgravity and extreme vacuum.
- **Structural Stability:** 3D scanning and modeling for assessment.
- **Reinforcement:** Developing cave lining materials from lunar regolith.

## 6. Conclusions
Lunar lava tubes represent a viable and advantageous solution for long-term human presence. Interdisciplinary cooperation between geologists, mining engineers, and architects is essential to overcome technical hurdles in excavation and structural stabilization.
