# A comprehensive review of lunar lava tube base construction and field research on a potential Earth test site

## Abstract
Lunar lava tubes are special underground caves formed by volcanic eruptions and are considered as ideal natural shelters and scientific laboratories for lunar base construction. This paper provides an in-depth overview of geological origins, exploration history, and distribution. It delves into the advantages and typical concepts for constructing bases within lava tubes and summarizes ground-based attempts. Field studies on a lava tube in Hainan revealed rock compositions similar to Apollo missions. Challenges in geotechnical engineering regarding exploration, in-situ testing, and stability assessment are discussed.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar lava tubes are naturally occurring underground spaces on the Moon. They offer unique environmental advantages such as stable temperature, low radiation levels, and large internal volumes. This study aims to present latest advancements in lava tube base establishment, formation mechanisms, and conceptual frameworks.

## 2. Lunar Lava Tubes
### 2.1. Geological Origins
Lava tubes (pyroducts) form from volcanic eruptions where flowing lava solidifies into a sturdy, insulating shell. Because of the Moon's 1/6 gravity, lunar lava tubes can be significantly larger than those on Earth, spanning hundreds to thousands of meters in width.

### 2.2. Exploration History
*   **Apollo Era:** Skepticism due to lack of low-altitude images.
*   **SELENE (2009):** Identified the Marius Hills Hole (MHH) with a 10 m resolution.
*   **LRO:** Identified over 300 pits, many potentially serving as skylights.
*   **GRAIL (2013):** Used gravity measurements to identify massive subsurface cavities.

## 3. Lunar Lava Tube Base Advantages
### 3.1. Stable Temperature Environment
Surface temperatures range from -170°C to 110°C. Inside lava tubes, temperatures remain relatively stable, consistently hovering around -20°C. At a depth of 1 m, regolith exhibits exceptionally low thermal conductivity.

### 3.2. Radiation Protection
Regolith thickness between 1 and 6 m makes the detrimental effects of Solar Particle Events (SPEs) and Galactic Cosmic Rays (GCRs) negligible.

### 3.3. Protection from Dust and Meteorites
Lava tubes are shielded from the solar wind, meaning they contain very low levels of charged lunar dust and provide robust protection against micrometeorite impacts.

## 4. Analog Sites and Field Research
### 4.1. Terrestrial Attempts
*   **CHILL-ICE (Iceland):** Tested inflatable tubular habitats.
*   **Wulong (China):** Proposed karst caves as simulation platforms.
*   **Hainan (China):** The "Seventy-Two Caves" site in Haikou features basalt with high similarity to Apollo 14 samples.

### 4.2. Geotechnical Findings (Hainan Site)
*   **Density:** 2.152 to 2.680 g/cm³.
*   **Compressive Strength:** 76.40 MPa (dense basalt) vs 63.25 MPa (porous basalt).
*   **Composition:** Primarily SiO2, Al2O3, FeO, and CaO, matching lunar regolith proportions.

## 5. Challenges for Geotechnical Engineering
*   **Exploration:** Need for rovers with 3D laser scanners and microseismic sensors.
*   **In-situ Testing:** Drilling and sampling under microgravity and extreme vacuum.
*   **Material Properties:** Understanding rock degradation and high friction angles in lunar environments.
*   **Construction:** Excavation and reinforcement of skylight entrances.
