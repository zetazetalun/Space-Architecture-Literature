# A comprehensive review of lunar lava tube base construction and field research on a potential Earth test site

## Abstract
Lunar lava tubes are underground caves formed by volcanic eruptions, considered ideal natural shelters for lunar base construction. This paper reviews their geological origins, exploration history, and distribution. It highlights advantages like stable temperature, radiation protection, and large volume. Field studies on a lava tube in Hainan (Haikou) revealed rock compositions similar to Apollo missions, suggesting it as a promising analog site. Challenges in geotechnical engineering, in-situ testing, and structural stability assessment are discussed.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar lava tubes offer unique environmental advantages: stable temperature, low radiation, and protection from meteorites. Exploration has entered a 'golden age' with discoveries of skylights and gravity gradient features.

## 2. Lunar Lava Tubes
### 2.1. Geological Origins
Formed by volcanic eruptions; thermal and mechanical erosion widen these 'pyroducts'. Moon's 1/6 gravity allows for significantly larger tubes (hundreds to thousands of meters wide) compared to Earth (meters to tens of meters).

### 2.2. Exploration History
*   **Apollo Era:** Ronald Greeley utilized remote sensing.
*   **SELENE (2009):** First high-definition images of lunar pits (MHH - Marius Hills Hole).
*   **GRAIL Mission (2013):** Gravity measurements identified mass deficits indicating large cavities.
*   **Chang'e-3:** GPR detected buried underground cavities.

## 3. Lunar Lava Tube Base Advantages
| Feature | Lunar Surface | Lava Tube Internal |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Temperature | -170°C to 110°C | -20°C to -30°C (relatively stable) |
| Radiation | High (GCRs and SPEs) | Negligible (shielded by meters of basalt) |
| Micrometeorites | Constant threat | High protection |
| Dust | Pervasive/clogging | Minimal movement |

### 3.2. Base Concepts
*   **Swarm Habitat:** 100 truncated octahedron modules (Mizuguchi & Ikeda).
*   **Harbin Institute Concept:** Integrated 3D scanning, drilling, and reinforcement. Includes 3D printing robots using lunar soil to create bricks.

## 4. Analog Sites: Seventy-Two Caves (Haikou, China)
*   **Length:** Over 780 m.
*   **Basalt Composition:** Highly similar to Apollo 14 regolith samples (rich in SiO2, Al2O3, FeO, and CaO).
*   **Mechanical Properties:** Dense basalt compressive strength ~76.40 MPa; Porous basalt ~63.25 MPa.

## 5. Geotechnical Challenges
1.  **Exploration:** Precision rovers and 3D laser scanners in pitch-black environments.
2.  **In-situ Testing:** Drilling and sampling under microgravity and vacuum.
3.  **Stability:** assessing the structural integrity of thin-roofed tubes under lunar quakes.
4.  **Excavation:** Reinforcement of skylight entrances.
