# A comprehensive review of lunar lava tube base construction and field research on a potential Earth test site

## Abstract
The Moon plays a pivotal role in deep space exploration. Lunar lava tubes, special underground caves formed by volcanic eruptions, are considered ideal natural shelters. This paper reviews geological origins, exploration history, and distribution of lunar lava tubes. It presents four advantages and conceptual frameworks for base construction, summarizes ground-based attempts, and introduces a field study on a lava tube in Hainan as a promising analog site. Challenges in geotechnical engineering, such as in-situ testing and excavation/reinforcement techniques, are discussed.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar lava tubes offer unique environmental advantages: stable temperature, low radiation, and large internal volumes. They are considered optimal locations for human research bases. Recent proposals include inflatable habitats and ISRU-based construction adapted for these subterranean environments.

## 2. Lunar Lava Tubes
### 2.1. Geological origins
Lava tubes (pyroducts) form during volcanic eruptions as lava flows solidify on the exterior to create an insulating shell while the interior remains fluid. On the Moon, lower gravity (1/6g) allows for much larger tubes (hundreds to thousands of meters in diameter) compared to Earth.

### 2.2. Exploration history and evidence
*   **Sinuous rilles and skylights:** Indicators of subterranean voids.
*   **SELENE/Kaguya (2009):** Discovered the Marius Hills Hole (MHH), a ~65m diameter skylight.
*   **GRAIL (2013):** Gravity data identified mass deficits indicating large cavities (e.g., 4km wide tube in Rima Sharp).
*   **Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR):** Detected subsurface cavities, including a 3.1m deep void at the Chang’e-3 landing site.

## 3. Lunar Lava Tube Base
### 3.1. Advantages
*   **Thermal Stability:** While surface temperatures fluctuate by ~300°C, internal tube temperatures remain constant (estimated at -20°C or 17°C depending on location).
*   **Radiation Protection:** 1-6m of regolith shielding makes GCR/SPE effects negligible.
*   **Dust/Meteorite Shielding:** Tubes protect against micrometeorite impacts and minimal lunar dust movement compared to the surface.

### 3.2. Base Design Concepts
*   **"Green" Habitat (Grandl):** Inflatable modules inside the Mare Tranquillitatis Pit.
*   **"Swarm Habitat" (Mizuguchi & Ikeda):** 100 truncated octahedron modules designed for irregular terrain.
*   **Harbin Institute of Technology (2023):** A multi-step plan involving robotic 3D scanning, drilling projectiles to enlarge entrances, and using lunar soil bricks for 3D-printed internal structures.

## 4. Analog Sites
*   **CHILL-ICE (Iceland):** Tested inflatable tubular habitats.
*   **Wulong Karst (China):** Proposed as a simulation site for laboratory structures.
*   **Hainan "Seventy-Two Caves" (China):** Field study revealed basalt compositions similar to Apollo 14 samples (SiO2, Al2O3, FeO, CaO). Dense basalt compressive strength averaged 76.40 MPa.

## 5. Geotechnical Engineering Challenges
1.  **Exploration Devices:** Development of autonomous rovers for pitch-black, GPS-denied environments.
2.  **In-situ Testing:** Need for deep drilling and sampling under microgravity/vacuum.
3.  **Material Properties:** Understanding rock degradation under extreme thermal shocks.
4.  **Stability Assessment:** Modeling of roof thickness vs. span (roofs of 500m can support spans up to 5km).
5.  **Excavation & Reinforcement:** Techniques for skylight enlargement and wall lining using ISRU materials.
