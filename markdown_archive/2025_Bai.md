# Effect of Low-Temperature Fluctuations on Martian Steel Structures: Numerical and Analytical Studies

**Authors:** Yongtao Bai, Jing Li, and Xuhong Zhou  
**Publisher:** American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE)  
**Journal:** Journal of Cold Regions Engineering, 2025, 39(2): 04025002  
**DOI:** 10.1061/JCRGEI.CRENG-875

## Abstract
To harness a variety of unique resources, humanity is progressively venturing deeper into space. This endeavor necessitates a comprehensive investigation into the formidable challenges presented by hostile environments, particularly the impact of extreme low-temperature fluctuations on extraterrestrial engineering and construction. This paper employs numerical simulation methodologies to scrutinize the displacement variations and stress distribution within a full-scale circular arch steel structure subjected to diverse thermal cycles, thereby assessing the influence of severe cryogenic temperature fluctuations on Martian steel infrastructure.

## Introduction
Mars is considered the most viable candidate for human migration. Selecting appropriate metal materials for extraterrestrial structures is critical. Steel structures are proposed due to low cost, easy processing, and excellent low-temperature resistance. Most importantly, the existence of easily available iron and nickel in a reduced, elemental form on the surface of Mars supports in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) for construction.

## Results and Discussion
- **Static vs. Thermal Loads:** Without temperature cycles, max apex displacement was 2.704 mm. Under Martian thermal cycles (+20°C to -110°C), displacement significantly increased.
- **Fatigue and Stabilization:** Maximum displacement reached a peak of 13.1124 mm at 1,374 cycles and then stabilized around 12.6 mm in subsequent cycles (up to 6,870 cycles/10 Martian years).
- **Stress Distribution:** Under static pressure, stress concentrated at the arch zenith. Under thermal fluctuations, stress concentration shifted to the connection between the floor and the arch member.

## Conclusion
The 50-mm-thick steel structure is capable of surviving 1-10 Martian years. Recommendations include thickening the floor member and refining the dome-to-floor interface to mitigate stress concentrations induced by thermal expansion and contraction.
