# Effect of Low-Temperature Fluctuations on Martian Steel Structures: Numerical and Analytical Studies

## Authors
Yongtao Bai, Jing Li, and Xuhong Zhou (Chongqing University, China)

## Abstract
This paper employs numerical simulation methodologies to scrutinize the displacement variations and stress distribution within a full-scale circular arch steel structure subjected to diverse thermal cycles on Mars. The findings contribute to understanding the resilience of such structures within fluctuating low-temperature conditions (-110°C to +20°C) and provide a foundation for architectural design and material selection for a Martian base.

## Introduction and Background
Mars is considered a primary candidate for human migration. The study highlights that metals, specifically steel, are viable construction materials due to recyclability and the availability of elemental iron and nickel on the Martian surface (ISRU). Previous studies have focused on lunar environments; this research specifically addresses the 24-hour Martian temperature cycle and its impact on structural fatigue.

## Martian Environment and Loading Conditions
- **Gravity:** 3.73 m/s².
- **Atmosphere:** Tenuous (1% of Earth's), leading to extreme diurnal temperature shifts.
- **Temperature Cycle:** Max +20°C, Min -110°C over a 24-hour cycle (16h cooling, 8h warming).
- **Internal Pressure:** 101.325 kPa (1 atm) for crew health.
- **External Pressure:** 18.687 kPa (from a 3m regolith shielding layer).

## Structural Design
The study analyzes a 50mm-thick steel semicircular arch with a 5m radius and a 10m longitudinal span. This design meets NASA specifications for a four-person crew (93 m²).

## Key Results
- **Durability:** The structure maintained integrity without cracking or failure over a simulated 10-year Martian lifespan (6,870 cycles).
- **Stress Distribution:** Under simple pressure, stress was localized on the arch. After thermal cycling, stress concentration shifted to the juncture between the floor member and the arch member.
- **Displacement:** The apex displacement recorded was 2.704 mm. Displacement followed a cyclical pattern rather than a monotonic increase, suggesting the structure stabilizes after initial fatigue.

## Conclusions
1. A 50-mm-thick steel circular arch is capable of surviving the Martian thermal environment.
2. Design recommendations: Thicken floor members and optimize joints between the arch and floor to mitigate stress concentrations induced by thermal cycles.
