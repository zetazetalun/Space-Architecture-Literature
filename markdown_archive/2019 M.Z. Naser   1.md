# Extraterrestrial construction materials

**Author:** M.Z. Naser  
**Journal:** Progress in Materials Science 105 (2019) 100577  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pmatsci.2019.100577

## Abstract
NASA and ESA plan to establish permanent human presence on the Moon and Mars by 2040. To promote sustainable exploration, habitats are envisioned to be built from in-situ resources. This review explores the suitability of materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, concrete derivatives, space-native metals, and composites, along with processing techniques suitable for the alien environment (vacuum, low gravity).

## 2. In-Situ Resources
- **Regolith:** Covers the surface; contains silica, alumina, calcium oxide, and iron oxide. Potentially usable for concrete and sintered blocks.
- **Metals:** Magnesium, Aluminum, Iron, and Titanium can be extracted from regolith and rocks through carbothermal reduction or electrolysis.
- **Near-Earth Objects (NEOs):** Asteroids containing metals (Ni, Fe, Co) and water ice.

## 3. Properties of Extraterrestrial Construction Materials

### 3.1 Regolith Derivatives
- **Cast and Sintered Basalt:** High compressive strength (up to 490 MPa) and superior mechanical properties compared to standard concrete.
- **Sulfur Concrete:** Waterless; gains strength quickly (12-75 MPa). However, it is susceptible to sublimation in vacuum and degradation under thermal cycles.
- **Polymer Concrete:** Uses thermoplastic/thermosetting polymers as binders for regolith (e.g., polyethylene, epoxy). Strengths vary (5.7 to 129 MPa).
- **Geopolymer Concrete:** Formed via alkali-activation of aluminosilicates. Low water consumption and high thermal/vacuum stability.

### 3.2 Metals and Alloys
- **Magnesium:** Lightest construction metal; favorable for casting and shielding.
- **Aluminum:** High strength-to-weight ratio, especially at low temperatures.
- **Titanium:** Excellent structural properties, 40% lighter than steel.

### 3.3 Advanced Materials
- **Shape Memory Materials (SMMs):** Potential for self-deployable mechanisms and joining structural members.
- **Bio-inspired Materials:** Utilizing biological organisms (e.g., spider silk, protein-bound regolith) for construction.
- **Ice-based Construction:** Proposed for Martian habitats (e.g., NASA Ice House) due to shielding properties.

## 4. Processing Techniques
- **Melting and Sintering:** Using solar, microwave, or laser energy to fuse regolith grains. Sintering occurs at 50-70% of melting point.
- **Combustion Synthesis (SHS):** Uses exothermic reactions (e.g., thermite) to produce ceramics/composites with minimal energy input.
- **Dry-Mix/Steam-Injection (DMSI):** Exposing dry-mixed cement/regolith to steam to induce hydration while minimizing water loss.
- **Cold-pressing:** Utilizing adhesive forces and trace moisture/binders to press regolith into blocks (duricretes).

## 5. Summary and Conclusions
- Class III habitats (ISRU-based) are critical for sustainable presence.
- Regolith and its derivatives (sintered basalt, geopolymer) show the highest potential.
- Processing in extreme environments (vacuum, low gravity) remains a major technical challenge.
