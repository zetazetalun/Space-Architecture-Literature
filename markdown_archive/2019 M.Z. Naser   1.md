**Author:** M.Z. Naser

## Abstract
In recognition of the 50th anniversary of the first manned lunar landing, NASA and ESA revealed plans to establish permanent human presence on the Moon and Mars by 2040. This review explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith, concrete derivatives, space-native metals and composites, and advanced materials. It identifies processing techniques suitable for vacuum and low-gravity environments and highlights future directions like AI integration and 3D/4D printing.

## 2. In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)
ISRU is defined as operations that collect and process indigenous resources to minimize Earth-dependency. The concept includes building outposts and expanding into In-Situ Repair, Fabrication, and Expansion (ISRFE).

### 2.1 Regolith
Regolith (diameter < 10 mm) is the primary building material. 
- **Moon:** Rich in SiO2 (45-46%), Al2O3 (9-24%), and CaO (8-12%).
- **Mars:** Rich in SiO2 (43-55%) and Al2O3 (7-12%), with higher Fe content.

### 2.2 Metals
Metals like Magnesium, Aluminum, Iron, and Titanium can be extracted from regolith via carbothermal reduction, electrolysis, or fluoridation.

## 3. Properties of Extraterrestrial Construction Materials
### 3.1 Regolith Derivatives
- **Cast Basalt:** Produced by melting and sintering. High compressive strength (300-550 MPa for lunar samples).
- **Glass:** Lunar regolith contains 25-30% agglutinates. Glass fibers and rebars can be manufactured for structural reinforcement.

### 3.2 Concrete-like Products
- **Ordinary Concrete:** Requires water, which is scarce. Lunar concrete stiffness is ~21.4 GPa.
- **Sulfur Concrete:** Waterless; sulfur melts at 119°C and binds regolith. Compressive strength ranges from 12-75 MPa.
- **Geopolymer Concrete:** Near-zero water consumption. Lunar geopolymer can reach 37.6 MPa compressive strength.
- **Polymer Concrete:** Uses thermoplastic (e.g., polyethylene) or thermosetting binders.

### 3.3 Metals and Alloys
- **Magnesium:** Lightest, good strength-to-weight ratio (114.7), and excellent radiation shielding properties.
- **Aluminum:** High strength at low temperatures, does not sublime easily.

## 4. Processing Techniques
1.  **Melting and Sintering:** Utilizing solar concentrators, microwaves, or lasers to fuse regolith. Vacuum sintering requires ~100K lower temperature than atmospheric sintering.
2.  **Combustion Synthesis (SHS):** Exploits exothermic reactions to produce stable ceramic-composite materials.
3.  **Dry-Mix/Steam-Injection (DMSI):** Uses steam to hydrate cement-regolith mixes in vacuum/low-pressure environments.
4.  **Additive Manufacturing (3D Printing):** Contour Crafting and D-Shape technologies are being adapted for regolith-based inks and laser sintering.

## 5. Future Directions
- **Artificial Intelligence:** Identifying critical parameters for structural health monitoring and material behavior modeling.
- **Robo-scientists:** Autonomous systems for survey, collection, and autonomous processing.
- **4D Printing:** Active structures that change shape/properties according to the environment.
