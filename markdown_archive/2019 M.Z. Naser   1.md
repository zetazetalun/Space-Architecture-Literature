# Extraterrestrial Construction Materials

**Journal:** Progress in Materials Science 105 (2019) 100577
**Author:** M.Z. Naser
**Affiliation:** Glenn Department of Civil Engineering, Clemson University, Clemson, SC 29634, USA

## Abstract
In recognition of the 50th anniversary of the first manned lunar landing, NASA and ESA revealed plans to establish a permanent human presence on the Moon and Mars by 2040. To promote feasible and sustainable space exploration, these habitats are envisioned to be built from lunar and Martian in-situ resources. This review provides a comprehensive assessment of the physical features and property characteristics of extraterrestrial construction materials, including those exploited from the Moon and Mars, mined from near-earth objects (NEOs), or cultured through modern technologies. It explores regolith derivatives, concrete derivatives, space-native metals, composites, and non-traditional materials, alongside processing techniques suitable for vacuum and low-gravity environments.

## 1. Introduction and Background
The document outlines the history of space exploration from the 1940s to the current era of commercial space flight (SpaceX, Blue Origin). It defines In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) as operations that minimize Earth-dependency by collecting and processing indigenous resources. The text emphasizes that ISRU can reduce exploration costs by factors of 10 to 50.

## 3. Extraterrestrial Construction Materials
### 3.1 Regolith Derivatives
*   **Regolith:** Finer fractions (<10mm) of unconsolidated surface material. High silica (SiO₂) and alumina (Al₂O₃) content.
*   **Cast and Sintered Basalt:** Melting or sintering regolith produces black, amorphous solids with high compressive strength (300–550 MPa).

### 3.2 Concrete Derivatives
*   **Ordinary Concrete:** Requires water, which is a scarce resource in space. Investigated for vacuum performance and low-gravity segregation.
*   **Polymer Concrete:** Replaces water/cement with thermoplastic or thermosetting polymers (e.g., polyethylene). Achieves strength of 17–129 MPa.
*   **Sulfur Concrete:** Molten sulfur (120–150 °C) used to bind regolith. Water-free and rapidly gains strength. Compressive strengths reach 12–75 MPa.
*   **Geopolymer Concrete:** Produced via aluminosilicate materials mixed with alkaline solutions. High resistance to vacuum and freezing.

### 3.3 Metals and Alloys
Four primary metals extractable from regolith: **Magnesium, Aluminum, Iron, and Titanium**. 
*   Magnesium is highlighted for its light weight and ease of casting.
*   Aluminum is noted for high strength and resistance to sublimation.
*   Titanium is prized for its high yield strength and low density.

### 3.4 Composites
Focus on graphite-epoxy (Gr/E) and metal matrix composites (MMC). Composites offer high strength-to-weight ratios and dimensional stability.

### 3.5 Advanced Materials
*   **Shape Memory Materials (SMMs):** Alloys and polymers for self-deployable mechanisms.
*   **Biotechnology:** Bio-based materials such as spider silk produced by engineered microbes.
*   **Regolith Bio-Composite (RBC):** Integrating blood proteins (BSA) with regolith.

## 4. Processing Techniques
*   **Melting and Sintering:** Solar, microwave, and laser sintering are evaluated for local resource consolidation.
*   **Combustion Synthesis (SHS):** Self-sustaining exothermic reactions for material fabrication.
*   **Additive Manufacturing:** 3D printing using regolith-based inks (e.g., with PLGA) or laser sintering (LENS).

## 5. Summary and Conclusions
1. Space environments impose severe conditions requiring resilient materials.
2. Moon and Mars contain abundant resources for minimal human-interaction processing.
3. Regolith-derived products have the highest potential for efficient production.
4. Processing remains energy-extensive and complex under extreme conditions.
