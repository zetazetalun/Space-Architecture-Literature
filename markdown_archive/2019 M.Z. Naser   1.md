# Extraterrestrial construction materials

**M.Z. Naser**
*Glenn Department of Civil Engineering, Clemson University, Clemson, SC 29634, USA*

## Abstract
In recognition of the 50th anniversary of the first manned lunar landing, NASA and ESA revealed plans to resume manned exploration missions and to establish permanent human presence in outposts (habitats) on the Moon and Mars by 2040. In order to promote feasible and sustainable space exploration, these habitats are envisioned to be built from lunar and Martian in-situ resources. This review explores the suitability of construction materials derived from lunar and Martian regolith along with concrete derivatives, space-native metals and composites, as well as advanced and non-traditional materials for interplanetary construction. Processing techniques suitable to produce non-terrestrial construction materials in space environments (i.e. vacuum, low gravity, etc.) are identified.

## 1. Introduction and History
Space exploration has transitioned from short-term manned orbital missions to extended crew landings and on-site stays. Milestone events include the Apollo missions (1969-1972) and the current International Space Station (ISS). Future goals focus on Moon and Mars colonization, where In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) is critical to minimize Earth-dependency and reduce costs by 10-50 times.

## 2. Space Environment and Resources
### 2.1. Space Environment
The Moon and Mars present hostile environments characterized by low gravity (16.7% and 38% of Earth's gravity, respectively), hard vacuum, extreme radiation, and thermal fluctuations.

### 2.3. Lunar and Martian in-situ resources
Regolith is the most abundant resource. Apollo samples (382 kg) revealed high amounts of silica (SiO₂) and alumina (Al₂O₃). These constituents are essential for producing cementitious materials.

## 3. Properties of extraterrestrial construction materials

### 3.1. Regolith derivatives
*   **Regolith:** Loose material with density varying from 1500 kg/m³ at the surface to 1660 kg/m³ at 60 m depth.
*   **Cast and sintered basalt:** Basalt can be melted (~1300 °C) to produce cast basalt or sintered (1000–1200 °C) into bricks. Sintered basalt strength increases significantly with temperature (e.g., from 3.4 MPa at 1000°C to 14.47 MPa at 1100°C).

### 3.3. Concrete and concrete-like products
*   **Ordinary Concrete:** Requires water, which is scarce. Lunar concrete can achieve strengths of 14.6–75.5 MPa.
*   **Polymer Concrete:** Uses thermoplastic/thermosetting polymers as binders. Achieves 70–75% of strength after one day. Lee et al. (2015) achieved 12.75 MPa in vacuum.
*   **Sulfur Concrete:** Molten sulfur (120–150 °C) mixed with regolith. Strength reaches 12-75 MPa. Does not require water but is sensitive to sublimation in vacuum.
*   **Geopolymer Concrete:** Non-hydraulic concrete dependent on Si:Al ratio. Lunar geopolymer can reach 2–37.6 MPa.

### 3.4. Metals and alloys
Magnesium, aluminum, iron, and titanium are primary candidates. Aluminum is valued for its high strength-to-weight ratio and resistance to sublimation.

## 4. Processing of extraterrestrial construction materials
*   **Melting and Sintering:** Consolidation through heat. Methods include solar, microwave, and laser sintering.
*   **Combustion Synthesis (SHS):** Exploits exothermic reactions (e.g., FeTiO₃ + Al + C) to produce ceramic-composites with strengths of 18 ± 3.7 MPa.
*   **Dry-Mix/Steam-Injection (DMSI):** Efficient concrete production using steam to initiate hydration, significantly reducing water requirements.

## 6. Summary and conclusions
Sustainable lunar and Martian settlements require utilizing indigenous resources. Regolith derivatives and concrete-like products show high potential. Processing remains energy-extensive, and future research must focus on autonomous processing and high-fidelity simulants.
