# Lunar base infrastructure construction: Challenges and future directions

**Authors:** Yifeng Xia, Yuyue Gao, Wenbin Han, Xinyi Li, Cheng Zhou, Yan Zhou, Lieyun Ding
**Journal:** Automation in Construction 176 (2025) 106251
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.autcon.2025.106251

## Abstract
As deep space exploration advances, agencies such as NASA and ESA, along with other nations, have developed mid-to-long-term plans for lunar habitation. This article reviews existing lunar construction concepts and designs, analyzing their importance for sustained human presence on the moon. It summarizes research progress in transportation, navigation and communication, energy, and habitation infrastructure, addressing construction processes, site selection, building materials, and structural design. Additionally, the article highlights the challenges and future directions in lunar infrastructure construction.

## 1. Introduction
The Moon serves as a focal point for global space initiatives, acting as a testbed for Mars exploration technologies. Major programs include the US Artemis Program, the European Terrae Novae 2030+, and China's International Lunar Research Station (ILRS). Constructing infrastructure in the extreme lunar environment requires overcoming bottlenecks in materials science, manufacturing, and intelligent equipment.

## 2. Transportation Infrastructure
Transportation costs between Earth and the Moon remain high. Research focuses on:
- **Landing and Launching Pads:** Specialized structures to reduce dust ejection and support lander weight. Methods include robotic teleoperation and in-situ manufacturing (e.g., vacuum sintering of regolith bricks).
- **Roadbed and Pavement:** Necessary for stable traffic. Techniques include microwave sintering, solar sintering, and laser melting of regolith to create solid surfaces.
- **Elevators:** Long-term concepts like the 'Galactic Harbor' using high-strength cable materials (carbon nanotubes/graphene).

## 3. Navigation and Communication Infrastructure
Future exploration relies on remotely operated robots, necessitating robust CPNT (Communications, Positioning, and Navigation Technology). NASA's LunaNet is a key framework for an expandable lunar network.

## 4. Energy Infrastructure
- **Solar Power Stations:** Utilizing high-sunlight areas at the poles. Projects like TLT and VSAT utilize In-Space Assembly (iSA).
- **Nuclear Power Stations:** Fission surface power (40 kW) provides continuous energy regardless of sunlight.
- **Energy Storage:** Utilizing processed regolith for thermal storage or regenerative fuel cells.

## 5. Habitation Infrastructure
- **Launched Habitation:** Prefabricated rigid, expandable, or inflatable structures (e.g., NASA TransHab).
- **In-situ Habitation:** Utilizing 3D printing (e.g., NASA's Lunar Lantern, HUST's Lunar Ampullae) and masonry assembly (e.g., HUST's 'Xuan Wu' plan using sintered bricks).
- **Lava-tube based Habitation:** Subterranean caves providing natural shielding from radiation, thermal cycles, and micrometeoroids.

## 6. Challenges and Future Directions
1. **Material Fabrication:** Difficulty in producing large components from regolith under extreme conditions.
2. **Transportation:** Lack of regular Earth-Moon cargo/crew transfer.
3. **Power Scaling:** Need for industrial-scale energy beyond 40 kW.
4. **CNPT Availability:** Lack of high-precision positioning and stable communication.
5. **Long-term Habitability:** Ensuring life support and radiation protection for extended durations.
