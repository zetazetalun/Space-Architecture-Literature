# Design and Analysis of 3D-Printable Thin-Shell Dome Structures for Extraterrestrial Habitation

**Authors:** Matthew Troemner, Elham Ramyar, Benton Johnson, and Gianluca Cusatis  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## Abstract
Extreme environmental conditions and novel construction techniques dictate the form of Martian habitats. This paper presents the design and analysis of a dome-shaped Martian habitat developed for NASA’s 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The structure utilizes a composite hemispheric-parabolic dome optimized for Martian loads and 3D-printed using 'Marscrete' (Martian regolith and sulfur) on an inflatable pressure vessel. Structural performance is assessed under wind, gravity, regolith deposition, and meteorite impact.

## Introduction
Martian missions require semi-permanent structures built with in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and automated fabrication due to the difficulty of transporting materials from Earth. NASA's 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge (launched 2015) sought designs for four astronauts for a one-year mission, requiring 93 square meters of living space and all necessary life support systems.

## Design Basis
Drivers included constructability (minimizing overhangs/supports), usability, and functionality. The final design is an internal hemispherical dome with an outer parabolic dome shell. This form ensures that external loads result primarily in compressive stresses, which is ideal for the quasi-brittle nature of Marscrete. The shell thickness varies from 21 to 108 cm.

## Construction Method
1. Site preparation by autonomous rovers.
2. 3D-printing of the foundation via an articulated arm.
3. Deployment and inflation of a collapsible pressure vessel as a backing for printing.
4. Printing of the main shell using a small 'crawler' robot that circumnavigates the pressure vessel.
5. Deflation of the pressure vessel to allow for independent thermal deformation.

## Environment and Loading
- **Gravity:** 3.71 m/s² (38% of Earth).
- **Wind Load:** Calculated at 9.79 Pa (conservative estimate of 70 mph), increased to 60.0 Pa for dust particles.
- **Dust Accumulation:** A static pressure load of 2,780 Pa (0.5m sand depth) applied to half the structure.
- **Meteorite Impact:** Critical load analysis using Lattice Discrete Particle Model (LDPM).

## Load Analysis
Finite element static analysis showed structural response remained in the elastic region under all load combinations, with deformations less than 1.0 mm.

## Meteorite Impact Performance
Simulations determined a critical impact load for a 7,390g spherical meteorite at 6.29 km/sec. The probability of such an impact during a one-year mission was calculated at approximately 6.9 x 10⁻⁹.

## Proposed Layout
Inspiration from 'tiny house' designs and NASA HI-SEAS. Features include a central common area with a transparent oculus, public zones (kitchen, workspace, lab), and private zones (bedrooms, lavatory).

## Conclusions
The design surpasses hurdles for Martian habitation, utilizing a unique construction scheme that eliminates the need for printed supports and performs well under typical Martian loads and meteorite impacts up to 7 kg.
