# Design and Analysis of 3D-Printable Thin-Shell Dome Structures for Extraterrestrial Habitation

**Authors:** Matthew Troemner, Elham Ramyar, Benton Johnson, and Gianluca Cusatis  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, pp. 1335-1341  
**Publisher:** © ASCE

## ABSTRACT

Extreme environmental conditions, unusual loadings, and most importantly, the availability of novel construction techniques will likely dictate the form of any extraterrestrial habitat built on Mars. While a habitat could be constructed by astronauts, it is highly preferred for such a structure to already exist when the first humans land on the Martian surface. Thus, automated structure fabrication equipped with 3D-printing technologies that use in situ resources is an intriguing approach to consider. This paper presents an overview of the design and analysis of a dome-shaped Martian habitat that was designed at Northwestern University in collaboration with Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM) as part of NASA’s 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The structure has a novel composite hemispheric-parabolic dome that is optimized to sustain self-weight and environmental loads, and to be 3D-printed on an inflatable pressure vessel with Marscrete, a Martian concrete manufactured primarily with local Martian regolith and sulfur. This study examines the structural performance of such a habitat under expected Martian loading conditions, including wind, regolith deposition from storms, and gravity. Furthermore, the habitat performance is assessed under meteorite impact of varying masses and velocities. Finally, a construction scheme, potential internal layout, and functional usability of spaces are also envisioned for a four people unit.

## INTRODUCTION

Future missions to Mars will require substantial habitats for their extraterrestrial explorers. Martian missions will carry a prolonged timeline and thus require significant semi-permanent structures. Transit for materials across 34 million miles is cumbersome, making it desirable to design structures utilizing locally-sourced, *in situ* materials and automated fabrication techniques. 

NASA's 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge (launched in 2015) required habitats to:
* Sustain four astronauts for one Earth-year.
* Provide 93 square meters of living space.
* Include all necessary mission spaces (kitchen, sanitation, bedrooms, workspaces, etc.).
* Include MEP and ECLSS equipment.

## DESIGN BASIS

The habitat form was driven by constructability, usability, and functionality. To facilitate 3D-printing, overhanging regions were minimized to reduce the need for supports. An internal hemispherical dome with an outer parabolic dome structure was selected. The internal features match an underlying hemispherical pressure vessel, which serves as both atmospheric control and construction support. The exterior parabolic form ensures that most external loading results in compressive rather than tensile stresses, which is ideal for the quasi-brittle Marscrete material.

## CONSTRUCTION METHOD

1. **Site Preparation:** Autonomous rovers clear debris and prepare a shallow cut for the foundation.
2. **Foundation:** 3D-printed using an articulated arm-type printer.
3. **Deployment:** A collapsible dome pressure vessel is deployed and inflated.
4. **3D-Printing:** The pressure vessel acts as a backer. An articulating arm reaches the apex, where a small crawler robot is deployed to circumnavigate the vessel for printing Marscrete from bottom to top.
5. **Finishing:** An oculus is affixed at the peak. The pressure vessel is slightly deflated to provide a thermal offset from the Marscrete shell.

## ENVIRONMENT AND LOADING

* **Gravity:** 3.71 m/s² (38% of Earth's gravity), reducing design self-weight.
* **Wind Load:** Low air pressure results in nearly null wind load. Analysis used a conservative 60.0 Pa.
* **Dust/Sand Buildup:** Static pressure load of 2,780 Pa (based on 0.5m sand depth and 1,500 kg/m³ density).
* **Meteorite Risk:** Assessed via Lattice Discrete Particle Model (LDPM) simulations.

## METEORITE IMPACT PERFORMANCE

Impact simulations determined that a surface-normal impact of a 7,390g spherical meteorite at 6.29 km/sec is a critical load. Using the atmospheric meteorite flux equations:

* **Critical entry mass:** 12,022 grams.
* **Probability of hazardous impact:** Approximately 6.9 × 10⁻⁹ during a one-year mission (exceptionally low risk).

## PROPOSED LAYOUT

Inspired by "tiny house" designs and NASA HI-SEAS, the layout features:
* **Center:** A round common area with high ceilings and a transparent oculus.
* **Semicircular Regions:** One public (kitchen, workspaces, lab) and one private (bedrooms, lavatory).
* **Access:** Common passageway connecting to a personnel airlock and a docking port for the Multi-Mission Space Exploration Vehicle (MMSEV).

## CONCLUSIONS

The habitat design surpassed structural and functional requirements for a one-year Martian mission. The hemispheric-parabolic dome structure eliminates the need for printed supports by using an inflatable vessel and performs well under Martian loads and meteorite impacts up to 7 kg. The design is applicable to other extraterrestrial sites like the Moon.
