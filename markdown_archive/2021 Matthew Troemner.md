# Design and Analysis of 3D-Printable Thin-Shell Dome Structures for Extraterrestrial Habitation

**Authors:** Matthew Troemner, Elham Ramyar, Benton Johnson, and Gianluca Cusatis
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## Abstract
Extreme environmental conditions and the availability of novel construction techniques dictate the form of Martian habitats. This paper presents an overview of the design and analysis of a dome-shaped Martian habitat designed as part of NASA’s 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The structure utilizes a novel composite hemispheric-parabolic dome optimized for self-weight and environmental loads, intended to be 3D-printed on an inflatable pressure vessel with 'Marscrete'—a Martian concrete made from local regolith and sulfur.

## Introduction
Martian missions require semi-permanent structures that utilize in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and automated fabrication due to the prohibitive cost of transporting materials from Earth. NASA's 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge required designs to sustain four astronauts for one year with 93 square meters of living space.

## Design Basis
Key drivers were constructability, usability, and functionality. The design features an internal hemispherical dome (matching an underlying pressure vessel) and an outer parabolic dome (to ensure compressive stresses). The shell thickness varies from 21 to 108 cm.

## Construction Method
1. **Site Preparation:** Autonomous rovers clear debris.
2. **Foundation:** 3D-printed using an articulated arm.
3. **Inflatable Deployment:** A collapsible pressure vessel is inflated to act as backing for printing, eliminating the need for temporary supports.
4. **3D Printing:** A novel scheme involving an articulating arm and a 'crawler' robot to print Marscrete over the inflatable vessel.

## Environment and Loading
*   **Gravity:** 3.71 m/s² (38% of Earth's).
*   **Wind Load:** Calculated at 9.79 Pa (conservative estimate 60.0 Pa used for analysis).
*   **Sand Buildup:** Static pressure load of 2,780 Pa (0.5 m depth).
*   **Radiation:** Acknowledged but not addressed in the structural performance section.

## Meteorite Impact Performance
Lattice Discrete Particle Model (LDPM) simulations determined that the structure can withstand impacts from spherical meteorites up to a critical mass of 7,390 grams at 6.29 km/sec. The probability of a hazardous impact during a one-year mission is estimated at 6.9 × 10⁻⁹.

## Proposed Layout
The interior follows a 'tiny house' philosophy with a central common area under a transparent oculus. The layout includes semicircular regions for public (kitchen, lab) and private (bedrooms) spaces, connected to an airlock and docking port for the Multi-Mission Space Exploration Vehicle (MMSEV).

## Conclusions
The design surpasses hurdles for Martian habitation, offering a construction scheme that eliminates printed supports and withstands typical Martian loads and meteorite impacts.
