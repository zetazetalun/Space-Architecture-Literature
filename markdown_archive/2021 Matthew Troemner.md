# Design and Analysis of 3D-Printable Thin-Shell Dome Structures for Extraterrestrial Habitation

**Authors:** Matthew Troemner, Elham Ramyar, Benton Johnson, and Gianluca Cusatis  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## ABSTRACT
Extreme environmental conditions, unusual loadings, and most importantly, the availability of novel construction techniques will likely dictate the form of any extraterrestrial habitat built on Mars. This paper presents an overview of the design and analysis of a dome-shaped Martian habitat designed at Northwestern University in collaboration with Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM) as part of NASA’s 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The structure features a novel composite hemispheric-parabolic dome optimized for self-weight and environmental loads, intended to be 3D-printed on an inflatable pressure vessel with 'Marscrete'—a Martian concrete manufactured from local regolith and sulfur. The study examines structural performance under wind, regolith deposition, gravity, and meteorite impacts.

## DESIGN BASIS
The habitat form was driven by constructability, usability, and functionality. To minimize overhanging regions (reducing the need for temporary supports) and maximize continuity, an internal hemispherical dome with an outer parabolic dome structure was selected. This configuration ensures that most external loads result in internal compressive rather than tensile stresses, which is ideal for quasi-brittle materials like Marscrete. The shell thickness varies from 21 to 108 cm.

## CONSTRUCTION METHOD
The proposed scheme involves autonomous rovers clearing the site and 3D-printing a foundation. A collapsible dome pressure vessel is then deployed and inflated to serve as a backing for the 3D-printing process. A novel printing scheme utilizes an articulated arm-type printer to deploy a small crawler robot onto the apex of the inflatable vessel. This crawler circumnavigates the vessel to print the structure from the bottom up. Once printing is complete, the pressure vessel is slightly deflated to allow for independent thermal deformations.

## ENVIRONMENT AND LOADING
- **Gravity:** 3.71 m/s² (38% of Earth's gravity).
- **Wind Load:** Calculated at 9.79 Pa (70 mph) and increased to 60.0 Pa for analysis to account for airborne dust.
- **Sand Buildup:** A static pressure load of 2,780 Pa was applied to one half of the structure to simulate unbalanced dunes.
- **Meteorite Impact:** Assessed using a Lattice Discrete Particle Model (LDPM).

## RESULTS
### Load Analysis
Finite element static analysis showed that under all loads (self-weight, wind, and sand buildup), the structural response remained in the elastic region with deformations less than 1.0 mm.

### Meteorite Impact Performance
- **Critical Load:** A surface-normal impact of a 7,390 g spherical meteorite (3.5 g/cm³) at 6.29 km/sec was determined to be critical.
- **Risk Assessment:** For a 300 m² habitat over a one-year mission, the probability of a hazardous meteorite impact is approximately 6.9 × 10⁻⁹.

## CONCLUSIONS
The design surpasses benchmarks for the NASA 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The hemispheric-parabolic dome structure eliminates the need for printed supports and allows construction via a singular crawler apparatus. The structural shell performs exceptionally well under Martian environmental loads and meteorite impacts up to 7 kg.
