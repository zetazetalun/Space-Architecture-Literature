# Microbially-Enhanced Vanadium Mining and Bioremediation Under Micro- and Mars Gravity on the International Space Station

**Authors:** Charles S. Cockell, Rosa Santomartino, Kai Finster, et al.
**Published:** 01 April 2021
**Journal:** Frontiers in Microbiology
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmicb.2021.641387

## Abstract
As humans explore and settle in space, they will need to mine elements to support industries such as manufacturing and construction. The ESA BioRock experiment on the ISS investigated whether biological mining could be accomplished under extraterrestrial gravity conditions. The study tested the hypothesis that gravity level influenced the efficacy of bioleaching from basalt, a material abundant on the Moon and Mars. Results showed that *Sphingomonas desiccabilis* and *Bacillus subtilis* enhanced vanadium (V) leaching across microgravity, simulated Mars, and Earth gravities by 184.92% to 283.22% compared to controls. Gravity did not significantly affect mean leaching, demonstrating the potential for biomining on various Solar System objects.

## Introduction
To support permanent space settlement, industrial processes like mining natural resources must be implemented. Biomining offers an affordable and sustainable alternative to traditional methods. Vanadium is of particular interest as it is used to fabricate high-strength, corrosion-resistant structural materials for buildings and rovers. The experiment aimed to determine if altered gravity conditions (microgravity and Martian gravity) affect microbial interactions with minerals and subsequent bioleaching.

## Methods

### BioRock Experiment
- **Microorganisms:** *Sphingomonas desiccabilis* CP1D, *Bacillus subtilis* NCIB 3610, and *Cupriavidus metallidurans* CH34.
- **Substrate:** Olivine basalt rock collected from Iceland (Moon/Mars analog).
- **Conditions:** Microgravity, simulated Mars gravity (0.38 x g), and Earth gravity (1 x g) on the ISS; and a 1 x g ground control.
- **Duration:** 21 days.

### Analysis
- **Vanadium Concentration:** Quantified using Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry (ICP-MS).
- **Bioleaching Efficacy:** Measured as the absolute quantity of vanadium released into the culture medium.

## Results
- **Vanadium in Basalt:** The substrate contained 175.03 ± 21.01 ppm of vanadium.
- **Microbial Enhancement:** *S. desiccabilis* and *B. subtilis* significantly increased vanadium leaching across all gravity conditions.
- **Gravity Effects:** No significant difference was observed in the final quantities of leached vanadium among the different gravity conditions for any of the three microorganisms (p > 0.05).

## Discussion
The study confirms that microorganisms can extract vanadium from basalt in space environments. The lack of gravity influence suggests that bioleaching processes are robust across different planetary surfaces. This findings support the use of miniature bioreactors for ISRU to provide elements for the construction of high-quality metal alloys in space.

## Conclusion
Biological vanadium sequestration is functional under Martian and asteroidal gravity conditions. This provides evidence for the feasibility of using biomining to support extraterrestrial construction and bioremediation efforts.
