# Microbially-Enhanced Vanadium Mining and Bioremediation Under Micro- and Mars Gravity on the International Space Station

**Published:** 01 April 2021  
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmicb.2021.641387

## Abstract
As humans explore and settle in space, they will need to mine elements to support industries such as manufacturing and construction. In preparation for the establishment of permanent human settlements across the Solar System, we conducted the ESA BioRock experiment on board the International Space Station to investigate whether biological mining could be accomplished under extraterrestrial gravity conditions. We tested the hypothesis that the gravity (g) level influenced the efficacy with which biomining could be achieved from basalt, an abundant material on the Moon and Mars, by quantifying bioleaching by three different microorganisms under microgravity, simulated Mars and Earth gravitational conditions. One element of interest in mining is vanadium (V), which is added to steel to fabricate high strength, corrosion-resistant structural materials for buildings, transportation, tools and other applications. The results showed that *Sphingomonas desiccabilis* and *Bacillus subtilis* enhanced the leaching of vanadium under the three gravity conditions compared to sterile controls by 184.92 to 283.22%, respectively. Gravity did not have a significant effect on mean leaching, thus showing the potential for biomining on Solar System objects with diverse gravitational conditions.

## Introduction
To move permanently into space, we must be able to implement industrial processes that can support settlement, such as mining natural resources. Biomining using microorganisms offers advantages in affordability and environmental sustainability. Beyond Earth, these processes could be applied for In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) on the Moon or Mars. Vanadium is a metal of strong industrial interest, particularly as an additive in ferrous and non-ferrous alloys to enhance hardness and tensile strength for structural materials, pressurized buildings, and rovers exposed to abrasive planetary dust.

## Methods

### BioRock Experiment
The experiment was conducted on the ISS in 2019 using the KUBIK incubators. It compared bioleaching at three gravity levels: microgravity, simulated Mars gravity, and simulated Earth gravity.

### Microorganisms
Three bacterial species were used:
1. *Sphingomonas desiccabilis* CP1D (Gram-negative)
2. *Bacillus subtilis* NCIB 3610 (Gram-positive)
3. *Cupriavidus metallidurans* CH34 (Gram-negative)

### Substrate
Olivine basalt collected from Iceland was used as a substrate. The basalt was cut into slides (1.5 cm x 1.6 cm x 3 mm) with a mean mass of 1.87 g. The vanadium concentration in the basalt was determined to be 175.03 ± 21.01 ppm.

## Results

### Vanadium Mining Efficiency
Across all gravity conditions on the ISS, *S. desiccabilis* and *B. subtilis* significantly increased the quantity of vanadium leached into the medium compared to sterile controls.

### Impact of Gravity
Statistical analysis (ANOVA) showed no significant difference in the final quantity of vanadium leached among the four gravity conditions for any of the microorganisms tested. This suggests that biomining is technically feasible across a range of gravitational environments from microgravity to Earth gravity.

## Conclusion
The BioRock experiment successfully demonstrated that microorganisms can enhance the recovery of vanadium from basalt in extraterrestrial environments. These results support the potential for upscaling biological reactors to provide essential elements for the construction of high-quality metal alloys and other high-technology products required for permanent space settlement.
