# Microbially-Enhanced Vanadium Mining and Bioremediation Under Micro- and Mars Gravity on the International Space Station

**Journal:** Frontiers in Microbiology  
**Published:** 01 April 2021  
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmicb.2021.641387

## Abstract
As humans explore and settle in space, they will need to mine elements to support industries such as manufacturing and construction. In preparation for the establishment of permanent human settlements across the Solar System, we conducted the ESA BioRock experiment on board the International Space Station to investigate whether biological mining could be accomplished under extraterrestrial gravity conditions. We tested the hypothesis that the gravity (g) level influenced the efficacy with which biomining could be achieved from basalt, an abundant material on the Moon and Mars, by quantifying bioleaching by three different microorganisms under microgravity, simulated Mars and Earth gravitational conditions. One element of interest in mining is vanadium (V), which is added to steel to fabricate high strength, corrosion-resistant structural materials for buildings, transportation, tools and other applications. The results showed that *Sphingomonas desiccabilis* and *Bacillus subtilis* enhanced the leaching of vanadium under the three gravity conditions compared to sterile controls by 184.92 to 283.22%, respectively. Gravity did not have a significant effect on mean leaching, thus showing the potential for biomining on Solar System objects with diverse gravitational conditions. Our results demonstrate the potential to use microorganisms to conduct elemental mining and other bioindustrial processes in space locations with non-1 × g gravity.

## Introduction
To move permanently into space, we must be able to implement industrial processes that can support settlement, such as mining natural resources. Microorganisms are used in a variety of industrial processes on Earth, including biomining. Beyond Earth, the same processes could be applied on other planetary bodies, for example on the Moon or Mars, allowing in situ resource utilization (ISRU). One element with strong industrial interest is vanadium, a metal that can be extracted from minerals and used in ferrous and non-ferrous alloys to enhance hardness, tensile strength and fatigue resistance. These alloys find use in structural materials, rails, metallurgical tools, and applications that beyond Earth could apply to the fabrication of rovers and pressurized buildings.

## Methods
### BioRock Experiment
The experiment was conducted on the International Space Station (ISS) over a 3-week period in 2019. It compared bioleaching at three levels of gravity: microgravity, simulated Mars, and simulated terrestrial gravity.

### Microorganisms
*   *Sphingomonas desiccabilis* CP1D
*   *Bacillus subtilis* NCIB 3610
*   *Cupriavidus metallidurans* CH34

### Substrate
Olivine basalt collected near Reykjavik, Iceland, chosen for its chemical composition similar to basalts found on the Moon and Mars.

## Discussion and Conclusion
The results demonstrate that microorganisms can extract vanadium from basalt at similar efficiencies across diverse gravitational environments. This suggests that biomining is a viable technology for ISRU on the Moon, Mars, and asteroids. The study highlights how biological agents can be integrated into space industrial processes to reduce the mass of material required to be transported from Earth by utilizing local resources for construction and manufacturing.
