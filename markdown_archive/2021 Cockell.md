# Microbially-Enhanced Vanadium Mining and Bioremediation Under Micro- and Mars Gravity on the International Space Station

**Authors:** Charles S. Cockell, Rosa Santomartino, Kai Finster, et al.
**Published:** 01 April 2021, Frontiers in Microbiology
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmicb.2021.641387

## Abstract
As humans explore and settle in space, they will need to mine elements to support industries such as manufacturing and construction. In preparation for the establishment of permanent human settlements across the Solar System, we conducted the ESA BioRock experiment on board the International Space Station to investigate whether biological mining could be accomplished under extraterrestrial gravity conditions. The results showed that *Sphingomonas desiccabilis* and *Bacillus subtilis* enhanced the leaching of vanadium under three gravity conditions (microgravity, simulated Mars, and Earth gravity) compared to sterile controls by 184.92 to 283.22%, respectively. Gravity did not have a significant effect on mean leaching.

## Introduction
To move permanently into space, we must be able to implement industrial processes that can support settlement, such as mining natural resources. Vanadium is a metal of strong industrial interest, used in alloys to enhance hardness, tensile strength, and fatigue resistance for structural materials, pressurized buildings, and objects exposed to corrosive planetary environments.

## Methods
### BioRock Experiment
- **Platform:** International Space Station (ISS) in 2019.
- **Microorganisms:** *Sphingomonas desiccabilis* CP1D, *Bacillus subtilis* NCIB 3610, and *Cupriavidus metallidurans* CH34.
- **Substrate:** Olivine basalt rock (Moon/Mars analog) from Iceland.
- **Conditions:** Microgravity, simulated Mars gravity (0.38 g), and simulated Earth gravity (1.0 g) using KUBIK centrifuges.

### Key Findings
- **Biomining Efficacy:** *S. desiccabilis* and *B. subtilis* significantly enhanced vanadium leaching (two-fold increase) compared to sterile controls.
- **Gravity Impact:** Gravity levels did not significantly influence the final quantities of leached vanadium for any of the tested microbes.
- **ISRU Potential:** The experiment demonstrates that biological extraction of structural metals is feasible in extraterrestrial environments regardless of gravity levels.

## Discussion
The lack of significant difference among gravity conditions suggests that microbial metabolic processes and microbe-mineral interactions required for ISRU can function effectively on asteroids, the Moon, and Mars. This supports the development of biological mining reactors for upscaling to industrial sizes to provide materials for high-quality metal alloys in space construction.
