# Microbially-Enhanced Vanadium Mining and Bioremediation Under Micro- and Mars Gravity on the International Space Station

**Published:** 01 April 2021  
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmicb.2021.641387  
**Authors:** Charles S. Cockell, Rosa Santomartino, et al.

## Abstract
As humans explore and settle in space, they will need to mine elements to support industries such as manufacturing and construction. In preparation for the establishment of permanent human settlements across the Solar System, we conducted the ESA BioRock experiment on board the International Space Station to investigate whether biological mining could be accomplished under extraterrestrial gravity conditions. One element of interest is vanadium (V), which is added to steel to fabricate high strength, corrosion-resistant structural materials for buildings and transportation.

## Key Findings
- *Sphingomonas desiccabilis* and *Bacillus subtilis* enhanced the leaching of vanadium under microgravity, simulated Mars gravity, and Earth gravity compared to sterile controls.
- Bioleaching efficacy was increased by 184.92% to 283.22% over sterile controls.
- Gravity did not have a significant effect on mean leaching rates, demonstrating the potential for biomining on various Solar System objects.

## Methods
- **Microorganisms:** *Sphingomonas desiccabilis*, *Bacillus subtilis*, and *Cupriavidus metallidurans*.
- **Substrate:** Olivine basalt slides (analog for Lunar and Martian regolith).
- **Location:** International Space Station (ISS) using KUBIK incubators with centrifuges to simulate 1 x g (Earth) and 0.4 x g (Mars) gravity, alongside static microgravity.

## Conclusion
The results demonstrate the potential for biological vanadium sequestration under Martian and asteroidal low gravity conditions. This can support the construction of high-quality metal alloys in space and aid in bioremediation and elemental recycling for sustainable human presence beyond Earth.
