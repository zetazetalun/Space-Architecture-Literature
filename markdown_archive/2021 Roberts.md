# Blood, sweat, and tears: extraterrestrial regolith biocomposites with in vivo binders

**Authors:** A.D. Roberts, D.R. Whittall, R. Breitling, E. Takano, J.J. Blaker, S. Hay, N.S. Scrutton
**Journal:** Materials Today Bio 12 (2021) 100136
**Published:** Elsevier Ltd., 10 September 2021

## Abstract
The high cost and significant time delay associated with delivering payloads to the Martian surface means that exploitation of resources in situ — including inorganic rock and dust (regolith), water deposits, and atmospheric gases — will be an important part of any crewed mission to the Red Planet. In this work, we explore the use of human serum albumin (HSA) — a common protein obtained from blood plasma — as a binder for simulated Lunar and Martian regolith to produce so-called ‘extraterrestrial regolith biocomposites (ERBs).’ HSA-based ERBs were produced and displayed compressive strengths as high as 25.0 MPa. The incorporation of urea — which could be extracted from the urine, sweat, or tears of astronauts — could further increase the compressive strength by over 300% in some instances, with the best-performing formulation having an average compressive strength of 39.7 MPa. Furthermore, we demonstrate that HSA-ERBs have the potential to be 3D-printed.

## 1. Introduction
Early extraterrestrial colonies will likely exploit loose unconsolidated rock and dust (regolith) as a bulk material for radiation and meteor shielding. Mechanical stabilization is necessary to prevent erosion or serve as a construction material for habitat expansion. While previous research explored biological, synthetic polymer, or cement-based binders, this study focuses on resources produced by the crew themselves (in vivo).

## 2. Materials and Methods
- **Simulants:** Lunar Highlands Simulant 1 (LHS-1) and Martian Global Simulant 1 (MGS-1).
- **Binder:** Human Serum Albumin (HSA), with urea as an additive.
- **Fabrication:** HSA was dissolved in DI water (30 wt%) and infused into packed regolith in syringes. Samples were dehydrated at 65 °C overnight.
- **Testing:** Uniaxial compression tests (ASTM C39/C39M-20), Circular dichroism (CD) for structural analysis, and 3D printing via manual extrusion.

### 3.2. Compressive Strength
- HSA-ERBs showed positive correlation between protein concentration and UCS up to 35 wt%.
- **LHS-1 (Lunar)** consistently showed higher UCS than **MGS-1 (Mars)**, attributed to coarser particle size distribution facilitating better stress distribution.
- The highest UCS recorded for HSA-only ERBs was **25.0 ± 3.1 MPa** (LHS-1, 35 wt% HSA).

### 3.4. Incorporation of Urea
- Urea acts as a protein denaturant, facilitating the unfolding of HSA into a densely hydrogen-bonded β-sheet structure.
- For LHS-1 with 30 wt% HSA, adding 3 M urea increased UCS from 12.3 MPa to **39.7 ± 3.9 MPa** (a 324% increase).

### 3.6. 3D-Printing Potential
- A mixture of MGS-1 and 15 wt% HSA was manually extruded to create a 25 x 25 mm square structure. The 3D-printed construction achieved a UCS of approximately 1.9 MPa, which could be improved with higher concentrations.

## 5. Conclusions
HSA and urea can produce regolith biocomposites with compressive strengths exceeding standard terrestrial concrete (20–32 MPa). This method circumvents the need for heavy, malfunction-prone binder production equipment. Calculations suggest that over a 72-week mission, a crew of six could produce enough HSA to construct habitat space for one additional crew member.
