# Blood, sweat, and tears: extraterrestrial regolith biocomposites with in vivo binders

## Abstract
This work explores the use of human serum albumin (HSA) — a common protein obtained from blood plasma — as a binder for simulated Lunar and Martian regolith to produce 'extraterrestrial regolith biocomposites (ERBs)'. HSA produced by astronauts in vivo could be extracted and combined with Lunar or Martian regolith to create construction materials. HSA-based ERBs displayed compressive strengths as high as 25.0 MPa. The incorporation of urea — which could be extracted from urine, sweat, or tears — further increased the compressive strength by over 300%, with top performing formulations reaching 39.7 MPa. The potential for these materials to be 3D-printed is also demonstrated.

## 1. Introduction
Due to the high cost of extra-orbital payload delivery, early extraterrestrial colonies will likely exploit loose regolith as a bulk material for radiation and meteor shielding. Mechanical stabilization of this regolith is necessary to prevent erosion from exhaust plumes or dust storms. This work explores exploitation of natural resources produced by the astronauts themselves (in vivo) as binders for such materials.

## 2. Materials and Methods
- **Binder Feedstock:** Human Serum Albumin (HSA), Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA), and recombinant spider silk.
- **Regolith Simulants:** Lunar Highlands Simulant 1 (LHS-1) and Martian Global Simulant 1 (MGS-1).
- **Urea Incorporation:** Urea was investigated as a protein denaturant to affect cohesive strength.
- **3D-Printing:** Feasibility was tested via manual extrusion of an MGS-1 and HSA (15 wt%) mixture.

### 3.4 3D-Printing Potential
A simple scoping experiment demonstrated that HSA-ERBs could be 3D-printed. A printed structure sustained an ultimate compressive force of 600 N, equivalent to a mass of 162 kg on Mars or 370 kg on the Moon. The ultimate compressive strength of the 3D-printed part was approximately 1.9 MPa.

## 5. Conclusions
Human blood plasma and urine can produce biocomposites with strength comparable to terrestrial concrete. Calculations suggest that each astronaut could produce enough binder to construct habitat space to support an additional astronaut over the course of a 72-week Mars mission. This method circumvents the need for heavy, malfunction-prone binder production equipment.
