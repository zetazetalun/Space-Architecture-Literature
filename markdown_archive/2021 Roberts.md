# Blood, sweat, and tears: extraterrestrial regolith biocomposites with in vivo binders\n\n**Authors:** A.D. Roberts, D.R. Whittall, R. Breitling, E. Takano, J.J. Blaker, S. Hay, N.S. Scrutton  \n**Journal:** Materials Today Bio 12 (2021) 100136  \n**DOI:** 10.1016/j.mtbio.2021.100136\n\n## Abstract\nThe exploitation of resources in situ — including inorganic rock and dust (regolith) — will be an important part of any crewed mission to Mars. In this work, the authors explore the use of human serum albumin (HSA), a common protein from blood plasma, as a binder for simulated Lunar and Martian regolith to produce 'extraterrestrial regolith biocomposites' (ERBs). HSA-based ERBs displayed compressive strengths as high as 25.0 MPa. The incorporation of urea — which could be extracted from the urine, sweat, or tears of astronauts — could further increase the compressive strength to 39.7 MPa. The mechanism of adhesion was attributed to the dehydration-induced reorganization of the protein secondary structure into a densely hydrogen-bonded β-sheet network. The potential for 3D-printing HSA-ERBs is also demonstrated.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nEarly extraterrestrial colonies will likely exploit regolith as a bulk material for radiation and meteor shielding. Traditional binders require heavy equipment or spare parts from Earth. Exploiting natural resources produced by the astronauts themselves (in vivo) is proposed as a method to reduce payload mass and mission cost.\n\n## 2. Results and Comparison\n\n### Table 1: Comparison of regolith stabilization technologies\n| Method | UCS (MPa) | Processing Energy | Primary disadvantages |\n|---|---|---|---|\n| Melted and cast regolith | 550 | 360 (very high) | Extremely high energy/temp (1200-1500 C) |\n| Sintered regolith | 14.5 | 156 (high) | High processing energy/temp (1000-1200 C) |\n| Extraterrestrial concrete | 75.5 | High | High energy and water consumption |\n| **ERBs with HSA** | **25.0** | **Low** | Limited production |\n| **ERBs with HSA and urea** | **39.7** | **Low** | Limited production |\n| Sand-bagging | 2 | Low | Poor mechanical properties |\n\n### Mechanical Performance\n- **HSA Concentration:** A positive correlation was found between HSA concentration and ultimate compressive strength (UCS) up to 35 wt%.\n- **Regolith Type:** Lunar Highlands Simulant (LHS-1) displayed higher UCS than Martian Global Simulant (MGS-1), likely due to the coarser particle size of LHS-1.\n- **Urea Addition:** Urea acts as a protein denaturant, facilitating the unfolding of HSA into a stable β-sheet structure. This increased UCS by over 300% in some instances (up to 39.7 MPa).\n\n### 3D-Printing Demonstration\n- A mixture of MGS-1 and HSA (15 wt%) was manually extruded to form a square wall structure (approx. 25x25x20 mm).\n- After dehydration at 65 C, the structure sustained an ultimate compressive force of 600 N (equivalent to 162 kg on Mars).\n\n## 3. Conclusions\nHSA-ERBs can be produced with minimal ex situ components using simple equipment (syringes and a hot plate). A liter of human blood plasma contains enough HSA to produce about 300 g of ERB. Over a 72-week mission, one crew member could produce enough HSA to construct habitat space to support an additional astronaut.
