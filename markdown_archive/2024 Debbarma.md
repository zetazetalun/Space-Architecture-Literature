# Fiber-reinforced lunar geopolymers synthesized using lunar regolith simulants

**Solomon Debbarma, Xijun Shi, Anthony Torres, Mehrab Nodehi**

**Published:** Acta Astronautica 214 (2024) 593–608
**Available online:** 11 November 2023
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2023.11.013

---

## Abstract
In-situ resource utilization is extremely important to not only support additive construction on the Moon but also to minimize the weight of transportation. The work outlined in this study presents a possible solution to supporting lunar additive construction by the formulation of fiber-reinforced geopolymers using lunar regolith simulants. Two types of regolith simulants were utilized: Lunar Mare Simulant (LMS-1) and Lunar Highlands Simulant (LHS-1). Key variables investigated include simulant type, flowability, curing temperature, fiber type, and flexural strength. Results show that LHS-1 geopolymers exhibited higher compressive strength than LMS-1. High-temperature curing (93 °C) for 72 h significantly improved strength for LHS-1. Basalt and human hair fibers improved flexural strength by up to 32% and 15%, respectively, though they reduced compressive strength slightly due to increased porosity.

## 1. Introduction
Successful construction of a lunar or Martian base requires building materials that utilize in-situ materials like regolith. Traditional Portland cement is impractical due to the absence of limestone and the high energy required for sintering. Geopolymers, formed by alkali activation of silica and alumina-rich materials, offer a viable alternative. They provide high mechanical strength and resistance to temperature fluctuations and radiation.

## 2. Research Significance and Objectives
This study aligns with NASA’s priorities for ISRU and extreme environment technologies. The primary objectives were:
1. Use lunar regolith simulants (LMS-1 and LHS-1) to synthesize geopolymers.
2. Improve mixture efficiency using combined simulants.
3. Improve flexural performance using Basalt Fibers (BF) and Human Hair Fibers (HHF).

## 3. Materials and Methods

### 3.1. Lunar Regolith Simulants
- **LMS-1 (Lunar Mare Simulant):** Simulates dark mare terrain; basaltic.
- **LHS-1 (Lunar Highlands Simulant):** Simulates highlands; anorthositic.

### Table 2: Physical properties of lunar regolith simulants
| Property | LMS-1 | LHS-1 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |

### 3.2. Fibers
- **Basalt Fiber (BF):** 12 mm length, 16 μm diameter.
- **Human Hair Fiber (HHF):** Length up to 15 mm.

### 3.4. Formulation and Curing
- **Liquid-to-binder (l/b) ratio:** 0.45.
- **Alkaline Activator:** Reagent sodium silicate (40% Na4O4Si, 60% water).
- **Curing Regimes:** Ambient (20 °C), High-temperature (93 °C or 99.6 °C), and Low-temperature conditioning (-30 °C).

## 4. Results and Discussion

### 4.1. Flowability
- Mix B (LHS-1) showed higher flow (85 mm) than Mix A (LMS-1, 74 mm).
- Mix D (50:50 LMS/LHS) showed the highest flow at 94 mm.
- Addition of fibers reduced flowability by 9–26%.

### 4.2. Compressive Strength
- LHS-1 (Mix B) heat-cured at 93 °C achieved 31.26 MPa.
- LMS-1 (Mix A) remained around 8.24–8.45 MPa regardless of curing temperature.
- Required compressive strength for lunar construction is estimated at ~5.8 MPa (1/6th of Earth's requirements).

### 4.3. Flexural Strength
- BF increased flexural strength by up to 32% (at 1.2% dosage).
- HHF (at 1.5% dosage) increased flexural strength by 15%.
- Fibers act as nucleation sites for geopolymer products and provide crack-bridging mechanisms.

## 6. Conclusions
- LHS-1 geopolymers are superior in compressive strength to LMS-1 due to lower Si/Al ratios and higher calcium content.
- Curing at lunar daytime temperatures (93 °C) is critical for strength development in high-anorthosite simulants.
- Basalt and human hair fibers are effective ISRU reinforcements for improving the ductility and flexural capacity of lunar concrete.
