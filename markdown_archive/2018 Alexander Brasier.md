# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?

**Authors:** Alexander Brasier, David Wacey, Mike Rogerson, Paul Guagliardo, Martin Saunders, Siri Kellner, Ramon Mercedes-Martin, Tim Prior, Colin Taylor, Anna Matthews, John Reijmer

**Published in:** Geobiology, 2018; 16:540–555
**DOI:** 10.1111/gbi.12292

## Abstract
Lacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. If these were bioinfluenced constructions, they could be priority targets in the search for early and extraterrestrial microbial life. Here, we use correlative microscopy to show that microbes were living around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. A plausible interpretation is that benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) formed tubular biofilms around rising sublacustrine spring vent waters, binding calcium ions and trapping and binding detrital silicate sediment. These biofilms provided initial sites for calcite nucleation and encouraged primary organised crystal growth. Such carbonate chimneys represent a promising macroscale target in the exploration for ancient or extraterrestrial life.

## 1. Introduction
Distinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in astrobiological investigation. Free-standing chimneys composed of carbonate, sulphate, or silicate minerals provide a potential set of targets for terrestrial and extraterrestrial investigation. Mono Lake, California, is a global site for studying biogeochemical processes creating "tufa" limestone chimney constructions.

## 2. Methods
- **Fieldwork:** Conducted in October 2014 near Mono City on Pleistocene chimney structures (3–4 m height, 1.5–3 m width).
- **Analysis:** XRD analysis, biomarker analysis (GC-MS), Focused Ion Beam (FIB) preparation, TEM analysis, SEM-EDS, and NanoSIMS ion mapping.

## 3. Results
### 3.1 Petrography of chimney pipes
Walls of the ~3 cm wide chimney pipes are 0.5 to 1 cm thick, constructed of columns of calcite that grew radially around a ~1 cm central conduit. Calcite crystal microspar columns often branch into millimetre to centimetre scale shrub-like morphologies.

### 3.2 Microfossils within chimney pipes
Two main types of filaments were identified:
- **Type 1:** Diameters 0.8 to 8 μm; dominated by empty sheaths.
- **Type 2:** Diameters 10 to 14.5 μm; well-preserved trichomes with clear interior mineralisation and segmentation.

### 3.3 Tufa and microfossil chemistry
XRD showed ~99% of chimney pipe rock is calcite with an average Mg content of 8.1%. Silicate nanograins (quartz, alkali feldspar, chlorite) were found surrounding filaments, trapped by microbial EOM.

## 4. Discussion
Microbial EOM likely stripped calcium from rising vent waters, favoring calcite nucleation and subsequent organized crystal growth. This suggests chimney fabric development was influenced at the nano- to microscale by microbes, which has direct applicability to searching for extraterrestrial life.

## 5. Conclusions
Mono City chimney pipes are packed with fossil microbial filaments. These represent centimetre-thick tubular and vertically stacked calcified microbial mats. Biota exerted a strong control on the fabric of these large-scale constructions.
