# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?

**Authors:** Alexander Brasier, David Wacey, Mike Rogerson, Paul Guagliardo, Martin Saunders, Siri Kellner, Ramon Mercedes-Martin, Tim Prior, Colin Taylor, Anna Matthews, John Reijmer

**Journal:** Geobiology, 2018; 16: 540–555
**DOI:** 10.1111/gbi.12292

## Abstract
Lacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. If these were bioinfluenced constructions, they could be priority targets in the search for early and extraterrestrial microbial life. Here, we use correlative microscopy to show that microbes were living around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. A plausible interpretation is that benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) formed tubular biofilms around rising sublacustrine spring vent waters, binding calcium ions and trapping and binding detrital silicate sediment. Decay of these biofilms would locally have increased calcium and carbonate ion activity, inducing calcite precipitation on and around the biofilms. Much of the calcite crystal growth was likely abiotic, but it seems likely that the biofilms provided initial sites for calcite nucleation and encouraged the primary organised crystal growth.

## 1. Introduction
Distinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in the search for extraterrestrial life. Free-standing chimneys composed of carbonate, sulphate, or silicate minerals provide a potential set of targets for astrobiological investigation. Mono Lake, California, is a global site for studying biogeochemical processes creating "tufa" limestone chimney constructions around sublacustrine vents.

## 2. Results
### 2.1 Chimney Morphology
*   **Dimensions:** Pleistocene chimneys vary from c. 3 to 4 m in height and 1.5 to 3 m in width.
*   **Internal Structure:** Constructed of stacks of numerous calcitic cones or pipes, each 30–60 cm in height and ~3 cm in width. Each pipe has a central ~1 cm conduit.
*   **External Coating:** Encrusted in 20- to 50-cm-thick blankets of "thinolite" (ikaite pseudomorphs).

### 2.2 Petrography and Mineralogy
*   **Composition:** ~99% high-Mg calcite with an average magnesium content of 8.1(1)%. The remaining ~1% is quartz.
*   **Microfossils:** Two main types of filaments found:
    *   **Type 1:** Diameters 0.8 to 8 μm (peaks at 1 μm and 2.5 μm). Mostly empty sheaths.
    *   **Type 2:** Diameters 10 to 14.5 μm. Segmented trichomes with interior mineralisation.

### 2.3 Chemical Analysis
*   **NanoSIMS:** Showed carbon and nitrogen distribution consistent with degraded extracellular organic material (EOM).
*   **Silicates:** Abundant silicate nanograins (quartz, feldspar, chlorite) are spatially associated with the EOM surrounding the cells.

## 3. Conclusions
*   Mono City chimney pipes are packed with fossil microbial filaments (likely cyanobacteria).
*   Biofilms influenced tufa chimney fabric development at the nano- and micro-scales.
*   Microbial EOM trapped and bound detrital clastic grains and provided nucleation sites for calcite.
*   These findings assist in identifying macro-scale targets for exploration of ancient or extraterrestrial life (e.g., on Mars).
