# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?

**Alexander Brasier, David Wacey, Mike Rogerson, et al.**  
*Geobiology*, 2018;16:540–555.  
DOI: 10.1111/gbi.12292

## Abstract
Lacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. If these were bioinfluenced constructions, they could be priority targets in the search for early and extraterrestrial microbial life. Here, we use correlative microscopy to show that microbes were living around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. A plausible interpretation is that benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) formed tubular biofilms around rising sublacustrine spring vent waters, binding calcium ions and trapping and binding detrital silicate sediment. Decay of these biofilms would locally have increased calcium and carbonate ion activity, inducing calcite precipitation on and around the biofilms.

## 1 | INTRODUCTION
Distinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in the search for early and extraterrestrial life. Free-standing chimneys composed of carbonate, sulphate or silicate minerals, if requiring the influence of organisms to develop, provide a potential set of targets for terrestrial geological and astrobiological investigation.

## 3 | RESULTS
### 3.1 | Petrography of chimney pipes
The walls of the c. 3 cm wide chimney pipes are mostly 0.5 to 1 cm thick, constructed of columns of calcite that grew radially around the c. 1 cm wide central conduit. In zones closer to pipe conduits, the microsparry calcite columns were separated from each other by elongated voids running parallel to the column long axis.

### 3.2 | Microfossils within chimney pipes
Clusters of filaments are ubiquitous within the columnar to shrub-like calcite growths. Filaments are divided into two main types:
- **Type 1:** Diameters 0.8 to 8 μm (modal peaks at 1 and 2.5 μm).
- **Type 2:** Diameters 10 to 14.5 μm, well-preserved trichomes with clear interior mineralisation.

### 3.3 | Tufa and microfossil chemistry
XRD data showed ~99% of chimney pipe rock to be calcite with an average magnesium content of 8.1(1)%. The remaining ~1% constituent was quartz. NanoSIMS, SEM-EDS, and TEM-EDS show abundant silicate nanograins surrounding many filaments, spatially associated with C and N interpreted as fossilised EOM.

## 5 | CONCLUSIONS
Our results show that the Mono City chimney pipes are packed full of fossil microbial filaments. Evidently there was strong growth of a cyanobacterial biofilm around rising spring waters, and these microbes influenced tufa chimney fabric development at least at the nano- and micro-scales. These findings have direct applicability to the search for ancient and extraterrestrial microbial life.
