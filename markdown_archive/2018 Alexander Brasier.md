# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?

**Journal:** Geobiology (2018; 16:540–555)
**Authors:** Alexander Brasier, David Wacey, Mike Rogerson, et al.
**DOI:** 10.1111/gbi.12292

## Abstract
Lacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. If these were bioinfluenced constructions, they could be priority targets in the search for early and extraterrestrial microbial life. Here, we use correlative microscopy to show that microbes were living around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. A plausible interpretation is that benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) formed tubular biofilms around rising sublacustrine spring vent waters, binding calcium ions and trapping and binding detrital silicate sediment. Decay of these biofilms would locally have increased calcium and carbonate ion activity, inducing calcite precipitation on and around the biofilms. Such carbonate chimneys represent a promising macroscale target in the exploration for ancient or extraterrestrial life.

## 1 | INTRODUCTION
Distinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in the search for early and extraterrestrial life. Free-standing chimneys composed of carbonate, sulphate or silicate minerals, if requiring the influence of organisms to develop, provide a potential set of targets for terrestrial geological and astrobiological investigation.

## 3 | RESULTS
Internally the chimneys are constructed of stacks of numerous calcitic cones or pipes, each 30–60 cm in height and around 3 cm in width. Each pipe has a central 1 cm-sized void or conduit. The outsides of these pipes are commonly coated in botryoids of calcite. Individual pseudomorphs after ikaite crystals in these “thinolite” blankets mostly measured around 5 cm in length.

### 3.1 | Petrography of chimney pipes
The walls of the c. 3 cm wide chimney pipes are mostly 0.5 to 1 cm thick, constructed of columns of calcite that grew radially around the c. 1 cm wide central conduit. Calcite crystal microspar columns may also branch and fan outwards resulting in millimetre to centimetre scale shrub-like morphologies.

### 3.2 | Microfossils within chimney pipes
Clusters of filaments, observed in thin sections, are a major component of the chimneys. Morphologies include:
- **Type 1:** Diameters 0.8 to 8 μm, dominated by empty sheaths.
- **Type 2:** Diameters 10 to 14.5 μm, well-preserved trichomes with interior mineralisation and clear segmentation.

### 3.3 | Tufa and microfossil chemistry
XRD data showed ~99% of chimney pipe rock to be calcite with an average magnesium content of 8.1(1)%. The remaining ~1% constituent was quartz. Silicates (quartz, alkali feldspar, chlorite) were found surrounding the cyanobacteria, interpreted as detrital grains trapped by microbial EOM.

## 5 | CONCLUSIONS
The Mono City chimney pipes are packed full of fossil microbial filaments, representing centimetre-thick tubular and vertically stacked calcified microbial mats. There was strong growth of a cyanobacterial biofilm around rising spring waters in the Pleistocene Mono Lake, and these microbes influenced tufa chimney fabric development at the nano- and microscales. Chimney fabric development was influenced by microbes that colonised the fertile and relatively nutrient-rich vent sites, providing direct applicability to the search for extraterrestrial microbial life.
