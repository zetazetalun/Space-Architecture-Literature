# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?

**Authors:** Alexander Brasier, David Wacey, Mike Rogerson, Paul Guagliardo, Martin Saunders, Siri Kellner, Ramon Mercedes-Martin, Tim Prior, Colin Taylor, Anna Matthews, John Reijmer
**Journal:** Geobiology
**Year:** 2018
**DOI:** 10.1111/gbi.12292

## Abstract
Lacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. If these were bioinfluenced constructions, they could be priority targets in the search for early and extraterrestrial microbial life. However, there are questions over whether such chimneys are built on a geobiological framework or are solely abiotic geomorphological features produced by mixing of lake and spring waters. Here, we use correlative microscopy to show that microbes were living around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. A plausible interpretation, in line with some recent works by others on other lacustrine carbonates, is that benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) formed tubular biofilms around rising sublacustrine spring vent waters, binding calcium ions and trapping and binding detrital silicate sediment. Decay of these biofilms would locally have increased calcium and carbonate ion activity, inducing calcite precipitation on and around the biofilms. Early manganese carbonate mineralisation was directly associated with cell walls, potentially related to microbial activity though the precise mechanism remains to be elucidated. Much of the calcite crystal growth was likely abiotic, and no strong evidence for either authigenic silicate growth or a clay mineral precursor framework was observed. Nevertheless, it seems likely that the biofilms provided initial sites for calcite nucleation and encouraged the primary organised crystal growth. We suggest that the nano-, micro- and macroscale fabrics of these Pleistocene Mono Lake chimneys were affected by the presence of centimetre-thick tubular and vertically stacked calcifying microbial mats. Such carbonate chimneys represent a promising macroscale target in the exploration for ancient or extraterrestrial life.

## 1 | INTRODUCTION
Distinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in the search for early and extraterrestrial life. Free-standing chimneys composed of carbonate, sulphate or silicate minerals, if requiring the influence of organisms to develop, provide a potential set of targets for investigation. Mono Lake, California, is a globally important site for studying potential biogeochemical processes creating “tufa” limestone chimney constructions around sublacustrine vents.

## 2 | METHODS
### 2.1 | Fieldwork and sample processing
Fieldwork was undertaken in October 2014. Samples of Pleistocene chimney structures close to Mono City were examined. These chimneys are linearly arranged, presumably along a fault.
### 2.2 | XRD analysis
XRD data were collected using a PANAlytical Empyrean X-ray diffractometer. Data indicated that the chimney pipe rock is ~99% calcite with an average magnesium content of 8.1%.
### 2.3 | Biomarker analysis
Analysis was conducted on a further off-cut using GC-MS to identify entombed organic carbon.
### 2.4 | FIB-SEM and NanoSIMS
Thin sections were prepared and targeted for NanoSIMS (CAMECA NanoSIMS 50), FIB-SEM, and TEM (FEI Titan G2 80-200) to determine spatial relationships between cell walls and minerals.

## 3 | RESULTS
The Mono City chimneys vary from c. 3 to 4 m in height and 1.5 to 3 m in width. Internally, they are constructed of stacks of numerous calcitic cones or pipes (30–60 cm height, 3 cm width) with central 1 cm-sized conduits. The walls are constructed of columns of calcite that grew radially around the conduit. High abundances of filamentous microfossils (Type 1 and Type 2) were found within the columnar calcite growths. Biomarker data (n-C17 and monomethylalkanes) are consistent with the presence of cyanobacteria.

## 4 | DISCUSSION
Evidence suggests the chimneys are not solely the result of abiotic mixing. Microbes colonizing nutrient-rich vent sites influenced the fabric development. Tubular sheets of Extracellular Organic Material (EOM) from microbes provided preferential sites for calcite crystal nucleation. This biogenic control on the location and nucleation of mineral growth assisted in the formation of the carbonate chimney pipes.

## 5 | CONCLUSIONS
The results show that Mono City chimney pipes are packed with fossil microbial filaments. Microbial mats influenced tufa chimney fabric development at nano- to microscales. These findings have direct applicability to the search for ancient and extraterrestrial microbial life, highlighting how biological activity can influence macroscale structural mineral formations in extreme environments.
