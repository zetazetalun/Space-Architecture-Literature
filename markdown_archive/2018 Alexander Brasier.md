# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?

**Authors:** Alexander Brasier, David Wacey, Mike Rogerson, Paul Guagliardo, Martin Saunders, Siri Kellner, Ramon Mercedes-Martin, Tim Prior, Colin Taylor, Anna Matthews, John Reijmer
**Journal:** Geobiology (2018; 16:540–555)
**DOI:** 10.1111/gbi.12292

## Abstract
Lacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. This study uses correlative microscopy to show that microbes were living around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. A plausible interpretation is that benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) formed tubular biofilms around rising sublacustrine spring vent waters, binding calcium ions and trapping/binding detrital silicate sediment. Such carbonate chimneys represent a promising macro-scale target in the exploration for ancient or extraterrestrial life.

## 1. Introduction
Distinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in astrobiological investigation. Mono Lake, California, is a globally important site for studying potential biogeochemical processes creating “tufa” limestone chimney constructions around sublacustrine vents.

## 2. Methods
- **Fieldwork:** Conducted in October 2014 at Mono Lake Tufa State Natural Reserve.
- **Analysis Techniques:** 
    - XRD analysis for bulk mineralogy.
    - Biomarker analysis (GC-MS).
    - Focussed Ion Beam (FIB) preparation of TEM samples.
    - NanoSIMS ion mapping.
    - SEM-EDS for elemental distribution.

## 3. Results
- **Structure:** Chimneys vary from 3 to 4 m in height and 1.5 to 3 m in width. Internally, they consist of stacks of numerous calcitic pipes (30–60 cm height, 3 cm width) with central 1 cm conduits.
- **Mineralogy:** Chimney pipe rock is ~99% high-Mg calcite (8.1% Mg). Exterior blankets consist of "thinolite" (calcitic pseudomorphs after ikaite).
- **Microfossils:** High abundance of organic filaments (Type 1: 0.8–8 μm; Type 2: 10–14.5 μm) interpreted as fossilized cyanobacteria.

## 4. Discussion & Conclusions
- **Bio-influence:** Biofilms provided initial sites for calcite nucleation and encouraged primary organized crystal growth. The morphology of the chimneys is a result of complex interplay between lake dynamics (faults, venting) and benthic microbial communities.
- **Astrobiological Implications:** These structures provide macro-scale targets for Mars exploration and serve as models for how biological activity can influence structural fabric in extreme environments.
