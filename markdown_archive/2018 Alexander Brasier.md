# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?

**Authors:** Alexander Brasier, David Wacey, Mike Rogerson, et al.
**Journal:** Geobiology, 2018; 16:540–555.
**DOI:** 10.1111/gbi.12292

## Abstract
Lacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. This study uses correlative microscopy to show that microbes were living around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. A plausible interpretation is that benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) formed tubular biofilms around rising sublacustrine spring vent waters, binding calcium ions and trapping and binding detrital silicate sediment. Decay of these biofilms induced calcite precipitation. These findings suggest that carbonate chimneys represent a promising macro-scale target in the exploration for ancient or extraterrestrial life.

## 1. Introduction
Distinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in astrobiological investigation. Free-standing chimneys composed of carbonate, sulphate, or silicate minerals provide potential targets for extraterrestrial investigation. Mono Lake, California, is a globally important site for studying biogeochemical processes creating "tufa" limestone chimney constructions.

## 2. Methods
- **Fieldwork:** Samples taken from Pleistocene chimney structures near Mono City.
- **XRD Analysis:** Used to determine bulk rock mineralogy.
- **Biomarker Analysis:** Conducted to identify organic carbon entombed within the rock.
- **Advanced Microscopy:** Utilized Focussed Ion Beam (FIB) preparation, TEM analysis, SEM-EDS, and NanoSIMS ion mapping to observe spatial relationships between cell walls and minerals.

## 3. Results
- **Physical Structure:** Chimneys range from 3 to 4 m in height and are constructed of numerous calcitic pipes (30–60 cm height, 3 cm width).
- **Mineralogy:** Chimney pipe rock is ~99% calcite with an average magnesium content of 8.1%. 
- **Microfossils:** High abundance of organic filaments (cyanobacteria) identified within the columnar calcite growths.
- **3D Distribution:** Reconstructions show carbonaceous cell walls and EOM spatially linked to silicate grains and Mn-Fe rich carbonates.

## 4. Discussion
- **Biological Influence:** Microbial EOM likely inhibited initial calcite formation but, upon degradation, provided preferential sites for crystal nucleation. 
- **Morphology Control:** The tubular shape of the chimneys is attributed to biofilm growth around rising spring waters.
- **Astrobiological Implication:** These bio-influenced constructions provide models for identifying similar structures on other planets.

## 5. Conclusions
Mono City chimney pipes are packed with fossil microbial filaments. These microbes influenced tufa chimney fabric development at the nano- and micro-scales. The study concludes that the calcitic nature and morphology of these structures are significantly bio-influenced, offering direct applicability to the search for extraterrestrial life and the understanding of biological construction in extreme environments.
