# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?

## Abstract
Lacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. This study uses correlative microscopy to show that microbes lived around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. Benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) likely formed tubular biofilms around rising spring waters, binding calcium ions and trapping silicate sediment. These biofilms provided initial sites for calcite nucleation and encouraged primary organized crystal growth. Such chimneys represent macro-scale targets in the exploration for ancient or extraterrestrial life.

## 1. Introduction
Distinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in astrobiology. Free-standing chimneys (carbonate, sulphate, or silicate) are potential targets for terrestrial geological and astrobiological investigation.

## 2. Methods
- **Fieldwork:** Undertaken in 2014 at Mono Lake, focusing on Pleistocene chimney structures near Mono City.
- **XRD Analysis:** Determined bulk mineralogy (99% high-Mg calcite).
- **Biomarker Analysis:** Conducted using GC-MS to identify lipid remnants of cyanobacteria (n-C17 alkanes).
- **Microscopy:** Used Focused Ion Beam (FIB), Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), and Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) to analyze microstructures and elemental distribution.

## 3. Results
- **Chimney Structure:** 3–4 m in height, constructed of stacks of calcitic pipes (3 cm wide) with central conduits.
- **Microfossils:** Two main types of filaments (Type 1: 0.8–8 μm; Type 2: 10–14.5 μm) identified within the calcite, consistent with cyanobacteria.
- **Mineralogy:** Identified carbonaceous cell walls, Mn–Fe-rich carbonates associated with walls, and detrital silicate grains trapped by EOM.

## 4. Discussion
- **Environmental Changes:** Pleistocene deposits (high-Mg calcite) contrast with Holocene aragonitic mounds, suggesting changes in lake Mg/Ca ratios over time.
- **Microbial Mediation:** Silicate grains were trapped by "sticky" EOM. Biofilms formed templates for calcite nucleation.
- **Bioconstruction:** While crystal growth was largely abiotic, the location and morphology were microbially mediated by tubular microbial mats.

## 5. Conclusions
The Mono City chimney pipes are packed with fossil microbial filaments. The presence of biofilms around rising nutrient-rich spring waters influenced chimney fabric development at nano- to micro-scales.
