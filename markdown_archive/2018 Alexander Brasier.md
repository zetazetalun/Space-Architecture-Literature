# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?

**Authors:** Alexander Brasier, David Wacey, Mike Rogerson, Paul Guagliardo, Martin Saunders, Siri Kellner, Ramon Mercedes-Martin, Tim Prior, Colin Taylor, Anna Matthews, John Reijmer

**Journal:** Geobiology (Wiley)
**Date:** 2018; 16:540–555
**DOI:** 10.1111/gbi.12292

## Abstract
Lacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. Here, we use correlative microscopy to show that microbes were living around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. A plausible interpretation is that benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) formed tubular biofilms around rising sublacustrine spring vent waters, binding calcium ions and trapping and binding detrital silicate sediment. Decay of these biofilms induced calcite precipitation. These carbonate chimneys represent a promising macroscale target in the exploration for ancient or extraterrestrial life.

## 1. Introduction
Distinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in astrobiological investigation. Free-standing chimneys composed of carbonate minerals provide a potential set of targets for terrestrial and extraterrestrial investigation (e.g., Mars).

## 2. Methods
- **Fieldwork:** Undertaken in October 2014 at Mono Lake, California.
- **Analysis:** XRD analysis, biomarker analysis (GC-MS), Focused Ion Beam (FIB) preparation of TEM samples, TEM analysis, SEM-EDS, and NanoSIMS ion mapping.

## 3. Results
### 3.1 Petrography of chimney pipes
- **Structure:** Chimneys are constructed of stacks of calcitic pipes (30–60 cm height, ~3 cm width).
- **Composition:** Pipe walls are 0.5 to 1 cm thick, constructed of columns of calcite growing radially around a 1 cm central conduit.
- **Mineralogy:** XRD data showed ~99% of chimney pipe rock is high-Mg calcite with an average magnesium content of 8.1(1)%. The remaining ~1% is quartz.

### 3.2 Microfossils
- **Type 1 Filaments:** Diameters 0.8 to 8 μm; dominated by empty sheaths.
- **Type 2 Filaments:** Diameters 10 to 14.5 μm; well-preserved trichomes with clear interior mineralisation and segmentation.
- **Coccoids:** Identified but rarer (6.8 to 15 μm diameter).

### 3.3 Chemistry
NanoSIMS and TEM-EDS show silicate nanograins (feldspar, chlorite) surrounding filaments, spatially associated with carbon and nitrogen interpreted as fossilised EOM.

## 4. Discussion
- **Microbial Mediation:** Tubular sheets of EOM from microbes inhabited zones around rising vent waters, providing preferential sites for calcite nucleation.
- **Environmental Factors:** Calcitic nature reflects low lake water Mg/Ca ratios during high lake levels. 

## 5. Conclusions
Mono City chimney pipes are packed with fossil microbial filaments. Microbial activity influenced tufa chimney fabric development at nano- and micro-scales. These findings have direct applicability to the search for ancient and extraterrestrial microbial life.
