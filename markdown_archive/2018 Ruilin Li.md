# Arching Effect of Planetary Regolith Simulant under Extraterrestrial Gravities

**Authors:** Ruilin Li, Guoqing Zhou, and Matthew R. Hall
**Journal:** Journal of Aerospace Engineering (ASCE)
**Volume/Issue:** 31(6)
**Date:** 2018
**DOI:** 10.1061/(ASCE)AS.1943-5525.0000927

## Abstract
Attention has been devoted to the construction of off-earth base construction and mineral mining in recent years. This paper studies the influence of extraterrestrial gravities on the arching effect surrounding deeply buried structures in planetary regolith using trapdoor experiments. Off-earth regoliths are physically simulated using a magnetically sensitive planetary regolith simulant, whereas the gravities are simulated by applying a uniform magnetic body force. The results show that the gravity has a significant influence on both the unloading rate for soil pressure on the trapdoor and the reduction in soil pressure. This study provides an approach to estimate the downward loading on trapdoor-like underground structures in off-earth engineering applications.

## Introduction
Off-earth base construction and mineral mining will be primary missions when humans land on planets like Mars and the Moon. However, significantly different environments and geomaterial properties (0-15m thick regolith) bring challenges to geotechnical engineering. Soil pressure and the arching effect—a redistribution of soil pressure on underground structures—play vital roles in terrestrial and extraterrestrial geotechnical design.

## Experimental Methodology
### Planetary Regolith Simulant
A magnetically sensitive simulant was developed by bonding Fe3O4 magnetic nanoparticles around quartz particles. This allowed the simulation of porosity/density-dependent weak interparticle cohesion.

### Gravity Simulation
Extraterrestrial gravities were simulated using a magnetic device consisting of a long solenoid and conjugate short solenoids. This generated a linear axial gradient magnetic field to simulate gravitational acceleration ($g_m$) from 0.3g to 2.9g.

## Results and Discussion
- **Trapdoor Displacement:** Soil pressure on the trapdoor showed significant unloading when the trapdoor was moved down. 
- **Gravity Dependency:** Under lower gravity (e.g., 0.3g), both soil pressure and normalized soil pressure unload more rapidly when moving the trapdoor down compared to higher gravity levels.
- **Unloading Stages:** The process is divided into two stages: (1) a rapid unloading stage (0 to 0.5 mm displacement) and (2) a continuous, slower unloading stage (0.5 to 2.0 mm).
- **Theoretical Solution:** A solution was proposed considering gravity-induced stress gradients and stress levels on interparticle friction. The predicted soil pressures show good agreement with experimental data, particularly under low gravity conditions.

## Conclusions
Gravity significantly affects the arching effect in planetary regolith. The soil pressure reduction increases when gravity increases, but its normalized quantity decreases. The findings imply that gravity on planets and asteroids (generally <1.0g) has an apparent influence on soil arching, which must be accounted for in the structural design of off-earth buried habitats.
