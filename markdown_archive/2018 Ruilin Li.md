# Arching Effect of Planetary Regolith Simulant under Extraterrestrial Gravities

**Ruilin Li**; **Guoqing Zhou, Ph.D.**; and **Matthew R. Hall, Ph.D.**

**Abstract:** Attention has been devoted to the construction of off-earth base construction and mineral mining in recent years. This paper studies the influence of extraterrestrial gravities on the arching effect surrounding deeply buried structures in planetary regolith using trapdoor experiments. Off-earth regoliths are physically simulated using a magnetically sensitive planetary regolith simulant, whereas the gravities are simulated by applying a uniform magnetic body force. The results show that the gravity has a significant influence on both the unloading rate for soil pressure on the trapdoor and the reduction in soil pressure. Both soil pressure and normalized soil pressure unload rapidly when moving the trapdoor down at a lower gravity. The unloading rate for soil pressure increases when gravity increases. However, that of the normalized soil pressure decreases. With further increases in gravity, the normalized soil pressure appears to reach a stable state. A theoretical solution for the soil pressure on the trapdoor is proposed which includes the effect of gravity-induced stress level and stress gradient on interparticle friction resistance. The predicted soil pressure demonstrates good agreement with the experimental results. This study provides an approach to estimate the downward loading on trapdoor-like underground structures in off-earth engineering applications.

## Introduction
Off-earth base construction and mineral mining will be the primary mission when humans first land on planets and asteroids. Significant challenges arise in the form of off-earth geotechnical engineering and mining. Previous observations have found that the lunar surface is covered by granular regolith with a thickness of 0–15 m. The gravity-related mechanical behavior of off-earth regoliths needs to be studied before landing, constructing, or mining on the Moon, Mars, or other planets and asteroids.

## Trapdoor Experiments

### Planetary Regolith Simulant
The authors developed a magnetically sensitive planetary regolith simulant to physically model off-earth regoliths. Typical characteristics simulated: (1) granular morphology; (2) fine-grained particles; and (3) porosity/density-dependent weak interparticle cohesion.

### Extraterrestrial Gravities
Extraterrestrial gravities were simulated by applying a magnetic body force to the magnetically saturated planetary regolith simulant. A magnetic device consisting of a long solenoid and a pair of conjugate short solenoids was used to generate a uniform axial magnetic field and a linear axial gradient magnetic field.

## Experimental Results

### Validation of Experimental System
Measured soil pressure demonstrated good agreement with theoretical values ($σ_{ini} = ρg_mh$). This suggests the soil pressure sensor had good performance and boundary friction effects were negligible.

### Evolution of Soil Pressure Related to Trapdoor Displacement
Results showed significant unloading when the trapdoor moved down. The reduction in soil pressure was related to gravity: significantly more soil pressure unloaded at the same displacement when gravity increased.

## Theoretical Considerations
A theoretical solution was presented to account for the effect of gravity on soil arching. The solution includes:
1. Effect of gravity on soil strength (internal friction angle $\phi$ and dilation angle $\psi$).
2. Soil arching assumptions (parabolic slip surfaces, circular stress arching trace).
3. Gravity-related soil pressure equations.

## Conclusions
1. Off-earth construction requires understanding the difference in gravity's effect on geotechnical mechanics.
2. Both soil pressure and normalized soil pressure unloaded rapidly at first, followed by a slow reduction at lower gravity.
3. The unloading rate for soil pressure increased with gravity; however, the normalized soil pressure rate decreased.
4. Gravity weakens the soil arching effect in granular planetary regolith.
5. The theoretical solution provides a reasonable prediction of soil pressure on trapdoor-like underground structures for off-earth applications.
