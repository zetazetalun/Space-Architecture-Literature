# Arching Effect of Planetary Regolith Simulant under Extraterrestrial Gravities

**Authors:** Ruilin Li; Guoqing Zhou, Ph.D.; and Matthew R. Hall, Ph.D.  
**Publication:** Journal of Aerospace Engineering, Vol. 31, Issue 6 (November 2018)  
**DOI:** 10.1061/(ASCE)AS.1943-5525.0000927

## Abstract
Attention has been devoted to the construction of off-earth base construction and mineral mining in recent years. This paper studies the influence of extraterrestrial gravities on the arching effect surrounding deeply buried structures in planetary regolith using trapdoor experiments. Off-earth regoliths are physically simulated using a magnetically sensitive planetary regolith simulant, whereas the gravities are simulated by applying a uniform magnetic body force. The results show that the gravity has a significant influence on both the unloading rate for soil pressure on the trapdoor and the reduction in soil pressure. Both soil pressure and normalized soil pressure unload rapidly when moving the trapdoor down at a lower gravity. The unloading rate for soil pressure increases when gravity increases. However, that of the normalized soil pressure decreases. With further increases in gravity, the normalized soil pressure appears to reach a stable state.

## 1. Introduction
Off-earth base construction and mineral mining will be primary missions for Moon, Mars, and asteroid exploration. Geotechnical engineering challenges arise due to significantly different environments and geomaterial properties. Lunar regolith (0–15 m thick) and Martian regolith exhibit unique porosity, density, and interparticle cohesion influenced by electrostatic charging and vacuum conditions. This study focuses on the **arching effect**, a phenomenon resulting in soil pressure redistribution on underground structures, using physical modeling under reproduced extraterrestrial gravities (0.3g to 3.0g).

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1 Planetary Regolith Simulant
A magnetically sensitive simulant was developed by bonding Fe3O4 magnetic nanoparticles around quartz particles.

### 2.2 Extraterrestrial Gravity Simulation
Gravity was simulated using a magnetic device consisting of a long solenoid (uniform field for saturation) and conjugate short solenoids (linear gradient field to simulate gravity). The simulated acceleration $g_m$ is given by:  
$g_m = g + \mu_0 \vartheta_s \frac{\partial H}{\partial z}$

### 2.3 Trapdoor Experiments
- **Model Box:** Cylindrical, 85 mm inner diameter, 500 mm height.
- **Trapdoor:** 85 x 8 mm, installed at the bottom.
- **Sensors:** Fiber Bragg grating (FBG) soil pressure sensors (range 0–10 kPa, precision ±0.16%).
- **Gravity range:** 0.3g, 0.6g, 1.0g, 1.5g, 2.0g, 2.5g, and 2.9g.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### 3.1 Soil Pressure Unloading
- Soil pressure unloads rapidly in the first stage (0–0.5 mm trapdoor displacement) and more slowly in the second stage (0.5–2.0 mm).
- Reductions in soil pressure on the trapdoor are strongly related to gravity. More soil pressure unloaded at the same trapdoor displacement when gravity increased.

### 3.2 Theoretical Solution
A theoretical solution was proposed accounting for gravity-induced stress levels and stress gradients on interparticle friction resistance. The predicted soil pressure demonstrates good agreement with experimental results, especially under low gravity conditions (< 2.0g).

## 4. Conclusions
- The unloading rate for soil pressure on buried structures increases with gravity.
- Normalized soil pressure reaches a stable state as gravity increases, implying gravity weakens the relative soil arching effect in granular planetary regolith.
- The study provides a simple approach to estimate downward loading on underground structures in off-earth engineering applications.
