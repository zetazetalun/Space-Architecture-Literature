# Arching Effect of Planetary Regolith Simulant under Extraterrestrial Gravities

**Authors:** Ruilin Li, Guoqing Zhou, and Matthew R. Hall
**Journal:** Journal of Aerospace Engineering (ASCE)
**DOI:** 10.1061/(ASCE)AS.1943-5525.0000927

## Abstract
Attention has been devoted to the construction of off-earth base construction and mineral mining in recent years. This paper studies the influence of extraterrestrial gravities on the arching effect surrounding deeply buried structures in planetary regolith using trapdoor experiments. Off-earth regoliths are physically simulated using a magnetically sensitive planetary regolith simulant, whereas the gravities are simulated by applying a uniform magnetic body force. The results show that the gravity has a significant influence on both the unloading rate for soil pressure on the trapdoor and the reduction in soil pressure. Both soil pressure and normalized soil pressure unload rapidly when moving the trapdoor down at a lower gravity.

## Introduction
Off-earth base construction and mineral mining will be the primary mission when humans first land on the Moon, Mars, or asteroids. Significantly different environments and geomaterial properties bring challenges in geotechnical engineering. The arching effect is a universal phenomenon that results in a redistribution of soil pressure on underground structures. This study uses trapdoor tests to study extraterrestrial gravity effects on regolith.

## Experimental Apparatus and Schedule

## Key Conclusions
1. **Gravity Influence:** Gravity significantly affects the unloading rate of soil pressure; lower gravity environments (like the Moon/Mars) lead to faster unloading and different arching behaviors.
2. **Soil Pressure Reduction:** The reduction in soil pressure on the trapdoor is related to gravity. Significantly more soil pressure unloaded at the same displacement when gravity increased.
3. **Geotechnical Design:** Gravity weakens the soil arching in granular regolith. Theoretical predictions considering gravity-induced stress levels and gradients match experimental results, providing a model for estimating loads on off-earth underground structures.
