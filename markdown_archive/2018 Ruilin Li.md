# Arching Effect of Planetary Regolith Simulant under Extraterrestrial Gravities

**Ruilin Li; Guoqing Zhou, Ph.D.; and Matthew R. Hall, Ph.D.**

**Abstract:** Attention has been devoted to the construction of off-earth base construction and mineral mining in recent years. This paper studies the influence of extraterrestrial gravities on the arching effect surrounding deeply buried structures in planetary regolith using trapdoor experiments. Off-earth regoliths are physically simulated using a magnetically sensitive planetary regolith simulant, whereas the gravities are simulated by applying a uniform magnetic body force. The results show that the gravity has a significant influence on both the unloading rate for soil pressure on the trapdoor and the reduction in soil pressure. Both soil pressure and normalized soil pressure unload rapidly when moving the trapdoor down at a lower gravity. The unloading rate for soil pressure increases when gravity increases. However, that of the normalized soil pressure decreases. With further increases in gravity, the normalized soil pressure appears to reach a stable state. A theoretical solution for the soil pressure on the trapdoor is proposed which includes the effect of gravity-induced stress level and stress gradient on interparticle friction resistance. The predicted soil pressure demonstrates good agreement with the experimental results. This study provides an approach to estimate the downward loading on trapdoor-like underground structures in off-earth engineering applications.

## Introduction

The limited resource reserves, increased environmental pollution, and potential nuclear threat on Earth have all promoted planetary exploration in recent decades. In addition to the Moon and Mars, attention has also been devoted to asteroids. Off-earth base construction and mineral mining will be the primary mission when humans first land on these planets and asteroids. However, significantly different environments and geomaterial properties on the surface of the Moon, Mars, or asteroids, brings a series of new challenges in the form of off-earth geotechnical engineering and mining. Previous observations have found that the lunar surface is covered by granular regolith with a thickness of 0–15 m. This lunar regolith features particle radii of 30 μm–1 mm (mean radius 70 μm), and a specific gravity of 2.9–3.4. The lunar regolith possesses a porosity/density-related weak interparticle cohesion, which is believed to result from the electrostatic charge and the ultrahigh vacuum; a similar phenomenon also exists on other planets and asteroids. A large portion of the Martian surface is covered by a fine granular regolith with a wide range particle diameters: ≤5 μm–256 mm. The exploration of asteroids, such as asteroid Eros and Ida, for example, has led to observations that the surfaces of these small bodies may also be covered by a layer of granular regolith.

The gravity-related mechanical behavior of off-earth regoliths needs to be studied before landing, constructing, or mining on the Moon, Mars, or other planets and asteroids. A series of in situ penetrometer tests was performed on the Moon during the Apollo 15 and Luna 17 missions. In situ mechanical responses of Martian regolith have also been studied. These in situ tests, however, generally involve high cost, rare opportunity, and complicated operation. An alternative approach was adopted on Earth by way of physical modeling under reproduced extraterrestrial gravities. Sture et al. (1988) demonstrated quite different behavior of Ottawa sand under microgravity by performing triaxial tests in a space shuttle. The gravity-related bearing capacity, penetration resistance, and interaction between a wheel and planetary regolith simulants have all been studied in the last few decades. Boles et al. (1997) and Goodings et al. (1992) modeled gravity-related dynamic excavation.

Soil pressure plays an important role in both terrestrial and extraterrestrial geotechnical engineering. The arching effect is a universal phenomenon that results in a redistribution of soil pressure on underground structures. It was first proposed by Terzaghi (1936) and has been widely studied. These studies demonstrated that the arching effect is closely related to the interparticle friction resistance of granular media. Because the interparticle friction is related to the stress level, some physical models for soil arching have also been performed under high gravities (45g–160g). However, these results may not be directly adapted to off-earth construction or mining because of (1) low gravity conditions, (2) porosity/density-dependent weak interparticle cohesion, and (3) fine-grained regolith. This research uses trapdoor tests to study the effect of extraterrestrial gravities on the arching effect of planetary regolith. Off-earth regoliths are physically simulated using a magnetically sensitive planetary regolith simulant. The extraterrestrial gravities are generated by applying a uniform magnetic body force to the planetary regolith simulant. Finally, a theoretical solution for soil pressure on the trapdoor is proposed after accounting for the effect of gravity-induced stress level and stress gradient on the interparticle friction resistance.

## Trapdoor Experiments

### Planetary Regolith Simulant

The authors developed a magnetically sensitive planetary regolith simulant to physically model off-earth regoliths. The following typical characteristics of the prototype planetary regoliths were simulated: (1) granular morphology; (2) fine-grained particles; and (3) porosity/density-dependent weak interparticle cohesion. The planetary regolith simulant particles were prepared by bonding Fe3O4 magnetic nanoparticles around quartz particles. The quartz particles and magnetic nanoparticles had a diameter ratio approximating 100 to enhance bonding efficiency. ### Extraterrestrial Gravities

Extraterrestrial gravities were simulated by applying a magnetic body force to the magnetically saturated planetary regolith simulant. A magnetic device was used to apply the magnetic body force. This magnetic device consisted of a long solenoid and a pair of conjugate short solenoids. The long solenoid generated a uniform axial magnetic field inside the experimental space. The intensity of this uniform magnetic field could be adjusted from 0 to 128 kA/m with a precision of 0.1 kA/m by controlling the driving current from the power supply regulators. The conjugate short solenoids generated a linear axial gradient magnetic field inside the experimental space.

The superimposed axial magnetic field inside the experimental space provided (1) a sufficiently large intensity to ensure that the planetary regolith simulant was magnetically saturated, and (2) a constant axial gradient to simulate a constant gravitational acceleration on planetary regolith simulant. A uniform axial magnetic body force f^m was applied to the planetary regolith simulant when it was magnetically saturated by the superimposed magnetic field.

### Experimental Apparatus

A trapdoor experimental apparatus was designed to be applicable for the experimental space of the magnetic device. The apparatus consisted of a cylindrical model box, a base seat, and a strip trapdoor with a shifter. The model box was made of nonmagnetic polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA). To avoid the influence of the magnetic field, a soil pressure sensor was designed using a fiber Bragg grating (FBG) sensing element. The soil pressure sensor was embedded inside the trapdoor to make sure it did not disturb in the planetary regolith simulant.

### Trapdoor Schedule

Future planetary bases may need to be buried deeply to avoid potential damage from the extreme environment of the ground surface. Minerals will also be unavoidably mined in the deep underground. Therefore, a deep-buried condition of the trapdoor was considered by controlling the ratio of the cover depth of planetary regolith simulant to trapdoor width to 15.0. This study applied extraterrestrial gravities of 0.3g–3g.

### Evolution of Soil Pressure Related to Trapdoor Displacement
As with the results of the conventional trapdoor tests, the soil pressure showed a significant unloading when the trapdoor was moved down under various extraterrestrial gravities. However, an obvious difference was that all of the soil pressures demonstrated a continuous decrease even after the trapdoor displacement reached a large value of 2.0 mm. 

### Evolution of Soil Pressure Related to Gravity
Before moving the trapdoor down, the initial geostatic soil pressure on the trapdoor increased linearly when the gravity increased. The soil pressures on the trapdoor at different trapdoor displacement levels (nonzero) all demonstrated a nonlinear increase along with the increasing gravity. With the increase in gravity, the reduction in soil pressure increased at first and was followed by nearly equivalent values at a certain trapdoor displacement. Meanwhile, the retained soil pressure increased when the gravity increased.

## Theoretical Considerations

The experimental study observed a gravity-related soil pressure on the trapdoor. Meanwhile, the planetary regolith simulant above the deeply buried trapdoor demonstrated a nonlimit equilibrium state even after the trapdoor reached a large displacement. The current theories cannot satisfactorily predict the observed soil pressures because (a) most theories only focus on the minimum and the ultimate soil pressures; and (b) they only account for the gravity in the initial geostatic soil pressure.

## Conclusions

Off-earth base construction and mineral mining will be the primary missions when humans first begin to land on other planets or asteroids. However, the extreme environments on the surfaces of these bodies pose a significant challenge to geotechnical engineering, especially because of the difference in gravity. Previous observations have found out that the surfaces of most planets and asteroids are covered by a layer of electrostatically charged granular regolith.

This paper studied the effect of gravity on the arching effect in planetary regolith above deeply buried structures with a series of trapdoor experiments. A fine-grained, magnetically sensitive planetary regolith simulant was adopted for physical modeling and simulating the weak interparticle cohesion that exists. 

The trapdoor experiments indicated that the unloading rate for soil pressure on the trapdoor and the reduction in soil pressure are both strongly affected by gravity. Both the soil pressure and normalized soil pressure unloaded rapidly at first, followed by a slow and continues reduction when the trapdoor was moved down at a lower gravity. The unloading rate for soil pressure increased when gravity increased; however, that of the normalized soil pressure decreased. With further increases in gravity, a stable and gravity-related normalized soil pressure state appeared to be reached.

A gravity-related theoretical solution for soil pressure on the trapdoor was proposed. Because the soil arching is closely related to the interparticle friction resistance, this theory considered the effects of the gravity-induced stress level and the gravity-induced stress gradient on the critical friction angle and the dilation angle.

Because the gravities on extraterrestrial planets and asteroids are generally less than 2.0g, the experimental results and the theoretical solution presented in this study provide a simple approach to estimate the downward loading on trapdoor-like underground structures in off-earth engineering.
