# Determining the yield stress of a Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite for extrusion-based 3D printing applications. ## Abstract. Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite (BSC) is a novel class of construction materials with potential use in extraterrestrial infrastructure development and more sustainable construction on Earth. This paper presents methods for testing BSC yield stress and proposes hydrodynamic and frictional yield stress models for predicting the static and dynamic yield stress of BSC mixtures. Using experimental results, a soil volume fraction range for BSC is identified that may be suitable for E3DP based on extrudability and shape stability requirements. ## 1. Introduction. Extrusion-based 3D printing (E3DP) has emerged in the construction industry as a viable alternative to form-based construction methods. BSC has potential to be used in extraterrestrial construction to support long duration missions to the moon and mars due to its adoption of In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) concepts. BSC mixtures discussed in this paper consist of lunar regolith simulant (JSC-1A), bovine blood proteins as the binder, and deionized water. ## 2. BSC-E3DP mixture design considerations. BSC strength originates from the biopolymer forming rigid cross-links that bind the soil particles together. To ensure pumpability, the BSC soil volume fraction (phi_BSC) is limited to <80% of the soil's maximum packing fraction (phi_mp). ## 3. Materials and methods. Seven BSC mixtures were evaluated with a range of proportions of JSC-1A Lunar regolith simulant, bovine blood proteins (tradename: AP920), and deionized water. Yield stress was measured using a vane geometry on an ARES-G2 Rheometer. ## 7. Conclusion. This paper found that BSC using low-cohesion soil is suitable for E3DP within a soil volume fraction range of 0.435 to 0.548. The study presented hydrodynamic and frictional models as a method for determining the static and dynamic yield stress, aiding in assessing printability and shape stability.
