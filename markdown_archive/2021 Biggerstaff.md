# Determining the yield stress of a Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite for extrusion-based 3D printing applications

**Authors:** Adrian Biggerstaff, Gerald Fuller, Michael Lepech, David Loftus
**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 305 (2021) 124730
**Published:** 8 September 2021

## Abstract
Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite (BSC) is a novel class of construction materials with potential use in extraterrestrial infrastructure development and more sustainable construction on Earth. This paper presents methods for testing BSC yield stress and proposes hydrodynamic and frictional yield stress models for predicting the static and dynamic yield stress of BSC mixtures. A soil volume fraction range for BSC is identified that may be suitable for E3DP based on extrudability and shape stability requirements.

## 1. Introduction
Extrusion-based 3D printing (E3DP) is a viable alternative to form-based construction. BSC mixtures are being explored as an alternative to cementitious materials for missions to the moon and mars due to their adoption of In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU). This paper explores the rheological properties of a BSC consisting of lunar regolith simulant, bovine blood proteins (binder), and deionized water.

## 2. BSC-E3DP Mixture Design
BSC strength originates from biopolymer cross-links binding soil particles. The research limits the BSC soil volume fraction (phi_BSC) to <80% of the soil's maximum packing fraction (phi_mp) to ensure pumpability.

## 3. Materials and Methods
- **Soil:** JSC-1A Lunar regolith simulant.
- **Binder:** Bovine blood proteins (AP920).
- **Testing:** Seven BSC mixtures evaluated using a vane geometry on an ARES-G2 Rheometer. Steady stress sweep (SS) and constant shear rate (CSR) tests were performed.

## 4. Results and Discussion
- The SS method measured static yield stress, while CSR determined dynamic yield stress.
- For phi_BSC < 0.52, the SS produced higher yield stress than CSR (indicating thixotropy).
- Above phi_BSC = 0.52, values converged within 4%.

## 5. Yield Stress Model
- **Hydrodynamic Regime:** The Chateau-Ovarlez-Trung (COT) model accurately predicts dynamic yield stress up to phi_BSC = 0.52.
- **Frictional Regime:** De Larrard’s (DL) model accurately predicts static yield stress across a wide range of soil volume fractions.

## 6. Conclusion
BSC using low-cohesion soil is suitable for E3DP within a soil volume fraction range of 0.435 to 0.548. The transition point between hydrodynamic and frictional regimes occurs at phi_BSC = 0.52.
