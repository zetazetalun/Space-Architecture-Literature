# Determining the yield stress of a Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite for extrusion-based 3D printing applications\n\n**Authors:** Adrian Biggerstaff, Gerald Fuller, Michael Lepech, David Loftus\n**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 305 (2021) 124730\n**Published:** 8 September 2021\n\n## Abstract\nBiopolymer-bound Soil Composite (BSC) is a novel class of construction materials with potential use in extraterrestrial infrastructure development and more sustainable construction on Earth. This paper presents methods for testing BSC yield stress and proposes hydrodynamic and frictional yield stress models for predicting the static and dynamic yield stress of BSC mixtures. A soil volume fraction range for BSC is identified that may be suitable for E3DP based on extrudability and shape stability requirements.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nExtrusion-based 3D printing (E3DP) has emerged as a viable alternative to form-based construction. BSC mixtures are being explored as an alternative to cementitious materials for sustainable construction and extraterrestrial missions (Moon and Mars) due to In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) concepts. This paper explores the rheological properties of a BSC consisting of lunar regolith simulant (JSC-1A), bovine blood proteins (AP920), and deionized water.\n\n## 2. BSC-E3DP Mixture Design Considerations\nBSC transitions from a paste-like state to a granular material state with increasing soil volume fraction (φBSC). To ensure pumpability, the soil volume fraction is limited to <80% of the soil's maximum packing fraction (φmp).\n\n## 3. Materials and Methods\nSeven BSC mixtures were evaluated using JSC-1A simulant and bovine blood proteins. Yield stress was measured using a vane geometry on an ARES-G2 Rheometer. Tests included Steady Stress Sweep (SS) for static yield stress and Constant Shear Rate (CSR) for dynamic yield stress.\n\n### Table 1: BSC Mixtures\n| Mix Name | Biopolymer Sol. Results and Discussion\nStatic yield stress (τy) ranged from 46 Pa to 3644 Pa. Dynamic yield stress values were lower than static values for mixtures with φBSC < 0.52. Above this value, the values converged within 4%. A transition point between hydrodynamic and frictional regimes was identified at φBSC = 0.52.\n\n## 5. Yield Stress Model\nTwo models were applied: the Chateau-Ovarlez-Trung (COT) model for the hydrodynamic regime and De Larrard’s (DL) model for the frictional regime. The DL model accurately predicts static yield stress, while the COT model predicts dynamic yield stress up to φBSC = 0.52.\n\n## 6. Conclusion\nBSC using low-cohesion soil is suitable for E3DP within a soil volume fraction range of 0.435 ≤ φBSC ≤ 0.548. This range ensures both extrudability and shape stability.
