# Determining the yield stress of a Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite for extrusion-based 3D printing applications

**Authors:** Adrian Biggerstaff, Gerald Fuller, Michael Lepech, David Loftus  
**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 305 (2021) 124730  
**Published:** 8 September 2021  

## Abstract
Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite (BSC) is a novel class of construction materials with potential use in extraterrestrial infrastructure development and more sustainable construction on Earth. Little is known about the rheological properties of this new material and its suitability for extrusion-based 3D printing (E3DP). This paper presents methods for testing BSC yield stress and proposes hydrodynamic and frictional yield stress models for predicting the static and dynamic yield stress of BSC mixtures. Using experimental results, a soil volume fraction range for BSC is identified that may be suitable for E3DP based on extrudability and shape stability requirements.

## 1. Introduction
Extrusion-based 3D printing (E3DP) has emerged as a viable alternative to form-based construction. Material rheological properties—yield stress, viscosity, and thixotropy—are critical for pumpability and shape stability. BSC mixtures are being explored as an alternative to cementitious materials due to their lower environmental impact and potential for In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) on the Moon and Mars. This paper explores a BSC consisting of lunar regolith simulant (JSC-1A), bovine blood proteins, and deionized water.

## 2. Materials and Methods
Seven BSC mixtures were evaluated using JSC-1A Lunar regolith simulant and bovine blood proteins (AP920). Testing was performed using an ARES-G2 Rheometer with a vane geometry to determine static yield stress (Steady Stress Sweep) and dynamic yield stress (Constant Shear Rate).

## 3. Key Findings
- **Printable Range:** A soil volume fraction (φBSC) between 0.435 and 0.548 was identified as suitable for E3DP, balancing the Von Mises yield criterion for shape stability against nozzle extrudability limits.
- **Rheological Models:** The study successfully applied the Chateau-Ovarlez-Trung (COT) model for the hydrodynamic regime and De Larrard’s (DL) model for the frictional regime to predict yield stresses.
- **Transition Point:** A φBSC of approximately 0.52 marks the transition from a hydrodynamic-dominated regime to a frictional-dominated regime.

## 4. Conclusion
BSC using low-cohesion soil like JSC-1A is suitable for 3D printing. The upper bound for soil volume fraction is limited by extrudability (approx. 80% of maximum packing fraction), which may result in lower compressive strength compared to high-compaction form-based methods.
