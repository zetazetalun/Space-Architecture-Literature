# Determining the yield stress of a Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite for extrusion-based 3D printing applications

**Authors:** Adrian Biggerstaff, Gerald Fuller, Michael Lepech, David Loftus
**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 305 (2021) 124730
**Published:** Elsevier Ltd.

## Abstract
Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite (BSC) is a novel class of construction materials with potential use in extraterrestrial infrastructure development and more sustainable construction on Earth. Little is known about the rheological properties of this new material and its suitability for extrusion-based 3D printing (E3DP). This paper presents methods for testing BSC yield stress and proposes hydrodynamic and frictional yield stress models for predicting the static and dynamic yield stress of BSC mixtures. Using experimental results, a soil volume fraction range for BSC is identified that may be suitable for E3DP based on extrudability and shape stability requirements.

## 1. Introduction
Extrusion-based 3D printing (E3DP) has emerged as a viable alternative to form-based construction. BSC mixtures are being explored as an alternative to cementitious materials due to their lower environmental impact and high compressive strengths. Additionally, BSC has potential for extraterrestrial construction to support long duration missions to the moon and mars due to its adoption of In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) concepts. This paper explores the rheological properties of a BSC consisting of lunar regolith simulant (JSC-1A), bovine blood proteins, and deionized water.

## 4. Results and Discussion
Static yield stress (measured via steady stress sweep) and dynamic yield stress (measured via constant shear rate) were collected. The study found that below a soil volume fraction of 0.52, BSC behaves in a hydrodynamic regime. Above 0.52, frictional forces between particles dominate.

## 7. Conclusion
Biopolymer-bound Soil Composite using low-cohesion soil is suitable for E3DP within a soil volume fraction range of 0.435 to 0.548. The lower bound ensures shape stability (minimum height), while the upper bound (80% of maximum packing fraction) ensures extrudability. Hydrodynamic (COT) and frictional (DL) models effectively predict yield stresses, aiding in material design for extraterrestrial construction.
