# Lunar Regolith Improvement by Inducing Interparticle Adhesion with Capillary Forces

**Authors:** Karol Brzeziński, Joanna Julia Sokołowska, and Bartłomiej Przybyszewski  
**Journal:** Materials 2025, 18, 2390  
**Published:** 20 May 2025  
**DOI:** 10.3390/ma18102390

## Abstract
This paper concerns the assessment of the lunar regolith ability to consolidate in the presence of liquid water and develop and sustain cohesion after drying. This type of cohesion originates from interparticle adhesion and can be potentially improved through grading modification. The research was conducted using the lunar regolith simulant (EAC-1A) reproducing the PSD of real lunar soil delivered from the Moon. LRS was subjected to water and elevated temperature (equal to the highest temperature on the Moon) to produce specimens of consolidated material, CCR (Capillary-Consolidated Regolith) and to test flexural strength. CCR developed flexural strength exceeding 0.025 MPa. Then, analogous tests were performed using LRS subjected to grinding in a disc mill prior to consolidation. It was shown that simple mechanical grinding enabled the improvement of interparticle adhesion with capillary forces, resulting in improved flexural strength of the consolidated material (0.123 MPa).

## 1. Introduction
Large-scale lunar exploration requires the creation of a stationary base ("lunar habitat"). The cost of transporting cargo from Earth is extremely high (estimated ca. USD 1.5 million per kg for upcoming missions). Scientists are opting for solutions that maximize the use of local resources (ISRU). This research proposes a method of transforming loose lunar dust into a solid material (Capillary-Consolidated Regolith) by exploiting the inherent potential of the material without the need for additional components beyond water (which could be sourced from lunar ice).

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.2 Material
EAC-1A lunar regolith simulant was used. It is a basanite material from Germany that reasonably reproduces the properties of lunar regolith.

| Mode [µm] | 187.33 | 16.25 |

### 2.3 Specimens' Preparation
*   Mix 400g dry LRS with 100g distilled water (ratio 0.25).
*   Pour into custom PTFE-coated steel molds (20mm x 20mm x 80mm).
*   Air-dry for 2h.
*   Oven-dry at 127°C for 22h.

## 3. Results and Discussion
The experiment confirmed that capillary consolidation transformed loose dust into a solid material. Grinding the regolith allowed for obtaining a more developed specific surface area (over 2.5 times larger). This resulted in significantly higher flexural strength.

## 4. Conclusions
*   Capillary forces can induce interparticle adhesion to create a solid material from loose regolith.
*   CCR produced with finer material (ground) is four times stronger than original PSD CCR.
*   The workflow allows for water recovery and reuse, minimizing consumption.
*   Potential applications include building insulation barriers, pavements, or foundation layers.
