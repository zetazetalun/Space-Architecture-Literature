# In-situ additive manufacturing with lunar regolith for lunar base construction: A review

## Abstract
Construction of lunar bases is critical to further space exploration and decrease dependency on Earth-based resources. Abundant energy, water, and minerals on the Moon favor it as an ideal candidate for building extraterrestrial habitats. This paper discusses the feasibility of using in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) of lunar regolith (LR) for additively manufactured technologies for a lunar base. This work synthesizes current research in an overview at the intellectual level for the state-of-the-art methodologies of lunar regolith utilization in additive manufacturing (AM) processes. It makes a comparison of various techniques of AM, several kinds of materials, their maturity, and unique challenges of the lunar environment: extreme temperatures and microgravity.

## 1. Introduction
The Moon, as the closest celestial body to Earth, has drawn significant interest due to its diverse resources. Dwelling building modules are more complex and require more functions and structural shapes than space stations. NASA, ESA, and CNSA have all conducted work towards these goals. However, due to the uniqueness of the lunar environment, existing technologies are not yet sophisticated enough. The challenge of transporting related materials from Earth remains unresolved.

## 3. In-situ material: Lunar regolith (LR) and lunar regolith simulant (LRS)
Lunar regolith is composed of low-titanium basalt, high-titanium basalt, highland plagioclase, and Krip basalt. The overall particle size distribution resembles a normal logarithmic curve with an average particle size of 45–100 μm. Sintering or heat treatment of lunar particles often results in a porous ceramic material with relatively poor compressive and tensile strength compared to some terrestrial materials.

## 4. Additive manufacturing process for lunar construction
### 4.1. binder/ink extrusion or jetting
- **Contour Crafting (CC):** Utilizes a computer-controlled robotic system to extrude feedstock. NASA collaborated on the ACES system for on-site construction.
- **D-shape:** Uses movable nozzles to spray adhesive on regolith surfaces. Compressive strength of 17–20 MPa achieved with LRS and magnesium salt solution.
- **Ink-jetting:** Flexible range of printing parameters; supports various ink formulations including alginate and PEG.
- **Direct Ink Writing (DIW):** Mixing LRS with a liquid matrix to prepare a slurry. Cui et al. prepared slurry with 86 wt% LRS achieving 9.83 MPa compressive strength.

### 4.2. Selective sintering/melting
- **Solar Light Sintering (SSS):** Uses sunlight via Fresnel lenses. Reached temperatures over 1100 °C.
- **Selective Laser Sintering/Melting (SLS/SLM):** Sub-millimeter printing resolution. Hardness of 670 ± 11 HV achieved.
- **Microwave Sintering (SMS):** Penetrates deeply into regolith. 2.45 GHz microwaves can penetrate to 65 cm depth.
- **Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS):** Rapid heating/cooling (1000 °C/min) in vacuum. Higher elastic modulus (8.51 GPa) than pressureless sintering.

### 4.3. Stereolithography / Digital Light Processing (SLA/DLP)
Photopolymerization offers the highest printing resolution (0.01 mm). Compressive strength reached 428.1 MPa for structural parts after debinding and sintering.

## 6. Conclusions
AM technology is a potential linchpin for lunar construction. Sintering technologies (SSS, SMS) are preferable for utilizing raw regolith for large-scale outdoor structures like shielding walls and launch pads. Liquid-based AM (SLA/DLP) is better suited for high-precision components and instrument casings despite complex post-processing needs.
