# In-situ additive manufacturing with lunar regolith for lunar base construction: A review

**Authors:** Chengwei Bao, Yanen Wang, Garth Pearce, Ray Tahir Mushtaq, Minyan Liu, Pan Zhao  
**Journal:** Applied Materials Today 41 (2024) 102456  
**Date:** October 2024

## Abstract
Construction of lunar bases is critical to further space exploration and decrease dependency on Earth-based resources. This paper discusses the feasibility of using in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) of lunar regolith (LR) for additively manufactured technologies for a lunar base. It synthesizes current research on state-of-the-art methodologies, comparing various AM techniques, materials, maturity, and challenges of the lunar environment (extreme temperatures and microgravity).

## 1. Introduction
Establishing extraterrestrial bases on the Moon is a primary priority for space agencies (NASA, ESA, CNSA). Key challenges include the prohibitive cost of transporting materials from Earth ($22,000/Kg). ISRU is essential to minimize logistics. AM is recognized as a suitable technology for extreme lunar environments due to simplicity, efficiency, and minimization of manual operations.

## 2. Bibliometric Study
Analysis of 515 publications (2004–2024) shows an overall growth trend, particularly since 2020. The USA, ESA, and China are leading research. Key research hotspots include lunar simulants, 3D printing, ISRU, and mechanical properties.

## 3. In-Situ Material: Lunar Regolith (LR) and Simulants (LRS)
- **Composition:** Low-titanium basalt, high-titanium basalt, highland plagioclase, and Krip basalt. Dominant oxides: SiO2 (>40%), Al2O3, FeO, CaO, and MgO.
- **Geotechnical Properties:** Particle size distribution (average 45–100 μm), irregular shapes, and complex phase compositions. Transitioning LR into porous ceramics often results in poor compressive/tensile strength due to cracks during sintering.
- **Simulants:** Development of LRS (e.g., JSC-1, MLS, CAS-1, FJS) is crucial for Earth-based testing due to limited real samples.

## 4. Additive Manufacturing Processes

### 4.1 Binder/Ink Extrusion or Jetting
- **Contour Crafting (CC):** Uses a robotic system to extrude regolith-based concrete. NASA's ACES system uses sulfur as an additive binder.
- **D-shape:** Sprays adhesive on regolith layers. Requires magnesium salt solutions as binders. Achieved 17–20 MPa compressive strength.
- **Ink-jetting:** High material compatibility but sensitive to low gravity and radiation impacts on organic compounds.
- **Direct Ink Writing (DIW):** Involves mixing LRS with liquid matrix/resin. Cui et al. achieved 9.83 MPa compressive strength using UV/thermal dual-curing.

### 4.2 Selective Sintering/Melting
- **Selective Solar Sintering (SSS):** Uses concentrated sunlight via Fresnel lenses. Ideal for polar regions (perpetual light). Low resolution but energy efficient.
- **Selective Laser Sintering/Melting (SLS/SLM):** High resolution (sub-millimeter). Requires high energy; challenges include powder management in vacuum/low-g.
- **Selective Microwave Sintering (SMS):** Penetrates deeply into regolith. Effective for lunar mare regions (higher ilmenite content).
- **Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS):** Rapid heating/cooling. Produced parts with 10 times higher compressive strength than typical concrete but requires complex, high-pressure facilities.

### 4.3 Stereolithography/Digital Light Processing (SLA/DLP)
Uses photopolymerized resins mixed with regolith. Offers the highest precision (0.01 mm) and high mechanical performance (up to 444 MPa compressive strength) but requires significant post-processing and Earth-supplied resins.

## 6. Conclusions and Future Outlook
AM technology is a potential linchpin for lunar construction. Integration of multiple technologies is required: binder extrusion for large structures (roads, landing pads) and SLA/DLP for high-precision components (instrument casings). Future focus areas include advanced simulation in lunar conditions, material recycling, and international standardization of regolith simulants.
