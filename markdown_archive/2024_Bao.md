# In-situ additive manufacturing with lunar regolith for lunar base construction: A review

**Authors:** Chengwei Bao, Yanen Wang, Garth Pearce, Ray Tahir Mushtaq, Minyan Liu, Pan Zhao  
**Journal:** Applied Materials Today  
**Volume/Article:** 41 (2024) 102456  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmt.2024.102456

## Abstract
Construction of lunar bases is critical to further space exploration and decrease dependency on Earth-based resources. This paper discusses the feasibility of using in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) of lunar regolith (LR) for additively manufactured technologies for a lunar base. It synthesizes current research on methodologies, maturity, and challenges like extreme temperatures and microgravity. Results compare AM techniques and post-processing treatments.

## 1. Introduction
Spacefaring nations like the USA (NASA), ESA, and China (CNSA) are prioritizing lunar base construction. High transport costs (>$22,000/kg) necessitate ISRU. Additive Manufacturing (AM) is recognized as suitable for the lunar environment due to its efficiency, customization, and minimization of manual operations.

## 3. In-situ Material: Lunar Regolith (LR) and Simulants (LRS)
- **Composition:** Low-titanium basalt, high-titanium basalt, highland plagioclase, and Krip basalt. Dominant oxides: SiO2 (>40%), FeO, Al2O3, CaO.
- **Geotechnical Properties:** Particle sizes range from hundreds of millimeters to micrometers (avg. 45-100 μm). Shapes are irregular, contributing to porous ceramic properties after sintering.
- **Simulants:** Common simulants include JSC series, MLS series, CAS, and CLRS series. Simulants are critical for Earth-based testing but differ from real regolith (e.g., lack of nano-metallic iron and presence of water/air).

## 4. Additive Manufacturing Processes for Lunar Construction

### 4.1 Extrusion/Ink-jetting
- **Contour Crafting (CC):** Uses sulfur-based concrete or regolith-binder mixtures. NASA's ACES system is an example. Challenges: sulfur sublimation in vacuum.
- **D-shape:** Selective spray of magnesium salt solution onto regolith layers. Results: Compressive strength 17-20 MPa.
- **Ink-jetting/DIW:** High-content LRS slurries with epoxy or organic binders. Compressive strengths: 90-156 MPa (with SC80 epoxy mixture).

### 4.2 Selective Sintering/Melting
- **Selective Solar Sintering (SSS):** Uses concentrated sunlight. Low resolution, but no additives required. Compressive strength: ~2.31 MPa.
- **Selective Laser Sintering/Melting (SLS/M):** High resolution (sub-millimeter). Hardness ~670 HV. Feasible for engineering parts.
- **Microwave Sintering (SMS):** Rapid heating, extracts volatiles. Compressive strength: up to 74 MPa with ilmenite additives.
- **Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS):** Rapid densification in minutes. Highest compressive strength: 328-498 MPa.

### 4.3 Stereolithography/Digital Light Processing (SLA/DLP)
- **Performance:** Highest precision (0.01-0.025 mm). Compressive strength: ~428.1 MPa. Challenges: Liquid resin storage and transport costs.

## 6. Conclusions
- AM is a linchpin for lunar construction.
- Selection of AM method depends on application: SSS/SMS/SLS for large outdoor structures (roads, shields); DIW/DLP for high-precision components (casings, tools).
- Future research must focus on advanced simulation under lunar-like conditions and material recycling.
