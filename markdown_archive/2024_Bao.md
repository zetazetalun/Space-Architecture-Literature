# In-situ additive manufacturing with lunar regolith for lunar base construction: A review

**Authors:** Chengwei Bao, Yanen Wang, Garth Pearce, Ray Tahir Mushtaq, Minyan Liu, Pan Zhao
**Journal:** Applied Materials Today 41 (2024) 102456
**Published:** October 2024

## Abstract
Construction of lunar bases is critical to further space exploration and decrease dependency on Earth-based resources. This paper discusses the feasibility of using in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) of lunar regolith (LR) for additively manufactured technologies for a lunar base. This work synthesizes current research for the state-of-the-art methodologies of lunar regolith utilization in additive manufacturing (AM) processes. It makes a comparison of various techniques of AM, several kinds of materials, their maturity, and unique challenges of the lunar environment: extreme temperatures and microgravity.

## 1. Introduction
Establishing extraterrestrial bases on the Moon is a primary priority for space agencies (NASA, ESA, CNSA). Key challenges include the prohibitive cost of transporting materials from Earth (over $22,000/Kg) and the extreme lunar environment (low gravity, high vacuum, temperature fluctuations, radiation, and meteorite impacts).

## 2. Bibliometric Study
A survey of 515 publications from 2004 to 2024 shows a significant increase in research since 2020. The USA, Europe, and China are the leading contributors.

## 3. In-situ Material: Lunar Regolith (LR) and Simulants (LRS)
LR is composed of basalt (high/low Ti), highland plagioclase, and Krip basalt. Key characteristics include:
- **Particle size:** Average 45–100 μm.
- **Composition:** Primarily oxides (SiO2 > 40%), plus FeO, Al2O3, CaO, MgO, and TiO2.
- **Simulants:** Common LRS include JSC-1, MLS series (USA), CAS/CLRS series (China), and FJS series (Japan).

## 4. Additive Manufacturing Processes
### 4.1. Binder/Ink Extrusion or Jetting
- **Contour Crafting (CC):** Uses robotic systems to extrude concrete-like feedstock. NASA's ACES system is a key example. Challenges include sulfur sublimation in vacuum.
- **D-shape:** Sprays adhesive onto powder layers. Feasible for large-scale components (e.g., Radiolaria structures).
- **Ink-jetting:** High material compatibility; uses evaporators and binders like PLGA.
- **Direct Ink Writing (DIW):** Involves mixing LRS with liquid matrix/binder. Requires auxiliary curing (UV/heat).

### 4.2. Selective Sintering/Melting
- **Solar Light Sintering (SSS):** Uses concentrated sunlight (Fresnel lenses). Low energy demand but low precision and dependent on lighting conditions.
- **Selective Laser Sintering/Melting (SLS/SLM):** High resolution (sub-millimeter). Effective for engineering parts but high energy consumption.
- **Microwave Sintering (SMS):** Rapid heating; utilizes nanophase-Fe0 for coupling. Deep penetration (up to 65 cm).
- **Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS):** Rapid heating/cooling; produces parts with superior density and strength (up to 10x concrete).

### 4.3. Stereolithography (SLA/DLP)
Uses photopolymerized grout. Highest precision (0.01 mm) but limited by liquid feedstock storage and post-processing requirements.

## 6. Conclusions
- AM is a linchpin for lunar exploration.
- Different technologies suit different applications: Large-scale (CC, D-shape) vs. High-precision components (SLA, DLP).
- Future research must focus on material recycling, automation integration (robotic arms), and testing under simulated vacuum/low-gravity conditions.
