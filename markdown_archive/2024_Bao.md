# In-situ additive manufacturing with lunar regolith for lunar base construction: A review

**Authors:** Chengwei Bao, Yanen Wang, Garth Pearce, Ray Tahir Mushtaq, Minyan Liu, Pan Zhao  
**Journal:** Applied Materials Today  
**Volume:** 41  
**Year:** 2024  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.apmt.2024.102456

## Abstract
Construction of lunar bases is critical to further space exploration and decrease dependency on Earth-based resources. This paper discusses the feasibility of using in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) of lunar regolith (LR) for additively manufactured technologies for a lunar base. This work synthesizes current research for the state-of-the-art methodologies of lunar regolith utilization in additive manufacturing (AM) processes. It makes a comparison of various techniques of AM, several kinds of materials, their maturity, and unique challenges of the lunar environment: extreme temperatures and microgravity.

## 1. Introduction
The Moon possesses diverse resources such as water, oxygen, metals, and silicon. Establishing extraterrestrial bases on the Moon would lay the foundation for future deep space exploration. NASA, ESA, and CNSA have all conducted work towards these goals. However, the uniqueness of the lunar environment (low gravity, vacuum, temperature fluctuations, radiation, and micrometeoroids) requires sophisticated technologies. Additive manufacturing (AM) or 3D printing is recognized as a suitable technology for these extreme environments due to its simplicity, efficiency, and ability to customize products with minimal feedstock requirements.

## 2. Bibliometric Study
A bibliometric analysis using Web of Science (WOS) data from 2004 to 2024 identified 515 related publications. Research on lunar construction has shown significant growth since 2020. Major research areas include lunar simulants, 3D printing, ISRU, and mechanical properties. The USA and European countries lead in citations, while China has rapidly increased its publication output.

## 3. In-situ Material: Lunar Regolith (LR) and Simulants (LRS)
- **Composition:** LR is composed of basalt (high/low Ti), highland plagioclase, and Krip basalt. Mineral debris includes olivine, plagioclase, clinopyroxene, and ilmenite.
- **Particle Size:** Average size ranges from 45 to 100 μm. Particles <1 mm are defined as lunar dust or fines.
- **Preparation of Simulants:** Due to the limited availability of real lunar soil, simulants like JSC series, MLS series, and CAS-1 are used. They attempt to replicate mineral content and particle size distribution but often differ in air and water content.

## 4. Additive Manufacturing Processes for Lunar Construction

### 4.1. Binder/Ink Extrusion or Jetting
- **Contour Crafting (CC):** Developed at USC, uses robotic systems to extrude feedstock. It can utilize sulfur as an additive for concrete-like structures.
- **D-shape:** Uses movable nozzles to spray adhesive onto LR layers. It can produce large-scale components like "Radiolaria" structures.
- **Ink-jetting:** Includes extrusion and control systems using various ink formulations (alginate, gelatin, PEG). 
- **Direct Ink Writing (DIW):** Involves mixing LRS with a liquid matrix to prepare a slurry. Cui et al. prepared slurries with up to 86 wt% LRS content.

### 4.2. Selective Sintering/Melting
- **Solar Light Sintering (SSS):** Uses concentrated sunlight via Fresnel lenses. Achieving temperatures >1100 °C is possible.
- **Selective Laser Sintering/Melting (SLS/SLM):** High resolution (sub-millimeter). Fiber lasers can manufacture regolith-based structures with high geometric accuracy.
- **Microwave Sintering (SMS):** Microwaves penetrate deeply into LR, heating it through coupling with nanophase-Fe0.
- **Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS):** Rapid heating using pulse current in a vacuum. Produces high-density parts with superior strength.

### 4.3. Stereolithography (SLA) / Digital Light Processing (DLP)
- Uses photopolymerizable grout. Offers the highest printing resolution (0.01 mm for SLA, 0.025 mm for DLP). Suitable for high-precision engineering components.

## 6. Conclusions
- AM technology is critical for sustainable lunar base construction.
- Integrated multiple manufacturing technologies are required: extrusion for large-scale shielding and sintering/vat polymerization for precision parts.
- Challenges remain in material consistency, apparatus robustness in vacuum, and reliance on Earth-supplied additives.
