# Rapid formation of high-performance lunar regolith composite via combined mold pressing and thermocuring processing with low content of resin-based additives

**Authors:** Rihan Zhang, Tongcai Wang, Tingting Zhao, Gong Wang  
**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 455 (2024) 139132  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.conbuildmat.2024.139132

## Abstract
Establishing lunar bases is pivotal for furthering lunar research and propelling deep space exploration. Lunar regolith based in-site construction is identified as the most feasible method for lunar structures. This study proposes a novel way for in-site lunar regolith construction by utilizing a small amount of resin-based binding material and a combined processing method of molding and thermosetting. A specific thermosetting resin is developed to achieve full solidification of the mixed material at lunar daytime temperatures of approximately 120 °C, addressing the high energy consumption associated with the sintering process. Mold pressing is used to provide the rapid pre-forming of mixed materials and greatly reduce the proportion of additives. 

## Key Parameters and Materials
- **Simulant:** Low-titanium basaltic lunar regolith simulant (CLRS-1).
- **Binder:** Epoxy resin mixture (Component A) and Acrylic Ester mixture (Component B).
- **Process:** Mold pressing at 22 MPa for 2 minutes followed by thermal curing at 120 °C (ambient lunar daytime temperature).
- **Binder Ratios:** Investigated from 1 wt% to 5 wt%.

## Results and Discussion
- **Mechanical Performance:** 
  - 2 wt% binder: Compressive strength > 26 MPa.
  - 5 wt% binder: Compressive strength > 100 MPa.
  - Curing for over 30 hours leads to complete curing and maximum strength.
- **Thermal Properties:** 
  - Thermal conductivity at 119.5 °C: ~0.371 W/(m·K).
  - Thermal diffusivity: 0.2695 mm²/s.
- **Vacuum Stability:** The resin formulations showed stability under vacuum conditions (3.2 x 10⁻² Pa) with minimal mass loss (< 1.5%).

## Conclusion
The proposed method offers advantages of high in-situ utilization rate, low energy consumption, and rapid formation rate. By utilizing the Moon's natural thermal cycle for curing, the energy demand for high-temperature sintering is eliminated, while maintaining structural performance suitable for large-scale lunar infrastructure.
