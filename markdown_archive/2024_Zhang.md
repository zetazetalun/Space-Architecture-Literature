# Rapid formation of high-performance lunar regolith composite via combined mold pressing and thermocuring processing with low content of resin-based additives

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 455 (2024) 139132

**Authors:** Rihan Zhang, Tongcai Wang, Tingting Zhao, Gong Wang

## Abstract
Establishing lunar bases is pivotal for furthering lunar research and propelling deep space exploration. Lunar regolith based in-site construction is identified as the most feasible method for lunar structures. This study proposes a novel way for in-site lunar regolith construction by utilizing a small amount of resin-based binding material and a combined processing method of molding and thermosetting. A specific thermosetting resin is developed to achieve full solidification of the mixed material at lunar daytime temperatures of approximately 120 °C, addressing the high energy consumption associated with the sintering process. It was found that the formed part with a binder proportion of 2 wt% could achieve a compressive strength exceeding 26 MPa and a low thermal conductivity of about 0.371 W/(m·K). The proposed method has advantages of high in-situ utilization rate, low-energy consumption and rapid formation rate.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar regolith, widely distributed and easily accessible on the Moon, is an ideal candidate for in-situ manufacturing of various facilities. Recent exploration goals have shifted from understanding the Moon to utilizing its resources. This study focuses on reducing additive content and energy requirements for lunar construction by combining pre-pressing and low-temperature thermal curing.

## 2. Experimental Setup and Method

### 2.1. Experimental Materials
- **Simulant:** Low-titanium basaltic lunar regolith simulant (CLRS-1), simulated from Apollo 14 samples.
- **Binder:** A thermosetting resin mixture consisting of Component A (Epoxy Resin mixture) and Component B (Acrylic Ester mixture).

### 2.2. Forming Method
1. Sieving of CLRS-1 (< 0.1 mm) and drying at 60 °C.
2. Mixing of resin components.
3. Uniform combination of resin with regolith powder.
4. Molding under a pressure of 22 MPa for 2 minutes.
5. Thermal curing at 120 °C to simulate lunar daytime conditions.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### 3.1. Mechanical Performance
- **Binder content influence:** Increasing the binder content from 1 wt% to 5 wt% significantly improved compressive strength.
- **2 wt% binder:** Achieved > 26 MPa after 30+ hours of curing.
- **5 wt% binder:** Achieved compressive strengths exceeding 100 MPa.
- **Wettability:** Mixtures with a mass ratio of Components A:B between 5:5 and 6:4 showed optimal integration and strength.

## 4. Conclusion
The study validated a feasible technique for lunar in-situ construction. The method offers high forming speed, low energy consumption, and high adaptability to the lunar environment. With only 2 wt% additive, the material meets the general strength requirements (> 4 MPa) for lunar building materials.
