# Recent advances on ISRU technologies and study of microgravity impact on blood cells for deep space exploration

**Authors:** Giacomo Cao et al.
**Journal:** Frontiers in Space Technologies
**Date:** 19 July 2023
**DOI:** 10.3389/frspt.2023.1146461

## Abstract
The objective of this work is to analyze current advancements in technology development for deep space exploration and colonization. First, a method for producing tangible goods suited for industrial or civil installations on the Moon, Mars, or asteroids, using in situ available regolith as the main resource, is discussed. Next, an ISRU process for producing consumables (oxygen, food, fuels) on Mars is analyzed. Finally, the potential for cultivating Spirulina platensis for nourishment and the impact of microgravity on blood cells are examined.

## 1. Manufacturing physical assets in extraterrestrial environments
Until 2011, few works addressed the development of processes for the fabrication of protection structures making use of available in situ Lunar resources. Cao et al. developed an In Situ Fabrication and Repair (ISFR) process based on combustion synthesis-type reactions (SHS) to obtain ceramic composites from regolith simulants. 

### The SHS Process Stages:
1. **Regolith Extraction:** From lunar or martian soil.
2. **Enrichment:** Magnetically separating components like iron oxide.
3. **Mixing:** Combining enriched regolith with aluminum powder (carried from Earth).
4. **Combustion Synthesis:** Spontaneous propagation of a high-temperature (1,800–2,000 K) combustion wave upon local ignition.

**Key Result:** Compressive strength values of the obtained SHS samples reached 25.8–27.2 MPa, which is significantly higher than earlier geothermite-based processes (10–18 MPa).

## 2. Integrated ISRU process for Mars missions
A synergistic combination of ISRU and Environmental Control and Life Support Systems (ECLSS) is proposed to minimize the mass of resources transported from Earth.

## 3. Optical characterization of regolith for Energy Storage
Thermal energy storage (TES) is critical for surviving lunar nights. Regolith can be processed into bulk bodies to improve thermal properties. Experiments using Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) on JSC-1A lunar regolith simulant showed that sintering temperatures (700–900°C) significantly increase spectral solar absorptance (0.88–0.92) compared to raw powder. This makes sintered regolith blocks suitable for solar receivers and thermal storage in lunar architectures.

## 4. Biological ISRU: Spirulina platensis
The study demonstrated that *Spirulina platensis* could potentially grow better under simulated Martian conditions (CO2-rich atmosphere and microgravity) than under terrestrial conditions, with productivity reaching around 11 g L−1 day−1. This biomass provides a high-protein food source for the crew.

## 5. Conclusions
- Novel SHS-based techniques can produce structural elements on Mars and Moon.
- Integrated ISRU/ECLSS systems can achieve high levels of self-sufficiency.
- Sintered regolith offers superior optical properties for solar energy harvesting.
