# Recent advances on ISRU technologies and study of microgravity impact on blood cells for deep space exploration

**Authors:** Giacomo Cao, Alessandro Concas, Roberto Orrù, Roberta Licheri, Elisa Sani, Aldo Dell’Oro, Giacomo Fais, Cristina Manis, Alessia Manca, Giuseppe Uras, Pierluigi Caboni, Antonio Mario Locci, Alberto Cincotti, Nicola Lai, Terenzio Congiu, Gavino Faa, Massimo Pisu, Gavin Brelstaff, and Antonella Pantaleo

**Published:** 19 July 2023
**Journal:** Frontiers in Space Technologies
**DOI:** 10.3389/frspt.2023.1146461

## Abstract
This work analyzes current advancements in technology development for deep space exploration and colonization. It covers methods for producing tangible goods using in situ available regolith via self-propagating high-temperature synthesis (SHS), theoretical analysis of combustion synthesis under different gravity conditions, and processes for sustaining human missions on Mars by integrating ISRU and ECLSS. Additionally, it investigates the optical characterization of sintered lunar regolith for thermal energy storage and the impact of microgravity on human red blood cells.

## 1. Introduction
In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) and In Situ Fabrication and Repair (ISFR) technologies are essential for space exploration. ISRU provides life support consumables, propellants, construction materials, and energy. ISFR focuses on fabrication and repair at the operational location. The paper highlights an Italian task force's efforts to use SHS for structural elements on the Moon and Mars.

## 2. Manufacturing Physical Assets in Extraterrestrial Environments
The authors developed an ISFR process to manufacture facilities using regolith as the main resource. The process involves:
1. Regolith extraction.
2. Enrichment (e.g., magnetic separation of iron oxide).
3. Mixing with aluminum powder (reducing agent).
4. Ignition to trigger Self-propagating High-temperature Synthesis (SHS).

SHS allows for the production of ceramics and composites at high temperatures (1,800–2,000 K) with low energy input. Structural elements produced reached compressive strengths of 25.8–27.2 MPa.

## 3. ISRU for Mars Missions

## 4. Optical Characterization of Lunar Regolith Simulant
Sintered samples of JSC-1A lunar regolith simulant were prepared via Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) at 700°C and 900°C. Sintering increased the relative density to 98% (at 900°C) and improved solar absorptance (0.88–0.92), making it suitable for solar energy receivers and thermal storage.

## 5. Conclusions
- SHS can produce structural elements with higher compressive strength than previous methods.
- Integrated ISRU-ECLSS systems can significantly reduce mission payload.
- SPS processing improves the optical properties of lunar regolith for energy applications.
- Microgravity significantly impacts red blood cell morphology and oxidative stress.
