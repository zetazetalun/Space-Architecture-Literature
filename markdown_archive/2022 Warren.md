# Effect of Sintering Temperature on Microstructure and Mechanical Properties of Molded Martian and Lunar Regolith

**Authors:** Peter Warren, Nandhini Raju, Hossein Ebrahimi, Milos Krsmanovic, Seetha Raghavan, Jayanta Kapat, Ranajay Ghosh
**Affiliation:** University of Central Florida, Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering
**Date:** May 17, 2022

## Abstract
Cylindrical specimens of Martian and Lunar regolith simulants were molded using a salt water binder and sintered at various temperatures to compare microstructure, mechanical properties, and shrinkage. Results show that the optimal sintering temperature for both regolith types is between 1100°C and 1200°C. Compressive strength at these temperatures was found sufficient for the construction of extraterrestrial structures, demonstrating potential for extra-terrestrial masonry and Binder Jet Technique (BJT) additive manufacturing.

## 1. Introduction
The high cost of transporting materials from Earth drives the need for in-situ resource utilization (ISRU). Martian and Lunar regolith are primary candidates for construction material due to their abundance and ability to withstand thermal cycles and radiation. This work investigates the impact of sintering on regolith parts bound with a sustainable salt-water binder.

## 2. Materials and Methods
- **Simulants:** MGS-1 (Mars Global Simulant) and LMS-1 (Lunar Mare Simulant).
- **Binder:** Pure distilled H2O mixed with NaCl (6.25% concentration).
- **Manufacturing:** Injection molding into 3D-printed PLA molds to replicate the 'green state' of Binder Jet Technique (BJT).
- **Sintering Protocol:** Pre-sintering at 200°C for 1 hour, followed by high-temperature sintering at 1000°C, 1100°C, or 1200°C for 1 hour.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Microstructure:** At 1200°C, samples showed a glassy encasement of rocky particles. The bond is driven by the smaller particles (less than 50 microns).
- **Compression Testing:** Samples sintered at 1000°C and 1100°C were too brittle for significant testing. At 1200°C, Martian samples showed slightly higher compressive strength (25.46 MPa) than Lunar samples (21.73 MPa).
- **Shrinkage:** Significant shrinkage occurred at 1200°C, with vertical shrinkage (16-18%) being greater than radial shrinkage (6-10%).

## 4. Conclusion
Martian and Lunar regolith can be implemented in BJT-style additive manufacturing using a salt-water binder. The resulting material strength is comparable to Earth masonry bricks, making it suitable for load-bearing structures. Optimal sintering occurs between 1100°C and 1200°C, though anisotropic shrinkage must be accounted for in design.
