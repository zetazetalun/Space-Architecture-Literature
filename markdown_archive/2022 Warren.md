# Effect of Sintering Temperature on Microstructure and Mechanical Properties of Molded Martian and Lunar Regolith

**Authors:** Peter Warren, Nandhini Raju, Hossein Ebrahimi, Milos Krsmanovic, Seetha Raghavan, Jayanta Kapat, Ranajay Ghosh
**Affiliation:** University of Central Florida, Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering
**Date:** May 17, 2022 (arXiv Preprint)

## Abstract
Cylindrical specimens of Martian and Lunar regolith simulants were molded using a salt water binder and sintered at various temperatures (1000°C, 1100°C, and 1200°C) for comparing microstructure, mechanical properties, and shrinkage. The specimens were fabricated via injection molding designed to replicate the green stage of Binder Jet Technique (BJT). Results show that for both Martian and Lunar regolith, the optimal sintering temperature was between 1100°C and 1200°C. Compressive strength was determined to be sufficient for extraterrestrial structures, demonstrating regolith potential as parent material for BJT additive manufacturing.

## 1. Introduction
The cost of transporting materials to the Moon or Mars drives the need for in-situ resource utilization (ISRU). Martian and Lunar regolith are primary candidates for construction due to their abundance and ability to withstand thermal cycles and radiation. This work investigates the impact of sintering temperature on regolith parts made with a sustainable salt-water binder.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1. Regolith Simulant
- **Martian Simulant:** MGS-1 (Mars Global Simulant)
- **Lunar Simulant:** LMS-1 (Lunar Mare Simulant)

### 2.2. ISRU Binder
A solution of distilled H2O mixed with 6.25% NaCl (salt) was used as the binder. This simplistic material is effective for ISRU as salts and water deposits exist on both Mars and the Moon.

### 2.3. Manufacturing & Molding
Samples were cylindrical (1 inch length, 0.5 inch diameter) molded in 3D-printed PLA molds. The regolith-to-binder ratio was 3.5:1. 

### 2.4. Sintering Protocol
- **Pre-sintering:** 200°C for 1 hour.
- **Sintering:** 1000°C, 1100°C, or 1200°C for 1 hour.
- **Ramp rate:** 25°C per minute.

## 3. Results and Discussion
### 3.1. Microstructure and Mineral Composition
At 1200°C, samples exhibited a glassy encasement of rocky particles. Glass-rich basalt and pyroxene reach melting temperatures within the sintering range (984°C – 1260°C).

### 3.2. Mechanical Testing
Samples sintered at 1000°C and 1100°C were too brittle for significant testing. Samples sintered at 1200°C were sturdy enough to provide reliable data, showing strengths comparable to terrestrial brick (6-82 MPa).

### 3.3. Shrinkage Behavior
Significant shrinkage occurred between 1100°C and 1200°C. Lunar samples experienced more vertical shrinkage (18.13%) than Martian samples (16.03%), likely due to the higher glass-rich basalt content in LMS-1.

## 4. Conclusion
Martian and Lunar regolith can be implemented in Binder Jetting additive manufacturing using a 6.25% salt-water binder. Sintering at 1200°C yields compressive strengths (21-25 MPa) suitable for load-bearing structures such as foundations and buildings. Optimal properties are achieved through controlled sintering that accounts for the mineral-specific melting points and resulting shrinkage.
