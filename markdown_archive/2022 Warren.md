# Effect of Sintering Temperature on Microstructure and Mechanical Properties of Molded Martian and Lunar Regolith

**Authors:** Peter Warren, Nandhini Raju, Hossein Ebrahimi, Milos Krsmanovic, Seetha Raghavan, Jayanta Kapat, Ranajay Ghosh
**Affiliation:** University of Central Florida, Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering
**Date:** May 17, 2022

## Abstract
Cylindrical specimens of Martian and Lunar regolith simulants were molded using a salt water binder and sintered at various temperatures for comparing microstructure, mechanical properties and shrinkage. The experimental protocol used Mars global simulant (MGS-1) and Lunar mare simulant (LMS-1). Specimens were fabricated via injection molding, designed to replicate typical masonry units and the green stage of the Binder Jet Technique (BJT). Results show optimal sintering temperatures between 1100°C and 1200°C. Compressive strength was determined to be sufficient for extraterrestrial structural construction.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) is driven by the prohibitive cost of transporting raw materials to the Moon or Mars. Martian and Lunar regolith are primary candidates for construction material due to their abundance and ability to withstand thermal cycles and radiation. This work investigates the effect of sintering temperature on green regolith parts using a sustainable salt water binder.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1. Regolith Simulant
- **Martian:** MGS-1 (Mars Global Simulant)
- **Lunar:** LMS-1 (Lunar Mare Simulant)

### 2.2. ISRU Binder
- Mixture of distilled H2O and NaCl (6.25% salt solution).

### 2.3. Manufacturing Method
- **Molding:** Injection molding using 3D-printed PLA molds.
- **Ratio:** 3.5 parts regolith to 1 part binder.
- **Sintering:** Tabletop furnace (Rapidfire Standard Pro I). Pre-sintering at 200°C for 1 hour, followed by sintering at 1000°C, 1100°C, and 1200°C for 1 hour.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### 3.1. Mechanical Properties
Samples sintered at 1000°C and 1100°C were too brittle for significant testing. Samples sintered at 1200°C provided usable data for compression modulus and strength.

**Table 1: Material properties of sintered Lunar and Martian samples (at 1200°C)**

| Property | Martian Sample | Lunar Sample |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1000°C Radial Shrinkage | 1.06% | 0.84% |
| 1000°C Height Shrinkage | 0.57% | 1.16% |
| 1100°C Radial Shrinkage | 2.34% | 1.85% |
| 1100°C Height Shrinkage | 2.52% | 3.15% |
| 1200°C Radial Shrinkage | 10.54% | 6.17% |
| 1200°C Height Shrinkage | 16.03% | 18.13% |
| 1200°C Compression Modulus (MPa) | 67.80 | 55.73 |
| 1200°C Compressive Strength (MPa) | 25.46 | 21.73 |

### 3.2. Failure Mode
Failure occurred via rapid propagation of vertical cracks. Digital Image Correlation (DIC) indicated brittle failure behavior consistent with ceramic materials. 

### 3.3. Shrinkage Behavior
Significant shrinkage occurred between 1100°C and 1200°C. Lunar samples experienced more vertical shrinkage than Martian samples, likely due to a higher percentage of glass-rich basalt (32% vs 22.9%), which has a lower melting point.

## 4. Conclusion
Martian and Lunar regolith can be implemented in binder jetting processes using salt-water binders. Optimal sintering occurs between 1100°C and 1200°C, yielding compressive strengths comparable to Earth-based brick (6 MPa - 82 MPa). This method is well-suited for foundations, structures, and miscellaneous components in extraterrestrial environments.
