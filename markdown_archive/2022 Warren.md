# Effect of Sintering Temperature on Microstructure and Mechanical Properties of Molded Martian and Lunar Regolith

**Authors:** Peter Warren, Nandhini Raju, Hossein Ebrahimi, Milos Krsmanovic, Seetha Raghavan, Jayanta Kapat, Ranajay Ghosh

**Affiliation:** University of Central Florida, Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering

**Date:** May 17, 2022 (arXiv:2205.06855v1)

## Abstract
Cylindrical specimens of Martian and Lunar regolith simulants were molded using a salt water binder and sintered at various temperatures to compare microstructure, mechanical properties, and shrinkage. Results show that for both regolith types, the optimal sintering temperature is between 1100°C and 1200°C. Compressive strength at these temperatures was determined to be sufficient for extraterrestrial construction, demonstrating potential for regolith as a parent material for Binder Jet Technique (BJT) additive manufacturing.

## 1. Introduction
Transporting materials from Earth is cost-prohibitive, driving the need for in-situ resource utilization (ISRU). Martian and Lunar regolith are primary candidates for construction due to their abundance. This study investigates the impact of sintering temperature on 'green' regolith parts (molded with a saltwater binder) to understand microstructural evolution, shrinkage, and strength.

## 2. Materials and Methods
- **Simulants:** MGS-1 (Mars Global Simulant) and LMS-1 (Lunar Mare Simulant).
- **Binder:** Pure distilled H2O mixed with NaCl (saltwater). A ratio of 6.25% NaCl was found to improve binding stability in the green state.
- **Manufacturing:** Injection molding into 3D-printed PLA molds (1 inch length, 0.5 inch diameter).
- **Sintering Protocol:** Presintering at 200°C for 1 hour, followed by high-temperature sintering at 1000°C, 1100°C, and 1200°C for 1 hour.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### 3.1. Compression Testing
Samples sintered at 1000°C and 1100°C remained too brittle for reliable testing. Only samples sintered at 1200°C provided significant data.

### 3.3. Failure Mechanism
Failure occurred through rapid propagation of vertical cracks, typical of brittle ceramic materials. Digital Image Correlation (DIC) indicated anisotropic behavior, with vertical bonding potentially stronger due to gravitational forces during sintering.

## 4. Conclusion
Sintered Martian and Lunar regolith exhibit compressive strengths (21–25 MPa) comparable to terrestrial brick, making them suitable for load-bearing structures. Optimal sintering requires balancing strength gains against significant shrinkage (up to 20%), which is largely driven by the presence of glass-rich basalt and pyroxene components reaching their melting points.
