# Building a lunar base with 3D printing\n\n**Date:** January 31, 2013\n\n**Source:** Phys.org (provided by European Space Agency)\n\nSetting up a lunar base could be made much simpler by using a 3D printer to build it from local materials. Industrial partners including renowned architects Foster + Partners have joined with ESA to test the feasibility of 3D printing using lunar soil.\n\n\"Terrestrial 3D printing technology has produced entire structures,\" said Laurent Pambaguian, heading the project for ESA. \"Our industrial team investigated if it could similarly be employed to build a lunar habitat.\"\n\n### Design Concept\nFoster + Partners devised a weight-bearing 'catenary' dome design with a cellular structured wall to shield against micrometeoroids and space radiation, incorporating a pressurised inflatable to shelter astronauts. A hollow closed-cell structure – reminiscent of bird bones – provides a good combination of strength and weight.\n\nThe base's design was guided in turn by the properties of 3D-printed lunar soil, with a 1.5 tonne building block produced as a demonstration.\n\nOnce assembled, the inflated domes are covered with a layer of 3D-printed lunar regolith by robots to help protect the occupants against space radiation and micrometeoroids.\n\n### Printing Technology\n\"3D printing offers a potential means of facilitating lunar settlement with reduced logistics from Earth,\" added Scott Hovland of ESA's human spaceflight team.\n\nThe UK’s Monolite supplied the D-Shape printer for ESA's 3D-printed lunar base study, with a mobile printing array of nozzles on a 6 m frame to spray a binding solution onto a sand-like building material. 3D ‘printouts’ are built up layer by layer.\n\n#### Material Process:\n1.  **Preparation:** Simulated lunar material is mixed with magnesium oxide. This turns it into 'paper' that can be printed with.\n2.  **Structural Binding:** For structural 'ink', a binding salt is applied which converts the material to a stone-like solid.\n\nMonolite founder Enrico Dini explained: \"Our current printer builds at a rate of around 2 m per hour, while our next-generation design should attain 3.5 m per hour, completing an entire building in a week.\"\n\n### Challenges and Environment\nItalian space research firm Alta SpA worked with Pisa-based engineering university Scuola Superiore Sant'Anna on adapting 3D printing techniques to a Moon mission and ensuring process quality control. The effect of working in a vacuum was also assessed.\n\n\"The process is based on applying liquids but, of course, unprotected liquids boil away in vacuum,\" said Giovanni Cesaretti of Alta. \"So we inserted the 3D printer nozzle beneath the regolith layer. We found small 2 mm-scale droplets stay trapped by capillary forces in the soil, meaning the printing process can indeed work in vacuum.\"\n\n### Future Considerations\n*   **Material Sourcing:** Basaltic rock from a volcano in central Italy was found to have a 99.8% resemblance to lunar soil.\n*   **Dust and Temperature:** Controlling lunar dust (hazardous to breathe) and thermal factors require further study.\n*   **Location:** 3D printing works best at room temperature. The lunar poles offer the most moderate temperature range for potential settlement.
