# Building a lunar base with 3D printing

**January 31, 2013**

(Phys.org)—Setting up a lunar base could be made much simpler by using a 3D printer to build it from local materials. Industrial partners including renowned architects Foster + Partners have joined with ESA to test the feasibility of 3D printing using lunar soil.

"Terrestrial 3D printing technology has produced entire structures," said Laurent Pambaguian, heading the project for ESA. "Our industrial team investigated if it could similarly be employed to build a lunar habitat."

Foster + Partners devised a weight-bearing 'catenary' dome design with a cellular structured wall to shield against micrometeoroids and space radiation, incorporating a pressurised inflatable to shelter astronauts. A hollow closed-cell structure – reminiscent of bird bones – provides a good combination of strength and weight.

The base's design was guided in turn by the properties of 3D-printed lunar soil, with a 1.5 tonne building block produced as a demonstration.

### Construction Process
Once assembled, the inflated domes are covered with a layer of 3D-printed lunar regolith by robots to help protect the occupants against space radiation and micrometeoroids.

"3D printing offers a potential means of facilitating lunar settlement with reduced logistics from Earth," added Scott Hovland of ESA's human spaceflight team.

The UK’s Monolite supplied the D-Shape printer for ESA's 3D-printed lunar base study, with a mobile printing array of nozzles on a 6 m frame to spray a binding solution onto a sand-like building material. 3D ‘printouts’ are built up layer by layer.

**Material and Mechanism:**
- **Simulated Material:** Lunar material mixed with magnesium oxide to turn it into 'paper'.
- **Structural 'Ink':** A binding salt is applied which converts the material to a stone-like solid.
- **Current Speed:** 2 m per hour.
- **Next-generation Speed:** 3.5 m per hour (capable of completing a building in a week).

### Environmental Challenges
Italian space research firm Alta SpA worked with the Scuola Superiore Sant'Anna on adapting techniques to a Moon mission and ensuring process quality control. 

**Vacuum Conditions:** Liquids typically boil away in a vacuum. The team discovered that by inserting the 3D printer nozzle 2 mm beneath the regolith layer, capillary forces keep the droplets trapped, allowing the process to function in a vacuum.

**Simulant Source:** Basaltic rock from a volcano in central Italy was found to have a 99.8% resemblance to lunar soil.

**Future Considerations:**
- Control of hazardous lunar dust.
- Thermal factors (3D printing works best at room temperature, while lunar temperatures fluctuate wildly).
- Settlement location: Lunar poles offer the most moderate temperature ranges.
