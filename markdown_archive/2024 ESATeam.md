# Building a lunar base with 3D printing

**January 31, 2013**

Setting up a lunar base could be made much simpler by using a 3D printer to build it from local materials. Industrial partners including renowned architects **Foster + Partners** have joined with **ESA** to test the feasibility of 3D printing using lunar soil.

"Terrestrial 3D printing technology has produced entire structures," said Laurent Pambaguian, heading the project for ESA. "Our industrial team investigated if it could similarly be employed to build a lunar habitat."

### Design and Structure
Foster + Partners devised a weight-bearing **'catenary' dome design** with a cellular structured wall to shield against micrometeoroids and space radiation, incorporating a pressurised inflatable to shelter astronauts. A hollow closed-cell structure—reminiscent of bird bones—provides a good combination of strength and weight.

The base's design was guided by the properties of 3D-printed lunar soil, with a **1.5 tonne building block** produced as a demonstration.

### Construction Process
The UK’s **Monolite** supplied the **D-Shape printer** for the study, featuring a mobile printing array of nozzles on a 6 m frame to spray a binding solution onto a sand-like building material. 

*   **Material Preparation:** Simulated lunar material is mixed with magnesium oxide, turning it into 'paper' suitable for printing.
*   **Structural Binding:** A structural 'ink' (binding salt) is applied, converting the material into a stone-like solid.
*   **Printing Speed:** Current printers build at approximately 2 m per hour; next-generation designs aim for 3.5 m per hour, potentially completing a building in a week.

### Challenges and Solutions
**Vacuum Conditions:** To prevent liquids from boiling away in a vacuum, the team inserted the 3D printer nozzle beneath the regolith layer. They discovered that small 2 mm-scale droplets stay trapped by capillary forces in the soil, allowing the process to function in a vacuum.

**Regolith Simulant:** The team identified basaltic rock from a volcano in central Italy that bears a 99.8% resemblance to lunar soil, providing an abundant source of simulant for testing.

**Future Considerations:**
*   Controlling hazardous lunar dust.
*   Thermal factors (3D printing works best at room temperature, but lunar temperatures fluctuate wildly). 
*   The lunar poles are identified as having the most moderate temperature ranges for settlement.
