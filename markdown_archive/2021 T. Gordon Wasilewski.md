# Experimental Investigations of Water Extraction Process within Permanently Shadowed Regions of the Moon

**T. Gordon Wasilewski**

*Space Research Centre PAS, Space Mechatronics and Robotics Laboratory, Warsaw, PL; Colorado School of Mines, Center for Space Resources, Golden, CO.*

## Abstract
Heat and mass transfers occurring on the surface and within the subsurface of extraterrestrial bodies during natural and future industrial heating processes require knowledge of thermal properties of porous dry and icy materials. A series of experiments have been employed to simulate borehole heating of icy regolith samples under low vacuum (0.1 to 1.5 Torr) and very low temperatures (>93 K), using JSC-1A analog prepared as “mud-pies” with water contents from 0 to 20% wt. Real-time measurements of temperatures in an array of points and vacuum chamber pressures were recorded. To decipher thermal properties data in this inverse heat equation problem, a 3D finite element method modelling has been used to simulate the behaviour of both the samples and the vacuum chamber. The same model has been used to determine the validity of thermal properties of Lunar icy regolith under extremely high vacuum. The determined thermal conductivities vary with temperature, pressure, ice content, and porosity, while heat capacities vary with temperature, ice content, and porosity. These results should be applicable to research on properties and composition of other rocky and icy extraterrestrial objects or design of water ice prospecting instruments. Most importantly, they should improve our understanding of processes applied in extraterrestrial water mining and visualize the engineering challenges of these processes.

## Introduction
The current paradigm in research of rocky planets, moons and small objects within the Solar System is to “follow the water”. Our understanding of water and other volatiles directly influences knowledge of the evolution of the Solar System and composition of extraterrestrial objects but also it reiterates our plans for space resources utilization. Of all material resources in Space, water has the most promising and possibly fastest commercial utilization potential.

This paper investigates both thermal conductivities and heat capacities of icy JSC-1A materials of ice contents from 0 to 20% wt based on experimental measurements of temperature change in 3 different points of samples during heating under vacuum. Properties of those materials are determined and fitted through a recreation of the experimental situation using COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2 software.

## Experimental Method and Setup
A series of experiments had been conducted using the facilities of the Center for Space Resources at the Colorado School of Mines. The experiments involved use of a medium vacuum chamber (total measured volume 0.3375 m³), which is the base for the In Situ Experimental Probe (IEP), an instrument designed to support studies of dry and icy regolith physical properties.

### Sample Preparation
* Mud-pie batch preparation with specific regolith-water proportions
* Cylindrical samples preparation (~500 g, 2.5 in height, 3.28 in diameter)
* Samples freezing to -30 °C
* Cryo-cooling of samples in a liquid nitrogen bath
* Heater and thermocouples insertion
* Sample heating in simulated PSR conditions in the vacuum chamber

## Data Processing
This research tried to understand the experimental situation by recreating it through FEM simulations and validating and iterating its dependent parameters to obtain the best fit for heater temperature within first hundreds of seconds of laboratory measurements.

### Heat Transfer Model
The heat transfer process is governed by a three-dimensional heat equation accounting for phase change:

$$\rho C_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} + \rho C_p \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla T + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{q} = Q$$

Where the thermal conductivity of dry material ($k_{eff}$) is expressed as:

$$k_{eff} = k_{rad} + k_{cond} + k_{gas}$$

## Conclusions
Heat and mass transfer within icy planetary deposits is an extremely computationally intensive process. The study proved that our understanding of thermal properties of dry materials and their volumetric mixtures with ice is proper only if dynamism of ice phase change and pressure domain are taken into account. Co-dependent thermal, pressure and mass evolution render the process of heat transfer and water extraction in planetary environments counter-intuitive and non-linear.
