# Experimental Investigations of Water Extraction Process within Permanently Shadowed Regions of the Moon

**T. Gordon Wasilewski**
Space Research Centre PAS, Space Mechatronics and Robotics Laboratory, Warsaw, PL; Colorado School of Mines, Center for Space Resources, Golden, CO.

## ABSTRACT
Heat and mass transfers occurring on the surface and within the subsurface of extraterrestrial bodies during natural and future industrial heating processes require knowledge of thermal properties of porous dry and icy materials. A series of experiments have been employed to simulate borehole heating of icy regolith samples under low vacuum (0.1 to 1.5 Torr) and very low temperatures (>93 K), using JSC-1A analog prepared as “mud-pies” with water contents from 0 to 20% wt. Real-time measurements of temperatures in an array of points and vacuum chamber pressures were recorded. To decipher thermal properties data in this inverse heat equation problem, a 3D finite element method modelling has been used to simulate the behaviour of both the samples and the vacuum chamber. The same model has been used to determine the validity of thermal properties of Lunar icy regolith under extremely high vacuum. The determined thermal conductivities vary with temperature, pressure, ice content, and porosity, while heat capacities vary with temperature, ice content, and porosity. These results should be applicable to research on properties and composition of other rocky and icy extraterrestrial objects or design of water ice prospecting instruments. Most importantly, they should improve our understanding of processes applied in extraterrestrial water mining and visualize the engineering challenges of these processes.

## INTRODUCTION
The current paradigm in research of rocky planets, moons and small objects within the Solar System is to “follow the water”. Our understanding of water and other volatiles directly influences knowledge of the evolution of the Solar System and plans for space resources utilization. On airless or thin-atmosphere bodies between the Sun and the frost line, water exists in the form of ice on the surface and within the subsurface.

Thermal properties of water ice deposits in planetary environments are not well-constrained due to the dynamic and complex nature of water ice phase change within the porous space. This paper investigates both thermal conductivities and heat capacities of icy JSC-1A materials of ice contents from 0 to 20% wt based on experimental measurements and recreation using COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2.

## EXPERIMENTAL METHOD AND SETUP
A series of experiments used a medium vacuum chamber (0.3375 m³) at the Center for Space Resources, Colorado School of Mines. The chamber inner diameter is 45 cm and inner length is 163 cm.

### Regolith Analog
JSC-1A regolith was used. Optical testing showed an average particle size by mass (D50) of ~400 micrometers and by number (Dn50) of ~40 micrometers. Each batch was baked at 93 °C for 24 hours before use.

### Procedure
- Mud-pie batch preparation (~500 g samples, 2.5 in height, 3.28 in diameter).
- Samples freezing to -30 °C.
- Cryo-cooling in liquid nitrogen.
- Heater (OMEGA CSH-1011001) and thermocouple insertion.
- Sample heating in simulated PSR conditions in the vacuum chamber (0.1 to 0.2 Torr).

## CONCLUSIONS
- Understanding thermal properties of dry materials and volumetric mixtures with ice requires accounting for the dynamism of ice phase change and the pressure domain.
- Co-dependent thermal, pressure, and mass evolution make the process of heat transfer and water extraction counter-intuitive and non-linear.
- Low ice content surfaces are easier to heat to a specific temperature, whereas high ice content surfaces are very difficult to heat up due to higher thermal conductivity and latent heat of sublimation.
