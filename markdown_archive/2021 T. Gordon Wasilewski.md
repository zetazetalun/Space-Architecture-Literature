# Experimental Investigations of Water Extraction Process within Permanently Shadowed Regions of the Moon

## Abstract
Heat and mass transfers occurring on the surface and within the subsurface of extraterrestrial bodies during natural and future industrial heating processes require knowledge of thermal properties of porous dry and icy materials. A series of experiments have been employed to simulate borehole heating of icy regolith samples under low vacuum and very low temperatures. The results should improve our understanding of processes applied in extraterrestrial water mining and visualize the engineering challenges of these processes.

## Introduction
The current paradigm in research is to “follow the water”. Water has the most promising commercial utilization potential for space resource utilization. On airless bodies like the Moon, water exists as ice in the subsurface. This paper investigates thermal conductivities and heat capacities of icy JSC-1A materials (0-20% wt ice) based on experimental measurements and FEM simulations.

## Experimental Method and Setup
Experiments were conducted at the Colorado School of Mines using a medium vacuum chamber (0.3375 m³). The setup involved:
- JSC-1A regolith analog baked at 93 °C.
- "Mud-pie" batch preparation with specific water proportions.
- Cryo-cooling of cylindrical samples in a liquid nitrogen bath.
- Insertion of an OMEGA CSH-1011001 cartridge heater and thermocouples.
- Heating in simulated PSR (Permanently Shadowed Region) conditions.

## Data Processing
A 3D FEM model (COMSOL Multiphysics) was used to recreate the experiment. The heat transfer model accounted for phase change (sublimation) using a three-dimensional heat equation:

$$\rho C_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} + \rho C_p \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla T + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{q} = Q$$

## Results and Discussion
- **Gas Conductivity:** Permeability significantly influences the heating process. Unsuccessful pressure decrease in pores might switch phase change from sublimation to fusion.
- **Icy Lunar Regolith:** Ice conductivity becomes dominant even at very low ice filling fractions.
- **Phase Change:** Sublimation transition occurs between -20 and 20 °C, with rapid spikes observed at 0 °C.

## Conclusions
The study proves that understanding the thermal properties of dry materials and their volumetric mixtures with ice requires accounting for the dynamism of ice phase change and the pressure domain. Co-dependent thermal, pressure, and mass evolution make the process of water extraction counter-intuitive and non-linear.
