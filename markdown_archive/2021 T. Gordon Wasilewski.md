# Experimental Investigations of Water Extraction Process within Permanently Shadowed Regions of the Moon

**Author:** T. Gordon Wasilewski

## Abstract
Heat and mass transfers occurring on the surface and within the subsurface of extraterrestrial bodies during natural and future industrial heating processes require knowledge of thermal properties of porous dry and icy materials. A series of experiments have been employed to simulate borehole heating of icy regolith samples under low vacuum (0.1 to 1.5 Torr) and very low temperatures (>93 K), using JSC-1A analog prepared as 'mud-pies' with water contents from 0 to 20% wt. Real-time measurements of temperatures in an array of points and vacuum chamber pressures were recorded. To decipher thermal properties data in this inverse heat equation problem, a 3D finite element method modelling has been used to simulate the behaviour of both the samples and the vacuum chamber. The determined thermal conductivities vary with temperature, pressure, ice content, and porosity, while heat capacities vary with temperature, ice content, and porosity. These results are applicable to research on properties and composition of other rocky and icy extraterrestrial objects or design of water ice prospecting instruments.

## Introduction
The current paradigm in research of rocky planets, moons and small objects within the Solar System is to 'follow the water' for scientific and commercial reasons. Understanding volatiles influences knowledge of the evolution of the Solar System and plans for space resources utilization (ISRU). Of all material resources in Space, water has the most promising and possibly fastest commercial utilization potential.

## Experimental Method and Setup
A series of experiments were conducted at the Center for Space Resources at the Colorado School of Mines using a medium vacuum chamber (0.3375 m³). The chamber houses the In Situ Experimental Probe (IEP). JSC-1A dry regolith was used as the analog, baked at 93°C for 24 hours before testing.

### Sample Preparation
- Mud-pie batch preparation with specific regolith-water proportions.
- Cylindrical samples (~500 g, 2.5 in height, 3.28 in diameter).
- Freezing to -30°C and cryo-cooling in liquid nitrogen.
- Heater and thermocouples insertion.
- Heating in simulated PSR conditions (vacuum chamber).

## Data Processing
The research used FEM simulations (COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2) to recreate experimental conditions and iterate parameters to fit heater temperature measurements. The process is governed by a 3D heat equation accounting for phase change: ρC_p (∂T/∂t) + ρC_p u · ∇T + ∇ · q = Q.

### Key Findings on Thermal Properties
- Ice conductivity becomes dominant even at very low ice filling fractions.
- Permeability highly influences the heating process. Unsuccessful pressure decrease in pores can switch phase change from sublimation to fusion.
- Heater temperature increase rates are higher in low ice content regolith.

## Conclusions
Heat and mass transfer within icy planetary deposits is computationally intensive. The study validated that understanding thermal properties requires accounting for the dynamism of ice phase change and the pressure domain. Co-dependent thermal, pressure, and mass evolution make water extraction in planetary environments non-linear and counter-intuitive.
