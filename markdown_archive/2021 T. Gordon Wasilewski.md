# Experimental Investigations of Water Extraction Process within Permanently Shadowed Regions of the Moon

**Author:** T. Gordon Wasilewski
**Affiliation:** Space Research Centre PAS, Warsaw, PL; Colorado School of Mines, Golden, CO

## Abstract
Heat and mass transfers occurring on the surface and within the subsurface of extraterrestrial bodies during natural and future industrial heating processes require knowledge of thermal properties of porous dry and icy materials. A series of experiments have been employed to simulate borehole heating of icy regolith samples under low vacuum (0.1 to 1.5 Torr) and very low temperatures (>93 K), using JSC-1A analog prepared as “mud-pies” with water contents from 0 to 20% wt. Real-time measurements of temperatures in an array of points and vacuum chamber pressures were recorded. To decipher thermal properties data in this inverse heat equation problem, a 3D finite element method modelling has been used to simulate the behaviour of both the samples and the vacuum chamber. The determined thermal conductivities vary with temperature, pressure, ice content, and porosity, while heat capacities vary with temperature, ice content, and porosity.

## Experimental Method and Setup
Experiments were conducted at the Center for Space Resources at the Colorado School of Mines using a medium vacuum chamber (0.3375 m³). The setup involved the In Situ Experimental Probe (IEP). 

**Process:**
- Mud-pie batch preparation with specific regolith-water proportions.
- Cylindrical samples (~500 g, 2.5 in height, 3.28 in diameter).
- Samples freezing to -30 °C followed by cryo-cooling in liquid nitrogen.
- Heater (OMEGA CSH-1011001) and thermocouples insertion.
- Sample heating in simulated PSR conditions (0.1 to 0.2 Torr).

## Data Processing and Modeling
A 3D FEM model (COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2) recreated the experimental situation. The model accounted for three-dimensional heat equations and Darcy's law for the pressure domain.

## Conclusions
- Understanding thermal properties of dry materials and their volumetric mixtures with ice requires accounting for the dynamism of ice phase change and the pressure domain.
- High ice content surfaces are significantly harder to heat up compared to low ice content regolith.
- The process of water extraction is non-linear and counter-intuitive due to co-dependent thermal, pressure, and mass evolution.
