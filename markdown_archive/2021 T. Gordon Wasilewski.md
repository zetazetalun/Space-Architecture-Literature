## Abstract
Heat and mass transfers occurring on the surface and within the subsurface of extraterrestrial bodies during natural and future industrial heating processes require knowledge of thermal properties of porous dry and icy materials. A series of experiments have been employed to simulate borehole heating of icy regolith samples under low vacuum (0.1 to 1.5 Torr) and very low temperatures (>93 K), using JSC-1A analog prepared as “mud-pies” with water contents from 0 to 20% wt. Real-time measurements of temperatures in an array of points and vacuum chamber pressures were recorded. To decipher thermal properties data in this inverse heat equation problem, a 3D finite element method modelling has been used to simulate the behaviour of both the samples and the vacuum chamber. The determined thermal conductivities vary with temperature, pressure, ice content, and porosity, while heat capacities vary with temperature, ice content, and porosity. These results should be applicable to research on properties and composition of other rocky and icy extraterrestrial objects or design of water ice prospecting instruments.

## Introduction
The current paradigm in research of rocky planets is to “follow the water”. Of all material resources in Space, water has the most promising and possibly fastest commercial utilization potential. This paper investigates both thermal conductivities and heat capacities of icy JSC-1A materials of ice contents from 0 to 20% wt based on experimental measurements of temperature change in 3 different points of samples during heating under vacuum.

## Experimental Method and Setup
A series of experiments were conducted at the Center for Space Resources at the Colorado School of Mines using a medium vacuum chamber (0.3375 m³). 

*   **Material:** JSC-1A dry regolith analog (baked at 93°C for 24h).
*   **Preparation:** "Mud-pie" batches with specific water proportions, frozen to -30°C then cryo-cooled in liquid nitrogen.
*   **Heating:** OMEGA CSH-1011001 cartridge heater (approx. 7W power).
*   **Vacuum:** Scroll pump providing ~0.1 Torr.

## Data Processing

## Findings
- **Variable Thermal Properties:** Thermal conductivity and heat capacity are highly dependent on temperature, ice content, and porosity.
- **Permeability:** Successful outgassing of samples during pump-down suggests high permeability in JSC-1A analogs; however, lower permeability would significantly shift phase change from sublimation to fusion.
- **Heating Rates:** Low ice content surfaces are easier to heat to target temperatures, while high ice content surfaces require substantially more energy.
- **Model Fit:** The study found that dry regolith thermal conductivity in models needed to be increased to better fit experimental data, likely due to gas conductance or particle size variations.

## Conclusions
Heat and mass transfer within icy planetary deposits is computationally intensive but can be accurately modeled using FEM. Understanding the dynamism of ice phase change and the pressure domain is essential for predicting the behavior of subsurface water extraction systems. Co-dependent thermal and mass evolution makes the extraction process non-linear and counter-intuitive.
