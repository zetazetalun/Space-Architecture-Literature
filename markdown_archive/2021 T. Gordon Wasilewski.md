## Abstract
Heat and mass transfers occurring on the surface and within the subsurface of extraterrestrial bodies during natural and future industrial heating processes require knowledge of thermal properties of porous dry and icy materials. A series of experiments have been employed to simulate borehole heating of icy regolith samples under low vacuum (0.1 to 1.5 Torr) and very low temperatures (>93 K), using JSC-1A analog prepared as “mud-pies” with water contents from 0 to 20% wt. Real-time measurements of temperatures in an array of points and vacuum chamber pressures were recorded. To decipher thermal properties data in this inverse heat equation problem, a 3D finite element method modelling has been used to simulate the behaviour of both the samples and the vacuum chamber. The determined thermal conductivities vary with temperature, pressure, ice content, and porosity, while heat capacities vary with temperature, ice content, and porosity. These results should improve our understanding of processes applied in extraterrestrial water mining and visualize the engineering challenges of these processes.

## Introduction
The current paradigm in research of rocky planets and moons is to “follow the water”. Understanding water and other volatiles directly influences our plans for space resources utilization (ISRU). Of all material resources in Space, water has the most promising commercial utilization potential. Regardless of the origin, thermal properties of water ice deposits in planetary environments are not well-constrained due to the dynamic nature of water ice phase change within porous space.

## Experimental Method and Setup
Experiments were conducted at the Center for Space Resources at the Colorado School of Mines using a vacuum chamber (0.3375 m³). The setup used **JSC-1A** regolith analog. 

**Experimental Steps:**
- Mud-pie batch preparation with specific regolith-water proportions.
- Cylindrical samples preparation (~500 g, 2.5 in height, 3.28 in diameter).
- Samples freezing to -30 °C followed by cryo-cooling in liquid nitrogen.
- Insertion of an OMEGA CSH-1011001 cartridge heater and thermocouples.
- Sample heating in simulated PSR conditions (0.1 to 0.2 Torr).

## Key Findings
1. **Thermal Interdependence:** Thermal conductivities are strongly pressure-dependent; the addition of gas to the system shifts heat transfer modes to gas conductance when the Knudsen number is lower than 10.
2. **Permeability Impact:** Successful pressure decrease in regolith pores is critical; otherwise, phase change might switch from sublimation-based to fusion-based (melting), which complicates extraction.
3. **Heating Rates:** Low ice content surfaces are easier to heat to a target temperature, while high ice content surfaces (above 10-14% wt) are significantly harder to heat due to the high thermal inertia and energy required for phase change.

## Conclusions
The study validated that understanding the thermal properties of dry materials and their volumetric mixtures with ice requires accounting for the dynamism of ice phase change and the pressure domain. Co-dependent thermal, pressure, and mass evolution makes the process of water extraction non-linear and counter-intuitive.
