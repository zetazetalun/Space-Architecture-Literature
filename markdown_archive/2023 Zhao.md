# Development of lunar regolith-based composite for in-situ 3D printing via high-pressure extrusion system

## Abstract
To fully utilize the in-situ resources on the moon to facilitate the establishment of a lunar habitat is significant for long-term residence. This work proposes a cost-effective melting extrusion system for lunar regolith-based composite printing, using engineering thermoplastic powders (PA12) as a bonding agent. High-pressure extrusion enhances interface affinity and maximizes the loading ratio of in-situ resources. Mechanical properties including tensile strength (up to 36.2 MPa) and impact toughness (5.15 kJ/m²) were evaluated. The system allows for an effective loading fraction of up to 80 wt.% and adapts easily to extreme conditions.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar exploration focuses on establishing extraterrestrial habitats. ISRU technologies are preferred to avoid the unaffordable cost of transporting materials from Earth. Lunar regolith is an ideal material for habitat construction due to its thermal and radiation protection properties. While previous AM methods like laser sintering or D-shape printing exist, they often involve high energy consumption or complex auxiliary materials. This study explores melt extrusion molding as a robust, stable, and cost-effective alternative.

## 2. Experimental
### 2.1 Materials
- **Lunar Regolith Simulant:** Basalt powder with particle size distribution (mean 101.084 µm) similar to Apollo samples.
- **Binder:** PA12 thermoplastic polymer (nylon).
- **Composites:** PA12-Basalt mixtures at 40, 50, and 60 wt.% basalt loading.

### 2.4 Specimens Fabrication
Two types of extruders were used:
1. **Piston-based pellet extruder:** Used for small-batch material performance testing.
2. **Screw-based pellet extruder:** Developed for robot-assisted large-scale component fabrication.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Thermal Stability:** TGA/DSC tests showed that PA12 remains stable below 350°C. Basalt particles promote the recrystallization of PA12, which is beneficial for extrusion molding.
- **Mechanical Performance:** Tensile strength decreases as basalt weight fraction increases (due to interface cracking), while elastic modulus increases (basalt acts as a hard reinforcement phase).
- **Impact Resistance:** Crucial for meteorite impact resistance. Maximum toughness (5.15 kJ/m²) was achieved at 60 wt.% basalt loading using a 45°/−45° infill pattern.
- **Large-Scale Printing:** A lunar habitat architectural model and honeycomb structures were successfully printed with loading up to 80 wt.% basalt.

## 4. Conclusions
The study demonstrates that high-pressure melt extrusion molding is an effective and energy-efficient approach for lunar ISRU construction. It achieves significantly higher regolith loading (up to 80 wt.%) than many alternative methods while maintaining structural integrity and impact resistance suitable for lunar environments.
