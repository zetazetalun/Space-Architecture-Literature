# Development of lunar regolith-based composite for in-situ 3D printing via high-pressure extrusion system

**Journal:** Front. Mech. Eng. 2023, 18(2): 29  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11465-022-0745-8

## Abstract
To fully utilize the in-situ resources on the moon to facilitate the establishment of a lunar habitat is significant to realize the long-term residence of mankind on the moon and the deep space exploration in the future. In this work, we proposed a cost-effective melting extrusion system for lunar regolith-based composite printing, and engineering thermoplastic powders (PA12) are employed as a bonding agent. The high-pressure extrusion system enhances the interface affinity of polymer binders with lunar regolith and maximizes the loading ratio of in-situ resources up to 80 wt.%. Mechanical properties (tensile strength, elastic modulus, impact toughness) were evaluated systematically. Maximum tensile strength and impact toughness reached 36.2 MPa and 5.15 kJ/m², respectively. 

## 1. Introduction
Lunar in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) technology utilizes abundant regolith resources to produce architectural and functional structures, avoiding the unaffordable cost of transporting materials from Earth. While various 3D printing methods (laser sintering, solar sintering, D-shape) have been explored, melt extrusion molding (MEM) offers high process stability and equipment simplicity. This study aims to evaluate process feasibility and printed material performance for future lunar surface fabrication.

### 3.4 Manufacturing verification via screw-based pellet extruder
A robot-assisted screw-based pellet extruder was developed for large-scale fabrication. This system achieved an 80 wt.% basalt loading fraction and successfully printed a lunar habitat architectural model, verifying the feasibility of the technology for lunar surface construction.

## 4. Conclusions
The study demonstrates an effective approach using high-performance thermoplastic polymers and lunar regolith for melt extrusion molding. This method minimizes energy and time consumption compared to direct melting of raw regolith. The resulting composite achieves high tensile strength (36.2 MPa) and impact toughness (5.15 kJ/m²), suitable for resisting meteorite impacts. The system allows for regolith loading up to 80 wt.%, maximizing ISRU efficiency.
