# Development of lunar regolith-based composite for in-situ 3D printing via high-pressure extrusion system

**Authors:** Hua ZHAO, Jihong ZHU, Shangqin YUAN, Shaoying LI, Weihong ZHANG
**Journal:** Front. Mech. Eng. 2023, 18(2): 29
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11465-022-0745-8

## Abstract
To fully utilize the in-situ resources on the moon to facilitate the establishment of a lunar habitat is significant to realize the long-term residence of mankind. This work proposes a cost-effective melting extrusion system for lunar regolith-based composite printing, using high-performance nylon (PA12) as a bonding agent. The high-pressure extrusion system enhances the interface affinity of polymer binders with lunar regolith and maximizes the loading ratio of in-situ resources. Mechanical properties, including tensile strength, elastic modulus, and impact toughness, were evaluated. The maximum tensile strength reached 36.2 MPa and impact toughness reached 5.15 kJ/m². High-pressure melt extrusion can increase the effective loading fraction up to 80 wt.%, adapting to extreme conditions for in-situ manufacturing.

## 1 Introduction
The construction of a lunar habitat requires protection from temperature differences, high vacuum, meteorite impact, and radiation, while utilizing in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) technology. Additive manufacturing (AM) provides a novel way to build habitats with complex geometries. This study evaluates melt extrusion molding as a simple, stable alternative to laser sintering for lunar regolith-based composites.

## 2 Experimental

### 2.1 Materials
Basalt powder was used as a lunar regolith simulant, with mean particle sizes (101.084 µm) consistent with Apollo 11-17 landing site samples. PA12 powder was used as the thermoplastic binder. Composites were prepared with basalt weight fractions of 40%, 50%, and 60%.

## 3 Results and discussion

### 3.3 Mechanical properties

### 3.4 Manufacturing verification via screw-based pellet extruder
A robot-assisted screw-based pellet extruder was developed for large-scale components. It achieved a regolith loading fraction of 80 wt.%, demonstrating an advantage in improving resource utilization.

## 4 Conclusions
Lunar regolith-based composites blended with thermoplastic polymers via high-pressure melt extrusion provide an effective, low-energy approach for in-situ construction. The system achieved a maximum tensile strength of 36.2 MPa and impact toughness of 5.15 kJ/m². The screw-based extruder allows for regolith loading up to 80 wt.%, suitable for lunar habitat construction.
