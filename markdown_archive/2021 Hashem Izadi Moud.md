# Energy Life Cycle in Extraterrestrial Construction Projects: Is Building Underground an Option?

**Authors:** Hashem Izadi Moud, Peyman Rahgozar, Sebastian T. Weber, Craig Capano, and Ian Flood
**Affiliations:** Florida Gulf Coast University; University of Florida
**Source:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE, pp. 1166-1176

## Abstract
Making life multi-planetary requires substantial knowledge of building structures in extraterrestrial bodies where natural resources are scarce, human resources are expensive, and energy sources differ vastly from Earth. This paper presents energy cycle flowcharts for construction on Earth versus space and argues against the feasibility of large-scale underground construction due to extreme energy costs associated with soil removal.

## Introduction
Future shelters on the Moon or Mars must protect occupants from no atmospheres, radiation, meteorite impacts, and extreme temperature changes. While inflatable and modular structures are proposed for initial phases, permanent habitation (Phase 3) often suggests underground structures or the use of lava tubes. However, the energy required for such excavation is a significant constraint often overlooked in construction management.

## Importance of Energy in Construction Projects
### Energy on Earth
On Earth, energy (primarily fossil fuels) is abundant and cheap, making it a low priority in construction planning. Decisions between above-ground and underground structures are typically driven by client needs rather than energy restraints.

### Energy in Space
In space, energy is scarce. Transporting fossil fuels is economically unfeasible ($15,000–$45,000 per kg). Solar energy is the most viable source but has limitations including low intensity and storage requirements. Space construction requires: 
1. Ultra-efficient processes.
2. Innovative harvesting/storage.
3. Audacious energy-conservative planning.

## Case Study: Small Structure on Mars
The study estimates the energy needed to dig a small underground room (600 m³). 

### Excavation Volume
To account for soil stability, two ramps are required for heavy equipment access. 
- **Room Volume:** 600 m³
- **Ramp/Backfill Volume:** 1200 m³
- **Total Soil Removed:** 1800 m³

### Energy Calculations
- **Excavation Energy (Earth benchmark):** 12.09 MJ/m³ (3.36 kWh/m³).
- **Total Energy Needed:** 6045 kWh (equivalent to 181 gallons of gasoline).
- **Solar Availability on Mars:** Mean solar radiance is ~200 W/m². With 30% panel efficiency and 90% useful daylight, a system yields ~655.20 Wh/m²/day.
- **Solar Field Requirements:** A 400 m² solar field yields 264 kWh per Martian day (Sol). To complete the excavation, it would take **29 Martian Days** just to harvest the energy for soil removal.

## Conclusion
Large-scale excavation using equipment like Tunnel Boring Machines (TBMs) is currently impractical on Mars due to the energy deficit. Future development must focus on ultra-efficient designs and smart construction techniques that minimize soil movement.
