# Energy Life Cycle in Extraterrestrial Construction Projects: Is Building Underground an Option?

**Authors:** Hashem Izadi Moud, Peyman Rahgozar, Sebastian T. Weber, Craig Capano, and Ian Flood
**Published in:** Earth and Space 2021 (ASCE)

## Abstract
Making life multi-planetary requires substantial knowledge of building structures in extraterrestrial bodies where resources are scarce and energy sources differ from Earth. This paper presents two energy cycle flowcharts for construction projects on Earth and beyond and argues about the expensive and unrealistic nature of building underground due to energy constraints.

## Introduction
Future shelters on the Moon or Mars must protect occupants from no atmospheres, meteorite impacts, lack of magnetic fields, extreme temperature changes, and radiation. While permanent structures are needed, shipping materials from Earth is economically infeasible due to high launch costs. Solar energy is the primary accessible source, necessitating ultra-efficient construction processes.

## Energy in Construction
### Earth vs. Space
On Earth, cheap and abundant fossil fuels have made energy planning a secondary concern in Construction Management (CM). In contrast, space construction faces severe energy scarcity. 

*   **Phase 1:** Temporary structures for short-term missions.
*   **Phase 2:** Inflatable structures in natural features (lava tubes/caves).
*   **Phase 3:** Permanent structures (surface or underground).

### Construction Industry Operation Flow
*   **Earth Flow:** High reliance on non-renewable energy sources and human workforce.
*   **Space Flow:** Reliance on solar energy, robotic agents, and automated systems. Figure 2 in the paper outlines the integration of robotic agents into the extraterrestrial construction cycle.

## Case Study: Underground Room on Mars
To evaluate the feasibility of underground construction, the study estimates the energy needed to excavate a small underground room (600 m³).

### Excavation Data
*   **Total Soil Removed:** 1800 m³ (includes room volume, ramps, and backfill).
*   **Energy Density for Excavation:** 12.09 MJ/m³ (based on Earth standards for silty sand).
*   **Total Energy Needed:** 21,762 MJ or 6,045 kWh.
*   **Energy Cost:** Transporting energy from Earth costs approximately $1,158 per kWh.

### Solar Energy Harvest on Mars
*   **Yearly Mean Solar Radiance:** ~200 W/m².
*   **Daylight Duration:** ~12.13 hours.
*   **Panel Efficiency:** 30%.
*   **Yield:** 655.20 Wh/m²/day.
*   **Standard Field (400 m²):** Collects ~264 kWh per Martian day (Sol).

**Findings:** It would take approximately 29 Martian Days for a 400 m² solar field to produce enough energy just for the excavation of one small room.

## Conclusion
Building underground via large-scale mechanical excavation (e.g., TBM) is currently impractical on Mars due to extreme energy constraints. Sustainable development requires: (1) ultra-efficient structural design, (2) resource-efficient planning, and (3) smart, energy-efficient construction machinery.
