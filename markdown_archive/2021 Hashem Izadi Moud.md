# Energy Life Cycle in Extraterrestrial Construction Projects: Is Building Underground an Option?

**Authors:** Hashem Izadi Moud, Peyman Rahgozar, Sebastian T. Weber, Craig Capano, and Ian Flood

## Abstract
Making life multi-planetary requires substantial knowledge of building structures in extraterrestrial bodies, where natural resources are scarce, human resources are expensive-to-utilize, and energy sources are vastly different from the Earth. On Earth, fossil fuels have been the main source of energy for construction projects. In other extraterrestrial bodies, including Mars and Moon, easy-to-access fossil fuels are non-existent. Solar energy is the primary source of energy accessible to extraterrestrial construction projects. This calls for ultra-efficient and sustainable design of structures, smart resource-efficient planning, and energy-efficient construction techniques. This paper presents energy cycle flowcharts for construction projects on Earth and beyond and argues against the feasibility of building underground due to the high energy costs of excavation.

## Introduction
Future shelters must protect occupants from extreme environmental conditions, including lack of atmosphere, radiation, and meteorite impacts. The transition to habitation may occur in three phases: 
1. Short-term missions.
2. Medium-term stays with designed structures.
3. Long-term permanent living.

Transporting materials and energy from Earth is economically infeasible for large-scale colonization. Solar energy is the most viable source but presents challenges such as low intensity and long nights on Mars.

## Importance of Energy in Construction Projects
On Earth, Construction Management (CM) focuses on the "iron triangle" (time, cost, scope), but energy availability is rarely a top priority due to cheap fossil fuels. In space, energy scarcity becomes a defining constraint. 

### Phases of Construction
- **Phase 1 & 2:** Focus on prefabricated and inflatable structures (e.g., SICA, spherical domes). Inflatables are compact, light, and can be integrated into natural features like lava tubes.
- **Phase 3:** Permanent structures. While underground locations offer protection from radiation, the energy required for excavation using equipment like Tunnel Boring Machines (TBM) is massive.

## Case Study: Building a Small Structure on Mars
The study estimates the energy needed to excavate a small underground room (600 m³) on Mars.

### Soil Removal Calculation
Total soil removed is calculated based on the room volume plus ramps and backfill requirements:
- **Room Volume:** 600 m³
- **Ramps (2 x 300 m³):** 600 m³
- **Backfill (2 x 300 m³):** 600 m³
- **Total Volume:** 1800 m³

### Energy Requirements
- **Excavation energy on Earth:** ~12.09 MJ/m³ (3.36 kWh/m³) for silty sand.
- **Total energy for 1800 m³:** 6045 kWh.
- **Solar Collection on Mars:** Assuming a 30% efficient solar panel, mean solar radiance yields ~655.20 Wh/m²/day.
- **Collection Time:** A 400 m² solar field yields ~264 kWh per Martian day. To produce the 6045 kWh needed for excavation, it would take **29 Martian Days**.

## Discussion and Conclusion
Developing multi-planetary life depends on building structures at scale. However, the energy required for large-scale excavation makes underground building via soil removal currently impractical. Construction must instead rely on:
1. Ultra-efficient and sustainable design.
2. Smart and resource-efficient project planning.
3. Energy-efficient smart construction machinery.
