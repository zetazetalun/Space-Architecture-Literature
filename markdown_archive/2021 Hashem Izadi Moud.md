# Energy Life Cycle in Extraterrestrial Construction Projects: Is Building Underground an Option?

**Authors:** Hashem Izadi Moud, Peyman Rahgozar, Sebastian T. Weber, Craig Capano, and Ian Flood

## Abstract
Making life multi-planetary requires knowledge of building structures in extraterrestrial bodies where natural resources are scarce and energy sources differ from Earth. While fossil fuels power Earth's construction, solar energy will likely be the primary source for space construction. This paper presents energy cycle flowcharts comparing Earth and space environments and argues that building underground may be unrealistic and expensive due to the energy required for soil removal.

## Introduction
Future shelters must protect occupants from extreme environments: lack of atmosphere, high radiation, meteorite impacts, and extreme temperatures. Habitats are proposed to evolve through three phases: (1) short-term missions, (2) medium-term designer structures, and (3) permanent colonization. A major constraint for permanent structures is the energy required for construction, as transporting materials or fuel from Earth is cost-prohibitive.

## Energy in Construction Processes
### Earth vs. Space
On Earth, cheap energy (fossil fuels) has made energy considerations secondary in construction planning. In space, "energy scarcity" is a real threat. 

*   **Phase 1 & 2:** Often involve inflatable structures which are compact and light.
*   **Phase 3:** More permanent structures inside caves, lava tubes, or underground for radiation protection.

## Case Study: Small Structure on Mars
The paper estimates the energy needed to dig a small underground room (600 m³) on Mars.

### Excavation Volume Calculations
*   **Room volume:** 600 m³
*   **Ramp/Backfill volume:** 1200 m³
*   **Total soil removed:** 1800 m³

### Energy Requirements
*   **Excavation energy (Earth benchmark):** 12.09 MJ/m³
*   **Total Energy for 1800 m³:** 21,762 MJ or 6,045 kWh.
*   **Solar harvesting on Mars:** Assuming 30% efficiency and mean solar radiance of 200W/m², a 400 m² solar field generates roughly 264 kWh per Martian day.
*   **Time Requirement:** It would take approximately **29 Martian Days** of harvesting energy from a 400 m² field just to power the excavation for a 600 m³ room.

## Conclusion
Underground construction requiring mass excavation is energy-extravagant. Large-scale equipment like Tunnel Boring Machines (TBMs) is likely infeasible without fossil fuels. Future development must focus on (1) ultra-efficient structural design, (2) resource-efficient project planning, and (3) energy-efficient automated machinery.
