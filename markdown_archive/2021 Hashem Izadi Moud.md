# Energy Life Cycle in Extraterrestrial Construction Projects: Is Building Underground an Option?

**Authors:** Hashem Izadi Moud, Peyman Rahgozar, Sebastian T. Weber, Craig Capano, and Ian Flood

**Published in:** Earth and Space 2021, pp. 1166–1176

## ABSTRACT
Making life multi-planetary requires substantial knowledge of building structures in extraterrestrial bodies, where natural resources are scarce, human resources are expensive-to-utilize, and energy sources are vastly different from the Earth. On Earth, fossil fuels have been the main source of energy for construction projects. In other extraterrestrial bodies, including Mars and Moon, easy-to-access fossil fuels are non-existent. Solar energy is, and probably will be, the primary source of energy accessible to extraterrestrial construction projects. This calls for ultra-efficient and sustainable design of structures and buildings, smart and resource-efficient planning of projects, and use of energy-efficient and smart construction techniques and machinery. This paper presents two energy cycle flowcharts for construction projects on Earth and beyond, which show the vastly different energy cycles in these environments. It further presents arguments about the expensive and unrealistic nature of building underground, as one of the key ideas for building beyond Earth.

## INTRODUCTION
Future shelters will have to be designed to adequately protect occupants from extreme environmental conditions, including no atmospheres, high or low atmospheric pressures, meteorite impacts, lack of magnetic fields, extreme temperature changes, and radiation. 

### Three-Phase Development Program:
1.  **Initial phase:** Short-term missions using prefabricated/modular structures.
2.  **Second phase:** Longer stays using inflatable structures or natural features (lava tubes).
3.  **Third phase:** Permanent colonization with structures built for long-term or permanent stays (ISRU-based).

## ENERGY IN CONSTRUCTION PROJECTS
### Earth vs. Space
On Earth, energy (fossil fuels and electricity) is unusually cheap and abundant, often making it a minor consideration in construction management. In space, energy is the primary constraint. Transporting fuel from Earth to Mars is estimated to cost between $45,000 and $170,000 per gallon, making it infeasible for large-scale colonization.

### Solar Energy on Mars
Solar energy is the most viable source. 
- **Max Solar Radiance:** ~590 W/m²
- **Yearly Mean Radiance:** ~200 W/m²
- **Martian Day (Sol):** 24.62 hours
- **InSight Lander Data:** Roughly 604 Wh/m²/d achieved on its first day with ~29.5% efficient panels.

## CASE STUDY: UNDERGROUND CONSTRUCTION ON MARS
The study estimates the energy needed to build a small underground room (600 m³).

### Excavation Volume
To create a 600 m³ space, ramps and slops are required for heavy equipment access.
- **Formula:** Total soil removed = room size volume + ramp(s) volume + backfill of ramp(s) volume
- **Calculation:** 600 + (2 * 300) + (2 * 300) = 1,800 m³

### Energy Calculation
- **Energy for excavation (Earth baseline):** 12.09 MJ/m³ (3.36 kWh/m³).
- **Total energy required:** 1,800 m³ * 3.36 kWh/m³ = 6,045 kWh.
- **Solar Collection:** A 400 m² solar field on Mars collects ~264 kWh per Martian day.
- **Duration:** It would take approximately 29 Martian Days just to produce the energy required for the excavation of this small structure.

## DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
Building underground via massive excavation is likely impractical on Mars due to extreme energy constraints. While Tunnel Boring Machines (TBMs) are efficient on Earth, their energy demand is too high for the current solar-harvesting capacities in space. Future extraterrestrial cities must rely on ultra-efficient designs and smart, energy-conservative planning rather than massive soil-moving operations.
