# Energy Life Cycle in Extraterrestrial Construction Projects: Is Building Underground an Option?

**Authors:** Hashem Izadi Moud, Peyman Rahgozar, Sebastian T. Weber, Craig Capano, and Ian Flood
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE
**Pages:** 1166–1176

## Abstract
Making life multi-planetary requires substantial knowledge of building structures in extraterrestrial bodies, where natural resources are scarce, human resources are expensive-to-utilize, and energy sources are vastly different from the Earth. On Earth, fossil fuels have been the main source of energy for construction projects. In other extraterrestrial bodies, including Mars and Moon, easy-to-access fossil fuels are non-existent. Solar energy is the primary source of energy accessible. This paper presents two energy cycle flowcharts for construction projects on Earth and beyond and argues about the expensive and unrealistic nature of building underground as a primary strategy.

## Introduction
Future shelters must protect occupants from extreme environmental conditions, including lack of atmosphere, radiation, meteorite impacts, and temperature extremes. Inhabitation models often follow a three-phase program: (1) short-term missions, (2) longer stays with inflatable structures, and (3) permanent colonization. While underground construction offers protection, the energy cost of excavation is a major constraint.

## Case Study: Small Underground Structure on Mars
The study estimates the energy needed for digging a small underground room (600 m³) on Mars.

### Excavation Volumes
Based on Formula 2, the total soil removed (including ramps for equipment) is calculated as:

`Total soil removed = 600 + (2 * 300) + (2 * 300) = 1800 m³`

### Energy Requirements
- **Energy for excavation (Earth baseline):** 12.09 MJ/m³ (3.36 kWh/m³).
- **Total energy for 1800 m³:** 21,762 MJ or 6045 kWh.
- **Solar Field Efficiency:** 30% efficiency assumed for cutting-edge panels.
- **Daily Solar Collection (Mars):** 655.20 Wh/m²/d (Formula 3).

### Results
- A 400 m² solar field collects ~264 kWh per Martian day.
- To produce the 6045 kWh needed for excavation, it would take approximately **29 Martian Days (Sols)**.
- Harvesting this energy in a single day would require a solar field of 11,424 m² (approx. 2.8 acres).

## Discussion and Conclusion
While underground structures offer radiation protection, the energy required for large-scale excavation using current solar technology is massive. The paper concludes that building cities beyond Earth will require a combination of:
1. Ultra-efficient and sustainable structural design.
2. Smart, resource-efficient project planning.
3. Energy-efficient automated construction machinery.

Large-scale excavation (e.g., using Tunnel Boring Machines) is currently deemed impractical due to energy restrictions.
