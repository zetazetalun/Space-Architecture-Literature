# Energy Life Cycle in Extraterrestrial Construction Projects: Is Building Underground an Option?

**Authors:** Hashem Izadi Moud, Peyman Rahgozar, Sebastian T. Weber, Craig Capano, and Ian Flood
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## Abstract
Making life multi-planetary requires substantial knowledge of building structures in extraterrestrial bodies, where natural resources are scarce, human resources are expensive-to-utilize, and energy sources are vastly different from the Earth. On Earth, fossil fuels have been the main source of energy for construction projects. In other extraterrestrial bodies, including Mars and Moon, easy-to-access fossil fuels are non-existent. Solar energy is, and probably will be, the primary source of energy accessible to extraterrestrial construction projects. This calls for ultra-efficient and sustainable design of structures and buildings, smart and resource-efficient planning of projects, and use of energy-efficient and smart construction techniques and machinery. This paper presents two energy cycle flowcharts for construction projects on Earth and beyond, which show the vastly different energy cycles in these environments. It further presents arguments about the expensive and unrealistic nature of building underground, as one of the key ideas for building beyond Earth.

## Introduction
Future shelters will have to be designed to adequately protect occupants from extreme environmental conditions; including no atmospheres, high or low atmospheric pressures, meteorite or asteroid impacts, lack of protective magnetic fields, extreme temperature changes and harmful radiation. Colonization of the Moon or Mars would be considered an excellent starting point. Inhabitants may use different phases: 
1. Initial phase for short-term missions.
2. Second phase for slightly longer stays.
3. Third phase for long-term and permanent living.

## Energy in Construction Projects

### Earth vs. Space
On Earth, energy is often a low priority in construction management due to its cheap and abundant availability via fossil fuels. In contrast, extraterrestrial construction must rely on solar energy, which has drawbacks such as low intensity and predictability issues but remains the most viable source. The cost of delivering energy from Earth is prohibitive (e.g., $15,000 - $45,000 per kg delivery cost to Mars).

### Construction Models
- **Inflatable Structures:** Suggested for initial phases; compact, light, and flexible (e.g., SICA design).
- **Natural Features:** Utilizing lava tubes or caves provides high protection but may still require excavation to clear debris or level surfaces.
- **Mechanical Excavation:** Using Tunnel Boring Machines (TBM) has been proposed (Rostami et al. 2018), but requires massive energy.

## Case Study: Underground Room on Mars
A case study was conducted to estimate the energy needed for a 600 m³ underground room requiring the removal of 1800 m³ of soil (including ramps and backfill).

**Total soil removed formula:**
Total soil removed = room size volume + ramp(s) volume + backfill of ramp(s) volume  (1)
Total soil removed = 600 + (2 * 300) + (2 * 300) = 1800 m³ (2)

**Energy Requirements:**
- Excavation of 1 m³ of soil (Earth baseline for silty sand): 12.09 MJ/m³ (3.36 kWh).
- Total energy for 1800 m³: 21,762 MJ or 6,045 kWh.

**Solar Energy Availability on Mars:**
Assumed Yearly Mean Solar Radiance: 200 W/m²
Efficiency of Solar Panels: 30%
Useful Daylight Factor: 90%

**Formula for Daily Collection:**
12.13 (h/d) * 90% * 200 (W/m²) * 30% = 655.20 Wh/m²/d (3)

### Findings
- A 400 m² solar field collects ~264 kWh per Martian day.
- To harvest the 6045 kWh needed for excavation, it would take approximately 29 Martian Days.
- To perform the excavation in a single day, a solar field of 11,424 m² (2.822 acres) would be required.

## Discussion and Conclusion
While solar energy is abundant, the energy-intensive nature of large-scale earthmoving (like using TBMs) makes underground construction impractical for early stages. Developing cities beyond Earth will require a combination of:
1. Ultra-efficient and sustainable design.
2. Smart and resource-efficient project planning.
3. Use of energy-efficient and smart construction techniques/machinery.
