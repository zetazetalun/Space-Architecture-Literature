# Energy Life Cycle in Extraterrestrial Construction Projects: Is Building Underground an Option?

**Authors:** Hashem Izadi Moud; Peyman Rahgozar; Sebastian T. Weber; Craig Capano; and Ian Flood  
**Published in:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## Abstract
Making life multi-planetary requires substantial knowledge of building structures in extraterrestrial bodies, where natural resources are scarce, human resources are expensive-to-utilize, and energy sources are vastly different from the Earth. On Earth, fossil fuels have been the main source of energy for construction projects. In other extraterrestrial bodies, including Mars and Moon, easy-to-access fossil fuels are non-existent. Solar energy is, and probably will be, the primary source of energy accessible to extraterrestrial construction projects. This paper presents two energy cycle flowcharts for construction projects on Earth and beyond and presents arguments about the expensive and unrealistic nature of building underground.

## Introduction
Future shelters will have to be designed to adequately protect occupants from extreme environmental conditions, including no atmospheres, high or low atmospheric pressures, meteorite impacts, lack of protective magnetic fields, extreme temperature changes, and harmful radiation. Colonization of the Moon or Mars would be considered an excellent starting point for humans to learn how to adapt. Successful inhabitation may require a three-phase development program: initial short-term missions, designed structures for slightly longer stays, and final permanent living structures.

## Importance of Energy in Construction Projects
- **Earth Context:** Construction Management (CM) rarely discusses the importance of energy cost due to the widespread availability of cheap fossil fuels. Energy is an integrated but often ignored part of the building process.
- **Space Context:** Transportation of fossil fuels from Earth is economically infeasible ($15,000–$45,000 per kg). Multi-planetary life depends on harvesting energy on-site, primarily via solar (PV/thermal), wind (limited), or nuclear (immature/high-risk).

## Case Study: Small Underground Structure on Mars
To estimate the energy needed for building purposes on Mars, a case study estimates the energy for an underground room requiring the removal of 600 cubic meters (m³) of soil.

### Excavation Volume Calculation
Total soil removed = room size volume + ramp(s) volume + backfill of ramp(s) volume

Formula (2):
Total soil removed = 600 + (2 * 300) + (2 * 300) = 1800 m³

### Energy Requirements
- **Earth Baseline:** Excavation of 1 m³ of soil varies between 12.09 MJ (3.36 kWh) for Silty Sand and 89 MJ (24.72 kWh) for Rock.
- **Mars Calculation:** Excavating 1800 m³ requires approximately 6045 kWh.

### Solar Energy Availability on Mars
- **Solar Radiance:** Mean radiance is ~200 W/m².
- **Formula (3):** 12.13 h (daylight) * 90% (efficiency factor) * 200 W/m² * 30% (panel efficiency) = 655.20 Wh/m²/d.
- **Solar Field Performance:** A 400 m² solar field collects ~264 kWh per Martian day.

## Results and Discussion
It would take approximately 29 Martian Days (Sols) to harvest the 6045 kWh of energy required purely for the excavation of a small 600 m³ room using a 400 m² solar field. To harvest this energy in a single day, a solar field of 11,424 m² (2.822 acres) would be required.

## Conclusion
The main conclusion is the impracticality of unusual/extensive excavation on Mars. Large-scale construction equipment like Tunnel Boring Machines (TBM) are energy-intensive. Future cities beyond Earth must focus on:
1. Ultra-efficient and sustainable structural design.
2. Smart and resource-efficient project planning.
3. Energy-efficient robotic construction techniques.
