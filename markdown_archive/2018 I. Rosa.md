# Multiscale Modeling and Testing of Protein-Bound Regolith and Soils

**Authors:** I. Rosa; M. D. Lepech; and D. J. Loftus
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Unlike Earth, there are very limited naturally occurring resources readily available in extraterrestrial environments, such as the Moon or Mars. Transporting large amounts of materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, this work focuses on a novel class of composites for use in limited resource environments: protein-bound soils. The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (protein or other biopolymer) to create a versatile composite with compressive strength comparable to that of ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite’s fracture properties in order to design a durable material that can resist extreme environments.

## Introduction
In 2015, NASA announced its *Journey to Mars Roadmap*, describing how in situ resource utilization (ISRU) was key to reduce the cost of space exploration. Regolith, abundant on both the Moon and Mars, has the potential of providing the raw materials needed to build protective structures against radiation and meteorite impacts. Protein-bound regolith could provide a low-energy, in situ construction material for roads, landing pads, and habitat infrastructure.

## Multiscale Framework

1.  **Nanoscale (< 1 µm):** Nanoindentation used for mechanical characterization of desiccated protein.
2.  **Microscale (1 – 5 µm):** MicroCT imaging and Statistically Equivalent Periodic Unit Cell (SEPUC) methods to capture geometric arrangements of soil, protein bridges, and voids.
3.  **Mesoscale (0.5 – 3 mm):** Rigid Body Spring Model (RBSM) for fracture modeling and crack propagation.
4.  **Macroscale (> 0.5 cm):** Triaxial and uniaxial testing coupled with Digital Image Correlation (DIC) for experimental validation.

## Conclusions & Future Work
The framework provides a methodological foundation for studying protein-bound soils. The goal is to synthesize computational simulations and material experiments to optimize mix designs for varying environments and load requirements. Future work will extend the framework to include system-scale modeling for full-scale extraterrestrial structures.
