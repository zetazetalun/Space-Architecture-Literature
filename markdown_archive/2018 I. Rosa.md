Unlike Earth, there are very limited naturally occurring resources readily available in extraterrestrial environments, such as the Moon or Mars. Transporting large amounts of materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, this work focuses on a novel class of composites for use in limited resource environments: protein-bound soils. For space applications, the term regolith is used to refer to the loose soil-like material on the Moon, asteroids, and other planetary bodies. The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (protein or other biopolymer) to create a versatile composite with compressive strength comparable to that of ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite's fracture properties in order to design a durable material that can resist extreme environments. At the nanoscale, nanoindentation is used to obtain the mechanical properties of the dry protein after dissolving in water and desiccating. At the microscale, the creation of statistically equivalent periodic unit cells captures the interactions between soil particles, protein, and voids. Virtual tests are then used to obtain basic mechanical parameters. At the mesoscale, a rigid body spring model (RBSM) is used to simulate fracture using the homogenized properties obtained at the microscale. At the macroscale, triaxial testing and digital image correlation tests are used to corroborate and modify the simulations. The final goal of this project is to have a robust computational simulation that can enable the prediction of the mechanical properties of the protein-bound regolith material to be used to design future structures on the Moon, Mars, or other celestial bodies.\n\n## INTRODUCTION\nOne of the most challenging aspects of space surface operations is providing protection to both equipment and personnel after landing at extraterrestrial destinations. Without a strong magnetic field or a protective gaseous atmosphere, radiation exposure and the risk of meteorite impacts on extraterrestrial surfaces is a significant hurdle to advance exploration. In 2015, NASA announced its Journey to Mars Roadmap, and in it, described how in situ resource utilization was key to reduce the cost of space exploration and provide key infrastructure for successful missions (NASA 2015). Regolith, abundant on both the Moon and Mars, has the potential of providing the raw materials needed to build protective structures against radiation (Miller et al. 2009; Simonsen 1991). Protein-bound regolith could provide a low-energy, in situ construction material that can provide this protective infrastructure as well as soil stabilization for roads and landing pads.\n\nCurrent approaches for providing radiation shielding or impact protection require the transport of structures constructed on Earth, resulting in high launch costs, or the use of energy intensive methods in situ to sinter, bind or stabilize extraterrestrial soils. To overcome these challenges, we have examined the concept of solidifying soil with a biopolymer binder to create a versatile construction composite. The protein-bound soil composite can be produced by mixing soil, water, and protein binder in situ, then removing water by evaporation or freeze drying... The binding proteins (or other biopolymers), which comprise less than 13% of the total composite mass, could either be produced on Earth and launched in small amounts to begin construction, or produced extraterrestrially by engineered microorganisms that are grown locally.\n\n## DEVELOPMENT OF A MULTISCALE FRAMEWORK\nEvery heterogeneous composite has unique constituents, geometric configurations and length scales that merit tailored multiscale frameworks when studying them. In creating a multiscale framework for protein-bound soils, the microstructure was the central focus of the modeling. At a resolution of approximately 1 µm, protein features, soil particles and void spaces can be clearly observed and the heterogeneity of the material becomes apparent.\n\n### Nanoscale: Nanoindentation for Mechanical Response of Desiccated Protein\nNanoindentation is a powerful technique that has been used with much success to understand the mechanical properties of both biological and cementitious materials.\n\n### Microscale: MicroCT Imaging and Micromechanical Modeling\nMicroscale computational models can reduce the number of time consuming laboratory experiments and measurements needed to understand the behavior of new and existing heterogeneous materials.\n\n### Mesoscale: Modeling of Crack Propagation in Homogenized Material\nMesoscale models, when there is good correspondence with experimental results, can give powerful insights into how cracks propagate within a homogenized material and their effects on ultimate strength. Although many models for crack simulations exist, the one implemented in this multiscale framework is a lattice model known as the Rigid Body Spring Method (RBSM).\n\n### Macroscale: Triaxial and Uniaxial Testing for Mechanical Properties\nAt the macroscale, experimental testing can provide validation of the numerical models and further insight into the material's overall behavior. In this framework, two experimental techniques are included: uniaxial tests, aided by 3-D digital image correlation (3D DIC); and triaxial tests.\n\n## CONCLUSIONS & FUTURE WORK\nThis paper presents the developing framework for a multi-scale understanding of protein-bound soils. The framework's primary engine is micromechanics modeling and microscale imaging to capture the material's heterogeneity. This is aided by nanoindentation tests to understand the mechanical behavior of dry proteins. The results at the microscale are then implemented in a mesoscale model to simulate fracture properties and crack development. Finally, results are validated by macroscale tests of the bulk material.
