# Multiscale Modeling and Testing of Protein-Bound Regolith and Soils\n\n**Authors:** I. Rosa, M. D. Lepech, and D. J. Loftus\n\n**Journal:** Earth and Space 2018\n\n**Publisher:** ASCE\n\n## Abstract\nExtraterrestrial construction presents challenges due to limited naturally occurring resources. This work focuses on protein-bound soils, a novel class of composites produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (protein). The paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite’s fracture properties to design durable materials for extreme environments. The framework spans from the nanoscale (mechanical properties of dry protein) to the macroscale (triaxial testing and digital image correlation) to predict mechanical properties for structures on the Moon or Mars.\n\n## Introduction\nProviding protection against radiation and meteorite impacts is a primary hurdle for space exploration. NASA's Journey to Mars Roadmap (2015) emphasizes that in situ resource utilization (ISRU) is key to reducing costs. Regolith is abundant on the Moon and Mars and can provide raw materials for protective structures. Protein-bound regolith offers a low-energy, in situ construction material for infrastructure like roads and landing pads.\n\n## Development of a Multiscale Framework\nThe framework integrates numerical and experimental techniques across four scales:\n\n1. **Nanoscale (< 1 µm):** Uses nanoindentation to characterize the elastic response, hardness, and plasticity of the desiccated protein binder. Preliminary tests show Young's modulus of approximately 4 MPa for the binder.\n2. **Microscale (1–5 µm):** Utilizes MicroCT imaging to create Statistically Equivalent Periodic Unit Cells (SEPUC) to capture interactions between soil particles, protein, and voids.\n3. **Mesoscale (0.5–3 mm):** Employs a Rigid Body Spring Model (RBSM) to simulate fracture response and crack propagation based on homogenized properties from the microscale.\n4. **Macroscale (> 0.5 cm):** Involves triaxial and uniaxial testing coupled with 3-D digital image correlation (DIC) to validate numerical models and analyze yield behavior.\n\n## Material Comparison: Protein-Bound Soil vs. Concrete\n\n| Property | Cement Mortar | Protein-Bound Regolith |\n| :--- | :--- | :--- |\n| Porosity | 7–20% | 20–40% |\n| Binder Weight % | Up to 25% | 5–15% |\n| Stiffness (GPa) | ~22 GPa | ~1.2 GPa |\n| Compressive Strength (MPa) | ~35 MPa | ~12 MPa |\n| Binder Young’s Modulus | 10–25 MPa | ~4 MPa |\n\n## Conclusions & Future Work\nThe proposed multiscale approach enables the prediction of mechanical properties for biopolymer-bound soil composites, facilitating the design of load-bearing structures for varying extraterrestrial environments. Future work includes optimizing mix designs and expanding the framework to full-scale system simulations.\n\n## References\nAkroyd, T. N. W. (1962). CHAPTER 3 - MIX DESIGN.
