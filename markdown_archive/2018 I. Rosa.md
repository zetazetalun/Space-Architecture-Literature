# Multiscale Modeling and Testing of Protein-Bound Regolith and Soils

**I. Rosa**; **M. D. Lepech**; and **D. J. Loftus**

**Abstract:** Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Unlike Earth, there are very limited naturally occurring resources readily available in extraterrestrial environments, such as the Moon or Mars. Transporting large amounts of materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, this work focuses on a novel class of composites for use in limited resource environments: protein-bound soils. For space applications, the term regolith is used to refer to the loose soil-like material on the Moon, asteroids, and other planetary bodies. The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (protein or other biopolymer) to create a versatile composite with compressive strength comparable to that of ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite’s fracture properties in order to design a durable material that can resist extreme environments. At the nanoscale, nanoindentation is used to obtain the mechanical properties of the dry protein after dissolving in water and desiccating. At the microscale, the creation of statistically equivalent periodic unit cells captures the interactions between soil particles, protein, and voids. Virtual tests are then used to obtain basic mechanical parameters. At the mesoscale, a rigid body spring model (RBSM) is used to simulate fracture using the homogenized properties obtained at the microscale. At the macroscale, triaxial testing and digital image correlation tests are used to corroborate and modify the simulations. The final goal of this project is to have a robust computational simulation that can enable the prediction of the mechanical properties of the protein-bound regolith material to be used to design future structures on the Moon, Mars, or other celestial bodies.

## INTRODUCTION

One of the most challenging aspects of space surface operations is providing protection to both equipment and personnel after landing at extraterrestrial destinations. Without a strong magnetic field or a protective gaseous atmosphere, radiation exposure and the risk of meteorite impacts on extraterrestrial surfaces is a significant hurdle to advance exploration. In 2015, NASA announced its Journey to Mars Roadmap, and in it, described how in situ resource utilization was key to reduce the cost of space exploration and provide key infrastructure for successful missions (NASA 2015). Regolith, abundant on both the Moon and Mars, has the potential of providing the raw materials needed to build protective structures against radiation (Miller et al. 2009; Simonsen 1991). Protein-bound regolith could provide a low-energy, in situ construction material that can provide this protective infrastructure as well as soil stabilization for roads and landing pads.

## DEVELOPMENT OF A MULTISCALE FRAMEWORK

### 1. Nanoscale: Nanoindentation for Mechanical Response of Desiccated Protein
Nanoindentation is used to test for a material’s constituent’s individual hardness and elastic properties in-situ. Preliminary tests have proven the feasibility of obtaining the linear elastic properties of dried protein.

### 2. Microscale: MicroCT Imaging and Micromechanical Modeling
Virtual 3-D representative volume elements were created utilizing Statistically Equivalent Periodic Unit Cells (SEPUC) to capture the geometric arrangement of soil particles, protein features, and void spaces.

### 3. Mesoscale: Modeling of Crack Propagation in Homogenized Material
A lattice model known as the Rigid Body Spring Method (RBSM) is implemented to simulate fracture behavior and crack propagation within the homogenized material.

### 4. Macroscale: Triaxial and Uniaxial Testing for Mechanical Properties
Experimental testing provides validation of numerical models. Techniques include uniaxial tests aided by 3-D digital image correlation (3D DIC) and triaxial tests to understand yield and plastic behavior under the Mohr-Coulomb criteria.

## CONCLUSIONS & FUTURE WORK

This paper presents a developing framework for a multi-scale understanding of protein-bound soils. The framework’s primary engine is micromechanics modeling and microscale imaging to capture heterogeneity, aided by nanoindentation of dry proteins. Results are implemented in a mesoscale model to simulate fracture and validated by macroscale tests. This approach has the potential to accelerate the development of protein-bound soil composites for varying environments and load requirements.
