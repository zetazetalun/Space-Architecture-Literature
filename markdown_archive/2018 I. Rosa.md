# Multiscale Modeling and Testing of Protein-Bound Regolith and Soils

**I. Rosa**; **M. D. Lepech**; and **D. J. Loftus**

## ABSTRACT

Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Unlike Earth, there are very limited naturally occurring resources readily available in extraterrestrial environments, such as the Moon or Mars. Transporting large amounts of materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, this work focuses on a novel class of composites for use in limited resource environments: protein-bound soils. For space applications, the term regolith is used to refer to the loose soil-like material on the Moon, asteroids, and other planetary bodies. The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (protein or other biopolymer) to create a versatile composite with compressive strength comparable to that of ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite’s fracture properties in order to design a durable material that can resist extreme environments. At the nanoscale, nanoindentation is used to obtain the mechanical properties of the dry protein after dissolving in water and desiccating. At the microscale, the creation of statistically equivalent periodic unit cells captures the interactions between soil particles, protein, and voids. Virtual tests are then used to obtain basic mechanical parameters. At the mesoscale, a rigid body spring model (RBSM) is used to simulate fracture using the homogenized properties obtained at the microscale. At the macroscale, triaxial testing and digital image correlation tests are used to corroborate and modify the simulations. The final goal of this project is to have a robust computational simulation that can enable the prediction of the mechanical properties of the protein-bound regolith material to be used to design future structures on the Moon, Mars, or other celestial bodies.

## INTRODUCTION

One of the most challenging aspects of space surface operations is providing protection to both equipment and personnel after landing at extraterrestrial destinations. In 2015, NASA announced its Journey to Mars Roadmap, and in it, described how in situ resource utilization (ISRU) was key to reduce the cost of space exploration and provide key infrastructure for successful missions. Regolith, abundant on both the Moon and Mars, has the potential of providing the raw materials needed to build protective structures against radiation. Protein-bound regolith could provide a low-energy, in situ construction material that can provide this protective infrastructure as well as soil stabilization for roads and landing pads.

## DEVELOPMENT OF A MULTISCALE FRAMEWORK

The framework incorporates micromechanical models that capture the geometric configuration of constituents, binder connections, and overall porosity. Key components include:

1.  **Nanoscale:** Nanoindentation to determine the mechanical properties of desiccated protein.
2.  **Microscale:** MicroCT imaging to create Statistically Equivalent Periodic Unit Cells (SEPUC) that capture soil, protein, and void arrangement.
3.  **Mesoscale:** Rigid Body Spring Modeling (RBSM) to simulate crack propagation and fracture in homogenized material.
4.  **Macroscale:** Triaxial and uniaxial tests to validate numerical models and understand the bulk failure surface.

### Material Properties

Preliminary results compare protein-bound regolith to Portland cement mortar:

*   **Cement Mortar:** Stiffness ~22 GPa, Compressive Strength ~35 MPa.
*   **Protein-Bound Regolith:** Stiffness ~1.2 GPa, Compressive Strength ~12 MPa.

## CONCLUSIONS & FUTURE WORK

This multiscale approach has the potential to accelerate the development of protein-bound soil composites by providing a predictive tool for tailoring mix designs for varying extraterrestrial environments and load requirements. Future work will extend the framework to include full-scale system modeling of extraterrestrial structures.
