## ABSTRACT
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Unlike Earth, there are very limited naturally occurring resources readily available in extraterrestrial environments, such as the Moon or Mars. Transporting large amounts of materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, this work focuses on a novel class of composites for use in limited resource environments: protein-bound soils. For space applications, the term regolith is used to refer to the loose soil-like material on the Moon, asteroids, and other planetary bodies. The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (protein or other biopolymer) to create a versatile composite with compressive strength comparable to that of ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite's fracture properties in order to design a durable material that can resist extreme environments. At the nanoscale, nanoindentation is used to obtain the mechanical properties of the dry protein after dissolving in water and desiccating. At the microscale, the creation of statistically equivalent periodic unit cells captures the interactions between soil particles, protein, and voids. Virtual tests are then used to obtain basic mechanical parameters. At the mesoscale, a rigid body spring model (RBSM) is used to simulate fracture using the homogenized properties obtained at the microscale. At the macroscale, triaxial testing and digital image correlation tests are used to corroborate and modify the simulations. The final goal of this project is to have a robust computational simulation that can enable the prediction of the mechanical properties of the protein-bound regolith material to be used to design future structures on the Moon, Mars, or other celestial bodies.

## INTRODUCTION
One of the most challenging aspects of space surface operations is providing protection to both equipment and personnel after landing at extraterrestrial destinations. Without a strong magnetic field or a protective gaseous atmosphere, radiation exposure and the risk of meteorite impacts on extraterrestrial surfaces is a significant hurdle to advance exploration. In 2015, NASA announced its *Journey to Mars Roadmap*, and in it, described how in situ resource utilization was key to reduce the cost of space exploration and provide key infrastructure for successful missions (NASA 2015). Regolith, abundant on both the Moon and Mars, has the potential of providing the raw materials needed to build protective structures against radiation (Miller et al. 2009; Simonsen 1991). Protein-bound regolith could provide a low-energy, in situ construction material that can provide this protective infrastructure as well as soil stabilization for roads and landing pads.

## MULTISCALE FRAMEWORK
The framework consists of four primary scales:
1. **Nanoscale (< 1 µm):** Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Nanoindentation for protein characterization.
2. **Microscale (1 µm - 5 µm):** Statistically Equivalent Periodic Unit Cells and MicroCT imaging for micromechanical properties.
3. **Mesoscale (0.5 mm - 3 mm):** Rigid Body Spring Model (RBSM) for fracture modeling.
4. **Macroscale (> 0.5 cm):** Triaxial testing and Digital Image Correlation (DIC) for experimental validation.

## CONCLUSIONS & FUTURE WORK
This paper presents the developing framework for a multi-scale understanding of protein-bound soils. The framework's primary engine is micromechanics modeling and microscale imaging to capture the material's heterogeneity. This is aided by nanoindentation tests to understand the mechanical behavior of dry proteins. The results at the microscale are then implemented in a mesoscale model to simulate fracture properties and crack development. Finally, results are validated by macroscale tests of the bulk material. This multiscale approach has the potential to accelerate the development of protein-bound soil composites and can serve as a guiding force for tailoring mix designs for varying environments and load requirements.
