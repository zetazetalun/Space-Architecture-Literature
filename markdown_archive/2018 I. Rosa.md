# Multiscale Modeling and Testing of Protein-Bound Regolith and Soils

**Authors:** I. Rosa, M. D. Lepech, and D. J. Loftus  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Unlike Earth, there are very limited naturally occurring resources readily available in extraterrestrial environments, such as the Moon or Mars. Transporting large amounts of materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, this work focuses on a novel class of composites for use in limited resource environments: protein-bound soils. The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (protein or other biopolymer) to create a versatile composite with compressive strength comparable to that of ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite's fracture properties in order to design a durable material that can resist extreme environments.

## Introduction
In 2015, NASA announced its *Journey to Mars Roadmap*, identifying in situ resource utilization (ISRU) as key to reducing the cost of space exploration. Regolith, abundant on both the Moon and Mars, can be used to build protective structures against radiation and meteorite impacts. Protein-bound regolith provides a low-energy, in situ construction material for infrastructure such as roads and landing pads.

## Multiscale Framework
The study defines four scales for modeling and testing:

1.  **Nanoscale (< 1 µm):** Uses nanoindentation and Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) for protein characterization and understanding the elastic response of desiccated protein binders.
2.  **Microscale (1 – 5 µm):** Micromechanical modeling using Statistically Equivalent Periodic Unit Cells (SEPUC) created from MicroCT scans to capture interactions between soil, protein, and voids.
3.  **Mesoscale (0.5 – 3 mm):** Uses a Rigid Body Spring Model (RBSM) to simulate crack propagation and fracture response in the homogenized material.
4.  **Macroscale (> 0.5 cm):** Experimental validation through triaxial testing and Digital Image Correlation (DIC) to understand failure yield surfaces and fracture behavior.

## Conclusions & Future Work
The proposed multiscale approach has the potential to accelerate the development of protein-bound soil composites by providing a guiding force for tailoring mix designs for varying environments. Future work includes expanding the framework to the systems scale to understand how full-scale extraterrestrial structures will behave.
