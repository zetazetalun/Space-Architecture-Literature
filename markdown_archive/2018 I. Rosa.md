# Multiscale Modeling and Testing of Protein-Bound Regolith and Soils

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial construction presents challenges due to limited resources. This work focuses on protein-bound soils, a novel class of composites for space applications. The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder to create a material with compressive strength comparable to ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite's fracture properties across nanoscale (nanoindentation), microscale (periodic unit cells), mesoscale (rigid body spring model), and macroscale (triaxial testing).

## Introduction
NASA's Journey to Mars Roadmap identifies in situ resource utilization (ISRU) as key to reducing exploration costs. Protein-bound regolith offers a low-energy, in situ construction material for radiation shielding, landing pads, and roads. While compared to concrete, protein-bound soils have higher porosity (20-40%) and a more compliant binder (5-15% by weight).

## Development of a Multiscale Framework
1. **Nanoscale**: Nanoindentation is used to obtain mechanical properties of the desiccated protein binder (estimated Young's modulus ~4 MPa).
2. **Microscale**: MicroCT imaging (1 um resolution) captures soil particles, protein film bridges, and voids. Statistically Equivalent Periodic Unit Cells (SEPUC) are created using genetic algorithms to represent the microstructure.
3. **Mesoscale**: A Rigid Body Spring Model (RBSM) simulates crack propagation and fracture within the homogenized material. This lattice model accounts for the high deformation and non-linear behavior of protein-bound soils.
4. **Macroscale**: Triaxial and uniaxial tests provide validation. 3-D digital image correlation (DIC) is used to observe strain development and crack initiation.

## Results and Comparison
- **Cement Mortar**: Stiffness ~22 GPa, Compressive Strength ~35 MPa.
- **Protein-Bound Regolith**: Stiffness ~1.2 GPa, Compressive Strength ~12 MPa.
- **Porosity**: 20-40% (compared to 7-20% for concrete).

## Conclusions
The multiscale approach accelerates the development of bio-inspired ISRU composites, enabling the tailoring of mix designs for specific extraterrestrial environments. Future work includes system-scale extrapolation to full extraterrestrial structures.
