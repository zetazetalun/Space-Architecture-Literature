# Multiscale Modeling and Testing of Protein-Bound Regolith and Soils

**Authors:** I. Rosa, M. D. Lepech, D. J. Loftus

**Affiliations:** Stanford University, NASA Ames Research Center

**Conference:** Earth and Space 2018

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Unlike Earth, there are very limited naturally occurring resources readily available in extraterrestrial environments, such as the Moon or Mars. Transporting large amounts of materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, this work focuses on a novel class of composites for use in limited resource environments: protein-bound soils. The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (protein or other biopolymer) to create a versatile composite with compressive strength comparable to that of ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite’s fracture properties in order to design a durable material that can resist extreme environments.

## Introduction
In 2015, NASA announced its Journey to Mars Roadmap, describing how in situ resource utilization (ISRU) was key to reduce the cost of space exploration and provide key infrastructure. Regolith, abundant on both the Moon and Mars, has the potential of providing raw materials to build protective structures against radiation and meteorite impacts. Protein-bound regolith could provide a low-energy, in situ construction material for roads and landing pads.

### Key Material Characteristics
- **Composition:** Soil, water, and protein binder (<13% of total mass).
- **Process:** Produced by mixing and then removing water by evaporation or freeze drying.
- **Binding Proteins:** Can be launched from Earth or produced extraterrestrially by engineered microorganisms.
- **Performance:** Ultimate compressive strengths similar to ordinary Portland cement concrete (~12 MPa for protein-bound regolith vs ~35 MPa for concrete).

## Development of a Multiscale Framework
The proposed framework encompasses four length scales:

1. **Nanoscale (< 1 µm):** Uses nanoindentation to obtain mechanical properties of the dry protein binder. Preliminary Young’s modulus estimated at ~4 MPa.
2. **Microscale (1 – 5 µm):** Creation of statistically equivalent periodic unit cells (SEPUC) from MicroCT scans to capture soil-protein-void interactions. Porosity ranges from 20-40%.
3. **Mesoscale (0.5 – 3 mm):** Employs a Rigid Body Spring Model (RBSM) to simulate fracture and predict strength.
4. **Macroscale (> 0.5 cm):** Triaxial and uniaxial testing coupled with Digital Image Correlation (DIC) for experimental validation.

## Comparison: Protein-Bound Soils vs. Cement Mortar
| Property | Protein-Bound Regolith | Cement Mortar |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Stiffness (Young's Modulus) | ~1.2 GPa | ~22 GPa |
| Compressive Strength | ~12 MPa | ~35 MPa |
| Porosity | 20-40% | 7-20% |
| Binder Weight % | 5-15% | Up to 25% |

## Conclusions & Future Work
This multiscale approach has the potential to accelerate the development of protein-bound soil composites and guide tailored mix designs for varying environments. Current efforts focus on optimizing mix components and proportions to meet specific load requirements for extraterrestrial structures.
