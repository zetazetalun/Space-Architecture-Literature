# Multiscale Modeling and Testing of Protein-Bound Regolith and Soils

**Authors:** I. Rosa, M. D. Lepech, D. J. Loftus
**Published in:** Earth and Space 2018 (ASCE), pp. 580-590

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Unlike Earth, there are very limited naturally occurring resources readily available in extraterrestrial environments, such as the Moon or Mars. Transporting large amounts of materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, this work focuses on a novel class of composites for use in limited resource environments: protein-bound soils. The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder (protein or other biopolymer) to create a versatile composite with compressive strength comparable to that of ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite’s fracture properties in order to design a durable material that can resist extreme environments.

## Introduction
One of the most challenging aspects of space surface operations is providing protection to both equipment and personnel. Without a strong magnetic field or a protective gaseous atmosphere, radiation exposure and the risk of meteorite impacts are significant hurdles. In 2015, NASA’s *Journey to Mars Roadmap* identified In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) as key to reducing costs. Regolith, abundant on the Moon and Mars, has the potential to provide raw materials for protective structures. Protein-bound regolith could provide a low-energy, in situ construction material for roads, landing pads, and radiation shielding.

## Multiscale Framework
1.  **Nanoscale (< 1 µm):** Uses nanoindentation and SEM to characterize protein properties (Young's modulus of desiccated protein estimated at ~4 MPa).
2.  **Microscale (Resolution 1-5 µm):** Employs Statistically Equivalent Periodic Unit Cells (SEPUC) and MicroCT imaging to model interactions between particles, protein bridges, and voids.
3.  **Mesoscale (Mesh Size 0.5-3 mm):** Uses a Rigid Body Spring Model (RBSM) to simulate crack propagation and fracture response.
4.  **Macroscale (> 0.5 cm):** Triaxial testing and Digital Image Correlation (DIC) for experimental validation of the numerical models.

## Conclusions & Future Work
The multiscale approach allows for the acceleration of protein-bound soil composite development by tailoring mix designs for varying environments. Future work includes optimizing mix proportions and extending the framework to system-scale structural modeling for full-scale extraterrestrial structures.
