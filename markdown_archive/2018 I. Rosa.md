# Multiscale Modeling and Testing of Protein-Bound Regolith and Soils

**Authors:** I. Rosa; M. D. Lepech; and D. J. Loftus  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, pp. 580-590

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial construction presents many interesting and new challenges. Transporting large amounts of materials from Earth is cost prohibitive. Thus, this work focuses on a novel class of composites for use in limited resource environments: protein-bound soils (regolith). The composite is produced by mixing soil, water, and a biopolymer binder to create a versatile composite with compressive strength comparable to that of ordinary Portland cement concrete. This paper proposes a multi-scale framework to model the composite’s fracture properties in order to design a durable material that can resist extreme environments.

## Introduction
One of the most challenging aspects of space surface operations is providing protection to both equipment and personnel. Without a strong magnetic field or atmosphere, radiation exposure and meteorite impacts are significant hurdles. In 2015, NASA’s *Journey to Mars Roadmap* identified ISRU as key to reducing exploration costs. Protein-bound regolith could provide a low-energy, in situ construction material for protective infrastructure and soil stabilization for roads and landing pads.

## Development of a Multiscale Framework
The study proposes a four-scale framework:

1.  **Nanoscale (< 1 µm):** Uses nanoindentation and Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) to characterize protein properties (Young’s modulus estimated at ~4 MPa for desiccated protein).
2.  **Microscale (1 – 5 µm):** Uses MicroCT imaging and Statistically Equivalent Periodic Unit Cells to capture interactions between soil particles, protein, and voids.
3.  **Mesoscale (0.5 – 3 mm):** Employs a Rigid Body Spring Model (RBSM) to simulate fracture response and crack propagation.
4.  **Macroscale (> 0.5 cm):** Involves triaxial and uniaxial testing coupled with Digital Image Correlation (DIC) for experimental validation.

## Key Findings and Material Properties
*   **Porosity:** Protein-bound soils range from 20-40% porosity, which is higher than typical concrete (7-20%).
*   **Binder Content:** Binder comprises 5-15% by weight.
*   **Mechanical Strength:** Protein-bound regolith has a compressive strength of approximately 12 MPa and a stiffness of approximately 1.2 GPa, compared to 35 MPa and 22 GPa for standard cement mortar.
*   **Water Source:** Water for manufacture could be harvested from lunar shadowed craters (estimated 5.6% water ice by mass) or Martian water ice deposits.

## Conclusions & Future Work
The framework provides a methodological foundation for optimizing ISRU material mix designs. Future work aims to synthesize computational simulations with material experiments to predict how full-scale extraterrestrial structures will behave under varied load requirements and environments.
