# A review towards the design of extraterrestrial structures: From regolith to human outposts

**Authors:** Nicos Kalapodis, Georgios Kampas, Olga-Joan Ktenidou  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 175 (2020) 540–569  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.actaastro.2020.05.038

## Abstract
The design of a permanent human habitat on a planetary body other than the Earth is a vision gaining momentum through missions like Artemis and Insight. This paper presents a meticulous selection of significant publications across three main areas: (a) geotechnical engineering aspects of Lunar and Martian regolith; (b) ground motions from moonquakes and impacts; and (c) concepts for extraterrestrial (ET) structures including inflatable, deployable, and 3D-printed habitats.

## 1. Introduction
Civil engineering must expand towards the design and construction of Lunar and Martian outposts. Key challenges include high temperature fluctuations (up to 280 K), hazardous radiation (GCR and SEO), meteoroid impacts (2.4 to 72 km/s), and low gravity (0.17g on Moon, 0.38g on Mars).

### 2.4 Anchoring and Landing Pads
Anchoring is essential for modular structures in microgravity to prevent uplifting. Landing pads mitigate dust/plume effects. Proposed solutions include ISRU concrete (KOHLS-1) and sintered regolith tiles.

## 3. Seismic Hazards
Seismometers from Apollo missions (1969-1977) recorded over 12,000 events. Sources include:
1. Deep moonquakes (700-1100 km depth).
2. Shallow moonquakes (most energetic, up to 4.8 mb).
3. Thermal moonquakes.
4. Meteorite impacts (approx. 4000 per year over 1kg).

## 4. Extraterrestrial (ET) Structures
Structures are categorized into:
*   **Inflatable:** Efficient for volume-to-mass ratio (e.g., TransHab, Bigelow).
*   **Deployable:** Compact for transport, including folding hinges and origami-inspired designs.
*   **3D-Printed (ISRU):** Using sintering (laser, solar, or microwave) or contour crafting to minimize Earth-transported mass.

## 5. Discussion
The evolution of design has moved from rigid Earth-integrated modules (1990s) to inflatable systems, and finally to 3D-printed ISRU-based structures (2010s). 3D printing offers the highest potential for sustainable expansion by utilizing local materials for radiation shielding and structural integrity.
