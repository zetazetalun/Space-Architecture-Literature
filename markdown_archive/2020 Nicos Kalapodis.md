# A review towards the design of extraterrestrial structures: From regolith to human outposts

**Authors:** Nicos Kalapodis, Georgios Kampas, Olga-Joan Ktenidou
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 175 (2020) 540–569

## Abstract
The design of a permanent human habitat on a planetary body other than the Earth is an idea introduced many decades ago. This paper presents a selection of significant publications related to (a) geotechnical engineering aspects (regolith properties, anchoring, landing pads); (b) ground motions (Moonquakes and meteoroid impacts); and (c) extraterrestrial (ET) structural types (generic, inflatable, deployable, 3D-printed).

## 1. Introduction
Civil engineering must expand towards the design and construction of Lunar and Martian structures. Key environmental challenges include low gravity, temperature fluctuations, radiation, lack of atmosphere, meteoroid impacts, and Lunar dust. High temperature fluctuations (up to 280 K) and gravity (0.17g on the Moon; 0.38g on Mars) significantly impact structural performance.

## 2. Aspects of Geotechnical Engineering

### 2.1. Lunar Regolith
Regolith density ($D_R$) increases abruptly between 10 and 20 cm depth. Near-surface soil is characterized by low cohesion ($c$) and internal friction angles ($\phi'$).

### 2.2. Martian Regolith
Martian regolith is a mix of weathered and windblown material. The upper 5-m layer is mainly cohesionless basaltic sand. Minimum cohesion for Mars is estimated at 1–2 kPa.

### 2.5. Regolith-based Landing Pads
Landing pads mitigate dust and plume effects. The use of Lunar concrete (e.g., KOHLS-1) and robotic pavers (PDM) for VTVL pads has been demonstrated as viable ISRU construction.

## 3. Extraterrestrial Ground Motions
Moonquakes are classified into four types: deep, shallow, thermal, and meteoroid impacts. Shallow Moonquakes are the most energetic ($m_b \approx 5.5$). Marsquakes are generally fewer and smaller than Moonquakes but exhibit long durations.

## 4. Extraterrestrial Structures

### 4.1. Generic Structural Concepts
Focus on trusses, cable systems, and tensegrity. Benaroya and Ettouney proposed flat 3D truss structures supporting regolith shields.

### 4.2. Inflatable Structures
Lightweight, low-volume during transport. Key materials include Kevlar 49 and Nomex. Concepts include TransHab and the inflatable "tuft pillow" (Bateman et al.).

### 4.3. Deployable Structures
Capable of changing shape/size for mass reduction. Examples include folding hinges (IFH) and origami-inspired structures.

### 4.4. 3D-printed ET Structures
Agglomeration of regolith through sintering (Laser, Solar, Microwave) or binder jetting. 
* **Contour Crafting (CC):** Uses sulfur concrete or regolith extrusion.
* **D-shape:** Uses powder and special binding fluid.

## 5. Human-centered Concepts
Holistic designs integrate inflatable volumes with 3D-printed regolith shielding (e.g., Foster + Partners/De Kestelier). Strategies include MALEO (Modular Assembly in Low-Earth Orbit).

## 6. Discussion
Habitable structures must withstand extreme radiation, internal pressure, and meteoroid impacts. Recent trends favor 3D-printed structures utilizing an ISRU framework. Future research should prioritize soil-structure interaction (SSI) in low gravity.
