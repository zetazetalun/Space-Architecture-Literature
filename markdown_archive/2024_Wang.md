# To boldly go where no one has gone before: Sulfur concrete, a promising construction material fulfilling the demands for a sustainable future on celestial objects: A review

**Authors:** Q. Wang, D. Snoeck
**Journal:** Materials Today, Volume 72, January/February 2024
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mattod.2023.12.005

## Abstract
Sulfur concrete is considered one of the most accessible building materials for the Moon and Mars due to ISRU. It requires no water, a critical advantage in space. This review summarizes the history of sulfur concrete, its unique advantages, the use of lunar and Martian soil simulants as aggregates, and potential improvements including self-healing and polymer modification to overcome challenges like thermal sublimation and shrinkage.

## 1. Introduction
The need for long-term or permanent bases on extraterrestrial bodies has led to research into local resources. Sulfur, being abundant on the Moon and Mars, is a primary candidate for waterless concrete. NASA's Artemis and ESA's space resource strategies emphasize local resource utilization by 2040.

## 4. Building on Moon and Mars (ISRU)
- **Moon:** Sulfur is found as troilite (FeS). Chandrayaan-3 detected sulfur at the south pole.
- **Mars:** Regarded as sulfur-rich; sulfates and sulfites exist in high amounts.
- **Additive Manufacturing:** Technologies include Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) for basalt, contour crafting, powder-based ink jetting, and selective laser melting.

## 5. Conclusions
Sulfur concrete exhibits superior mechanical properties and corrosion resistance compared to Portland concrete. Challenges include poor performance at high temperatures and sublimation in vacuum. However, using regolith layers for insulation and polymer modifications can mitigate these issues. Its recyclability makes it a key candidate for circular construction in space.
