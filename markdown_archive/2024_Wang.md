# To boldly go where no one has gone before: Sulfur concrete, a promising construction material fulfilling the demands for a sustainable future on celestial objects: A review

**Authors:** Q. Wang, D. Snoeck
**Journal:** Materials Today, Volume 72, January/February 2024
**Publisher:** Elsevier

## Abstract
The need to build long-term or permanent bases on celestial bodies is a significant concern. Sulfur concrete, first proposed in the 20th century, has attracted interest as sulfur is one of the most accessible resources on the Moon and Mars through In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU). This review summarizes the history of sulfur concrete, its unique advantage of requiring no water, and its performance using lunar and Martian soil simulants. It discusses recyclability, circularity, and technical challenges like sublimation and thermal stability.

## Introduction
Human space exploration has accelerated, with programs like NASA's Artemis and ESA's space resource strategy targeting permanent presence on the Moon and Mars. Building such infrastructure requires using local resources to avoid the prohibitive cost (approx. $130,000/kg) of transporting materials from Earth. Sulfur concrete is highlighted as a primary candidate because it forms a high-strength bond through solidification below 140°C without the need for water.

## Key Characteristics and Performance
- **Mechanical Properties:** Modified sulfur concrete can achieve compressive strengths up to 115 MPa, comparable to high-strength Portland cement.
- **Sustainability:** Recyclability is a key feature; sulfur concrete can be remelted (above 120°C) and recast with minimal loss of strength.
- **ISRU Potential:** Sulfur is abundant on Mars (SO₃ up to 37 wt%) and present at lunar poles (detected by Chandrayaan-3).

## Challenges in Space Environments
1. **Sublimation:** In a vacuum, sulfur can sublimate. A 1 cm layer could disappear in 2 hours at 120°C, though burying the material under 30 cm of regolith mitigates this effect.
2. **Thermal Cycling:** Large temperature swings (e.g., -233°C to 123°C on the Moon) cause internal stresses due to phase transformations (monoclinic to orthorhombic) and expansion coefficient mismatches between sulfur and aggregates.

## Construction Techniques
- **Casting:** Using microwaves to melt sulfur/regolith mixtures.
- **3D Printing:** Techniques include Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM), Contour Crafting, and Selective Laser Melting/Sintering.
- **DMSI:** Dry-Mix/Steam Injection and other specialized ISRU-based processing methodologies.

## Conclusions
Sulfur concrete is a strong candidate for Class III (ISRU-based) habitats. Future research should focus on utilizing Phase-Change Materials (PCMs) to improve thermal performance and developing self-healing capabilities to mitigate cracking in extreme environments.
