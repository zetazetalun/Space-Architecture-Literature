# To boldly go where no one has gone before: Sulfur concrete, a promising construction material fulfilling the demands for a sustainable future on celestial objects: A review

**Authors:** Q. Wang, D. Snoeck
**Journal:** Materials Today, Volume 72, January/February 2024
**Publisher:** Elsevier
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.mattod.2023.12.005

## Abstract
The need to build a long-term or even permanent base is now a significant concern with the development of the exploration of extraterrestrial celestial bodies. Sulfur concrete was first proposed as a new building material in the 20th century. Recently, sulfur concrete has attracted much interest, as sulfur is considered one of the most accessible resources on the Moon and Mars, thanks to the in-situ resource utilization methodology. This review paper summarizes the history of sulfur concrete development and different modified sulfur concretes. Previous research on extraterrestrial building materials is also reviewed.

## Introduction
Since the 1960s, exploration has focused on the Moon and recently Mars (e.g., NASA Artemis, ESA Space Resource Strategy). Establishing installations on the Moon (2028-2040) and Mars (from 2030 onwards) requires reliable construction materials. Conventional materials like reinforced concrete are unreliable due to low gravity, thermal cycles, and the high cost of transport from Earth (~$130,000/kg). Sulfur concrete is a promising path as it requires no water and utilizes local resources.

## Characteristics of Sulfur Concrete
Sulfur concrete forms a high-strength material when it cools below 140°C. Its advantages include no water consumption and high early strength. However, challenges include sublimation in vacuum and thermal instability at temperatures near its melting point (~120°C).

## Key Findings for Space Architecture
- **Sublimation:** In a vacuum, 1 cm of sulfur concrete can sublimate in 2 hours at 120°C. However, at room temperature, it takes years. Using a top layer of loose regolith (30 cm depth) can stabilize temperatures to -20°C, mitigating sublimation.
- **Additives:** Polymers (DCPD) prevent macro-sulfur crystal formation, reducing shrinkage from 7% to manageable levels and improving durability.
- **3D Printing:** Techniques like Contour Crafting (extrusion-based) and Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) are proposed for automated construction using sulfur-regolith mixtures.
- **Recyclability:** Sulfur concrete is fully recyclable as it can be remelted and recast; strength can even increase after the first recast.
