## Abstract
Several recent theories and conceptual frameworks in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology suggest that earth's soils are not just strongly influenced by biota, but represent selective pressures. These ideas point toward soils as expressions of the effects of genes through the effects of organisms (i.e., extended phenotypes). The cumulative, interacting, overlapping effects of these extended phenotypes as manifested in the soil represent an *extended composite phenotype*. Significant biological changes should be reflected in qualitative pedological changes in soil morphology. This is demonstrated via research in the Ouachita Mountains, Arkansas, where changes in selective pressures on trees (linked to fire frequency) are associated with changes in soil morphology. This concept has implications for the search for extraterrestrial life and understanding Earth as a system of interwoven components.

## 1. Introduction
This paper argues that Earth's soils are an expression of the cumulative impacts of the biosphere on surface processes. Soils may represent the single best indication of the nature, state, and condition of earth surface systems. If soils are expressions of biological evolution, the implications for understanding the evolution of earth systems are profound. This paper reviews conceptual frameworks including biomantles, extended phenotypes, ecosystem engineers, and niche construction to propose a view of soil as a manifestation of both biotic effects and biological evolution.

## 2. Conceptual Framework
### 2.1. Soils as Biotic Constructs
van Breemen (1993) argued that soils are influenced by biota to increase net primary productivity, modifying texture, structure, and chemistry to favor growth (e.g., increasing nutrient supply, reducing losses).

### 2.2. Biomantles
Pedologists increasingly view soils as continuously-mixed biomantles. Biomechanical effects (e.g., bioturbation) are often as important as biochemical ones in creating textural differentiation and soil layers.

### 2.3. Extended Phenotypes and Niche Construction
Dawkins (1982) introduced the extended phenotype, referring to all effects of a gene on the environment. Ecosystem engineering and niche construction further describe how organisms modify their environment, influencing selection pressures for themselves and other species.

## 3. Paleosols and Coevolution
If soils are extended composite phenotypes, biological evolution should be reflected in the stratigraphic record. Research shows that grassland soils coevolved with grasses and herbivores over the past 40 Ma. Pedotypes (paleosols recognized in the field) can be considered trace fossils of local ecosystems. Significant events, such as the Permian-Triassic mass extinction, are characterized by distinct shifts in soil assemblages.

## 4. Tree–Soil Interactions
### 4.1. Case Study: Ouachita Mountains
The study explores how changes in forest composition (hardwood vs. pine) driven by fire frequency selection pressures impact soil morphology. 

### 4.2. Results of Tree-Soil Interactions
1. **Colonization:** Pines are primary colonizers of exposed rock, initiating weathering and pedogenesis earlier than hardwoods.
2. **Uprooting:** Pines are more prone to uprooting in shallow soils, causing more frequent biomechanical mixing.
3. **Mounding:** Soil is mounded beneath shortleaf pines, influencing rock fragment displacement; hardwoods show little to no mounding.
4. **Growth Rates:** Pines grow faster and have shorter lifespans, leading to more rapid soil formation and turnover rates.

## 5. Discussion and Conclusions
Soils as extended composite phenotypes imply a tight coupling between the biosphere and pedosphere. This framework suggests that while atmospheric signals of life may be transient, soil morphology provides long-lasting signatures. This offers a potential method for identifying extraterrestrial life on planets like Mars, where extinct biospheres might leave permanent pedologic records.
