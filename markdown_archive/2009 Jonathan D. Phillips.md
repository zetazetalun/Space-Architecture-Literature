# Soils as extended composite phenotypes\n\n**Author:** Jonathan D. Phillips\n**Journal:** Geoderma 149 (2009) 143–151\n\n## Abstract\nSeveral recent theories in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology suggest that Earth's soils are not just strongly influenced by biota but represent selective pressures. Soils are expressions of genetic effects through organisms (extended phenotypes). This paper argues that cumulative biological effects manifested in soil represent an extended composite phenotype (ECP). This notion suggests that significant biological changes (evolution) are reflected in qualitative pedological changes in soil morphology. This has implications for interpreting soils in environmental sciences and the search for extraterrestrial life.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nEarth's soils are an expression of the cumulative impacts of the biosphere on surface processes. As a product of geology, climate, biota, topography, and time, soils may represent the single best indication of the nature and condition of Earth surface systems. If soils are expressions of biological evolution by means of natural selection, the implications for understanding the evolution of Earth systems are profound. Key concepts include:\n- **Biomantles:** Surficial layers continually mixed by flora and fauna.\n- **Extended Phenotype:** Effects of a gene on the environment.\n- **Ecosystem Engineers:** Organisms that modify the environment to influence natural selection.\n\n## 2. Conceptual Framework\n### 2.1. Soils as biotic constructs\nvan Breemen (1993) argued that soils are influenced by biota to increase net primary productivity. Biological processes like weathering, increasing nutrient supply, and increasing water holding capacity make the Earth's crust more favorable for growth.\n\n### 2.2. Biomantles\nSoils are viewed as continuously-mixed biomantles. For example, in Antarctica, penguins have been found to initiate the development of ornithogenic soils, which have a major influence on soil properties.\n\n### 2.3. Extended phenotypes and niche construction\nDawkins (1982) introduced the extended phenotype, including all effects of a gene on the environment. Organisms and their biotopes (including soils) reciprocally define and construct each other.\n\n### 2.4. Biogeomorphology\nBiogeomorphology recognizes that surface processes and landforms cannot be fully understood without accounting for the feedbacks and mutual adjustments between biological and geophysical phenomena.\n\n## 3. Paleosols and coevolution\nPaleosols show biochemical signatures associated with different vegetation. For example, Dokuchaev (1883) showed that grassland soils have specific morphological properties attributable to vegetation regardless of parent material. Grassland soils have coevolved over the past 40 Ma with grasses and herbivores.\n\n## 4. Tree–soil interactions (Ouachita Mountains Case Study)\nStudies in the Ouachita National Forest (ONF), Arkansas, examine how specific tree species (Pinus echinata vs. hardwoods) influence soil formation. Key findings include:\n1. Pines are the first trees to colonize exposed rock and initiate pedogenesis.\n2. Pines are more likely to uproot, causing biomechanical mixing and soil mounding.\n3. These biological changes due to selective pressures (like fire frequency) are associated with significant changes in soil type and morphology.\n\n## 5. Discussion and conclusions\nThe notion of soils as biomantles and biotic constructs suggests that biological variations are manifested in soils and regolith. Soil responses to biological activities are extended genetic expressions. This framework suggests that a living biosphere on other planets (like Mars) would likely leave long-lasting impacts on soil morphology rather than just transient atmospheric signals. Thus, an explicit view of soils as ECP may yield useful clues about potential extraterrestrial life.
