## Abstract
Several recent theories and conceptual frameworks in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology, taken together, suggest the notion that earth's soils are not just strongly influenced by biota, but represent selective pressures. These ideas point in the direction of many aspects of soils as expressions of the effects of genes through the effects of organisms (i.e., extended phenotypes). The cumulative, interacting, overlapping effects of these extended phenotypes as manifested in the soil represent an extended composite phenotype. If this is the case, then we should expect major changes in biological evolution to be reflected in major changes in the types of soils. A brief review of paleopedology literature supports the notion of coevolution of soils and biota, not just in the sense of both responding to the same environmental forcings, but also with respect to explicit pedological expressions of biological change. The extended composite phenotype notion also suggests that significant biological changes should be reflected in significant qualitative pedological changes (i.e., fundamental changes in soil morphology beyond quantitative changes in specific short-lived soil properties). The concept of soils as extended composite phenotypes has repercussions for pedology, evolutionary biology, and interpretations of soils in other earth and environmental sciences. The notion of genetic signatures in soil morphology also has implications for the search for extraterrestrial life, and extends the notion of Earth as a set of tightly-coupled, densely interwoven systems.

## 1. Introduction
This paper argues that Earth's soils are an expression of the cumulative impacts of the biosphere on surface processes. As the product of the combined, interacting influences of geology, climate, biota, topography, and time, soils may represent the single best indication of the nature, state, and condition of earth surface systems. Thus, if soils are expressions of biological evolution, rather than, or in addition to, being simply influenced by biotic processes, implications are profound for understanding the evolution of earth systems.

## 2. Conceptual Framework

### 2.1. Soils as biotic constructs
Van Breemen (1993) argued that soils are influenced by biota in such a way as to increase net primary productivity. Biological processes which generally make Earth's crust more favorable for plant growth include weathering, increasing nutrient supply, reducing nutrient losses, increasing water holding capacity, and decreasing aeration.

### 2.2. Biomantles
Soils are increasingly viewed as continuously-mixed biomantles (Johnson, 1990; Paton et al., 1995). This perspective incorporates the idea of continuous bioturbation. Biomechanical effects have increasingly been found to be of comparable importance to biochemical effects.

### 2.3. Extended phenotypes and niche construction
Dawkins (1982) introduced the idea of the extended phenotype, including all the effects of a gene or genes on the environment. Ecosystem engineers are organisms which have a disproportionate influence on the environment relative to their numbers.

## 3. Paleosols and Coevolution
If soils are indeed extended composite phenotypes, then there should be evidence of fundamental changes in soils associated with biological evolution. Paleosols show different biochemical signatures associated with different vegetation (e.g., isotopes reflecting C3 vs C4 plants).

## 5. Discussion and Conclusions
The notion of soils as biomantles and biotic constructs indicates that biological variations and changes may be manifested in soils and regoliths. This view suggests that soils reflect the cumulative genetic effects of multiple generations of multiple organisms. The ECP framework also suggests initial possibilities with respect to searches for life on other planets. An explicit view of soils as ECP may yield useful clues about potential extraterrestrial pedologic signatures of life.
