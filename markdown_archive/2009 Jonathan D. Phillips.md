# Soils as extended composite phenotypes

**Author:** Jonathan D. Phillips
**Journal:** Geoderma 149 (2009) 143–151
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.geoderma.2008.11.028

## Abstract
Several recent theories and conceptual frameworks in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology, taken together, suggest the notion that earth's soils are not just strongly influenced by biota, but represent selective pressures. These ideas point in the direction of many aspects of soils as expressions of the effects of genes through the effects of organisms (i.e., extended phenotypes). The cumulative, interacting, overlapping effects of these extended phenotypes as manifested in the soil represent an extended composite phenotype. If this is the case, then we should expect major changes in biological evolution to be reflected in major changes in the types of soils. A brief review of paleopedology literature supports the notion of coevolution of soils and biota... The notion of genetic signatures in soil morphology also has implications for the search for extraterrestrial life.

## 1. Introduction
This paper argues that Earth's soils are an expression of the cumulative impacts of the biosphere on surface processes. As the product of the combined, interacting influences of (at least) geology, climate, biota, topography, and time, soils may represent the single best indication of the nature, state, and condition of earth surface systems. If soils are indeed a phenotype of biota, then coevolution of soils and biota also occurs in the specific sense of biological evolution by means of natural selection.

### Key Conceptual Frameworks:
- **Biomantle:** A surficial layer mixed by floral and faunal-turbation.
- **Extended Phenotype:** (Dawkins, 1982) all effects of a gene on the environment.
- **Ecosystem Engineers / Niche Construction:** Organisms that modify their environment to influence natural selection.
- **Biogeomorphology:** Tight coupling among biological, geological, and pedological processes.

## 2. Conceptual Framework
### 2.1. Soils as biotic constructs
Soils are influenced by biota to increase net primary productivity. Organisms affect soil texture, structure, erosion, organic matter content, and chemistry. Biotic processes generally make Earth's crust more favorable for plant growth via weathering, increasing nutrient supply, and increasing water holding capacity.

### 2.3. Extended phenotypes and niche construction
Ecosystem engineers are organisms which have a disproportionate influence on the environment relative to their numbers. The extended phenotype concept includes all the effects of a gene or a combination of genes on the environment through an organism's actions or behaviors.

## 4. Tree–soil interactions
Knoll (2003) highlighted the importance of the emergence of woody plants. Wood represented a substantial innovation with respect to storage of carbon and nutrients, and trees/shrubs increased the penetration of substrates by roots. Interactions between trees and soils were considered in an evolutionary framework by Binkley and Giardina (1998).

### 4.1 Study area: Ouachita Mountains
The study used the Ouachita Mountains (Arkansas, USA) to examine how fire frequency and tree species (Pines vs. Hardwoods) create distinct pedological signatures. It found that pines are more likely to colonize rock and uproot, leading to different rock fragment distributions and soil mounding compared to hardwoods.

## 5. Discussion and conclusions
The ECP (Extended Composite Phenotype) framework suggests initial possibilities with respect to searches for life on other planets. An extinct biosphere (such as may have existed on Mars) would be unlikely to leave its fingerprints in atmospheric composition alone. However, effects on soil morphology (pedologic signatures) are more long-lasting and could yield useful clues about potential extraterrestrial life.
