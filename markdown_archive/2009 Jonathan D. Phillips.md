# Soils as extended composite phenotypes

## Abstract
Several recent theories and conceptual frameworks in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology, taken together, suggest the notion that earth's soils are not just strongly influenced by biota, but represent selective pressures. These ideas point in the direction of many aspects of soils as expressions of the effects of genes through the effects of organisms (i.e., extended phenotypes). The cumulative, interacting, overlapping effects of these extended phenotypes as manifested in the soil represent an extended composite phenotype. If this is the case, then we should expect major changes in biological evolution to be reflected in major changes in the types of soils. A brief review of paleopedology literature supports the notion of coevolution of soils and biota, not just in the sense of both responding to the same environmental forcings, but also with respect to explicit pedological expressions of biological change. The extended composite phenotype notion also suggests that significant biological changes should be reflected in significant qualitative pedological changes (i.e., fundamental changes in soil morphology beyond quantitative changes in specific short-lived soil properties). This is demonstrated via recent research in the Ouachita Mountains, Arkansas, where changes in selective pressures on trees associated with fire frequency are linked to changes in soil morphology. The concept of soils as extended composite phenotypes has repercussions for pedology, evolutionary biology, and interpretations of soils in other earth and environmental sciences. The notion of genetic signatures in soil morphology also has implications for the search for extraterrestrial life, and extends the notion of Earth as a set of tightly-coupled, densely interwoven systems.

## 1. Introduction
This paper argues that Earth's soils are an expression of the cumulative impacts of the biosphere on surface processes. As the product of the combined, interacting influences of (at least) geology, climate, biota, topography, and time, soils may represent the single best indication of the nature, state, and condition of earth surface systems. If soils are expressions of biological evolution, rather than, or in addition to, being simply influenced by biotic processes, implications are profound for understanding the evolution of earth systems.

## 2. Conceptual Framework
### 2.1. Soils as biotic constructs
A view of soils as "biotic constructs" was laid out by van Breemen (1993), who argued that soils are influenced by biota in such a way as to increase net primary productivity. Mechanisms making a (soil) environment more favorable at an ecosystem scale can develop only if the number of individuals and species contributing to these mechanisms increase over time.

### 2.2. Biomantles
Soils, once conceived as a primarily geological mantle and a medium for biota, are increasingly viewed by pedologists as continuously-mixed biomantles. Even in Antarctica, a study found that penguins had the greatest influence on soil properties, initiating the development of ornithogenic soils.

### 2.3. Extended phenotypes and niche construction
Dawkins (1982) argued that restricting the concept of the phenotype to the genetic expression evident in an organism's body is too restrictive, and introduced the idea of the extended phenotype. Ecosystem engineers are organisms which have a disproportionate influence on the environment relative to their numbers.

## 3. Paleosols and Coevolution
If soils are indeed extended composite phenotypes, then there should be some evidence of fundamental changes in soils associated with biological evolution. Grassland soils have been shown to have specific morphological properties attributable to vegetation, organic matter, and earthworms regardless of parent material.

## 4. Tree–soil Interactions
The forests of the Ouachita Mountains, USA, offer an opportunity to test the hypothesis that biological changes are reflected in qualitative soil changes. A dominant selective factor, fire frequency, has been manipulated to produce changes in vegetation composition.

### 4.4.1. Effects on soil morphology
One important mechanism for soil deepening by trees is via uprooting. Coarse roots typically extend into weathered bedrock and penetrate joints in unweathered bedrock. This results in a characteristic pattern of stony surface layers overlying relatively stone-poor horizons.

## 5. Discussion and Conclusions
The model of ecosystem engineers supports a view of soils as integrated ecosystems structured by keystone or dominant engineering species. The ECP framework also suggests initial possibilities with respect to searches for life on other planets. An explicit view of soils as ECP may yield useful clues about potential extraterrestrial pedological signatures of life.
