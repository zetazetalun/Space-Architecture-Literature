# Soils as extended composite phenotypes

**Author:** Jonathan D. Phillips  
**Journal:** Geoderma 149 (2009) 143–151  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.geoderma.2008.11.028

## Abstract
Several recent theories and conceptual frameworks in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology, taken together, suggest the notion that earth's soils are not just strongly influenced by biota, but represent selective pressures. These ideas point in the direction of many aspects of soils as expressions of the effects of genes through the effects of organisms (i.e., extended phenotypes). The cumulative, interacting, overlapping effects of these extended phenotypes as manifested in the soil represent an extended composite phenotype. This has implications for the search for extraterrestrial life and understanding soils in other environmental sciences.

## 1. Introduction
This paper argues that Earth's soils are an expression of the cumulative impacts of the biosphere on surface processes. Soils may represent the single best indication of the nature, state, and condition of earth surface systems. If soils are indeed a phenotype of biota, then coevolution occurs in the specific sense of biological evolution by means of natural selection.

## 2. Conceptual Framework

### 2.1. Soils as biotic constructs
Van Breemen (1993) argued that soils are influenced by biota to increase net primary productivity. This includes:
*   Weathering and physical transformation of rock.
*   Increasing nutrient supply.
*   Reducing nutrient losses.
*   Increasing water holding capacity.
*   Decreasing compaction and increasing aeration.

### 2.2. Biomantles
Soils are increasingly viewed as continuously-mixed biomantles. In Antarctica, studies found that penguins initiated the development of ornithogenic soils, showing that even in extreme environments, biological agents are the primary drivers of soil properties.

### 2.3. Extended phenotypes and niche construction
*   **Extended Phenotype:** (Dawkins, 1982) All effects of a gene on the environment.
*   **Ecosystem Engineers:** Organisms that have a disproportionate influence on the environment relative to their numbers.
*   **Niche Construction:** The process by which organisms alter the environment to influence selective pressures.

## 3. Implications for Extraterrestrial Research
The Extended Composite Phenotype (ECP) framework suggests that a living biosphere would likely leave a signal in atmospheric chemistry, but an extinct biosphere (such as may have existed on Mars) would likely leave impacts on soil morphology that are more long-lasting. Explicit views of soils as ECP may yield clues about potential extraterrestrial pedologic signatures of life.

## 4. Key Tree-Soil Interactions (Case Study)
| Parameter | Pinus echinata (Pine) | Hardwoods (e.g., Oak) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Growth Rate | Rapid | Slow to Moderate |
| Lifespan | Moderate | Long |
| Biomechanical Mixing | High (due to uprooting) | Lower (more trunk breakage) |
| Soil Influence | Stony surface layers, deep penetration | Basal mounding, nutrient-rich microsites |

## 5. Conclusions
Soils are integrated ecosystems structured by keystone engineering species. This view treats soils as 'planetary skins' or 'membranes' that capture and transform energy, serving as a functional link between the lithosphere, atmosphere, and biosphere.
