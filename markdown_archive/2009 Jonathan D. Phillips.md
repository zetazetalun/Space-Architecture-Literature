## Abstract
Several recent theories and conceptual frameworks in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology, taken together, suggest the notion that earth's soils are not just strongly influenced by biota, but represent selective pressures. These ideas point in the direction of many aspects of soils as expressions of the effects of genes through the effects of organisms (i.e., extended phenotypes). The cumulative, interacting, overlapping effects of these extended phenotypes as manifested in the soil represent an extended composite phenotype. If this is the case, then we should expect major changes in biological evolution to be reflected in major changes in the types of soils. A brief review of paleopedology literature supports the notion of coevolution of soils and biota. The notion of genetic signatures in soil morphology also has implications for the search for extraterrestrial life, and extends the notion of Earth as a set of tightly-coupled, densely interwoven systems.

## 1. Introduction
This paper argues that Earth's soils are an expression of the cumulative impacts of the biosphere on surface processes. If soils are expressions of biological evolution, rather than simply influenced by biotic processes, implications are profound for understanding the evolution of earth systems. There already exist several ways of viewing soils—as media for plant growth, habitats, weathered mantles, and engineering materials. A view of soils as extended composite phenotypes arises from several existing conceptual frameworks:
* The idea of a **biomantle**: a surficial layer continually mixed by floral and faunal-turbation.
* The notion of the **extended phenotype**: effects of a gene on the environment.
* Concepts of **ecosystem engineers** and **niche construction**.
* Emergence of **biogeomorphology** and **ecogeomorphology**.

## 2. Conceptual Framework

### 2.1. Soils as biotic constructs
Van Breemen (1993) argued that soils are influenced by biota to increase net primary productivity. Biological processes generally make Earth's crust more favorable for plant growth through weathering, increasing nutrient supply, and decreasing compaction.

### 2.2. Biomantles
Soils are increasingly viewed as continuously-mixed biomantles. Biomechanical effects (bioturbation) are found to be of comparable importance to biochemical effects. Examples include the formation of vertical texture contrast soils (TCS).

### 2.3. Extended phenotypes and niche construction
Ecosystem engineers (e.g., ants, termites, beavers) have a disproportionate influence on their environment. Niche construction is the process by which organisms alter the environment to influence selective pressures.

## 3. Paleosols and coevolution
If soils are extended composite phenotypes, there should be evidence of fundamental changes in soils associated with biological evolution. Paleopedological evidence shows:
* Grassland soils coevolved with grasses and herbivores over the past 40 Ma.
* Distinct pedotypes (trace fossils of ecosystems) reflect changes in vegetation, such as the emergence of bunch grassland or sagebrush.
* Iron (Fe) loss in paleosols reflects atmospheric oxygen evolution and the rise of vascular plants.

## 5. Discussion and conclusions
The notion of soils as extended composite phenotypes (ECP) suggests that soils reflect the cumulative genetic effects of multiple generations of organisms. This framework suggests initial possibilities with respect to searches for life on other planets. While an extinct biosphere (such as on Mars) might not leave atmospheric fingerprints, impacts on soil morphology would be more long-lasting. Thus, an explicit view of soils as ECP may yield useful clues about extraterrestrial pedologic signatures of life.
