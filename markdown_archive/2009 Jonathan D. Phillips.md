# Soils as extended composite phenotypes ## Abstract Several recent theories and conceptual frameworks in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology suggest that Earth's soils represent selective pressures and expressions of genes through organisms (extended phenotypes). This paper explores the concept of soils as an 'extended composite phenotype' (ECP). Changes in biological evolution are reflected in major soil type changes, supported by paleopedology and research in the Ouachita Mountains linking fire frequency, tree types, and soil morphology. The notion of genetic signatures in soil morphology has implications for searching for extraterrestrial life and understanding Earth as a coupled system. ## 1. Introduction This paper argues that Earth's soils are expressions of the biosphere's impact on surface processes. Soils may represent the best indication of the nature, state, and condition of Earth surface systems. If soils are expressions of biological evolution through natural selection, implications are profound. Soils are viewed as media for growth, habitats, weathered mantles, and engineering materials. Concepts include the biomantle, extended phenotypes (Dawkins), ecosystem engineers, and niche construction. ## 2. Conceptual Framework Soils as biotic constructs (van Breemen, 1993) argues soils are influenced by biota to increase net primary productivity. Biomechanical effects are increasingly recognized as important (e.g., vertical texture contrast soils). Dawkins' extended phenotype includes all effects of a gene on the environment. Ecosystem engineering is the ecological counterpart of niche construction. ## 3. Paleosols and Coevolution Evidence from paleosols supports coevolution, such as grassland soils evolving with grasses and herbivores over 40 Ma. Pedotypes function as trace fossils of local ecosystems. ## 4. Tree-soil interactions Case study in the Ouachita Mountains, Arkansas. Shortleaf pine (Pinus echinata) vs. hardwoods. Pines are early colonizers of rock surfaces, initiating pedogenesis. Pines are more prone to uprooting, which mounds soil and quarries rock fragments, leading to faster soil formation and distinct vertical clast distributions compared to hardwoods. ## 5. Discussion and Conclusions Soils as biomantles reflect cumulative genetic effects of multiple generations. The ECP framework suggests soil morphology as a long-lasting biosignature, useful for searching for life on planets like Mars. Nikiforoff's 'excited skin' metaphor describes soil as a dynamic planetary membrane.
