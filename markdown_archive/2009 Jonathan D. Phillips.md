# Soils as Extended Composite Phenotypes

**Author:** Jonathan D. Phillips
**Journal:** Geoderma 149 (2009) 143–151
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.geoderma.2008.11.028

## Abstract
Several recent theories and conceptual frameworks in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology, taken together, suggest the notion that earth's soils are not just strongly influenced by biota, but represent selective pressures. These ideas point in the direction of many aspects of soils as expressions of the effects of genes through the effects of organisms (i.e., extended phenotypes). The cumulative, interacting, overlapping effects of these extended phenotypes as manifested in the soil represent an extended composite phenotype. If this is the case, then we should expect major changes in biological evolution to be reflected in major changes in the types of soils. This concept has repercussions for pedology, evolutionary biology, and interpretations of soils in other earth and environmental sciences. The notion of genetic signatures in soil morphology also has implications for the search for extraterrestrial life.

## 1. Introduction
This paper argues that Earth's soils are an expression of the cumulative impacts of the biosphere on surface processes. Soils may represent the single best indication of the nature, state, and condition of earth surface systems. There already exist a number of valid ways of viewing soils—as media for plant growth, habitats and ecosystems, weathered mantles, and engineering materials.

## 2. Conceptual Framework

### 2.1. Soils as Biotic Constructs
Soils are influenced by biota in such a way as to increase net primary productivity. Biological processes include weathering, increasing nutrient supply, increasing water holding capacity, and decreasing compaction.

### 2.2. Biomantles
Soils are increasingly viewed as continuously-mixed biomantles—a surficial layer influenced by biochemical effects and mixed by floral and faunal-turbation.

### 2.3. Extended Phenotypes and Niche Construction
- **Extended Phenotype:** Refers to all effects of a gene on the environment.
- **Ecosystem Engineers:** Organisms that have a disproportionate influence on the environment relative to their numbers.
- **Niche Construction:** The process by which organisms alter the environment to influence selective pressures.

## 5. Discussion and Conclusions
Soils as extended composite phenotypes (ECP) imply a relationship between the bio- and pedospheres. The ECP framework suggests initial possibilities with respect to searches for life on other planets. A living biosphere would likely have a signal in atmospheric chemistry, but an extinct biosphere (such as may have existed on Mars) would be unlikely to leave fingerprints in atmospheric composition, whereas impacts on soil morphology would be more long-lasting.
