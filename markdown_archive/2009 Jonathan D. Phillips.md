# Soils as extended composite phenotypes

**Author:** Jonathan D. Phillips
**Journal:** Geoderma 149 (2009) 143–151
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.geoderma.2008.11.028

## Abstract
Several recent theories and conceptual frameworks in pedology, ecology, geomorphology, and evolutionary biology suggest that earth's soils represent selective pressures and expressions of genetic effects through organisms (extended phenotypes). The cumulative effects of these extended phenotypes represent an *extended composite phenotype*. This notion suggests that significant biological changes are reflected in qualitative pedological changes and has implications for the search for extraterrestrial life.

## 1. Introduction
Earth's soils are an expression of the cumulative impacts of the biosphere on surface processes. As a product of geology, climate, biota, topography, and time, soils indicate the nature and state of earth surface systems. If soils are expressions of biological evolution, there are profound implications for understanding the coevolution of earth systems.

## 2. Conceptual Framework
### 2.1. Soils as biotic constructs
Biota influence soil texture, structure, erosion, organic matter, and chemistry to increase net primary productivity. This includes weathering rock, increasing nutrient supply, and increasing water holding capacity.

### 2.2. Biomantles
Soils are increasingly viewed as continuously-mixed biomantles, where biomechanical effects (bioturbation) are as influential as biochemical effects on soil morphology.

### 2.3. Extended phenotypes and niche construction
'Ecosystem engineers' are organisms that disproportionately influence their environment. Soil is a manifestation of both biotic effects and biological evolution, where organisms and their biotopes reciprocally define each other.

### 2.5. Biosphere and Soil
V.I. Vernadsky and others viewed the biosphere as a web of feedback mechanisms. Soil is characterized as the 'excited skin' of the Earth's crust, a physical manifestation of the biosphere.

## 3. Paleosols and coevolution
Paleopedological evidence supports the coevolution of soils and biota. Grassland soils, for instance, have specific morphological properties attributable to vegetation and have coevolved over 40 million years. Pedotypes can be considered trace fossils of local ecosystems.

## 4. Tree–soil interactions
Interactions are categorized by coupling: tightly woven (fitness enhanced by soil effects), loosely woven, or frayed. In the Ouachita Mountains, trees act as ecosystem engineers by initiating weathering, quarrying rock fragments via uprooting, and creating nutrient-rich microsites.

## 5. Discussion and conclusions
Soils as extended composite phenotypes imply a link between molecular scales (DNA) and ecosystem levels. This framework suggests that an extinct biosphere (such as on Mars) might leave long-lasting morphological signatures in the 'soil' even if atmospheric signals have vanished. It shifts the view of soil from a static inorganic mantle to a dynamic, biologically-driven planetary membrane.
