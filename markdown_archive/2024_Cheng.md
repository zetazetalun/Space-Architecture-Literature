# Advances in in-situ resources utilization for extraterrestrial construction

**Authors:** Shanshan Cheng, Yuyue Gao, Yusheng Shi, Long Xiao, Lieyun Ding, Cheng Zhou, Yan Zhou  
**Journal:** Advances in Space Research 74 (2024) 3297–3325  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.asr.2024.06.029

## Abstract
To establish foundational support for forthcoming deep space exploration and settlement endeavors, the significance of extraterrestrial construction has become paramount. Recent developments underscore a growing acknowledgment of the imperative role played by in situ resource utilization, attributed to its potential for cost efficiency and facilitation of sustainable progress. This paper compiles and categorizes the advancements in this domain, focusing on three distinctive categories of in situ resources: regolith, water–ice, and energy resources. A review of their distribution, acquisition, and utilization procedures is presented. Subsequently, an intricate examination ensues, elucidating the applicability of each resource within the context of extraterrestrial construction, in conjunction with five prevalent technologies and the latest architectural constructs.

## 1. Introduction
Space exploration has evolved to facilitate further scientific exploration through a variety of ISRU applications. Major programs like NASA’s Artemis and the International Research Station prioritize ISRU due to cost-effectiveness and sustainability.

## 2. Regolith Resources for Construction
Regolith is a representative construction material available on Moon and Mars. It can be used for:
- Sintered bricks
- Concrete materials
- Radiation shielding and thermal insulation

### 2.1 Components of Regolith
- **Moon:** Lunar highlands (Ca, Al, Si) and lunar maria (Mg, Fe, Ti).
- **Mars:** Predominantly basalt (mafic silicate minerals, sulfides, and oxides).

### 2.2 Processing Phases
1. **Excavation:** Discrete (backhoe, scraper) and continuous (pneumatic, bucket wheel).
2. **Presorting:** Size classification (electrostatic, vibration, centrifuge).
3. **Beneficiation:** Electrostatic or magnetic separation.
4. **Reduction:** Pyrolysis, electrolysis (MRE, MSE), or chemical/biochemical reduction.

## 3. Water-ice Resources
Water-ice is found in Lunar Polar Shadowed Regions (PSRs) and Martian subsurface. It is used for:
- **Direct utilization:** Radiation shielding (2 cm water layer can decrease Solar Proton Event dose by >50%).
- **Concrete production:** Magnesian, calcareous, and geopolymer concrete.

## 4. Energy Resources
- **Atmosphere:** CO2 capture on Mars via freezing, temperature swing, or compression adsorption.
- **Solar Energy:** Collection via parabolic dishes or Fresnel reflectors.

## 5. Construction Technologies
| Technology | Key Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **3D Printing** | Concrete extrusion, contour crafting (CC), and binder jetting. |
| **Prefabrication** | Directly interlocking structures and folding (origami-inspired) structures. |
| **Inflatables** | Lightweight, low launch volume; e.g., NASA's "Regishell". |
| **Regolith Bags** | Automated filling and stacking; low energy consumption. |
| **Hybrid Construction** | Combining multiple methods (e.g., inflatables + 3D printed protective layer). |

## 6. Challenges and Recommendations
- **Gaps:** Compartmentalization of research, lack of deep processing, and sustainability.
- **Future Work:** Integrated construction frameworks, metallurgy of regolith/slag, and water-ice mining in PSRs.
