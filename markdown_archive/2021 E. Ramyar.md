# Numerical Simulation of 3D Printing of Infrastructure Materials

**Authors:** E. Ramyar; Z. Xinwei; and G. Cusatis  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE  

## Abstract
3D printing is poised to become a disruptive force for the terrestrial construction industry, and it is also the most likely technology that will make possible the safe and reliable development of extraterrestrial infrastructure. The main objective of this paper is to highlight the formulation of the discrete fresh concrete model (DFCM). DFCM is a particle-based fresh cementitious material model that simulates the rheological and mechanical behavior of material accurately before, during, and after printing to optimize the printing process and the material flow.

## Introduction
The significant influence of fresh concrete characteristics (flowability and workability) on its mechanical responses and on its printability have inspired the development of simulation techniques. For concrete 3D printing, the challenge is twofold: 3D printing technologies need to adapt to the rheological characteristics of concrete, and new materials for use with current 3D printing technologies need to be developed.

## Discrete Fresh Concrete Model (DFCM)
DFCM adopts rigid composite spherical particles of different sizes to simulate coarse stiff aggregate surrounding the layer of soft mortar. 
- **Interaction Mechanism:** A combination of parallel and series nonlinear springs and viscous dashpots describes the contact behavior of adjunct particles.
- **Constitutive Equations:** Formulated in the form of stress-strain relationships, allowing a seamless transition from fresh concrete to hardened concrete description.
- **Key Parameters:** Tensile strength, mortar normal modulus, shear yield stress, and plastic viscosity.

## Reproducing Experimental Results
The model was validated using:
1. **Slump Tests:** Successfully reproduced results for Self-Consolidating Concrete (SCC), ordinary concrete, and printable concrete.
2. **ICAR Rheometer Tests:** Matched vane resistant torque versus rotational speed for various mix designs.

## Numerically 3D Printing of the Northwestern Martian Habitat
Northwestern University, in collaboration with Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM), designed a dome-shaped Martian habitat for NASA’s 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. 
- **Purpose:** The numerical model monitors the 3D printing process for control, prediction, and optimization of material flow.
- **Autonomous Construction:** Development is essential for extraterrestrial landings where construction must be accomplished via autonomously remote operations with no human involvement.
- **Simulation Details:** The motion of the printer nozzle was synchronized with a DEM particle generator, accounting for gravity and pump pressure.

## Conclusion
The DFCM is an accurate tool for modeling the behavior of printable concrete. It can be used successfully to simulate the 3D printing process of concrete structures on any planets, enabling the design and analysis of safe extraterrestrial infrastructure.
