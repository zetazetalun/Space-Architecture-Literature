# Numerical Simulation of 3D Printing of Infrastructure Materials

**Authors:** E. Ramyar, Z. Xinwei, G. Cusatis
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## Abstract
3D printing is poised to become a disruptive force for the terrestrial construction industry, and it is also the most likely technology that will make possible the safe and reliable development of extraterrestrial infrastructure. The main objective of this paper is to highlight the formulation of the discrete fresh concrete model (DFCM). DFCM is a particle-based fresh cementitious material model that simulates the rheological and mechanical behavior of material accurately before, during, and after printing to optimize the printing process and the material flow.

## Discrete Fresh Concrete Model (DFCM)
DFCM adopts rigid composite spherical particles of different sizes to simulate coarse stiff aggregate surrounding a layer of soft mortar. A combination of parallel and series nonlinear springs and viscous dashpots describes the contact behavior of adjunct particles. The interaction among spherical particles is governed by visco-plastic constitutive equations based on stress-strain relationships, allowing a seamless transition from fresh concrete to hardened concrete description.

### Key Parameters
- Tensile strength ($\sigma_t$)
- Mortar normal modulus ($E_N$)
- Shear yield stress ($\sigma_{T0}$)
- Plastic viscosity ($\mu_\infty$)
- Mortar thickness ($h$)

## Reproducing Experimental Results
The model was validated against two typical fresh concrete tests:
1. **Slump Test:** Successfully simulated Self-Consolidating Concrete (SCC), ordinary concrete, and printable concrete (which shows minimal slump).
2. **ICAR Rheometer Test:** Reproduced the vane resistant torque versus rotational speed for regular and printable concrete mixes.

## Numerically 3D Printing of the Northwestern Martian Habitat
Northwestern University, in collaboration with Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM), designed a dome-shaped Martian habitat for NASA's 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The DFCM was used to monitor the 3D printing process, material flow, and final configuration. The simulation synchronized nozzle motion with a DEM particle generator, accounting for gravity and pump pressure to identify inter-particle rheological characteristics.

## Conclusion
The DFCM contact model satisfactorily simulates the rheological behavior of cementitious materials right after pouring. It is an accurate tool for modeling self-compacting, ordinary, and printable concrete. The model can be used successfully to simulate the 3D printing process of concrete structures on any planet.
