# Numerical Simulation of 3D Printing of Infrastructure Materials

**Authors:** E. Ramyar, Z. Xinwei, G. Cusatis  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, pp. 1242-1250  
**Publisher:** ASCE

## Abstract
3D printing is poised to become a disruptive force for the terrestrial construction industry, and it is also the most likely technology that will make possible the safe and reliable development of extraterrestrial infrastructure. The main objective of this paper is to highlight the formulation of the discrete fresh concrete model (DFCM). DFCM is a particle-based fresh cementitious material model that simulates the rheological and mechanical behavior of material accurately before, during, and after printing to optimize the printing process and the material flow.

## Introduction
The significance of fresh concrete characteristics (flowability and workability) on its mechanical responses and on its printability have inspired researchers to develop simulation techniques. For concrete 3D printing, the challenge is twofold: adapting technology to rheological characteristics and developing new materials. This paper presents the Discrete Fresh Concrete Model (DFCM), a novel approach to simulate the transport, placement, and compaction of fresh standard and printable concrete.

## Discrete Fresh Concrete Model (DFCM)
DFCM adopts rigid composite spherical particles of different sizes to simulate coarse stiff aggregate surrounding a layer of soft mortar, following the "excess paste theory." 
- **Contact Behavior:** Described by a combination of parallel and series nonlinear springs and viscous dashpots.
- **Constitutive Equations:** Governed by visco-plastic relationships based on stress-strain rather than force-displacement. 
- **Rheology:** The mortar layer is considered a Bingham fluid.

## Reproducing Experimental Results
The model was validated using:
1. **Slump Tests:** Successfully reproduced flowability for self-consolidating concrete (SCC), ordinary cement, and printable concrete (minimal slump).
2. **ICAR Rheometer Tests:** Matched vane resistant torque versus applied rotational speed for various mixes.

## Numerically 3D Printing of the Northwestern Martian Habitat
Northwestern University, in collaboration with SOM, designed a dome-shaped Martian habitat for NASA’s 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The DFCM was used to monitor the 3D printing process, optimizing material flow and final configuration. This model is essential for autonomous remote operations in extraterrestrial environments where human involvement is limited.

## Conclusion
The DFCM is an accurate tool for modeling the behavior of self-compacting, ordinary, and printable concrete. It successfully simulates the 3D printing process of concrete structures on other planets, accounting for gravity and pump pressure variables.
