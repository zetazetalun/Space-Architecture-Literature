# Numerical Simulation of 3D Printing of Infrastructure Materials\n\n**Authors:** E. Ramyar, Z. Xinwei, G. Cusatis\n**Venue:** Earth and Space 2021 (ASCE), pp. 1242-1250\n\n## ABSTRACT\n3D printing is poised to become a disruptive force for the terrestrial construction industry, and it is also the most likely technology that will make possible the safe and reliable development of extraterrestrial infrastructure. The main objective of this paper is to highlight the formulation of the discrete fresh concrete model (DFCM). DFCM is a particle-based fresh cementitious material model that simulates the rheological and mechanical behavior of material accurately before, during, and after printing to optimize the printing process and the material flow.\n\n## INTRODUCTION\nConcrete 3D printing is a challenge involving both technology adaptation to rheological characteristics and development of new materials. Analytical and computational approaches are crucial to deepen understanding of both the printing process and the behavior of printed products.\n\n## DISCRETE FRESH CONCRETE MODEL (DFCM)\nDFCM adopts rigid composite spherical particles of different sizes to simulate coarse stiff aggregate surrounding a layer of soft mortar. Unlike conventional DEM, DFCM constitutive equations are formulated as stress-strain relationships, allowing a seamless transition from fresh to hardened concrete descriptions. Key parameters include tensile strength, mortar normal modulus, shear yield stress, and plastic viscosity.\n\n## REPRODUCING EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS\nThe model was validated using Slump and ICAR rheometer tests. For printable concrete, numerical simulations showed minimal slump, with decreases in height and horizontal spread of only a couple of centimeters, matching experimental observations by Wolfs et al. (2018). The model successfully matched vane resistant torque versus rotational speed for printable concrete mixes.\n\n## NUMERICALLY 3D PRINTING OF THE NORTHWESTERN MARTIAN HABITAT\nNorthwestern University, in collaboration with SOM, designed a dome-shaped Martian habitat for NASA’s 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The DFCM was used to monitor the 3D printing process, optimizing material flow and nozzle motion. For planetary simulations, nozzle velocity is synchronized with discharge rates, which depend on gravity and pump pressure.\n\n## CONCLUSION\nThe DFCM is an accurate tool for modeling self-compacting, ordinary, and printable concrete. It successfully simulates the 3D printing process of concrete structures on other planets, supporting autonomous remote operations.
