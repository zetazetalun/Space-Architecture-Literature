# Numerical Simulation of 3D Printing of Infrastructure Materials

**Authors:** E. Ramyar; Z. Xinwei; and G. Cusatis  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, pp. 1242-1250

## ABSTRACT
3D printing is poised to become a disruptive force for the terrestrial construction industry, and it is also the most likely technology that will make possible the safe and reliable development of extraterrestrial infrastructure. While more and more researchers are working on developing novel and appropriate mixes, as well as 3D printing technologies, only a few and somewhat limited computational models are available in the literature for the design and analysis of 3D printed structures. The main objective of this paper is to highlight the formulation of the discrete fresh concrete model (DFCM). DFCM is a particle-based fresh cementitious material model that simulates the rheological and mechanical behavior of material accurately before, during, and after printing to optimize the printing process and the material flow. 

## INTRODUCTION
The significant influence of fresh concrete characteristics (flowability and workability) on its mechanical responses and on its printability has inspired researchers to develop a variety of simulation techniques. For concrete 3D printing, the challenge is twofold: adapting printing technologies to rheological characteristics and developing new materials for current printing technologies. Formulation and validation of analytical and computational approaches are crucial to deepen understanding of both the printing process and the behavior of printed products.

## DISCRETE FRESH CONCRETE MODEL (DFCM)
DFCM adopts rigid composite spherical particles of different sizes to simulate coarse stiff aggregate surrounding a layer of soft mortar. This approach is known as "excess paste theory".

*   **Constitutive Equations:** Governed by visco-plastic stress-strain relationships rather than conventional force-displacement relationships.
*   **Components:** Includes normal and tangential components of the contact model, taking into account viscous interaction between soft mortar layers and stiff interaction of hard aggregate pieces.
*   **Mortar Layer:** Considered as a Bingham fluid with specific rheological characteristics.

## REPRODUCING EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
The model was validated using two typical fresh concrete tests:
1.  **Slump Test:** Successfully generated accurate results for high flowable (SCC), medium workability (ordinary), and very stiff (printable) concrete. Printable concrete showed minimal slump (only a couple of centimeters decrease in height).
2.  **ICAR Rheometer Test:** Reproduced experimental results for regular and printable concrete. For printable concrete, simulated solid-like pieces of material moved around the vane, matching experimental observations that actual "flow" is difficult to identify in such stiff mixes.

## NUMERICALLY 3D PRINTING OF THE NORTHWESTERN MARTIAN HABITAT
Northwestern University, in collaboration with Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM), designed a dome-shaped Martian habitat for NASA's 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. 

*   **Simulation Purpose:** To control, predict, and optimize the printing process, material flow, and final configuration.
*   **Methodology:** The motion of the printer nozzle was synchronized with a DEM particle generator. 
*   **Extraterrestrial Application:** The model accounts for gravity and pump pressure to identify discharge rates, essential for autonomous remote operations with no human involvement on other planets.

## CONCLUSION
DFCM is an accurate tool for modeling the behavior of various concretes, from self-compacting to printable. It can be used successfully to simulate the 3D printing process of concrete structures on any planet, facilitating the development of extraterrestrial infrastructure.
