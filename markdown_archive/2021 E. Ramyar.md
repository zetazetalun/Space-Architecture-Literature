# Numerical Simulation of 3D Printing of Infrastructure Materials

**Authors:** E. Ramyar, Z. Xinwei, G. Cusatis
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021 (ASCE)

## ABSTRACT
3D printing is poised to become a disruptive force for the terrestrial construction industry, and it is also the most likely technology that will make possible the safe and reliable development of extraterrestrial infrastructure. The main objective of this paper is to highlight the formulation of the discrete fresh concrete model (DFCM). DFCM is a particle-based fresh cementitious material model that simulates the rheological and mechanical behavior of material accurately before, during, and after printing to optimize the printing process and the material flow.

## INTRODUCTION
The significant influence of fresh concrete characteristics (flowability and workability) on its mechanical responses and on its printability have inspired the development of simulation techniques. For concrete 3D printing, the challenge is twofold: adaptation of technologies to rheological characteristics and the development of new materials. Analytical and computational approaches are crucial for long-term extraterrestrial landing construction, which should be accomplished autonomously.

## DISCRETE FRESH CONCRETE MODEL (DFCM)
DFCM adopts rigid composite spherical particles of different sizes to simulate coarse stiff aggregate surrounding a layer of soft mortar (the "excess paste theory"). Unlike conventional Discrete Element Method (DEM) models using force-displacement, DFCM uses visco-plastic constitutive equations based on stress-strain relationships. This enables a seamless transition from the description of fresh concrete to hardened concrete.

### Key Parameters
- Tensile strength (σ_t)
- Mortar normal modulus (E_N)
- Shear yield stress (σ_τ0)
- Plastic viscosity (μ_∞)
- Mortar thickness (h)

## REPRODUCING EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
The model was validated using:
1. **Slump Tests:** Successfully simulated Self-Consolidating Concrete (SCC) with a 60 cm spread and 25 cm slump, and printable concrete showing minimal slump (a few centimeters height/spread decrease).
2. **ICAR Rheometer Tests:** Matches vane resistant torque versus applied rotational speed for both regular and printable concrete mixes. For printable concrete, simulations showed solid-like pieces moving around the vane rather than fluid flow.

## NUMERICALLY 3D PRINTING OF THE NORTHWESTERN MARTIAN HABITAT
In collaboration with Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM), Northwestern University designed a dome-shaped Martian habitat for NASA's 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The DFCM was used to simulate the layer-by-layer printing process. The nozzle velocity was synchronized with the material generator, taking into account planetary gravity and pump pressure.

## CONCLUSION
DFCM is an accurate tool for modeling the behavior of self-compacting, ordinary, and printable concrete. It can be used successfully to simulate the 3D printing process of concrete structures on any planet, enabling the control and optimization of autonomous remote operations.
