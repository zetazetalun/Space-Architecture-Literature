# Numerical Simulation of 3D Printing of Infrastructure Materials

**E. Ramyar**; **Z. Xinwei**; and **G. Cusatis**

**Abstract:** 3D printing is poised to become a disruptive force for the terrestrial construction industry, and it is also the most likely technology that will make possible the safe and reliable development of extraterrestrial infrastructure. While more and more researchers are working on developing novel and appropriate mixes, as well as 3D printing technologies, only a few and somewhat limited computational models are available in the literature for the design and analysis of 3D printed structures. The main objective of this paper is to highlight the formulation of the discrete fresh concrete model (DFCM). DFCM is a particle-based fresh cementitious material model that simulates the rheological and mechanical behavior of material accurately before, during, and after printing.

## Introduction
Concrete 3D printing is revolutionizing construction, yet challenges remain in adapting technology to the rheological characteristics of concrete and developing new materials. Analytical and computational approaches are crucial to deepen the understanding of the printing process and the behavior of printed products. This paper presents the Discrete Fresh Concrete Model (DFCM) to simulate transport, placement, and compaction of fresh concrete.

## Discrete Fresh Concrete Model (DFCM)
DFCM adopts rigid composite spherical particles of different sizes to simulate coarse stiff aggregate surrounding a layer of soft mortar (Figure 1). Unlike conventional Discrete Element Method (DEM) models using force-displacement relationships, DFCM constitutive equations are formulated in the form of stress-strain relationships. This allows for a seamless transition from fresh concrete description to hardened concrete.

### Key Parameters
- Tensile strength ($σ_t$)
- Mortar normal modulus ($E_N$)
- Shear yield stress ($σ_{τ0}$)
- Plastic viscosity ($μ_∞$)
- Mortar thickness ($h$)

## Reproducing Experimental Results
The model was validated using Slump and ICAR rheometer tests. 

- **Slump Tests:** The model accurately reproduced high, medium, and low flowability. Self-consolidating concrete (SCC) simulations showed spreads of 45.5 to 81.0 cm. Printable concrete simulations showed minimal slump, consistent with experimental requirements for shape retention.
- **ICAR Rheometer Tests:** DFCM matched vane resistant torque versus rotational speed for both regular and printable concrete mixes. Printable concrete was found to be less rate-dependent than standard concrete.

## Conclusion
The DFCM contact model successfully simulates the rheological behavior of cementitious materials during the printing process. It demonstrates accuracy for self-compacting, ordinary, and printable concrete. The model can be used to simulate 3D printing of structures on other planets, ensuring optimized autonomous construction processes.
