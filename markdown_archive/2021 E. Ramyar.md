# Numerical Simulation of 3D Printing of Infrastructure Materials

**Authors:** E. Ramyar, Z. Xinwei, and G. Cusatis
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021 (ASCE), pp. 1242-1250

## Abstract
3D printing is a disruptive force for terrestrial construction and the most likely technology for extraterrestrial infrastructure development. This paper highlights the Discrete Fresh Concrete Model (DFCM), a particle-based cementitious material model that simulates rheological and mechanical behavior before, during, and after printing. Unlike conventional models, DFCM uses visco-plastic constitutive equations based on stress-strain relationships, enabling a seamless transition from fresh to hardened concrete descriptions.

## Introduction
Concrete 3D printing faces two primary challenges: adapting technology to concrete's rheology and developing new materials for current printing tech. Simulation tools are essential for optimization, especially for remote, autonomous extraterrestrial construction.

## Discrete Fresh Concrete Model (DFCM)
DFCM adopts rigid composite spherical particles (rigid aggregate surrounded by soft mortar) based on "excess paste theory." Interaction is governed by nonlinear springs and viscous dashpots. 
- **Normal Component:** Reflects compression/tension interaction.
- **Tangential Component:** Governed by Bingham fluid characteristics for the mortar layer.
- **Key Parameters:** Tensile strength, mortar normal modulus, shear yield stress, and plastic viscosity.

## Reproducing Experimental Results
The model was validated using:
1. **Slump Tests:** Successfully reproduced high-flow (SCC), medium-flow (ordinary), and stiff (printable) concrete behaviors. Printable concrete showed minimal slump (only a few cm decrease).
2. **ICAR Rheometer Tests:** Matches experimental vane resistant torque versus rotational speed. Results show printable concrete is less rate-dependent than standard concrete.

## Numerically 3D Printing of the Northwestern Martian Habitat
In collaboration with Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM), a dome-shaped Martian habitat was designed for NASA’s 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. 
- **Simulation:** The DFCM was used to monitor material flow and final configuration.
- **Automation:** The nozzle motion was synchronized with the DEM particle generator, accounting for gravity and pump pressure.

## Conclusion
DFCM is an accurate tool for modeling the behavior of self-compacting, ordinary, and printable concrete. It can successfully simulate the 3D printing process of concrete structures on other planets, facilitating remote, autonomous construction.
