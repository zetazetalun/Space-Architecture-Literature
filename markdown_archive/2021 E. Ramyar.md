## ABSTRACT
3D printing is poised to become a disruptive force for the terrestrial construction industry, and it is also the most likely technology that will make possible the safe and reliable development of extraterrestrial infrastructure. While more and more researchers are working on developing novel and appropriate mixes, as well as 3D printing technologies, only a few and somewhat limited computational models are available in the literature for the design and analysis of 3D printed structures. The main objective of this paper is to highlight the formulation of the discrete fresh concrete model (DFCM). DFCM is a particle-based fresh cementitious material model that simulates the rheological and mechanical behavior of material accurately before, during, and after printing to optimize the printing process and the material flow. For DFCM, the interaction among spherical particles is governed by visco-plastic constitutive equations, which are based on stress-strain relationships. This allows a mathematical formulation enabling a seamless transition from the description of fresh concrete to the one of hardened concrete.

## INTRODUCTION
The significant influence of fresh concrete characteristics (flowability and workability) on its mechanical responses and on its printability has inspired researchers to develop a variety of simulation techniques. Concrete 3D printing is of great importance with its rapid development and a countless number of potential applications. The challenge is twofold: adapting 3D printing technologies to rheological characteristics and developing new materials for current 3D printing technologies.

This paper presents the Discrete Fresh Concrete Model (DFCM), a novel approach to simulate the behavior (transport, placement, and compaction) of fresh standard and printable concrete.

## DISCRETE FRESH CONCRETE MODEL (DFCM)
DFCM adopts rigid composite spherical particles of different sizes to simulate coarse stiff aggregate surrounding a layer of soft mortar. A combination of parallel and series nonlinear springs and viscous dashpots describes the contact behavior. The mortar layer is considered a Bingham fluid with rheological characteristics.

Key parameters for DFCM include:
- Tensile strength (σt)
- Mortar normal modulus (EN)
- Shear yield stress (στ0)
- Plastic viscosity (μ∞)
- Mortar thickness (h)

## REPRODUCING EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS OF STANDARD AND PRINTABLE CONCRETE
The model was validated using Slump and ICAR rheometer tests. DFCM succeeded in generating accurate results for high flowable, medium flowable, and very stiff concrete. Printable concrete simulations showed minimal slump, providing a decrease in height and horizontal spread of only a couple of centimeters, matching experimental observations.

## NUMERICALLY 3D PRINTING OF THE NORTHWESTERN MARTIAN HABITAT
Northwestern University, in collaboration with Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM), designed a dome-shaped Martian habitat as part of NASA’s 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge. The development of a numerical model is essential for long-term extraterrestrial landing where construction should be accomplished by autonomous remote operations.

In the simulation, the motion of the printer nozzle was synchronized with the DEM particle generator. DFCM governs the microscopic rheological and tribological behavior of inter-particle and surface-particle (nozzle-particle) interactions. The simulation successfully illustrated various stages of the layer-by-layer printing process for the Martian habitat.

## CONCLUSION
DFCM is an accurate tool for modeling the behavior of self-compacting, ordinary, and printable concrete. The model can be used successfully to simulate the 3D printing process of concrete structures on any planet, facilitating the optimization of material flow and final object configuration.
