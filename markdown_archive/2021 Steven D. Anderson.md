# Solar-Powered Additive Manufacturing in Extraterrestrial Environments\n\n**Authors:** Steven D. Anderson, Jekan Thangavelautham\n**Affiliation:** Space and Terrestrial Robotic Exploration Laboratory, Dept. of Aerospace and Mechanical Engineering, Univ. of Arizona, Tucson, AZ\n\n## ABSTRACT\nKickstarting a space economy will require building communication relays, refueling depots, repair depots, habitats, and mining bases from in-situ resources in strategic locations between Earth, Moon, and Mars. Due to the high costs inherent in transporting resources from the Earth’s surface to these locations, new methods of material extraction and construction are necessary. This paper proposes the development of solar additive manufacturing printers for melting and use of sand/regolith for construction. By replacing the laser in the Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) process with a large Fresnel lens, the system focuses sunlight to sinter material and create solid shapes, relying fully on renewable solar energy.\n\n## INTRODUCTION\nSpace is now open for business, creating a need for sustainable construction methods. Infrastructure required includes communication relays, refueling depots, human and robotic habitats, and mining bases. These need to be located on the Moon, Mars, asteroids, and other moons.\n\n## CHALLENGES TO EXTREME ENVIRONMENT ADDITIVE MANUFACTURING\n*   **Material Availability:** Importing materials is cost-prohibitive; In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) is essential.\n*   **Refinement:** Material needs extraction and separation into useable constituents (e.g., pelletizing for FDM).\n*   **Power Systems:** Gathering, refining, and heating material are energy-intensive operations. \n\n## OBJECTIVE\nThe goal is to develop an additive manufacturing method that is:\n1. Low-cost and low-energy.\n2. Adaptable to various extraterrestrial environments.\n3. Adherent to ISRU concepts.\n4. Suitable for autonomous operation.\n\n## DESIGN METHOD\nSelective Laser Sintering (SLS) is identified as more promising than Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) for extraterrestrial use because it requires minimal pre-processing and lower specific energy consumption (SLS: ~40 kWh/kg vs. FDM: ~115 kWh/kg). By replacing the laser with a Fresnel lens, the system utilizes direct solar thermal energy.\n\n## FEASIBILITY AND CALCULATIONS\n### Solar Irradiance at Various Locations (Mean)\n*   **Earth/Moon:** 1395 W/m^2\n*   **Mars:** 597.9 W/m^2\n*   **Asteroid Belt:** 189.5 W/m^2\n*   **Jupiter:** 50.99 W/m^2\n\n### Achievable Lens Power (1 m^2 Lens, 50% Efficiency)\n*   **Earth Surface:** 393 W (due to atmosphere)\n*   **Moon:** 548 W\n*   **Mars:** 235 W\n\n### Required Sinter Power vs. Feed Rate\n| Location | Material | Required Sinter Power (W) | Feed Rate (mm/s) |\n| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |\n| Earth Surface | Sand | 351 | 2.50 |\n| Moon | Regolith | 450 | 1.00 |\n| Mars | Basalt | 236 | 4.50 |\n| Deimos | Carbonaceous Chondrite | 97 | 5.00 |\n\n## EMPLOYMENT OF TECHNOLOGY\nThe technology is envisioned for a robotic fleet. A mobile platform with a Fresnel lens would sinter materials directly onto the surface. A grader blade would level the regolith before sintering to create foundations, roads, and structural components.\n\n## EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN\nA prototype uses an Arduino Mega and Ramps 1.4 board to control a 3-axis print bed (X, Y for motion, Z for layer lowering). The lens is maintained in an optimal position using a sun tracker and linear actuators.\n\n## CONCLUSION\nSolar sintering offers a practical, low-energy option for autonomous 3D printing in space. By adjusting parameters like travel rates and lens aperture, the system can be tuned to various environments and materials, making it a versatile tool for establishing space infrastructure.
