# Seismic Vulnerability Assessment of Non-Structural Elements Inside an Inflatable Lunar Habitat

**Authors:** Oscar D. Forero, Julio A. Ramirez, Shirley J. Dyke
**Affiliation:** Purdue University
**Source:** Earth and Space 2024 (ASCE)

## Abstract
The pursuit of human presence on extraterrestrial bodies involves overcoming numerous challenges, including moonquakes, which are often overlooked. Using NASA's Moon to Mars Architecture requirements, a procedure for vulnerability assessment is developed for essential non-structural elements (NSE)—specifically the environmental control and life support system (ECLSS)—inside an inflatable habitat. The study proposes an approach to assess vulnerability under seismic hazard uncertainty. Findings suggest that significant disturbances from moonquakes may result in long-lasting vibrations requiring effective mitigation measures beyond what is provided for the launch environment.

## Introduction
NASA's Artemis program aims for a short-term surface mission by the end of the decade. Inflatable habitats are a focus due to their high payload volume efficiency. While designed for launch vibrations, the risk posed by shallow moonquakes to structural integrity and internal contents remains poorly understood.

## Case Study
*   **Habitat Model:** A Class I torus-shaped inflatable structure (based on Sierra Space LIFE or similar concepts) mounted atop a lander.
*   **Dimensions:** 6 meters height, two floors, supported by a cantilever hydraulic jack system.
*   **NSE Focus:** ECLSS universal pallet (0.60m x 0.60m x 1.80m) from Collins Aerospace, situated on the second floor.

## Launch vs. Lunar Seismic Environment
*   **Launch Loads:** Include engine ignition, liftoff, and aerodynamic pressures, tested via sine sweep and random vibration tests (NASA-STD-7001B).
*   **Lunar Seismic Loads:** Based on Apollo Passive Seismic Experiment (APSE) data. 28 shallow moonquakes (High-Frequency Teleseismic events) were identified as the most energetic. Ground acceleration near the source can reach up to 1.6 gL (Watters et al. 2019) or even 4 gL for magnitude 4 events (Mohanty et al. 2020).

## Numerical Simulation Results
*   **Primary Structure Response:** At a scaled value of 1.0 gL, the deployed structure exhibited a maximum displacement of 2.4 cm, while the undeployed structure (launch configuration) experienced only 0.6 cm.
*   **Duration:** Lunar seismic responses can last over an hour, whereas launch vibrations persist for only minutes.
*   **Resonance:** Moonquakes operate at low frequencies (<10Hz), which may resonate with lunar habitat designs. The primary structure showed a greater transverse response to 1.0 gL seismic loads than anticipated during launch.

## Discussion
There is a disparity between launch design and seismic requirements. While the ECLSS pallet (higher fundamental frequency) responded more to launch vibrations, the primary structure was more susceptible to moonquakes. Long-term lunar settlements must consider the fatigue and fracture risks associated with prolonged seismic vibrations.
