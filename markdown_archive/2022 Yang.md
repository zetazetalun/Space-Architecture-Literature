# Laboratory construction and curation scheme for returned samples of the chang’e-5 mission

**Authors:** Hongwei Yang, Ruihong Yang and Qian Wang
**Journal:** Geoscience Letters (2022) 9:19
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s40562-022-00224-z

## Abstract
The analysis of samples returned from a planetary body is important to understand the origin, composition, evolution, and interactions with space or atmospheric weathering of the planet. The Chang’e-5 lander, the first Chinese science project involving the return of lunar samples, acquired less than 2 kg during this mission. The China Lunar Exploration and Space Engineering Center (CLESEC) is planning to construct at least two laboratories to analyze and preserve these samples. This paper establishes standard requirements for the preservation and curation of lunar samples, discussing laboratory conditions, contamination control, and future sampling mission demands.

## Introduction
To understand planetary surfaces, sampling is key to identifying materials sourced from both the surface and the deep. Analysis of returned samples improves knowledge of mineralogy, petrology, physics, chemistry, and chronology. The Chang'e-5 mission landed in the northern part of Oceanus Procellarum, returning 1731 g of material including a 1 m long core sample.

## Review of Sampling Programs
*   **Luna Missions (1970-1972):** Collected 0.3 kg of core samples.
*   **Apollo Program (1969-1972):** Collected 381.7 kg of lunar samples.
*   **Sample Types:** Pristine highland anorthosites, mafic/ultramafic rocks, and mare basalts.
*   **Regolith Characteristics:** A mixture of impact-induced breccias, volcanic extrusives, and meteoritic components. Upper layers range from 10 m to several km in thickness.

## Laboratory Construction and Requirements
The paper outlines the construction of specialized laboratories to prevent contamination and simulate lunar conditions:

### Contamination Control
*   **Cleanliness:** Air in glove box cabinets must have < 20 molecules of oxygen and 50 molecules of water per million molecules of nitrogen.
*   **Materials:** Tools must be made of aluminum, stainless steel, or Teflon to avoid chemical reactions.
*   **Pressure:** Nitrogen gas pressure in the cabinet must be kept slightly higher than the surrounding room to prevent inward leaks.

### Temperature and Environment
*   **Thermal Control:** Low temperatures are required to preserve samples and decrease chemical reactivity.
*   **Atmosphere:** Samples are stored in nitrogen-filled cabinets or vacuum containers.

## Curation Principles
1.  **Usage Ratio:** A significant portion is preserved for future generations/technologies (only ~15% of Apollo samples have been opened to date).
2.  **Digitalization:** All measurements are recorded and shared in digital formats.
3.  **Security:** Samples are stored in multiple locations to prevent loss from single-point catastrophes.

## Discussion on Future Missions
*   **Sampling Skills:** Need for more intensive development in sampling techniques (e.g., bedrock sampling).
*   **Landing Site Selection:** Targeting pristine rocks unaffected by space weathering.
*   **Non-destructive Testing:** Advancing X-ray and CT techniques to acquire 3D models of inner structures without opening containers.

## Conclusion
The collection and analysis of lunar samples are vital for understanding planetary evolution. By integrating Apollo experiences with new technologies, the Chang'e-5 curation scheme provides a standardized framework for processing extraterrestrial materials, ensuring their scientific value is maximized for the global community.
