# Laboratory construction and curation scheme for returned samples of the Chang’e-5 mission

**Authors:** Hongwei Yang, Ruihong Yang, and Qian Wang
**Journal:** Geoscience Letters (2022) 9:19
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s40562-022-00224-z

## Abstract
The analysis of samples returned from a planetary body is important to understand the origin, composition, evolution, and interactions with space or atmospheric weathering. The Chang’e-5 lander, China's first lunar sample return mission, acquired 1731 g of material. The China Lunar Exploration and Space Engineering Center (CLESEC) is constructing laboratories and determining regulations for preserving these samples. This paper establishes requirements for laboratory construction, curation, and analysis based on Apollo experiences and lunar surface conditions.

## Introduction
Remote sensing is a primary approach for determining mineralogical features, but samples are required to calibrate this data. Lunar regolith, which can reach several kilometers in depth, is affected by space weathering and impacts. Analysis of returned samples improves knowledge of mineralogy, petrology, physics, chemistry, and chronology.

## Review of Sampling and Laboratory Requirements
### Apollo and Luna Experiences
- **Luna missions (16/20/24):** Collected ~0.3 kg total.
- **Apollo missions (11-17):** Collected 381.7 kg total.
- **Sampling methods:** Picking, digging, scooping, and drilling.
- **Rock types:** Highland anorthosites, mafic/ultramafic rocks (dunites, troctolites, norites), and mare basalts.
- **Pristine Rocks:** Only 260 samples identified as confident pristine rocks, mostly <30 mm.

### The Chang’e-5 Sample
- **Landed:** December 1, 2020 (43.058 N, 51.916 W).
- **Total mass:** 1731 g.
- **Drilling core:** ~1 m long, ~250 g.

## Laboratory Construction Requirements
To prevent contamination and preserve sample integrity, specific environmental conditions are required:

| Parameter | Requirement |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Cleanliness** | Clean air with <10^4 ~ 2 x 10^5 particles per cm² |
| **Oxygen (O2)** | < 20 molecules per million molecules of nitrogen |
| **Water (H2O)** | < 50 molecules per million molecules of nitrogen |
| **Atmosphere** | High-pressure Nitrogen (N2) gas to prevent air leakage |
| **Materials** | Stainless steel, aluminum, Teflon, and neoprene; no lubricants or water for sawing |
| **Temperature** | Controlled low temperatures (frozen preservation for some samples) |

### Laboratory Layout (Sub-labs)
1. Change Rooms and Air Shower
2. Pristine Sample Lab
3. Pristine Sample Display Case
4. Core and Saw Room
5. Pristine Corridor and Vault
6. Experiment Lab
7. Return Sample Vault
8. Observation Room

## Curation and Usage Principles
1. **Ratio of Usage:** Only a portion is opened for research; the majority is preserved for future advanced technologies (Apollo currently has ~15% opened).
2. **Distribution:** Samples are split into numbered subsamples (e.g., Apollo's 2200 samples became 110,000 subsamples).
3. **Request Process:** Official procedures involve peer review and loan agreements through CLESEC/CNSA.

## Discussion and Future Directions
- Development of advanced sampling techniques for bedrock acquisition.
- Minimizing cost for collection on other planets.
- Increasing the use of digitalized data sets (3D CT models, spectral characteristics).

## Conclusions
The Chang'e-5 mission marks a milestone for China in planetary science. The established rules and specialized laboratories ensure the protection of lunar samples from Earth's atmosphere and magnetization, promoting long-term scientific value.
