# Laboratory construction and curation scheme for returned samples of the Chang’e-5 mission

**Authors:** Hongwei Yang, Ruihong Yang, Qian Wang
**Journal:** Geoscience Letters (2022) 9:19
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s40562-022-00224-z

## Abstract
The analysis of samples returned from a planetary body is important to understand the origin, composition, evolution, and interactions with space or atmospheric weathering. The Chang’e-5 lander, the first Chinese science project involving the return of lunar samples, acquired less than 2 kg. The CLESEC (China Lunar Exploration and Space Engineering Center) is planning to construct at least two laboratories and develop non-damaging technologies to analyze the samples. This paper establishes requirements and standardized conditions for constructing laboratories based on Apollo experiences.

## Introduction
Remote sensing is a primary approach for determining mineralogical features, but samples from the surface and deep subsurface (regolith) are necessary to calibrate data and understand interior substances. The materials in the shallow surface are affected by space weathering, high-energy particles, and meteorite impacts.

## Comprehensive studies on lunar sample laboratory
The returned lunar samples are stored in two locations: one for representative samples against calamity and another for preservation, research, and testing. The laboratory constructed at the Johnson Space Center in 1979 comprises eight sub-labs:
1. Change Rooms and Air Shower
2. Pristine Sample Lab
3. Pristine Sample Display Case
4. Core and Saw Room
5. Pristine Corridor and Vault
6. Experiment Lab
7. Return Sample Vault
8. Observation Room

### Contamination Control
- **Cleanliness:** All rooms must have clean air. Water and oxygen concentrations in the glove box cabinet must be < 20 molecules of oxygen and 50 molecules of water per million molecules of nitrogen.
- **Storage:** Samples must be stored in sealed glove box cabinets with airtight gloves (Teflon/neoprene) and tools made of aluminum and stainless steel.
- **Pressure:** Nitrogen gas pressure in the cabinet is kept slightly higher than the air pressure in the lab to prevent leak-in.

### Environmental Conditions
- **Temperature:** Low controlled temperatures preserve samples in environments similar to the lunar surface (−150 °C to 110 °C).
- **Atmosphere:** Inert gases (Nitrogen) prevent primary chemical reactions.

## Regulations for China’s Sample Return
### Required conditions to construct the laboratory
1. **Cleaner Change Room:** Maximum cleanliness level up to 10⁴~2×10⁵ particles per cm².
2. **Inert Gases:** Use of nitrogen to study chemical components and isotopes.
3. **Frozen Preservation:** Cabinets at low temperatures similar to the Moon.
4. **Imaging:** HD cameras with multiple-angle views, thermometers, and hygrometers.
5. **Analysis Tools:** X-ray and CT instruments for section analysis.

## Conclusion
The collection of samples from the Moon is significant for interpreting remote sensing and understanding planetary evolution. By integrating Apollo experiences with Chang’e-5 characteristics, China has developed strict rules for preservation and laboratory construction to promote the scientific value of returned samples.
