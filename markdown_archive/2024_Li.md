# A review on design and construction of the lunar launch/landing infrastructure\n\n**Authors:** Xinyi Li a,b,1, Yuyue Gao a,b,1, Yan Zhou a,b, Wenbin Han a,b,*, Cheng Zhou a,b,*\n\n**Affiliations:**\n- a National Center of Technology Innovation for Digital Construction, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, Wuhan 430074, China\n- b School of Civil and Hydraulic Engineering, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, Wuhan 430074, China\n\n**Received:** 23 April 2024; **Accepted:** 2 July 2024; **Available online:** 6 July 2024\n\n## Abstract\nWith advances in automated construction and deeper Moon exploration missions, the concept of \"building a house on the moon\" is becoming increasingly feasible. The launch/landing (LL) infrastructure plays a vital role in ensuring the safety of landers and astronauts and should be prioritized for construction. Initially, this review explores the variety of designs used at existing LL sites, analyzing the logic and unique features of each design. It then outlines three main construction methods for landing pads, from material production to basic construction processes. The review also covers experiments related to LL pads, including material, component, and construction testing. Finally, current challenges and future directions for the development of lunar LL infrastructure are discussed, and 3D-printed biomimetic structures are highlighted as a particularly promising option.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nThe Moon serves as humanity\u2019s primary gateway to exploring the universe. The safety of launch and landing for space landers is key to accomplishing lunar exploration and construction missions. Unlike on Earth, the Moon lacks established launch and landing facilities, presenting significant safety challenges for spacecraft attempting direct landings. Plumes ejected by lander engines cause damage to the lunar surface, and loose regolith can easily form large craters, exacerbating unevenness. Moreover, high-speed regolith particles pose a serious threat by eroding surrounding equipment.\n\n## 2. Design of the lunar landing/launching infrastructure\n### 2.1. Launch/landing area\nPlume simulations highlight the necessity of adapting LL site designs to accommodate thermal and flow impingement. The primary purpose is to ensure lander safety and mitigate hazards from plume-dust interactions. The diameter of the LL site is currently recommended to be approximately 100 m.\n\n### 2.2. Launch/landing pad\nPaving and 3D extrusion printing are the two main design approaches. The use of pavers is a cost-effective method for stabilizing the surface, particularly in peripheral regions. For central areas, robust designs such as 3D-printed tiles with underlying piles or hexagonal \"sett\" paving modules are proposed to withstand high-speed heat flow.\n\n### 2.3. Blast walls and trenches\nStructures for interception and collection of splashing regolith are necessary. Designs include inward-deflecting \"Sunflower Vault\" designs and vertical \"Eyelashes\" wall designs to collect and redirect dust into trenches.\n\n## 3. In situ construction for lunar launch/landing infrastructure\n### 3.1. 3D printing based on concrete extrusion\n3D extrusion involves preprocessing raw materials, manufacturing (printing), and post-processing. Researchers have explored lunar soil mixed with polymers, sulfur, or geopolymers. Examples include the \"Lunar Pad\" project utilizing gantry-style printers and Contour Crafting (CCC).\n\n### 3.2. Sintering, melting and assembly\nSintering transforms powdered material into dense objects below the melting point. NASA\u2019s LASSS (Large Area Surface Sintering System) uses resistive heating, while other methods utilize solar concentrators or lasers (Laser Powder Bed Fusion). Modular assembly of sintered tiles is a key strategy.\n\n### 3.3. Building with moon rocks\nRock pavements (armor stones) are cost-effective for peripheral zones. Layered rock sizes (armor stone ~145mm, bottom layer ~15mm) are used for soil stabilization. Bonding methods include self-bonding of heated basalt or welding using a torch.\n\n## 4. Challenges and future directions\n- **Multiphysics modeling:** Difficulties in simulating plume-particle interactions in vacuum/low-gravity.\n- **Cracking:** Preventing shrinkage-induced cracks in large-scale 3D printing and sintering.\n- **Terrestrial Analogues:** Limitations of Earth-based experiments in replicating lunar conditions.\n- **Future directions:** Development of hybrid-driven simulation methods, bionic/bio-inspired designs (e.g., Sunflower Vault), and fully autonomous maintenance systems based on AI and robotics.
