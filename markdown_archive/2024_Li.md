# A review on design and construction of the lunar launch/landing infrastructure

## Abstract
With advances in automated construction and deeper Moon exploration missions, the concept of 'building a house on the moon' is becoming increasingly feasible. The launch/landing (LL) infrastructure plays a vital role in ensuring the safety of landers and astronauts. This review explores LL designs, construction methods (from material production to assembly), and terrestrial testing results. It highlights 3D-printed biomimetic structures as a promising direction.

## 1. Introduction
The safety of launch and landing is key to accomplishing lunar exploration. Unlike Earth, the Moon lacks established facilities, and engine plumes cause damage to the lunar surface through erosion and dust dispersion. Well-constructed LL sites are necessary to ensure safe landings and protect surrounding equipment.

## 2. Design of Lunar Landing/Launching Infrastructure
### 2.1 Launch/Landing Area
Plume simulations reveal differences between Earth and Moon environments. A 40 MT lander can propel particles far; research suggests the LL site diameter should be approximately 100 m (though some proposed sizes range from 20 m to 100 m). 

### 2.2 Launch/Landing Pad
Two main design approaches: 
- **Paving**: Simplest and most cost-effective. Uses moon rocks (layered/graded) or 3D-printed tiles. 
- **3D Extrusion Printing**: Integrated designs like 'The Brute' (using crater slopes as berms) or 'Lunar Pad' (double-layer hollow structures).

### 2.3 Blast Walls and Trenches
Designed to intercept high-speed dust ejecta. Concepts include:
- **Sunflower Vault**: Inward-facing walls inspired by floral buds to deflect flow and capture dust.
- **Eyelashes**: Rows of columns inspired by animal eyelashes to dissipate particle energy.

## 3. In Situ Construction
### 3.1 3D Printing Based on Concrete Extrusion
Involves mixing lunar soil with polymers, sulfur, or geopolymers. 
- **Polyethylene/BP-1 mixture**: Cast at 230°C.
- **Contour Crafting (CCC)**: Capable of building complex structures like the Lunar Pad layers.
- **Sulfur Concrete**: Lighter equipment requirements, integrates mixing and extrusion.

### 3.2 Sintering, Melting, and Assembly
- **LASSS (Large Area Surface Sintering System)**: Rover-based heater for soil stabilization.
- **Solar Concentrator**: Uses Fresnel lenses to reach 1350°C for melting.
- **Selective Separation Sintering (SSS)**: Uses B-powder (base) and S-powder (separator, e.g., Al2O3) to produce interlocking pavers.
- **Laser Melting**: High-power CO2 lasers for high-strength tiles.

### 3.3 Building with Moon Rocks
Pavement using sorted rocks (Armor stone, second layer, bottom layer). Tests indicate stacking rocks in descending order of size yields the best stability.

## 4. Terrestrial Testing
### 4.1 Building Material Testing
| Material | Compressive Strength (MPa) | Findings |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Hawaiian Basalt (Sintered) | 21.48 | Equivalent to Portland concrete |
| Laser melted EAC 1A | 56.19–216.29 | Average 93.94 MPa |
| Solar sintered JSC-2A | 2.49 | Young's modulus 0.21 GPa |
| KOHLS-1 Polymer Concrete | 10.3–12.9 | Higher strength in vacuum |
| Geopolymer (Lunamer) | 16.6–33.1 | Potential radiation shielding |

### 4.2 Structural Component Testing
- **Plate Load Tests**: Polymer-concrete pads reached 2.7 MPa compressive strength.
- **Cold Thruster Tests**: KSC (2010) verified erosion protection of stone pavements.
- **Hot Fire Tests**: ICON and NASA verified 'Olympus' designs; Gabion cages remained intact during rocket plume exposure.

## 5. Challenges and Future Directions
1. **Multiphysics Modeling**: Difficulty simulating near-field and far-field plume-particle interactions.
2. **Cracking**: Large-scale 3D printing faces shrinkage and stress concentration issues.
3. **Terrestrial Simulation**: Hard to simulate combined vacuum, low gravity, and high thermal loads simultaneously.
