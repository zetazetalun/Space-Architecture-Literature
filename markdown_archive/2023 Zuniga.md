# SUITABILITY OF SILICONE FOR SOFT-ROBOTIC EXPLORATION OF TERRESTRIAL AND EXTRATERRESTRIAL OCEAN WORLDS\n\n## Authors\nA. Nuncio Zuniga, W. Fink\n\n## Abstract\nThis work evaluates the mechanical and chemical properties of silicone rubber (specifically Ecoflex 00-30) to assess its viability for soft-robotic construction and as a sealant in extreme planetary liquid environments. Testing included strain analysis at various temperatures, re-bonding properties for additive manufacturing, and resistance to dissolution in salt water and hydrocarbons.\n\n## Technical Data and Findings\n- **Material:** EcoflexTM 00-30 silicone rubber.\n- **Temperature Resilience:** Pliability is maintained above -50 °C. At -80 °C, the material freezes and becomes brittle, but the process is fully reversible upon thawing. Submersion in liquid nitrogen (-196 °C) causes shattering.\n- **Elasticity:** While the manufacturer claims 900% elongation, testing showed a breaking point at approximately 553.3 ± 20.5% for 50:50 mixing ratios.\n- **Additive Manufacturing Potential:** Cured silicone demonstrates excellent re-bonding properties with newly curing silicone (even after 6 days), allowing for layer-by-layer construction or encapsulation of electronics.\n- **Chemical Resistance:** Resistant to saltwater (3.5% and saturated salinity) and mineral oil (hydrocarbon stand-in) for at least 21 days and 2 months respectively, with no signs of degradation.\n- **Electrical Properties:** Non-conductive, making it an ideal sealant for electronics (e.g., Raspberry Pi units) in conductive liquid environments.\n\n## Conclusions\nEcoflex 00-30 is a robust candidate for constructing soft robotics and sensor housings for terrestrial oceans and water-based subsurface oceans on bodies like Europa. However, for ultra-low temperature environments like Titan's hydrocarbon lakes (~94 K), alternative polymers that remain flexible at lower temperatures would be required.
