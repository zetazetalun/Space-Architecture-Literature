# SUITABILITY OF SILICONE FOR SOFT-ROBOTIC EXPLORATION OF TERRESTRIAL AND EXTRATERRESTRIAL OCEAN WORLDS

**Journal:** Space Science and Technology (Космічна наука і технологія)
**Year:** 2023, Vol. 29, No. 3, pp. 47–56
**DOI:** 10.15407/knit2023.03.047
**Authors:** A. Nuncio Zuniga, W. Fink

## Abstract
This work revisits relevant mechanical and chemical properties of silicone rubber — Ecoflex in this study — to assess its suitability and viability for use in soft-robotic explorer construction and subsequent deployment and as a sealant for communication beacons, sensor pods, and other electronics in extreme planetary liquid environments, such as the depths of Earth’s oceans and extraterrestrial ocean worlds. Strain at a range of temperatures was tested under various endpoint clamp forces. The re-binding property of cured silicone rubber samples with newly curing samples was investigated for its potential for additive manufacturing in soft robotics. Finally, the dissolution resistance, non-polarity, and electrical non-conductivity of silicone rubber were studied to assess its suitability for sealing electronics in saline and hydrocarbon liquids.

## 1. Introduction
Subsurface oceans on planetary bodies known as ocean worlds, such as Jupiter’s moon Europa and Saturn’s moons Titan and Enceladus, are prime candidate locales for astrobiological exploration. This paper revisits experimentally the suitability of silicone rubber for constructing soft-robotic explorers and sealing components in the context of extreme planetary liquid environments.

## 2. Materials and Methods
*   **Material:** Ecoflex™ 00-30 (proprietary silicone rubber).
*   **Testing:** Strain testing across various temperatures (-5°C to 40°C), very low temperature testing (down to -196°C), re-bonding ability for additive manufacturing, and dissolution resistance in saline and hydrocarbon (mineral oil) environments.

## 4. Results
### 4.1. Strain Testing
Increased amounts of compound A led to a stiffer material. Strain results appeared largely temperature-independent within the measured range (-5°C to 40°C).

### 4.3. Very Low Temperature Testing
Samples did not freeze at -20°C but froze at -80°C. Freezing was fully reversible; all samples returned to pre-freezing pliability once thawed. In liquid nitrogen (-196°C), samples shattered but returned to pliability upon reaching room temperature.

### 4.4. Re-bonding Ability
Newly curing Ecoflex successfully bonded to samples cured up to 6 days prior. The resulting interface was indistinguishable and did not separate under stress, highlighting potential for additive manufacturing and encapsulation.

### 4.5. Dissolution and Conductivity
*   **Resistance:** No signs of degradation in saltwater (3.5% and saturated) for at least 21 days or in mineral oil for two months.
*   **Conductivity:** The material is electrically non-conductive, allowing direct encapsulation of exposed electronics (e.g., Raspberry Pi) which functioned perfectly while submerged.

## 5. Discussion & Conclusions
Ecoflex 00-30 is an excellent encapsulation option for soft-robotics and sensor pods. It remains pliable down to at least -20°C (sufficient for terrestrial oceans) and likely functions down to -50°C. While too cold for Titan's surface (-179°C) where it would freeze, it paves the way for soft-robotic explorers in subsurface oceans like those of Europa and Enceladus.
