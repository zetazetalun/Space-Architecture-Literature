# Laboratory-Scale Distributed Pressure Measurements of Blade Interaction with JSC-1A Lunar Simulant

**Authors:** Robert H. King and Andrew T. Brewer
**Publication:** Journal of Aerospace Engineering (ASCE), Vol. 26, No. 1, January 2013
**DOI:** 10.1061/(ASCE)AS.1943-5525.0000224

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial excavator blade design for in situ resource utilization (ISRU) requires high precision due to reliability and weight constraints. This project measured pressure distribution on laboratory-scale blades using JSC-1A lunar simulant to improve finite-element (FEA) and discrete-element models. Results indicate that V-shaped blades with tapered curves are optimal, and operating procedures should favor multiple passes at small depths.

## Introduction
Outpost development on the Moon or Mars requires soil handling tasks such as creating roads, landing sites, and excavating foundations. Robotic excavators must be lightweight due to launch costs, necessitating precise design rather than large terrestrial design factors. Nonuniform load distribution is typical as soil interaction varies across the blade surface.

## Methodology and Measurements
Tests were conducted using the Colorado School of Mines (CSM) soil excavation measurement apparatus with Tekscan pressure mapping sensors.

## Key Results
- **Blade Shape:** V-shaped blades (specifically at a 30° angle) minimize excavation loads by diverting soil to the sides, which is superior for lightweight machines.
- **Depth of Cut:** Excavation force increases with depth; therefore, multiple small-depth passes are recommended to conserve power and reduce machine mass.
- **Curvature:** Curved blades allow for higher rake angles which further reduce excavation forces.
- **Pressure Models:** The study developed quadratic and exponential pressure distribution models for use in FEA software to simulate realistic soil-blade interactions.

## Conclusions
To minimize the weight and power requirements of extraterrestrial excavators, engineers should employ V-shaped, curved blades and utilize an operational strategy of shallow, repeated passes. The distributed pressure models developed provide a foundation for more accurate structural design of lunar construction equipment.
