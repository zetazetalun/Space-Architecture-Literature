# Laboratory-Scale Distributed Pressure Measurements of Blade Interaction with JSC-1A Lunar Simulant\n\n**Authors:** Robert H. King and Andrew T. Brewer\n**Journal:** Journal of Aerospace Engineering (ASCE)\n**Volume/Issue:** Vol. 26, No. 1\n**Date:** January 2013\n**DOI:** 10.1061/(ASCE)AS.1943-5525.0000224\n\n## Abstract\nBecause of the requirement for high reliability and very low weight, extraterrestrial excavator blade design for in situ resource utilization (ISRU) must be more precise than for current terrestrial practice. This project measured the pressure distribution on a suite of laboratory-scale blades using JSC-1A lunar simulant and Ottawa Sand. The work concluded that a V-shaped blade with a tapered curve is the best configuration, and multiple passes of small-depth cuts is the best excavation operating procedure.\n\n## Introduction\nOutpost development on extraterrestrial bodies requires robotic machines to build berms, grade and compact roads, excavate foundations, and smooth landing pads. Precise design is required to minimize launch costs while ensuring structural integrity. Overdesign is costly, so understanding the exact pressure distributions on excavator blades using finite-element analysis (FEA) and discrete-element models (DEM) is critical. This project developed empirical models based on laboratory measurements of non-uniform pressure distributions.\n\n## Measurements and Soil Properties\nTests were conducted using a soil tray excavation apparatus. Two soils were tested:\n1. **Ottawa Sand:** A pure silicate used for comparison due to its resistance to settling and compaction.\n2. **JSC-1A Lunar Soil Simulant:** Mimics the angularity and particle size distribution of lunar regolith, making it prone to settling and compaction.\n\n### Table 1: Base Configuration and Range for Test Sets\n| Variable | Base | Range |\n| :--- | :--- | :--- |\n| Material | JSC-1A | JSC-1A, Ottawa Sand |\n| Cutting depth (cm) | 0.635 | 0.127–0.635 (0.127 increments) |\n| Rake angle (deg) | 0 | 0, 8, 12.5 |\n| Blade angle (deg) | 0 | 0, 15, 30, 45 |\n| Blade width (cm) | 30.48 | 20.32, 25.40, 30.48 |\n| Blade curvature | Flat | Flat, curved |\n\n## Results and Analysis\n- **Blade Shape:** V-shaped blades minimize loads by diverting soil to the sides. A 30-degree V-blade provides the widest cut with optimal force distribution.\n- **Curvature:** Curved blades are more efficient than flat blades, and higher rake angles reduce excavation forces.\n- **Operating Procedure:** Lightweight machines should prioritize multiple shallow cuts rather than single deep cuts to manage power requirements and reduce stress on the machine frame.\n\n### Table 4: Tekscan Sensor Calibration Constants\n| Sensor | Scale Factor A | Exponent B |\n| :--- | :--- | :--- |\n| 1 | 0.0467 | 0.692 |\n| 2 | 0.0347 | 0.703 |\n\n## Conclusions and Recommendations\n1. A V-shaped blade with a 30-degree angle and a curved face is the superior design for extraterrestrial excavation.\n2. Multiple small-depth cuts are the most effective excavation strategy for maintaining low power and force.\n3. Tapered blade curves can minimize weight and reduce material requirements for support members.
