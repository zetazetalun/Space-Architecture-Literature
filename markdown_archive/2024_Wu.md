# A comprehensive review of extraterrestrial construction, from space concrete materials to habitat structures

## Abstract
This review explores the development and potential applications of space concrete, a critical material for future extraterrestrial construction. Space concrete, adapted to withstand the harsh conditions of outer space, such as extreme temperatures, vacuum, microgravity, and radiation, offers a sustainable solution for building habitats and infrastructure on celestial bodies like the Moon and Mars. Emphasizing the innovative approaches in formulating space concrete, including the use of lunar and Martian soil as aggregates and the exploration of alternative binders to traditional water-based cement, this review highlights the significance of in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and 3D printing technologies in advancing extraterrestrial construction. Additionally, the current designs and applications of space concrete structures are discussed. By providing a detailed analysis of the challenges faced in space construction and the latest advancements in material and structural research, the review underlines the pivotal role of space concrete in supporting space exploration and long-term habitat.

## 1. Introduction
Traditional Earth-based materials may not satisfy space environment requirements, necessitating the development of innovative materials. Recent advancements include thermosetting materials, regolith melting/forming, laser sintering, microwave sintering, and space concrete. Space concrete offers several distinct advantages as compared to other materials: it does not need high temperatures or immense energy for production and utilizes available resources with minimal additional infrastructure.

## 2. Factors to consider in the preparation of space concrete
*   **Extreme temperature variations:** The Moon (-133 °C to 121 °C) and Mars (-125 °C to 20 °C) present significant thermal stresses.
*   **Vacuum and microgravity:** Vacuum causes rapid water evaporation in traditional cement; microgravity affects ettringite formation and increases air porosity.
*   **Radiation and seismic activity:** Concrete serves as effective shielding against GCRs and SPEs. Seismic risks (Moonquakes/Marsquakes) must be factored into structural designs.
*   **Limited resources:** The cost of transporting materials from Earth (approx. $3,000/kg) necessitates ISRU.

## 3. Types of space concrete
| Concrete Category | Material Composition | Water Requirement | Setting Time | Chemical Stability |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Portland** | Limestone and clay | High | Slow | Poor (Sulfate Attack) |
| **Calcium Aluminate** | Limestone and bauxite | High (Wet-mix)/Low (DMSI) | Moderate | Moderate |
| **Magnesia Silica** | MgO and silicate | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate |
| **Sorel** | MgO and MgCl₂ | No | Fast | Moderate (Water Sensitivity) |
| **Sulfur** | Sulfur and fillers/sand | No | Fast | Moderate (High Temp) |
| **Polymer** | Epoxy resins, polyethylene | No | Fast | Good |
| **Geopolymer** | Silicate/aluminate minerals | Low | Fast | Good |

## 4. Key Findings on Materials
*   **Geopolymer Concrete:** Identified as the most promising due to low water demand, rapid curing, and high compressive strength (potentially >100 MPa with additives like BFS or silica fume).
*   **Sulfur Concrete:** Waterless and resource-efficient but unsuitable for equatorial lunar regions due to low melting point (~120 °C) and sublimation in vacuum (est. 1 cm loss every 4-6 years).
*   **Sorel/Magnesia Silica:** Offer good mechanical properties but are limited by water availability or specific mineral requirements.

## 5. Design Principles and Habitat Structures
Habitat designs evolve through three phases: (I) Equipment shelters, (II) Temporary human habitats, and (III) Permanent bases. 
*   **Class I (Pre-fabricated):** Standard modules transported from Earth.
*   **Class II (Deployable):** Inflatable envelopes often covered with regolith for shielding.
*   **Class III (ISRU-based):** Structures printed or cast using local resources.

## 6. Fabrication Methods
*   **3D Printing:** Technologies like Contour Crafting (extrusion-based) and D-shape (binder jetting) are pivotal for automated construction.
*   **Automated Robotics:** Essential for handling microgravity and radiation during the construction process.

## 7. Conclusion
Geopolymer concrete is recommended as the primary material for future construction. Future research must focus on indigenous water extraction, ultra-high-performance formulations, and long-term durability studies in vacuum and radiation environments.
