# A comprehensive review of extraterrestrial construction, from space concrete materials to habitat structures

**Journal:** Engineering Structures 318 (2024) 118723  
**Authors:** Chengqing Wu, Zizheng Yu, Ruizhe Shao, Jun Li  
**Affiliation:** School of Civil and Environmental Engineering, University of Technology Sydney, Australia

## Abstract
This review explores the development and potential applications of space concrete, a critical material for future extraterrestrial construction. Adapted to withstand extreme temperatures, vacuum, microgravity, and radiation, space concrete offers a sustainable solution for building habitats on the Moon and Mars using in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and 3D printing technologies.

## 1. Introduction
Key technologies discussed include thermosetting materials, regolith melting/forming, laser sintering, microwave sintering, and space concrete. Space concrete is noted for its energy efficiency, simplicity of mixing and setting compared to high-energy sintering, and high compressive strength.

## 2. Factors to consider in the preparation of space concrete
- **Extreme temperature variations:** Moon (-133 to 121 °C), Mars (-125 to 20 °C). These fluctuations affect hydration and cause thermal stress.
- **Vacuum and microgravity:** Vacuum leads to rapid water evaporation and increased porosity. Microgravity influences crystal growth (e.g., ettringite) and air bubble distribution.
- **Radiation exposure:** Concrete acts as a radiation shield. Geopolymer cement shows shielding capacity comparable to Portland cement.
- **Seismic activity:** Moonquakes and Marsquakes necessitate seismically resilient structural designs.
- **Limited resources:** High transport costs ($3,000/kg) require using lunar and Martian regolith via ISRU.

## 3. Types of space concrete
### 3.1. Simulants
Common simulants used for research include MLS-1 (Mare), JSC-1A (Lunar), and MGS-1 (Mars).

### 3.2. Concrete Formulations
- **Portland Concrete (OPC):** Challenging due to water scarcity and microgravity-induced porosity.
- **Calcium Aluminate Concrete:** Faster hardening and better temperature resilience than OPC.
- **Sulfur Concrete:** Waterless; solidifies upon cooling. Issues include sulfur sublimation in vacuum and low melting points (120 °C).
- **Sorel Concrete:** Based on Magnesium Oxide and Magnesium Chloride; high strength (up to 73 MPa) but sensitive to water.
- **Magnesia Silica Concrete:** Potential for Martian infrastructure using local minerals.
- **Polymer Concrete:** High strength and durability but relies on Earth-sourced binders.
- **Geopolymer Concrete:** Excellent candidate; uses alkali-activated regolith; low water demand and rapid curing.

## 5. Design principles and challenges
- **Structural Concepts:** Prefabricated modules, inflatable structures, and resource-integrated concrete edifices.
- **Fabrication:** 3D printing (Contour Crafting, D-shape) and automated robotic systems (e.g., Chinese Super Mason - CSM).
- **Numerical Simulation:** Critical for assessing global resistant safety factors and structural integrity against extreme environments.
