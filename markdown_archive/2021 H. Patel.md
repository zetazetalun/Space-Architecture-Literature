# Applying Terrestrial Geo-Material Science Methodology to Lunar ISRU Construction

**Authors:** H. Patel; P. Suermann; N. Shumaker; S. Deitrick; J. W. Bullard; and R. C. Ewing
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## ABSTRACT
Building on research underway for military and expeditionary construction, this research aims to establish methods for the design and testing of the materials mixtures derived from the lunar surface with minimal or no additives that need to be transported from the Earth. This paper will review opportunities and challenges of demonstrated and experimental construction methods such as concreting and sintering as well as discuss the need to evaluate and develop these methods using regolith simulants more representative of the lunar highlands where the NASA Artemis mission proposes to land in both its mineralogical and agglutinated properties.

## INTRODUCTION
As the nation and the world prepare for the first lunar return in nearly 50 years, a pressing need exists to develop and test construction methods that use in-situ (locally available) materials for critical infrastructure. Building any exploratory base camp requires site preparation for horizontal (e.g., landing pads, roads) and vertical structures (e.g., berms, blast and radiation shielding).

## Background: Lunar surface environment— Terrain and material
The general landscape of the Moon is controlled by high-velocity impacts and volcanism. The entire surface is covered in regolith (dust and rock fragments). 
- **Mare:** Basaltic lava flows, younger, ~15% of the surface. 
- **Highlands:** Anorthosite, older, more cratered, thicker regolith layer.

### Regolith Characteristics
- **Size Distribution:** ~95% by mass is <1.37 mm; ~50% by mass is <50 μm.
- **Bulk Density:** 1.30 g/cm³ to 1.92 g/cm³.
- **Composition:** Varies from titanium-rich basalt (mare) to plagioclase feldspar-rich anorthosite (highlands).

### Agglutinates
Agglutinates are fused particles of impact glass, rock, and mineral fragments. Content increases with regolith maturity (5% to 65% by volume). They contain nanophase iron (NP-Fe), which provides magnetic properties.

## Lunar surface construction— Methods and considerations
Two primary methods to consolidate granular media:
1. **Concreting:** Binding grains within a chemically activated matrix (e.g., geopolymers or sulfur binders).
2. **Sintering:** Fusing grains by applying heat (60-80% of melting temperature).

### Concreting Methods
- **Challenges:** Scarcity of calcium and liquid water.
- **Potential Binders:** Geopolymers (aluminosilicate precursors) or Sulfur (troilite-derived).

### Sintering Methods
- **Advantages:** No water required; can be performed in vacuum.
- **Specific Techniques:** Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) shows promise for densifying lunar soils.

## Methodology
The team is adapting the **Thermodynamic Hydration and Microstructure Evolution Simulator (THAMES)** to predict mineralogical components and model 3D microstructures for lunar concrete and sintered products. 

## Materials and Processing Characterization
- **Simulants:** JSC-1A is common but more similar to mare regions. There is a critical need for highlands-specific simulants with realistic agglutinate content.
- **Basalt Glass Fiber:** Can be created by melting lunar regolith; useful for reinforcement in construction without polymer binders.

## CONCLUSION
The objective is to adapt terrestrial geomaterial models to evaluate regolith-based materials under lunar conditions, ultimately developing ASCE/ASTM standards for ISRU construction. This supports NASA’s "to stay" mission by 2024.
