# Applying Terrestrial Geo-Material Science Methodology to Lunar ISRU Construction

**Authors:** H. Patel, P. Suermann, N. Shumaker, S. Deitrick, J. W. Bullard, and R. C. Ewing

## ABSTRACT
Building on research underway for military and expeditionary construction, this research aims to establish methods for the design and testing of the materials mixtures derived from the lunar surface with minimal or no additives that need to be transported from the Earth. This paper will review opportunities and challenges of demonstrated and experimental construction methods such as concreting and sintering as well as discuss the need to evaluate and develop these methods using regolith simulants more representative of the lunar highlands where the NASA Artemis mission proposes to land in both its mineralogical and agglutinated properties.

## INTRODUCTION
As the world prepares for the first lunar return in nearly 50 years, a pressing need exists to develop and test construction methods that use in-situ (locally available) materials for critical infrastructure. Building any exploratory base camp requires site preparation for horizontal (e.g., landing pads, roads) and vertical structures (e.g., berms, blast and radiation shielding).

## LUNAR SURFACE ENVIRONMENT

### Terrain and Material
The surface is covered in regolith (dust and rock fragments). Two major classes exist:
- **Mare:** ~15% of the surface, basaltic lava flows, younger, thinner regolith layer (~5-10m).
- **Highlands:** Anorthosite, older, thicker regolith layer, more heavily cratered.

### Regolith Composition
- **Particle size:** ~95% by mass is <1.37 mm; average particle size of 72 μm.
- **Types:** Mineral fragments, crystalline rock fragments, breccia fragments, glasses, and **agglutinates** (fused particles of glass/rock from micrometeorite impacts).
- **Agglutinate Content:** 5% to 65% by volume; higher in older highland surfaces.

## LUNAR SURFACE CONSTRUCTION METHODS

### Concreting Methods
- **Challenges:** Scarcity of calcium-rich minerals and liquid water.
- **Solutions:** 
  - Geopolymeric cements (aluminosilicate precursors).
  - Sulfur concrete (melting point ~115 °C), though it has poor durability under thermal cycling.

### Sintering Methods
- **Advantages:** No water required; works in vacuum; takes advantage of glassy fractions for capillary attraction.
- **Challenges:** Requires fine particles (<10 μm) whereas regolith averages 70 μm.
- **Promising Tech:** Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) uses pulsed current and pressure to achieve nearly 100% theoretical density.

## METHODOLOGY
The research team is adapting the **THAMES (Thermodynamic Hydration and Microstructure Evolution Simulator)** model to predict mineralogical components and engineering properties of lunar binders. THAMES uses geochemical thermodynamic methods to inform 3D microstructure algorithms.

## CONCLUSION
The objective is to adapt existing terrestrial models to evaluate regolith-based novel materials under lunar conditions, ultimately aiming to develop ASCE/ASTM standards for ISRU construction activities in support of NASA's 2024 lunar goals.
