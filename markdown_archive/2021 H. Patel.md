# Applying Terrestrial Geo-Material Science Methodology to Lunar ISRU Construction

**Authors:** H. Patel, P. Suermann, N. Shumaker, S. Deitrick, J. W. Bullard, and R. C. Ewing
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021 (ASCE)

## Abstract
Building on research for military and expeditionary construction, this research aims to establish methods for the design and testing of material mixtures derived from the lunar surface with minimal or no additives. The paper reviews opportunities and challenges of concreting and sintering, emphasizing the need for regolith simulants representative of the lunar highlands (mineralogical and agglutinated properties). The work supports the development of an ASCE Manual of Practice for in-situ lunar construction.

## Introduction
Critical infrastructure such as landing pads, roads, berms, and radiation shielding requires in-situ geotechnical site assessment and material development. The scale of lunar operations precludes the exclusive use of imported resources, necessitating ISRU.

### Comparison to Terrestrial Expeditionary Construction
The US military uses Stabilized Soil and Aggregate Systems (SSAS) for rapid construction. Analogous to ISRU, SSAS relies on local minerals. Methodology incorporates thermodynamic-based methods and multiscale modeling to predict strength and durability.

## Background: Lunar Surface Environment
### Terrain and Material
- **Mare:** ~15% of surface, basaltic lava flows, relatively young, higher boulder concentration.
- **Highlands:** Anorthosite, original lunar crust, older, heavily cratered, thicker regolith layer.
- **Regolith Composition:** Broadly-graded silty sands/sandy silts. ~95% mass < 1.37 mm; ~50% mass < 50 μm. Mass average size: 72 μm. Bulk density: 1.30 to 1.92 g/cm³.
- **Lunar Soil:** <1 mm fraction (~70% mass).
- **Lunar Dust:** <20 μm fraction (~20% mass).

### Agglutinates
Fused particles of impact glass, rock, and mineral fragments. Content increases with regolith maturity (5% to 65% by volume; 25-30% average). They contain nanophase iron (NP-Fe), giving regolith magnetic properties.

## Lunar Surface Construction Methods

### 1. Concreting Methods
Involves binding grains within a chemically activated matrix. 
- **Challenges:** Scarcity of calcium and liquid water.
- **Potential Solutions:** 
  - **Geopolymers:** Produced from aluminosilicate precursors (common on the Moon) using a chemical activator.
  - **Water Extraction:** Carbothermal reduction of regolith (requires ~100 kg of hydrogen per metric ton of concrete).
  - **Sulfur Binder:** Sulfur present in troilite (~1% mass). Low melting point (~115°C). Challenges: Volatility, toxicity, and low durability (Mohs hardness of 2).

### 2. Sintering Methods
Fusing grains via heat (60% to 80% of melting temperature). 
- **Advantages:** No water required; works in vacuum.
- **Temperatures:** Lunar aluminosilicates have relatively low melting points (800°C to 1000°C).
- **Advanced Method:** **Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS)** uses pulse electric current and uniaxial pressure to achieve nearly 100% theoretical density with short processing times.

## Methodology: THAMES
The **Thermodynamic Hydration and Microstructure Evolution Simulator (THAMES)** is being adapted for lunar materials. It uses geochemical thermodynamic methods to predict mineralogical components and 3D microstructure algorithms to calculate engineering properties.

## Conclusion
The research aims to substitute accurate regolith simulants for terrestrial materials to evaluate construction requirements (energy intensity, strength, time, cost). The goal is to develop ASCE/ASTM standards for ISRU activities to support NASA’s goal of returning to the Moon "to stay."
