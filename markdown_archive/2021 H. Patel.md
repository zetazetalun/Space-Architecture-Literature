# Applying Terrestrial Geo-Material Science Methodology to Lunar ISRU Construction

**Authors:** H. Patel, P. Suermann, N. Shumaker, S. Deitrick, J. W. Bullard, and R. C. Ewing
**Affiliations:** Texas A&M Univ., Jacobs/NASA Johnson Space Center
**Conference:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## ABSTRACT
Building on research underway for military and expeditionary construction, this research aims to establish methods for the design and testing of the materials mixtures derived from the lunar surface with minimal or no additives that need to be transported from the Earth. This paper reviews opportunities and challenges of demonstrated and experimental construction methods such as concreting and sintering and discusses the need to develop these methods using regolith simulants more representative of the lunar highlands.

## INTRODUCTION
There is a pressing need to develop construction methods using in-situ (locally available) materials for critical infrastructure, including landing pads, roads, berms, and radiation shielding. On Earth, infrastructure requires in-situ geotechnical site assessment; on the Moon, a knowledge gap exists in measuring and predicting lunar geotechnical properties for engineering. This research targets the use of indigenously developed materials through In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU).

## LUNAR SURFACE ENVIRONMENT
### Terrain and Material
The lunar surface is covered in regolith (dust and rock fragments). Two major terrain classes exist:
1. **Mare:** ~15% of the surface, basaltic lava flows, relatively young, thinner regolith layer.
2. **Highlands:** Composed of anorthosite, much older, thicker regolith layer with higher concentrations of agglutinates.

### Agglutinates
Agglutinates are fused particles of impact glass, rock, and mineral fragments. They account for 5% to 65% by volume of the regolith. They introduce unique chemical and physical properties that affect material mixture performance in construction.

## LUNAR SURFACE CONSTRUCTION METHODS
### Concreting Methods
*   **Pros:** Can produce large volumes and complex shapes with low-tech methods.
*   **Cons:** Scarcity of calcium and water on the Moon. 
*   **Alternatives:** Geopolymeric cements or sulfur binders (though sulfur is volatile and has poor thermal durability).

### Sintering Methods
*   **Mechanism:** Fusing grains using heat (60% to 80% of melting temperature).
*   **Pros:** No water required; can be performed in a vacuum.
*   **Cons:** Requires fine powder; lunar regolith (70 µm) is coarser than typical sinterable powders (sub-10 µm).
*   **Advanced Techniques:** Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) has shown promise in effectively densifying lunar soils.

## METHODOLOGY
The research adapts the **Thermodynamic Hydration and Microstructure Evolution Simulator (THAMES)** to predict mineralogical components and microstructures in lunar material mixtures. This model allows for the calculation of engineering properties (compressive strength, shrinkage) of regolith-based binders.

## MATERIALS AND PROCESSING CHARACTERIZATION
*   **Agglutinates:** Highland regions have higher agglutinate content. New simulants from companies like Off Planet and Outward Technologies are needed for high-fidelity testing.
*   **Basalt Glass Fiber:** Regolith can be melted and drawn into fibers for construction, potentially eliminating the need for polymer binders.

## CONCLUSION
The research aims to identify how existing terrestrial models can be adapted for lunar conditions. The ultimate goal is to develop ASCE or ASTM standards for ISRU activities to support NASA’s Artemis missions.
