# Applying Terrestrial Geo-Material Science Methodology to Lunar ISRU Construction

**Authors:** H. Patel, P. Suermann, N. Shumaker, S. Deitrick, J. W. Bullard, and R. C. Ewing

## Abstract
Building on research underway for military and expeditionary construction, this research aims to establish methods for the design and testing of the materials mixtures derived from the lunar surface with minimal or no additives that need to be transported from the Earth. This paper reviews opportunities and challenges of demonstrated and experimental construction methods such as concreting and sintering as well as discussing the need to evaluate and develop these methods using regolith simulants more representative of the lunar highlands where the NASA Artemis mission proposes to land.

## Introduction
A pressing need exists to develop and test construction methods that use in-situ materials for critical infrastructure like landing pads, roads, berms, and radiation shielding. Generating building products from indigenously developed materials is required for sustained human occupation due to the cost of importing resources from Earth.

## Background: Lunar Surface Environment
### Terrain and Material
*   **Mare:** ~15% of surface, basaltic lava flows, relatively young.
*   **Highlands:** Anorthosite, older, heavily cratered, thicker regolith layer.
*   **Regolith:** Fine-grained silty sands/sandy silts. Bulk density: 1.30 to 1.92 g/cm³.
*   **Agglutinates:** Fused particles of impact glass, rock, and mineral fragments. Content ranges from 5% to 65% by volume, increasing with soil maturity.

## Lunar Surface Construction Methods
### Concreting Methods
*   **Challenges:** Scarcity of calcium and water.
*   **Solutions:** Geopolymeric cements from aluminosilicate precursors; sulfur binders (troilite-derived); oxygen extraction for water production.

### Sintering Methods
*   **Process:** Fusing grains via heat (60% to 80% of melting temperature).
*   **Advantages:** No water required; works in vacuum.
*   **Technologies:** Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) shows promise for effective densification.

## Methodology: THAMES
The research utilizes the **Thermodynamic Hydration and Microstructure Evolution Simulator (THAMES)**. This relies on geochemical thermodynamic methods to predict mineralogical components and inform 3D microstructure algorithms to calculate engineering properties and assess deleterious product development.

## Conclusion
The ultimate intent is to develop an ASTM or ASCE approved standard for future ISRU activities. The research identifies the extent to which existing models can be adapted to evaluate regolith-based materials under lunar conditions to support NASA's return to the Moon "to stay."
