# Applying Terrestrial Geo-Material Science Methodology to Lunar ISRU Construction

**Authors:** H. Patel, P. Suermann, N. Shumaker, S. Deitrick, J. W. Bullard, and R. C. Ewing  
**Conference:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE  

## ABSTRACT
This research aims to establish methods for the design and testing of material mixtures derived from the lunar surface with minimal or no additives. It reviews opportunities and challenges of methods such as concreting and sintering, emphasizing the need for regolith simulants representative of the lunar highlands, particularly regarding mineralogical and agglutinated properties.

## INTRODUCTION
The development of construction methods using locally available (in-situ) materials is critical for building lunar infrastructure like landing pads, roads, and radiation shielding. A significant knowledge gap exists in measuring and predicting lunar geotechnical properties necessary for permanent occupation.

### Background: Lunar Surface Environment
The Moon's surface is covered in regolith, composed of dust and rock fragments created by meteorite bombardment and radiation. There are two major terrain classes:
*   **Mare:** ~15% of the surface, basaltic lava flows, relatively young.
*   **Highlands:** Composed of anorthosite, older, more cratered, with a thicker regolith layer and higher agglutinate content.

**Regolith Characteristics:**
*   **Particle Size:** ~95% by mass is <1.37 mm; ~50% is <50 μm.
*   **Bulk Density:** 1.30 g/cm³ to 1.92 g/cm³.
*   **Composition:** Varies from titanium-rich basalt (mare) to plagioclase feldspar-rich anorthosite (highlands).
*   **Agglutinates:** Fused particles of impact glass and minerals, comprising 5% to 65% by volume of the regolith.

## LUNAR SURFACE CONSTRUCTION METHODS

### Concreting Methods
Concrete can produce large volumes and complex shapes. However, lunar applications face two main challenges:
1.  **Scarcity of Calcium:** Can be overcome using pozzolanic or geo-polymeric cements (aluminosilicate precursors).
2.  **Scarcity of Water:** Traditional concrete requires ~20% liquid water by mass. Alternatives include extracting oxygen/hydrogen or using sulfur binders (sulfur is ~1% of regolith mass, melting point ~115°C).

### Sintering Methods
Sintering fuses grains using thermal energy (60-80% of melting temperature). 
*   **Pros:** Requires no water, works in a vacuum.
*   **Cons:** Requires fine powder; lunar regolith (μavg ≈ 70 μm) is coarser than typical sinterable powders.
*   **Advanced Techniques:** Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) uses pulse electric current and pressure to achieve high density in short times.

## METHODOLOGY
The research adapts the **Thermodynamic Hydration and Microstructure Evolution Simulator (THAMES)** to predict mineralogical components and 3D microstructures for lunar concrete and sintered regolith. This model helps calculate engineering properties like compressive strength and assesses the potential for deleterious product development.

### Materials and Processing Characterization
*   **Agglutinates:** New simulants (e.g., from Off Planet and Outward Technologies) include agglutinates to better mimic highland properties.
*   **Basalt Glass Fiber:** Regolith can be melted into fibers for structural reinforcement, potentially eliminating the need for imported polymer binders.

## CONCLUSION
The research objective is to adapt existing terrestrial models (like THAMES) to evaluate regolith-based materials under lunar conditions, ultimately aiming to develop ASTM or ASCE standards for future ISRU construction activities.
