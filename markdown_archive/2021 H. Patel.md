# Applying Terrestrial Geo-Material Science Methodology to Lunar ISRU Construction\n\n**Authors:** H. Patel; P. Suermann; N. Shumaker; S. Deitrick; J. W. Bullard; and R. C. Ewing\n**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, pp. 171-180\n\n## Abstract\nBuilding on research underway for military and expeditionary construction, this research aims to establish methods for the design and testing of the materials mixtures derived from the lunar surface with minimal or no additives that need to be transported from the Earth. This paper reviews opportunities and challenges of demonstrated and experimental construction methods such as concreting and sintering as well as the need to evaluate and develop these methods using regolith simulants more representative of the lunar highlands where the NASA Artemis mission proposes to land.\n\n## Introduction\nA pressing need exists to develop and test construction methods that use in-situ (locally available) materials for critical infrastructure. Building any exploratory base camp requires site preparation for horizontal (e.g., landing pads, roads) and vertical structures (e.g., berms, blast and radiation shielding). Generating building products from materials available in-situ is required for the next phase of exploration, as the scale of operations precludes the exclusive use of imported resources.\n\n## Background: Lunar Surface Environment\n### Terrain and Material\n- **Mare:** (~15% of surface) Composed of basaltic lava flows; relatively young; thinner regolith layer; higher concentration of boulders.\n- **Highlands:** Composed of anorthosite; much older; thicker regolith layer; higher percentage of agglutinates due to longer exposure to meteoritic bombardment.\n\n### Regolith Properties\n- **Composition:** 95% by mass is <1.37 mm; 50% is <50 um.\n- **Average Particle Size:** 72 um.\n- **Bulk Density:** 1.30 g/cm³ to 1.92 g/cm³.\n- **Types:** Mineral fragments, crystalline rock fragments, breccia fragments, glasses, and agglutinates.\n\n## Construction Methods and Considerations\n\n### Concreting Methods\nConcrete is a candidate because it produces large volumes with complex shapes using low-tech methods. However, lunar applications face challenges:\n1. **Scarcity of Calcium:** Can be overcome using pozzolanic or geo-polymeric cements.\n2. **Scarcity of Water:** Requires extraction of oxygen/hydrogen or alternative binders.\n3. **Alternative Binders:** Sulfur is a candidate (melting point ~115 °C), though it has poor durability under thermal cycling and is toxic in molten form.\n\n### Sintering Methods\nSintering fuses grains using thermal energy (60% to 80% of melting temperature). It requires no water and works in a vacuum. Challenges include the need for fine powder (regolith at 70 um is coarser than typical sinterable powders) and thermal transfer issues. Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) is noted as a promising technology for densifying lunar soil.\n\n## Methodology\nThe research team is developing microstructure models:\n- **THAMES (Thermodynamic Hydration and Microstructure Evolution Simulator):** Relies on geochemical thermodynamic methods to predict mineralogical components and simulate 3D virtual microstructures to calculate engineering properties.\n- **Simulants:** Emphasizes the need for highlands-specific simulants (with agglutinates) rather than just mare-based simulants like JSC-1A.\n\n## Key Materials for Construction\n- **Basalt Glass Fiber:** Created by melting and drawing lunar regolith. Can be used with polymer binders (from waste) or directly sintered to create structures.\n- **Agglutinates:** Their high volume in highlands (25-30 vol% on average) introduces unique chemical and physical properties affecting material mixtures.\n\n## Conclusion\nThe ultimate intent is to identify how existing terrestrial models and testing methods (like ASTM or ASCE standards) can be adapted for lunar ISRU activities to support NASA's Artemis missions.
