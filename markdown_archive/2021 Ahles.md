# Mechanical properties of meteoritic Fe–Ni alloys for in-situ extraterrestrial structures

**Authors:** Aaron A. Ahles, Jonathan D. Emery, David C. Dunand  
**Journal:** *Acta Astronautica* 189 (2021) 465–475  
**Available online:** 4 September 2021

## Abstract
A critical requirement for outposts and settlements on the Moon and Mars is the use of in-situ resources to create pressurized, human-rated habitats with low mass, high reliability, and easy processability. This study assesses the capability of binary Fe–Ni alloys—with Ni content of 5–11 wt%, typical of M-type asteroids and iron meteorites—to meet these requirements. Hardness and tensile properties were measured at 21 °C and lower temperatures. As Ni concentration increases, strength increases near-linearly. A pressurized habitat (Quonset hut) was modeled via finite element analysis. A 10 mm thick shell of Fe–7Ni alloy is sufficient for a 400 m³ habitat, requiring 12–31 metric tons of material, equivalent to 20–60 meteorites identified on Mars. This confirms the feasibility of using meteorites for low-energy metallic structures.

## 1. Introduction
ISRU is favored for long-term space development due to the high cost of transportation. While sintered regolith is weak in tension, iron-based alloys (steels) are candidates for pressurized habitats due to their tensile strength and toughness. Meteorites on Moon/Mars surfaces are already fully reduced to metallic states, avoiding energy-intensive reduction processes.

## 2. Experimental Study

### 2.1 Materials and Methods
Binary Fe-xNi alloys (x = 5, 7, 9, 11, 13 wt%) were arc-melted and cast into ingots. Tensile testing was conducted at 21 °C, and compressive yield stress was measured between −89 and 21 °C to match Lunar/Martian environments.

### 2.2 Results and Discussion
- **Microstructure:** Alloys showed prior γ-Fe grains (~90 μm) transformed to martensite upon cooling.
- **Mechanical Properties:** Hardness and strength increase linearly with Ni content, while ductility decreases.
- **Temperature Dependence:** Yield stress increases steeply with decreasing temperature, following an Arrhenius-like model.

## 3. Finite Element Modeling
A 400 m³ "Quonset hut" (5 m radius, 10 m length) was simulated under 1 atm internal pressure. The model used flat Fe–7Ni plates welded into a paneled arch.

### 4. Results and Discussion
- For shells ≥ 10 mm, no plastic deformation is observed in mid-plate regions.
- Stress concentrations at welds are 3–4 times higher than predicted by thin-wall cylinder equations.
- A 10 mm shell requires ~12 metric tons of alloy.

## 5. Conclusions
Fe–Ni meteorites can credibly be used via melting, casting, and welding to create pressurized habitats. The required mass is achievable using known meteorites identified on the Martian surface, offering a low-energy pathway for early settlement infrastructure.
