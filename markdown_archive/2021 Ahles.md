# Mechanical properties of meteoritic Fe–Ni alloys for in-situ extraterrestrial structures

**Authors:** Aaron A. Ahles, Jonathan D. Emery, David C. Dunand  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 189 (2021) 465–475  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.actaastro.2021.09.001

## Abstract
A critical requirement for outposts and settlements on the Moon and Mars is the use of in-situ resources to create pressurized, human-rated habitats. This study assesses the capability of binary Fe–Ni alloys (5–11 wt% Ni), typical of M-type asteroids and iron meteorites, to meet these requirements. Hardness and tensile properties were measured at 21 °C and between -89 and 21 °C. Results confirm the feasibility of using Fe-Ni meteorites—after collection, melting, and casting—to create metallic sheets for pressurized structures at low energy and infrastructure costs.

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial settlements require materials with high tensile strength and toughness for pressurized habitats. While sintered regolith or concrete are weak in tension, iron-based alloys (steels) are excellent candidates. Meteoritic Fe-Ni alloys are particularly attractive because they are already in a reduced metallic state, minimizing the energy required for processing compared to oxide ores.

## 3. Finite Element Modeling
A pressurized half-cylindrical habitat (Quonset hut) with a 5 m radius and 10 m length was modeled using COMSOL. The habitat was simulated under 1 bar internal pressure and covered with regolith for radiation shielding.

## 4. Conclusions
- Fe-Ni alloys with 5-11% Ni are well-suited for tensile load-bearing applications in space.
- A plate thickness of 10 mm is sufficient for a 400 m³ habitat to resist plastic deformation under 1 bar pressure.
- The required 12-31 MT of metal could be sourced from approximately 24-60 local meteorites of the size previously identified on the Martian surface.
