# Mechanical properties of meteoritic Fe–Ni alloys for in-situ extraterrestrial structures

**Authors:** Aaron A. Ahles, Jonathan D. Emery, David C. Dunand
**Publication:** Acta Astronautica 189 (2021) 465–475
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2021.09.001

## Abstract
A critical requirement for outposts and settlements on the Moon and Mars is the use of in-situ resources to create pressurized, human-rated habitats. This study assesses binary Fe–Ni alloys (5–11 wt% Ni) typical of M-type asteroids and iron meteorites. As Ni concentration increases, yield stress increases from 260 to 440 MPa while ductility decreases from 20 to 3%. Finite element analysis indicates a pressurized Quonset hut (5 m radius) on the Moon/Mars would require Fe–7Ni plates approximately 10 mm thick, translating to 12–31 metric tons of material.

## 1. Introduction
Iron-based alloys (steels) are excellent candidates for pressurized habitats due to high tensile strength and toughness. Meteoritic Fe-Ni alloys are advantageous for ISRU as they are already fully reduced to the metallic state, requiring only melting and casting rather than complex energy-intensive ore reduction.

## 2. Experimental Study
- **Materials:** Binary Fe-xNi (x = 5, 7, 9, 11, 13 wt%) arc-melted and cast into ingots.
- **Testing:** Vickers microhardness, tensile testing at 21 °C, and compressive yield stress between -89 and 21 °C.
- **Microstructure:** Martensitic phase observed upon rapid cooling; grain sizes ~90 μm for 5-11% Ni.

## 3. Finite Element Modeling of Habitats
A half-cylindrical 'Quonset hut' (5m radius, 10m length) was modeled under internal pressure (1 atm) and external regolith loading. 
- **Findings:** At 10 mm thickness, the Fe-7Ni shell remains below yield stress despite stress concentrations at welds. 
- **Mass:** 12-31 MT required, equivalent to 24–60 meteorites of ~500 kg each.

## 5. Conclusions
Fe–Ni alloys from meteorites are mechanically suitable for tensile load-bearing applications in space. The temperature-dependence of yield stress follows an Arrhenius model, showing significantly increased strength at low Martian/Lunar night temperatures.
