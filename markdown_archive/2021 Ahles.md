# Mechanical properties of meteoritic Fe–Ni alloys for in-situ extraterrestrial structures

**Authors:** Aaron A. Ahles, Jonathan D. Emery, David C. Dunand
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 189 (2021) 465–475
**Published:** 4 September 2021

## Abstract
A critical requirement for outposts and settlements on the Moon and Mars is the use of in-situ resources to create pressurized, human-rated habitats with low mass, high reliability, and easy processability. This study assesses the capability of binary Fe–Ni alloys—with Ni content of 5–11 wt%, typical of M-type asteroids and iron meteorites—to meet these requirements. The study measures hardness and tensile properties at 21°C and compressive yield stress between -89°C and 21°C. A pressurized habitat model (Quonset hut, 5m radius) is simulated via finite element analysis to determine necessary plate thickness. Results confirm the feasibility of using Fe–Ni meteorites after in-situ collection, melting, and casting to create metallic sheets for pressurized structures.

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial settlements require materials with high tensile strength and toughness. While sintered regolith is weak in tension, metallic alloys extracted from regolith or found in meteorites are candidates for pressurized thin shells. Fe-Ni alloys are highly available as asteroids or meteorites on the surfaces of the Moon and Mars. Unlike processing oxides from regolith, Fe–Ni meteorites are already in a metallic state, significantly reducing energy requirements for refinement.

## 2. Experimental Study

### 2.1 Materials and Methods
- **Alloys:** Binary Fe-xNi (x = 5, 7, 9, 11, 13 wt%).
- **Processing:** Arc-melted under 0.5 atm Ar, water-cooled hearth for fast solidification (representative of melted/cast meteorites).
- **Testing:** Vickers microhardness, tensile testing (ASTM E8), and compressive yield stress at temperatures relevant to Lunar and Martian environments (21, -3, -32, -89°C).

## 3. Finite Element Modeling of Habitats

### 3.1 Numerical Methods
- **Software:** COMSOL Multiphysics.
- **Design:** Quonset hut (half-cylinder), 5 m radius, 10 m length (~400 m³ volume).
- **Configuration:** 10 flat plates welded together, covered with regolith for radiation shielding.
- **Loads:** 101 kPa internal pressure, Lunar/Martian gravity.

### 3.2 Modeling Results
- **Thickness:** For Fe–7Ni plates, a thickness of 10 mm is sufficient to prevent plastic deformation under 1 bar pressure.
- **Mass:** A 10 mm shell requires ~12 MT of alloy, corresponding to ~24 meteorites of 500 kg each.
- **Safety Factor:** Thicker plates (20-25 mm) provide a safety factor of nearly 2 with respect to UTS.

## 4. Discussion
Meteorites offer a low-energy pathway to metal production compared to oxide reduction. While Mars has macroscopic meteorites, the Moon contains metallic grains in the regolith that can be magnetically separated. Fabrication could involve melting, casting into slabs, and hot-rolling into sheets, or using additive manufacturing (DED/wire-based) if powders or wires are produced.

## 5. Conclusions
Fe–Ni alloys with 5–11 wt% Ni are well-suited for load-bearing structures in space. Their strength increases at the low temperatures found on the Moon and Mars. A basic pressurized shell can be constructed from flat plates with modest infrastructure, achieving a break-even point in mass savings vs. shipping from Earth early in habitat development.
