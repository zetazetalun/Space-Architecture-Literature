# Mechanical properties of meteoritic Fe–Ni alloys for in-situ extraterrestrial structures

**Authors:** Aaron A. Ahles, Jonathan D. Emery, David C. Dunand
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 189 (2021) 465–475
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2021.09.001

## Abstract
A critical requirement for outposts and settlements on the Moon and Mars is the use of in-situ resources to create pressurized, human-rated habitats with low mass, high reliability, and easy processability. This study assesses the capability of binary Fe–Ni alloys - with Ni content of 5–11 wt%, typical of M-type asteroids and iron meteorites - to meet these requirements. The study measures hardness and tensile properties of these alloys in the as-cast state. Results show that as Ni concentration increases from 5 to 11 wt%, yield stress increases from 260 to 440 MPa and ultimate tensile strength from 340 to 560 MPa, while ductility decreases from 20 to 3%. A finite element model of a 400 m³ habitat suggests that 10 mm thick Fe-7Ni plates are sufficient to withstand 1 bar of internal pressure on the Moon or Mars.

## 1. Introduction
ISRU is favored for long-term space exploration due to the high cost of Earth-based transport. While regolith-based materials (concrete/sintered regolith) are weak in tension, metallic alloys (Fe-Ni) derived from meteorites offer high tensile strength and toughness. Meteorites are readily available on Mars and the Moon and exist in a metallic, pre-alloyed state, requiring only simple melting and casting for processing.

## 2. Experimental Study
Binary Fe-xNi alloys (x = 5, 7, 9, 11, 13 wt%) were arc-melted and tested in as-cast conditions. Tensile testing was performed at 21 °C, and compressive yield stress was measured between -89 and 21 °C.

## 3. Finite Element Modeling
A "Quonset hut" habitat (5m radius, 10m length) was modeled using COMSOL. The design used ten flat Fe-Ni plates welded together, covered by regolith for radiation shielding.
- **Internal Pressure:** 101 kPa (1 atm).
- **Critical Plate Thickness:** 10 mm for Fe-7Ni plates to prevent plastic deformation.
- **Total Mass:** 12–31 metric tons (MT), equivalent to 24–60 meteorites of 500 kg each.

## 4. Conclusions
Fe–Ni alloys from meteorites are credible raw materials for pressurized structures. Melting and casting into metallic sheets for welding into structures offers a low-infrastructure path for early space settlements compared to complex oxide reduction processes.
