# Mechanical properties of meteoritic Fe–Ni alloys for in-situ extraterrestrial structures

**Authors:** Aaron A. Ahles, Jonathan D. Emery, David C. Dunand  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 189 (2021) 465–475  
**Received:** 24 June 2021; **Accepted:** 1 September 2021; **Available online:** 4 September 2021

## Abstract
A critical requirement for outposts and settlements on the Moon and Mars is the use of in-situ resources to create pressurized, human-rated habitats with low mass (i.e., high tensile strength), high reliability (i.e., high toughness) and easy processability. This study assesses the capability of binary Fe–Ni alloys - with Ni content of 5–11 wt%, typical of M-type asteroids and iron meteorites - to meet these requirements. Hardness and tensile properties were measured at 21 °C in the as-cast state. As Ni concentration increases from 5 to 11 wt%, yield stress increases from 260 to 440 MPa, and ultimate tensile strength from 340 to 560 MPa. Yield stress increases steeply with decreasing temperature (relevant to Moon/Mars). A pressurized Quonset hut habitat (5 m radius, 10 m length) was modeled via finite element analysis. For a 1 bar atmosphere, Fe–7Ni plate thickness can be as low as 10 mm to prevent plastic deformation, corresponding to 20–30 iron-nickel meteorites of ~½ ton mass.

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial settlements require pressurized habitats made from materials with high tensile strength and toughness. While sintered regolith/concrete are proposed, they are weak in tension. Metallic alloys like Fe-Ni, abundant in meteorites on Mars and the Moon, offer superior tensile properties. NASA rovers have identified 15 metallic meteorites on Mars, such as the "Heat Shield Rock" (Fe-7 wt.% Ni). Utilizing these reduced metallic resources minimizes energy-intensive refinement needed for oxide-based ores.

## 2. Experimental Study

### 2.1. Materials and Methods
Binary Fe-xNi alloys (x = 5, 7, 9, 11, 13 wt%) were arc-melted and cast. Specimens were studied in the as-cast condition. Tensile testing was performed at 21 °C, and compressive yield stress was measured between −89 and 21 °C.

### 2.2. Results and Discussion

#### 2.2.1. Microstructure
As-cast alloys showed prior γ-Fe grains (~90 μm) transformed to martensite. Porosity (1-5 mm) was observed, which can be eliminated via thermomechanical processing like hot-rolling.

## 3. Finite element modeling of pressurized habitats
A half-cylindrical "Quonset hut" design (5 m radius, 10 m length, 400 m³ volume) was simulated using COMSOL. The model assumed a 1 bar internal pressure and regolith shielding (1.5 g/cm³ density).

- **Findings:** At 10 mm thickness, Fe–7Ni plates avoid plastic deformation in the mid-plate region (Mises stresses ~120 MPa). Stress concentrations occur at weld joints (ROI 2). For 5 mm shells, Mises stresses at joints exceed 360 MPa, leading to plastic deformation.
- **Mass Estimates:** A 10-25 mm thick shell requires 12–31 metric tons (MT) of Fe-Ni alloy.

## 5. Conclusions
Fe–Ni alloys found in meteorites are suitable for tensile load-bearing structures in space. 20–30 half-ton meteorites could provide enough material for a 20-person habitat shell. In-situ melting, casting, and welding provide a low-infrastructure pathway for early colonization compared to shipping all materials from Earth.
