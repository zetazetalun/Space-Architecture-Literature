# SPACE RESEARCH AND EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION INDUSTRY

**Authors:** Yusuf Cengiz TOKLU (Okan University), Ali Erdem ÇERÇEVIK (Bilecik Seyh Edebali University)

## Abstract
Advance of civilization has been realized through the ages by exploration of unknown parts of the Earth and by exploiting new resources thus acquired. There are currently practically no new areas on this planet to be explored and exploited. As a natural continuation of this process, humans will now explore and exploit new areas beyond Earth, i.e. in space. Habitation on these new space bodies will firstly necessitate construction of outposts. Thus construction on the Moon, Mars, and other space bodies can be considered an important component of space research. This article presents an overview of space research, innovations awaited in the space construction industry, and the importance of feedback to the construction industry in general.

## I. Introduction
Human history is a loop of widening environmental areas to meet new "natures" and resources, which in turn increases technological and economic capabilities (Figure 1). This process of globalization is now leading toward the conquest of extraterrestrial places.

## II. Spatialization - World 2050
Following Tsiolkovsky’s sentiment that "Earth is the cradle of mankind," humans are projected to reach Mars by the 2050s. By 2100, the human "world" will likely encompass Earth, Moon, Mars, and their surrounding space.

## III. Pioneers of Going to Moon and Mars
While many countries have space programs, only a few have launch capabilities. It is expected that permanent settlements or long-term outposts will be established on the Moon and Mars within the next four decades, facilitated by both governmental and private organizations.

## IV. Humans on Space Bodies
Space bodies present hostile environments. Living there and exploiting resources will significantly enhance human capabilities. Feedback from space research has already produced over 30,000 innovations benefiting terrestrial life.

## V. Habitation / Construction on Space Bodies
Construction is vital for founding outposts. Due to the high cost of transport (approx. $20,000 per kg to the Moon), maximizing the usage of in-situ materials (ISRU) is mandatory.

Initial uses of in-situ materials include regolith coverage for radiation protection, reinforced regolith walls, and gabions filled with in-situ dust.

### B. A better understanding of structures
Unlike Earth, where gravity is the primary load, space structures must prioritize internal pressure management. New structural directions include:
- Constructions in lava tubes
- Habitats embedded in regolith
- Tensegric structures
- Inflatable structures
- Extensive use of prestressing

### C. Automated construction
Due to the dangers of manual labor, automated mining, earthwork, and construction techniques will be advanced. The space construction industry will be fully automated.

### D. Special construction machines
Machines will be electrically operated, fully automatic or tele-commanded, and designed to operate in low gravity and high-dust environments.

### E. Nonlinearity
Designers will exploit materials to their maximum limits, necessitating the inclusion of nonlinear effects in structural analysis due to high material costs.

### F. Building Information Systems
Structures will be "smart" or mechatronic systems, with sensors for pressure, temperature, CO2, and strain, managed by a central system to optimize resource use and safety.

### G. Waste treatment
Advanced systems will recycle all liquid, solid, and gaseous wastes for repeated use.

## Conclusions
Civil engineering must adapt to the "new Nature" of space bodies. This transition from globalization to "spatialization" marks the next great step in human history.
