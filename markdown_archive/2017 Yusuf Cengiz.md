# SPACE RESEARCH AND EXTRATERRESTRIAL CONSTRUCTION INDUSTRY

**Authors:** Yusuf Cengiz TOKLU (Okan University), Ali Erdem ÇERÇEVIK (Bilecik Seyh Edebali University)

**Abstract**— Advance of civilization has been realized through the ages by exploration of unknown parts of the Earth and by exploiting new resources thus acquired. There are currently practically no new areas on this planet to be explored and exploited. This is achieved by the completion of the period called globalization. As a natural continuation of this process, humans will now explore and exploit new areas beyond Earth, i.e. in space. Moon has already been explored partly, it awaits a complete exploration and colonization. The next step will be Mars and beyond. Habitation on these new space bodies will firstly necessitate construction of outposts on these bodies. Thus construction on the Moon, Mars, and other space bodies can be considered to be an important component of space research. In the present article, an overview of space research is presented together with the innovations awaited in space construction industry and the importance of feedback of these studies to construction industry in general.

**Keywords**—Space, Earth, Moon, Mars Civil Engineering.

## I. INTRODUCTION
The history of human expansion is described as a continuous loop of exploring new environments, exploiting resources, and advancing technology/economy (Fig. 1). This process has led to globalization on Earth and is now extending to extraterrestrial bodies.

## II. SPATIALIZATION - WORLD 2050
Mankind's reach has expanded from Earth's surface to higher altitudes and eventually to the Moon (400,171 km in 1970). Projections suggest that by 2050, human activity will extend to Mars (over 200 million km).

## III. PIONEERS OF GOING TO MOON AND MARS
Currently, only three nations (US, Russia, China) have sent humans to space. It is expected that manned missions to the Moon will increase by 2030, and Mars missions will commence around 2050, involving both national programs and private organizations.

## IV. HUMANS ON SPACE BODIES
Space environments are hostile, requiring special measures for survival. The feedback from space research (over 30,000 innovations) significantly benefits terrestrial technology.

## V. HABITATION / CONSTRUCTION ON SPACE BODIES
Construction is a multi-dimensional task. Transporting 1 kg of material to the Moon costs ~20,000 USD, necessitating the use of in-situ materials.

Initial ISRU uses include:
- Regolith coverage for radiation protection.
- Reinforced regolith walls.
- Gabions filled with in-situ dust.

### B. Better understanding of structures
Structural design must account for internal pressure, lack of cracks, and different loading conditions. Future designs include:
- Lava tube constructions.
- Regolith-embedded habitats.
- Tensegric and inflatable structures.
- Prestressing and lack of stress concentrations.

### C. Automated construction
Due to hazards, automated mining, earthwork, and construction techniques will be essential. Construction will be fully automated.

### D. Special construction machines
Machines must be electrically operated, telecommanded, lightweight, and dust-resistant.

### E. Nonlinearity
Space designers will exploit materials to their maximum limits, requiring nonlinear analysis due to high supply costs.

### F. Building Information Systems
Structures will be "smart" mechatronic systems managed by central systems with extensive sensor networks (BIM equivalent).

### G. Waste treatment
Advanced closed-loop systems for recycling liquid, solid, and gaseous wastes.

## CONCLUSIONS
Civil engineering will continue to adapt to new environments, marking the transition from globalization to spatialization.
