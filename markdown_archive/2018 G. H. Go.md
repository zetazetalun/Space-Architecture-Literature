# Soil Mechanics in Vacuum Chamber

**Authors:** G. H. Go, J. Lee, H. S. Shin, B. H. Rhu, and H. W. Jin  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE

## ABSTRACT
The dust vacuum chamber is an essential infrastructure for the verification of space exploration equipment, especially extraterrestrial missions for in situ resource utilization (ISRU). This study presents a new approach in soil mechanics that is particularly applicable to the dust vacuum chamber. One of the main issues in operating the dust vacuum chamber is soil disturbance problems during the evacuation. Thus, the appropriate vacuum rate was suggested to minimize the soil disturbance in the dust vacuum chamber using theoretical and numerical approaches. Firstly, the simple analytical solutions were used to provide approximate maximum discharge velocity and evacuation time to finish air flow through a soil specimen at a given vacuum pressure. Then, a two dimensional finite element model was developed which simulates the hydro-mechanical behavior of the soil specimen in a vacuum chamber with respect to different vacuum rates. From the numerical analysis results, optimum guidelines have been proposed to ensure that soil disturbance is minimized.

## INTRODUCTION
Recently, the trend in space exploration has been shifting from a remote exploration using orbiters to a surface mission using rover and lander. For the verification of the equipment used in the lunar surface mission, thorough performance tests in dirty vacuum chamber (DVC) that simulates lunar surface conditions are required. One of the main issues in operating the dirty vacuum chamber is soil disturbance problems during the evacuation. The soil disturbance caused by the depressurization causes difficulties in maintaining the initial condition of the soil bin.

## POROELASTICITY THEORY
During depletion of air pressure in the soil specimen, the stress is altered due to the change in pore air pressure. This study applied poroelasticity theory proposed by Biot (1941) to evaluate the behavior of the soil specimen. The constitutive equation governing the poroelastic behavior relates to stress, strain, and pore air pressure:

$$\sigma = C\epsilon - \alpha_B u_a I$$

Where:
- $\sigma$ is total stress
- $\epsilon$ is the strain tensor
- $u_a$ is the air pressure in the pore
- $\alpha_B$ is the Biot-Willis coefficient
- $C$ is the elastic matrix

The air velocity $q_a$ is calculated by Darcy’s law:

$$q_a = -\frac{K}{\mu} \frac{\partial u_a}{\partial y}$$

Where $K$ is the intrinsic permeability and $\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity. Soil disturbance starts when the effective stress becomes zero due to seepage stress (i.e., when seepage force exceeds the soil’s dead load).

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
As the absolute pressure approaches the state of vacuum, the air velocity increases rapidly due to an exponential increase in the pressure gradient, inducing high drag force inside the soil specimen. The calculated maximum air velocity ($q_{max}$) causing disturbance was approximately 0.000235 m/s. Results indicate that the vacuum rate must be reduced before the air flow reaches this limit to maintain soil stability.

## SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION
1. The new theoretical approach provides insight into the hydro-mechanical behavior of soil in DVC during depressurization.
2. Finite element models can be used to determine appropriate vacuum rates.
3. Vacuum rates should be controlled adequately (slowed down) as the pressure reaches a maximum allowable value (e.g., 2.5 Torr) to maintain the initial specimen condition.
