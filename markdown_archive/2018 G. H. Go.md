# Soil Mechanics in Vacuum Chamber

**Authors:** G. H. Go, J. Lee, H. S. Shin, B. H. Rhu, and H. W. Jin
**Conference:** Earth and Space 2018 (ASCE), pp. 804-809

## Abstract
The dust vacuum chamber (DVC) is essential for verifying space exploration equipment, especially for In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) missions. This study addresses soil disturbance issues during the evacuation process in a DVC. Using theoretical poroelasticity and a 2D finite element model, the research suggests optimal vacuum rates to minimize disturbance caused by seepage forces during depressurization.

## Introduction
Recent trends in space exploration have shifted toward surface missions using rovers and landers (e.g., NASA's LCROSS mission). Testing equipment for lunar or Martian surface missions requires a "Dirty Vacuum Chamber" (DVC) that simulates these environments. However, the process of depressurizing the chamber can cause soil disturbance (e.g., seepage stress exceeding the soil's dead load), which compromises the initial condition of the soil specimen.

## Poroelasticity Theory
The study applies Biot’s (1941) poroelasticity theory to evaluate the hydro-mechanical behavior of soil under depressurization.
- **Constitutive Equation:** $\sigma = C\epsilon - \alpha_B u_a I$
- **Continuity Equation for Flow:** $\rho_a S \frac{\partial u_a}{\partial t} + \nabla(\rho_a q_a) = -\rho_a \alpha_B \frac{\partial \epsilon_{vol}}{\partial t}$
- **Seepage Stress:** Soil disturbance starts when effective stress becomes zero due to seepage stress ($j \geq W_d$).

## Results and Discussion
- As absolute pressure approaches vacuum, air velocity increases rapidly due to exponential pressure gradients, inducing high drag forces.
- **Critical Velocity:** For the tested specimen, the maximum air velocity ($q_{max}$) causing disturbance was calculated at 0.000235 m/s.
- **Control Strategy:** By reducing the vacuum rate (e.g., from -0.33 kPa/sec to -0.11 kPa/sec) before the air flow reaches the maximum velocity limit, soil disturbance can be minimized. No disruptions are typically observed at pressures below 2.5 Torr.

## Conclusions
1. A new theoretical approach provides insight into soil hydro-mechanical behavior during depressurization.
2. Finite element modeling can determine appropriate vacuum rates to prevent soil simulant disruption.
3. Controlling the vacuum rate is essential to maintaining the initial conditions required for high-fidelity ISRU equipment testing.
