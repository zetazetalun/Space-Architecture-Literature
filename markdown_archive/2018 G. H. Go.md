## Abstract
The dust vacuum chamber (DVC) is essential for verifying space exploration equipment, especially for In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) missions. This study investigates soil disturbance during chamber evacuation using poroelasticity theory and numerical models to suggest appropriate vacuum rates.

## Introduction
Testing equipment for lunar and Martian surface missions requires high-fidelity simulations of environmental conditions. One major challenge is soil disturbance caused by depressurization in vacuum chambers, which alters the initial condition of soil simulants like JSC-1A.

## Poroelasticity Theory and Soil Disturbance
Biot's poroelasticity theory was used to model the interaction between pore air pressure and soil stress. 
- **Soil Disturbance Threshold:** Occurs when effective stress becomes zero due to seepage stress. This happens when the seepage force exceeds the soil's dead load ($j = W_d$).
- **Critical Air Velocity ($q_{max}$):** The maximum discharge velocity limit can be calculated based on intrinsic permeability ($K$), particle density ($ho_d$), and dynamic viscosity ($\mu$).

## Results and Discussion
Numerical analysis using a 2D finite element model showed that air velocity increases exponentially as the chamber approaches vacuum. 
- **Calculated Threshold:** For the given soil properties, $q_{max}$ was approximately 0.000235 m/s.
- **Timing:** At a vacuum rate of -0.33 kPa/sec, disturbance occurred 282.5 seconds after depressurization started.
- **Mitigation:** Reducing the vacuum rate to -0.11 kPa/sec before reaching the critical velocity limit prevents soil disturbance and maintains specimen integrity.

## Conclusion
The study provides a guideline for vacuum pump operation in DVCs: the pump rate must be adequately controlled (reduced) to prevent seepage-induced soil failure until the pressure drops below approximately 2.5 Torr, where disruptions are no longer observed regardless of the rate.
