# Soil Mechanics in Vacuum Chamber

**Authors:** G. H. Go, J. Lee, H. S. Shin, B. H. Rhu, and H. W. Jin  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE  
**Pages:** 804–809

## ABSTRACT
The dust vacuum chamber is an essential infrastructure for the verification of space exploration equipment, especially extraterrestrial missions for in situ resource utilization (ISRU). This study presents a new approach in soil mechanics that is particularly applicable to the dust vacuum chamber. One of the main issues in operating the dust vacuum chamber is soil disturbance problems during the evacuation. Thus, the appropriate vacuum rate was suggested to minimize the soil disturbance in the dust vacuum chamber using theoretical and numerical approaches. Firstly, the simple analytical solutions were used to provide approximate maximum discharge velocity and evacuation time to finish air flow through a soil specimen at a given vacuum pressure. Then, a two dimensional finite element model was developed which simulates the hydro-mechanical behavior of the soil specimen in a vacuum chamber with respect to different vacuum rates. From the numerical analysis results, optimum guidelines have been proposed to ensure that soil disturbance is minimized.

## INTRODUCTION
Recently, the trend in space exploration has been shifting from remote exploration using orbiters to surface missions using rovers and landers. Following the discovery of water ice by NASA’s LCROSS mission (2010), studies on lunar surface exploration technology have expanded. Performance tests in a Dirty Vacuum Chamber (DVC) that simulates lunar surface conditions (including regolith simulants like JSC-1A) are required for equipment verification.

A primary challenge in DVC operation is soil disturbance caused by depressurization, which makes maintaining the initial condition of the soil bin difficult. This study suggests a new operating guideline to minimize such disturbances through theoretical solutions and finite element analysis.

## POROELASTICITY THEORY
Biot's poroelasticity theory (1941) was applied to evaluate the hydro-mechanical behavior of the soil specimen. The constitutive equation relates stress, strain, and pore air pressure:

$$\sigma = C\epsilon - \alpha_B u_a I$$ (1)

Where:
- $\sigma$ is total stress
- $\epsilon$ is strain tensor
- $u_a$ is pore air pressure
- $\alpha_B$ is the Biot-Willis coefficient (typically 1 for soft soils)

The soil disturbance starts when the effective stress becomes zero due to seepage stress. The maximum air velocity limit ($q_{max}$) occurs when the seepage force exceeds the soil's dead load ($W_d$):

$$j = W_d$$
$$\rho_a \cdot g \cdot \frac{q_{max}}{k} \cdot h = \rho_d \cdot g \cdot h$$ (4)

## FINITE ELEMENT MODEL
A 2D finite element model coupled fluid flow and geomechanics for a soil specimen with a depth of 0.047m. Initial pressure was set to atmospheric pressure (101.3 kPa). Depressurization proceeded from the top, while other boundaries were undrained.

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Numerical analysis showed that as absolute pressure approaches vacuum, air velocity increases rapidly, inducing high drag forces inside the soil specimen. The calculated $q_{max}$ causing soil disturbance was approximately 0.000235 m/s. For a vacuum rate of -0.33 kPa/sec, disturbance occurred at 282.5 seconds (at -94 kPa).

To prevent disturbance, the vacuum rate must be controlled adequately before air flow reaches the maximum velocity limit. Reducing the rate from -0.33 kPa/sec to -0.11 kPa/sec effectively drops air velocity, maintaining the soil's initial condition until pressure reaches approximately 2.5 Torr.

## CONCLUSIONS
1. A new theoretical approach provided insight into the hydro-mechanical behavior of soil in DVCs.
2. The finite element model helps determine appropriate vacuum rates to prevent soil disturbance.
3. Vacuum rates should be reduced before reaching critical seepage velocities to maintain the integrity of the soil specimen.
