# Soil Mechanics in Vacuum Chamber

**Authors:** G. H. Go, J. Lee, H. S. Shin, B. H. Rhu, and H. W. Jin
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, pp. 804-809

## Abstract
The dust vacuum chamber is an essential infrastructure for the verification of space exploration equipment, especially extraterrestrial missions for in situ resource utilization (ISRU). This study presents a new approach in soil mechanics that is particularly applicable to the dust vacuum chamber. One of the main issues in operating the dust vacuum chamber is soil disturbance problems during the evacuation. Thus, the appropriate vacuum rate was suggested to minimize the soil disturbance in the dust vacuum chamber using theoretical and numerical approaches. Firstly, simple analytical solutions were used to provide approximate maximum discharge velocity and evacuation time to finish air flow through a soil specimen at a given vacuum pressure. Then, a two dimensional finite element model was developed which simulates the hydro-mechanical behavior of the soil specimen in a vacuum chamber with respect to different vacuum rates. From the numerical analysis results, optimum guidelines have been proposed to ensure that soil disturbance is minimized.

## Introduction
Recently, the trend in space exploration has been shifting from remote exploration using orbiters to surface missions using rovers and landers. Developing and verifying exploration equipment in a dirty vacuum chamber (DVC) that simulates lunar surface conditions is required. A major issue in operating a DVC is soil disturbance caused by depressurization, which makes maintaining the initial condition of soil specimens difficult.

## Poroelasticity Theory
Biot’s (1941) poroelasticity theory was applied to evaluate the hydro-mechanical behavior of soil under depressurization. The constitutive equation relates stress, strain, and pore air pressure:

$$\sigma = C\epsilon - \alpha_B u_a I$$

Where:
- $\sigma$ is total stress
- $\epsilon$ is the strain tensor
- $u_a$ is the air pressure in the pore
- $\alpha_B$ is the Biot-Willis coefficient

Soil disturbance begins when the effective stress becomes zero due to seepage stress. The maximum air velocity limit occurs when the seepage force exceeds the soil's dead load.

## Results and Discussion
Numerical analysis showed that as absolute pressure approaches vacuum, air velocity increases rapidly, inducing high drag forces. Based on the theoretical approach, the calculated maximum air velocity ($q_{max}$) causing soil disturbance was approximately 0.000235 m/s. This velocity was reached 282.5 seconds after starting depressurization at a rate of -0.33 kPa/sec. By reducing the vacuum rate before reaching this limit, soil disturbance can be successfully minimized.

## Summary and Conclusion
1. A new theoretical approach provided insight into the hydro-mechanical behavior of soil in DVCs.
2. Finite element models can determine appropriate vacuum rates during depressurization.
3. Vacuum rates must be controlled adequately to maintain the initial soil specimen conditions until a maximum allowable pressure (e.g., 2.5 Torr) is reached.
