# Soil Mechanics in Vacuum Chamber

**Authors:** G. H. Go, J. Lee, H. S. Shin, B. H. Rhu, and H. W. Jin
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018 (ASCE), pp. 804-809

## Abstract
The dust vacuum chamber is an essential infrastructure for the verification of space exploration equipment, especially extraterrestrial missions for in situ resource utilization (ISRU). This study presents a new approach in soil mechanics that is particularly applicable to the dust vacuum chamber. One of the main issues in operating the dust vacuum chamber is soil disturbance problems during the evacuation. Thus, the appropriate vacuum rate was suggested to minimize the soil disturbance in the dust vacuum chamber using theoretical and numerical approaches.

## Introduction
Verification of equipment used in lunar surface missions requires thorough performance tests in a dirty vacuum chamber (DVC) that simulates lunar surface conditions. A major issue is soil disturbance caused by depressurization, which complicates maintaining the initial condition of soil bins (e.g., JSC-1A lunar soil simulant).

## Poroelasticity Theory
Biot's poroelasticity theory (1941) was applied to evaluate the hydro-mechanical behavior. The constitutive equation relates stress, strain, and pore air pressure:
$$\sigma = C\epsilon - \alpha_B u_a I$$
Soil disturbance begins when the effective stress becomes zero due to seepage stress, i.e., when the seepage force exceeds the soil’s dead load.

## Results and Discussion
Numerical analysis showed that as absolute pressure approaches vacuum, air velocity increases rapidly due to exponential pressure gradients, inducing high drag force. 

*   **Critical Threshold:** The maximum air velocity ($q_{max}$) causing soil disturbance was calculated as approximately 0.000235 m/s.
*   **Time to Disturbance:** At a vacuum rate of -0.33 kPa/sec, disturbance occurs 282.5 seconds after depressurization begins.
*   **Mitigation Strategy:** Reducing the vacuum rate (e.g., from -0.33 kPa/sec to -0.11 kPa/sec) 10 seconds before reaching $q_{max}$ effectively prevents soil disruption.

## Summary and Conclusion
1. A new theoretical approach provides insight into the hydro-mechanical behavior of soil in DVCs.
2. The finite element model can determine appropriate vacuum rates to prevent soil disturbance.
3. Maintaining the initial condition of the soil specimen requires controlling the vacuum rate before reaching critical velocity thresholds.
