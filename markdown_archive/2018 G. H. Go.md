## Abstract
The dust vacuum chamber is an essential infrastructure for the verification of space exploration equipment, especially extraterrestrial missions for in situ resource utilization (ISRU). This study presents a new approach in soil mechanics that is particularly applicable to the dust vacuum chamber. One of the main issues in operating the dust vacuum chamber is soil disturbance problems during the evacuation. Thus, the appropriate vacuum rate was suggested to minimize the soil disturbance in the dust vacuum chamber using theoretical and numerical approaches. From the numerical analysis results, optimum guidelines have been proposed to ensure that soil disturbance is minimized.

## Introduction
Developing and verifying exploration equipment for lunar surface missions requires thorough performance tests in a dirty vacuum chamber (DVC) that simulates lunar surface conditions. A major issue is soil disturbance caused by depressurization, which makes maintaining the initial condition of the soil bin difficult. This study suggests a new operating guideline to minimize soil disturbance through theoretical solutions and finite element analyses.

## Poroelasticity Theory
Biot's (1941) poroelasticity theory was applied to evaluate the hydro-mechanical behavior of soil under depressurization. The constitutive equation relates stress, strain, and pore air pressure:

$$\sigma = C\epsilon - \alpha_B u_a I$$

Where:
- $\sigma$ is total stress
- $\epsilon$ is strain tensor
- $u_a$ is air pressure in the pore
- $\alpha_B$ is the Biot-Willis coefficient

Soil disturbance begins when the effective stress becomes zero due to seepage stress—the moment when seepage force exceeds the soil's dead load.

## Results and Discussion
As the absolute pressure approaches vacuum, air velocity increases rapidly due to an exponential pressure gradient, inducing high drag force inside the soil specimen. Theoretical calculations showed that for the given properties, soil disturbance occurs approximately 282.5 seconds after depressurization begins (at a vacuum rate of -0.33 kPa/sec). The study suggests reducing the vacuum rate before reaching the maximum velocity limit ($q_{max} \approx 0.000235$ m/s) to maintain the initial soil condition.

## Summary and Conclusion
1. A new theoretical approach provided insight into hydro-mechanical soil behavior in DVCs.
2. Finite element models can determine appropriate vacuum rates to prevent disturbance.
3. Vacuum rates must be controlled adequately until the pressure reaches a maximum allowable value (approx. 2.5 Torr) to ensure soil integrity for equipment testing.
