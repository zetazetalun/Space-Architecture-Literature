# Bearing capacity of lunar soil ground under extraterrestrial environmental effects

## Abstract
Lunar environment features a low gravity field and ultra-high vacuum which shows significant effects on the bearing behavior of lunar soil ground for future lunar habitation. Therefore, the 2D Discrete Element Method was employed to simulate the plate load tests under different gravity fields in non-vacuum and high-vacuum environments. The results show that a bilinear line bounded by 1g can be employed to describe the gravity effect on the gravity factors for ultimate bearing capacity and coefficient of subgrade reaction. The slopes are different in low and high-gravity fields, indicating that centrifuge data cannot be directly applied to predict lunar soil behavior. The affected area becomes deeper and wider with decreasing gravity in high-vacuum environments.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar habitation is central to future space exploration (Artemis, Moon Village). Accurate understanding of lunar soil-tool interaction is vital for designing foundations. The Moon's low gravity affects initial stress, while the high vacuum increases internal friction and cohesion via van der Waals forces.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### 3.1 Load-settlement behavior
Load increases rapidly to a peak at roughly 2.5 mm settlement. Under higher gravity fields, load increases faster due to larger additional soil surcharge. In high-vacuum environments, soil exhibits higher resistance due to inter-particle forces.

### 3.2 Ultimate bearing capacity (qu)
The ultimate bearing capacity increases non-linearly with the gravity field. Soil in vacuum shows higher ultimate bearing capacity compared to air conditions.

### 3.3 Coefficient of subgrade reaction (K)
K increases with gravity level. Coefficients in high-vacuum are larger than non-vacuum under the same gravity level due to higher deformation modulus.

## 4. Concluding Remarks
1. A bilinear model bounded by 1g describes gravity effects on bearing capacity factors.
2. High-gravity centrifuge testing is not a direct proxy for low-gravity lunar behavior due to differing slopes.
3. The affected soil zone becomes deeper and wider with decreasing gravity in high-vacuum conditions.
4. Lunar soil in low gravity can support larger masses than terrestrial soil because the gravity level reduction affects the base weight more than the bearing capacity loss.
