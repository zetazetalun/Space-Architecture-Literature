## Abstract
Lunar environment features a low gravity field and ultra-high vacuum which shows significant effects on the bearing behavior of lunar soil ground for future lunar habitation. Therefore, the 2D Discrete Element Method was employed to simulate the plate load tests under different gravity fields in non-vacuum (i.e., excluding the van der Waals force) and high-vacuum (i.e., including the van der Waals force) environments. Results show that a bilinear line bounded by 1g can describe the gravity effect on the gravity factors for ultimate bearing capacity and coefficient of subgrade reaction. The slopes are different in low and high-gravity fields, indicating that high-gravity effects in a centrifuge cannot be directly applied to predict bearing behavior in low gravity. 

## 1. Introduction
Lunar habitation requires an accurate understanding of lunar soil-tool interaction, which plays an important role in the design and construction of future settlements. The lunar environment's low gravity and ultra-high vacuum (causing van der Waals forces between particles) significantly impact the bearing behavior of lunar soil, necessitating full investigation to ensure foundations are safe and economical.

### Formulations
The rolling resistance $M$ and van der Waals force $F_v$ are formulated as:
$M = \min(K_n \beta^2 r^2 \theta / 12, K_n u_n \beta r / 6)$
$F_v = A \beta r / (6 \pi D^3)$
Where $A$ is the Hamaker constant ($4.3 \times 10^{-20}$ J) and $D$ is the thickness of adsorbed gas molecules.

## 6. Concluding Remarks
1. A bilinear line bounded by 1g describes gravity factors for ultimate bearing capacity ($q_u$). The slope in low-gravity fields is higher than in high-gravity fields.
2. Particle velocity fields show that soil moves downwards more easily in lower gravity and non-vacuum environments.
3. Affected areas become deeper and narrower with decreasing gravity in non-vacuum environments, but deeper and wider in high-vacuum environments.
