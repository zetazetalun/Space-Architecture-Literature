# Building Information Modeling (BIM) Workflows for Construction Sequencing & 4D-Planning of 3D-Printed ISRU Surface Habitats (Abstract 715110)

**Authors:** M. Yashar, P. Michealsen, B. Hammond, J. Alvizar, C. Ciardullo, M. Morris, R. Pailes-Friedman, G. Bell, R. Tucker, T. Shen, S. Austin, L. LeBlanc
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## ABSTRACT
Future autonomously constructed surface habitats on the Moon and Mars will leverage the versatility of additive manufacturing technologies to create airtight and durable structures which protect the crew from the harsh extraterrestrial environment. BIM workflows present significant value in the design and engineering of many space systems but particularly so for habitat facilities—which will include pre-integrated hardware components such as ECLS, airlocks, suitports, as well as hygiene modules. Synergies between BIM and construction management softwares with robotic control and software systems for emplacement and for additive manufacturing are yet to be realized—however there is no question that this research will become a necessary milestone in the development of autonomously constructed habitat systems. In particular, 4D-simulation in BIM software lends tremendous value to mission planning and mission architectures which conceive the autonomous robotic construction of a habitat system. In 2019 team SEArch+/Apis Cor won first place in Virtual Construction (BIM) Level 2 (100% Design) within NASA’s Phase 3 Challenge for a 3D-Printed Habitat on Mars, which was judged on criteria such as: BIM use functionality, Completeness of 3D model according to distinct levels of development for retaining walls, MEP, and ECLSS, Realism of 4D Model, among others. This paper will define the landscape of BIM use and simulation as it will apply to the future robotic deployment of space habitats, followed by a description of the project’s contribution to BIM workflows for the design of an ISRU surface habitat.

## INTRODUCTION
Future long duration exploration missions will benefit from robotic precursor missions which autonomously construct surface infrastructure and habitat systems prior to a crew’s arrival. For autonomously constructed habitats to successfully deploy on the Moon and Mars, the construction sequence must consider: required site conditions relevant to the foundation system, quantities of raw materials required at the site for ISRU collection, feedstock storage and access to the mixing apparatus for 3D-printing, access to power sources, as well as many other environmental and in-situ based resources necessary for autonomous deployment and construction of the habitat.

### Challenges of BIM-Integrated Frameworks for Automated Construction
While BIM has been largely adopted in the AEC (Architecture, Engineering, and Construction) industry, its application to robotics and automation remains an ongoing challenge. BIM within AEC has been traditionally conceived as a tool for project planning and scheduling rather than an integrated framework for the coordination of automated construction processes. Information may be shared between BIM models and numerical control outputs, but data acquisition and organization are yet to be streamlined for autonomous construction.

## BIM for Habitat Design
Integration between BIM and robotics is often achieved using IFC Schema for the BIM model and geometry within an STL file for 3D printing. The integration of CAD and BIM softwares with robotic tools such as Robot Operating System (ROS) could represent the next step in realizing automated construction processes. 

### System Architecture Logistics for Surface Site Infrastructure on Mars
Before 3D-printing is deployed, site establishment will rely on construction machinery for excavation, levelling, grading, and preparation of the terrain. Robots will assist in scouting and surveying build and excavation sites, in addition to prospecting and processing raw materials such as regolith.

## NASA’s Centennial Challenge for a 3D-Printed Habitat on Mars
The Phase 3 3D-Printed Habitat Competition was subdivided into multiple Virtual Design and Construction submission levels. SEArch+ / Apis Cor’s participation enabled the team to advance the applicability of BIM to large-scale additive manufacturing projects in space.

### Virtual Construction Levels
Teams were asked to create BIM models of autonomously constructed habitat structures and provide detailed information on materials, design, and construction sequencing. 

**Mars X-House Requirements:**
- Pressure-retaining living space of at least 93 m².
- Support for four astronauts for one year.
- Incorporation of a suit hatch, view port, equipment/rover hatch, and combined communications-power-instrumentation penetrations.
- Level of Development (LOD): MEP and ECLSS at LOD 200; structural and pressure retaining walls at LOD 400.

## Software Workflow
- **Cloud Platform:** Autodesk BIM 360 (real-time collaboration).
- **Parametric Modeling:** Rhino and Grasshopper (regolith structural shell).
- **Native Design:** Autodesk Revit (interior design and MEP).
- **4D Simulation:** Autodesk Navisworks (assigning construction durations and sequences).

## Conclusion
The deployment, positioning, and manipulation of hard-shell sub-systems will need to be autonomously executed by multiple mobile robots. The timing and scheduling of emplaced habitat elements must be closely correlated with 3D-printing technology through a unified construction sequence or 4D BIM plan. Testing and validation of pressurized 3D-printed structures remains a challenge to be undertaken in large-scale Earth-based prototypes.
