# Building Information Modeling (BIM) Workflows for Construction Sequencing & 4D-Planning of 3D-Printed ISRU Surface Habitats

**Authors:** M. Yashar, P. Michealsen, B. Hammond, J. Alvizar, C. Ciardullo, M. Morris, R. Pailes-Friedman, G. Bell, R. Tucker, T. Shen, S. Austin, L. LeBlanc

**Publisher:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## Abstract
Future autonomously constructed surface habitats on the Moon and Mars will leverage additive manufacturing technologies to create airtight and durable structures. This paper defines the landscape of BIM use and simulation for future robotic deployment of space habitats, describing the contribution of the SEArch+/Apis Cor project to BIM workflows for ISRU surface habitat design.

## Introduction
Autonomous construction on the Moon and Mars requires planning for site conditions, raw material quantities for ISRU, feedstock storage, and robotic access. The research leverages workflows between Rhino/Grasshopper, Autodesk Revit, Navisworks, and proprietary slicer software to estimate completion times and material quantities.

### Challenges of BIM for Automated Construction
While BIM is standard in AEC, its integration with robotics remains a challenge. Current software often lacks the ability to adequately represent robotic toolpaths or the real-time site dynamics of automated construction. The integration of CAD and BIM with the Robot Operating System (ROS) is cited as a potential next step.

## NASA’s Centennial Challenge for a 3D-Printed Habitat on Mars
The project developed for NASA’s Phase 3 Centennial Challenge involved the "Mars X-House." Key requirements included:
- A pressure-retaining living space of at least 93 m².
- Support for four astronauts for one year.
- 4D construction sequence simulation including machinery, 3D printing, and component emplacement (ECLSS, airlocks).
- Level of Development (LOD) standards: LOD 200 for MEP/ECLSS and LOD 400 for structural/pressure retaining components.

## Software Workflow
- **Autodesk BIM 360:** Cloud-based collaboration.
- **Rhino/Grasshopper:** Parametric modeling of the regolith structural shell.
- **Autodesk Revit:** Interior design and family assignment for material usage.
- **Autodesk Navisworks:** 4D construction sequencing and scheduling.

## Findings
- **4D-BIM Value:** 4D simulation (3D geometry + time) is essential for mission architecture planning, allowing for the coordination of multiple robotic agents.
- **Material Specification:** The simulation demonstrated two mobile 3D-printers coordinated to print a shell of regolith concrete and an interior structure of high-density polyethylene.
- **Digital Twin Requirement:** For autonomous systems to operate effectively, they must refer to a semantic model of the environment (a dynamic BIM or "digital twin") updated in real-time to account for anomalies.

## Conclusion
Autonomous construction requires the synchronization of 3D-printing systems, ISRU regolith acquisition machinery, and robotic labor for component installation. The 4D BIM plan serves as a critical framework for this synchronization, though testing of pressurized 3D-printed structures in large-scale prototypes remains an ongoing challenge.
