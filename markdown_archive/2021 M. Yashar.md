# Building Information Modeling (BIM) Workflows for Construction Sequencing & 4D-Planning of 3D-Printed ISRU Surface Habitats

**M. Yashar, P. Michealsen, B. Hammond, J. Alvizar, C. Ciardullo, M. Morris, R. Pailes-Friedman, G. Bell, R. Tucker, T. Shen, S. Austin, L. LeBlanc**

## Abstract
Future autonomously constructed surface habitats on the Moon and Mars will leverage additive manufacturing technologies to create airtight and durable structures using In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU). BIM workflows offer significant value in designing space systems, particularly habitat facilities that include pre-integrated hardware like Environmental Control and Life Support Systems (ECLSS), airlocks, and suitports. This paper defines the landscape of BIM use for robotic deployment of space habitats, describing the project's contribution to BIM workflows for an ISRU surface habitat, based on team SEArch+/Apis Cor’s first-place win in NASA’s Phase 3 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge.

## Introduction
Autonomous construction on the Moon and Mars requires planning site conditions, raw material quantities for ISRU collection, feedstock storage, and power access. For the NASA Centennial Challenge, the team integrated workflows across Rhino/Grasshopper, Autodesk Revit, Navisworks, and proprietary slicer software to estimate completion times and material quantities.

### BIM in AEC and Automated Construction
Building Information Modeling (BIM) enables holistic data management across a building's lifecycle. While standard in terrestrial Architecture, Engineering, and Construction (AEC), integrating BIM with automated construction remains a challenge. BIM allows for 4D simulation, correlating 3D objects with construction schedules to visualize site logistics and task sequencing.

### 3D-Printing and BIM
Additive manufacturing disrupts traditional construction. BIM software can convert 3D geometry into Computerized Numerical Control (CNC) codes (G-code) for 3D-print heads or robotic arms. This enables the integration of mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) elements and material schedules into the manufacturing sequence.

## NASA’s Centennial Challenge for a 3D-Printed Habitat on Mars
The challenge focused on habitat design and large-scale additive construction using in-situ materials (regolith) or recyclables. SEArch+/Apis Cor's entry, **Mars X-House**, won first place in the Final Virtual Design level.

### Requirements and Scoring
Habitats required a pressure-retaining living space of at least 93 m² supporting four astronauts for one year. Submissions included a BIM model and a 4D construction sequence simulating robotic activities, including the emplacement of airlocks and suitports.

## Simulation and Implementation
The 4D-BIM simulation demonstrated two mobile 3D-printers and mobile platforms coordinated in printing the habitat shell (regolith concrete) and the interior structure (High-Density Polyethylene - HDPE). In-progress components were represented as "ghosted" green to capture active manufacturing.

## Conclusion
Autonomous construction requires the robotic placement of windows, partitions, and pre-integrated hardware (ECLS, airlocks). The synchronization of these tasks within a unified 4D BIM plan is critical for ensuring resilience and autonomy in future Mars precursor missions.
