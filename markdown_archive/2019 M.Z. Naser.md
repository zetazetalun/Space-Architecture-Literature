# Space-native construction materials for earth-independent and sustainable infrastructure

**M.Z. Naser**

## Abstract

A successful space exploration requires establishing permanent and earth-independent infrastructure that are not only resilient to the extreme environment of space but also preferably made of sustainable and indigenous “space-native” construction materials. This review covers feasibility of exploiting in-situ lunar and Martian resources as well as harvesting of elements and compounds, from near Earth objects (NEOs), to produce extra-terrestrial materials suitable for construction of space-based infrastructure. This review also details material features and characteristics required to withstand the unique, and harsh, effects of space environment. In essence, this paper reviews past and recent advancements in construction-based materials that could be used in the design and development of space human bases and highlights design consideration for sustainable human settlements. Towards the end of this review, practical and technological challenges associated with development of lunar and Martian indigenous construction materials are identified and examined.

## 1. Introduction

Serious attention was first focused towards space exploration in the beginning of the twentieth century as well as during the Second World War... Whether the Moon or Mars is selected for manned exploration missions and to pave the way for large-scale human colonization, this migration is highly dependent upon development of resilient, economical, and sustainable infrastructure that can house and safely guard human subjects and crews. Since transportation of infrastructure (or raw/construction materials) can cost between $5000 to $20,000 to deliver 1 kg from Earth to the Moon; a cost with the potential to scale up if transportation is to reach Mars, another alternative would be to build and fabricate such infrastructure on the Moon and Mars using their own (i.e. indigenous) resources.

## 2. Space environment, and resources

### 2.1. Space environment
Space comprises of a multitude of environments (and actions) that are largely different their Earth counterparts. These actions not only affect fundamental aspects of material formation but also the behavior of materials as well as, their performance, and characteristics. The three main differences are radiation, vacuum, and low gravitational forces.

### 2.2. Space resources
Two main concepts have been proposed over the past few years to arrive at sustainable and Earth-independent space infrastructure:
1) Direct (or immediate) use of in-situ raw materials available on the Moon and Mars.
2) Mining and harvesting Near-Earth Objects (NEOs) for minerals and compounds.

## 3. Properties of space-native construction materials

### 3.1. Concrete-like hybrids
Concrete derivatives suitable for space include:
- **Polymer concrete**: Use of polymeric resin binders as cement replacements (epoxy-based variants).
- **Sulfur concrete**: Waterless concrete made by heating sulfur to 120–150 °C until liquid and mixing with aggregate.
- **Geopolymers**: Formed by interaction between aluminosilicate and an alkaline solution.

### 3.2. Metals and alloys
Extracted elements such as aluminum, magnesium, iron, and titanium can be processed into structural members. Aluminum and magnesium are particularly suitable for interplanetary construction due to their low density and relatively low melting points.

## 4. Design considerations for sustainable space infrastructures
Due to low gravity (17% of Earth on Moon; 38% on Mars), space infrastructure can carry 3 to 6 times the mass of terrestrial counterparts. This allows for larger spans and thinner, more slender load-bearing members. Habitat shielding via regolith burial (1–6 m depth) is proposed for radiation and meteorite protection.

## 6. Summary and conclusions
- The extreme environment of space presents a unique challenge to development of extraterrestrial constructions.
- High amounts of SiO2 in in-situ resources allow for the production of cementitious terrestrial-like materials.
- Utilizing lunar and Martian resources leads to sustainable infrastructure with minimum Earth-dependence.
