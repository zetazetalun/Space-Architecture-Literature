# Space-native construction materials for earth-independent and sustainable infrastructure

**Author:** M.Z. Naser
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 155 (2019) 264–273

## Abstract
A successful space exploration requires establishing permanent and earth-independent infrastructure that are not only resilient to the extreme environment of space but also preferably made of sustainable and indigenous “space-native” construction materials. This review covers feasibility of exploiting in-situ lunar and Martian resources as well as harvesting of elements and compounds, from near Earth objects (NEOs), to produce extraterrestrial materials suitable for construction of space-based infrastructure. This review also details material features and characteristics required to withstand the unique, and harsh, effects of space environment.

## 1. Introduction
Transportation costs for raw materials from Earth are prohibitive, estimated between $5000 to $20,000 per kg to the Moon. Utilizing in-situ resources (ISRU) promotes the development of Earth-independent and sustainable infrastructure. Analysis of soils from lunar and Martian landing sites confirms the availability of materials that can be processed into terrestrial-like construction materials such as concrete and metals.

### 2.2. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU)
Regolith (fine particles of soil/rock) is found on both the Moon and Mars. Lunar and Martian soils contain silicon dioxide (SiO2), alumina (Al2O3), and iron oxide (FeO).

## 3. Properties of space-native construction materials
### 3.1. Concrete-like hybrids
- **Polymer concrete:** Comprises inorganic aggregates and polymeric resin binders. Compressive strengths vary from 17 to 60 MPa.
- **Geopolymers:** Produced by interaction between aluminosilicate and alkaline solutions. Average compressive strengths range from 16.6 to 33.1 MPa.
- **Sulfur concrete:** Water-free, created by heating sulfur (120–150 °C) and mixing with regolith. It gains strength rapidly but is susceptible to sublimation in vacuum.

### 3.2. Metals and alloys
- **Aluminum & Magnesium:** Suitable for structural members due to low density and availability in lunar highlands. Magnesium also provides electromagnetic shielding.
- **Titanium:** Available in ilmenite (FeTiO3) but highly reactive with oxygen.

## 4. Design considerations for sustainable space infrastructures
Due to lower gravity (17% on Moon, 38% on Mars), structures can carry 3 to 6 times more mass than on Earth, allowing for larger spans and more slender members. Shielding is required via regolith coverage (1-6m depth) to protect against radiation and micrometeorites.

## 6. Summary and conclusions
- The extreme environment presents unique challenges to traditional construction.
- High SiO2 content in ISRU materials enables cementitious terrestrial-like materials.
- Sustainable infrastructure can be fabricated with minimal Earth-based raw materials.
