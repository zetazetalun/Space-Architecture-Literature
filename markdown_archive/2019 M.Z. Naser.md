# Space-native construction materials for earth-independent and sustainable infrastructure\n\n## Abstract\nSuccessful space exploration requires permanent, Earth-independent infrastructure made from indigenous "space-native" materials. This review covers the feasibility of using lunar, Martian, and Near-Earth Object (NEO) resources to produce materials resilient to extreme space environments. It examines advancements in construction materials for human bases and highlights design considerations for sustainability and safety.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nTransportation costs to the Moon ($5,000–$20,000/kg) necessitate the use of in-situ resource utilization (ISRU). Moon and Mars soils (regolith) can be processed into concrete and metals. This review addresses knowledge gaps in material behavior under vacuum, radiation, and low gravity.\n\n## 2. Space Environment and Resources\n### 2.1 Space Environment\nDifferences include gravity (Earth: 1.0, Moon: 0.16g, Mars: 0.38g), atmosphere (Moon: negligible, Mars: 1% of Earth), and thermal fluctuations (−173 to 127°C on Moon). Lack of atmosphere increases risks from radiation and micrometeorites.\n\n### 2.2 Space Resources\n- **ISRU:** Utilizing surface/subsurface resources (regolith).\n- **NEOs:** Mining asteroids (e.g., Ryugu, Bennu) for minerals like Ni, Fe, and Co.\n\n## 3. Space-Native Construction Materials\n### 3.1 Concrete-like Hybrids\n- **Traditional Concrete:** Water-dependent; performance stable in vacuum if properly designed.\n- **Polymer Concrete:** Uses resins/epoxies; strengths range 17-60 MPa.\n- **Geopolymers:** Formed by alumino-silicate and alkaline interaction; strengths 16.6-33.1 MPa.\n- **Sulfur Concrete:** Water-free; sets quickly; lunar strengths 39-75.7 MPa, Martian strengths 20-63 MPa.\n- **Sintered Regolith:** Produced via heat (solar/microwave); lunar simulant bricks achieved 0.84-20.9 MPa.\n\n### 3.2 Metals and Alloys\nExtraction of Al (15% in lunar highlands), Mg (in olivine), Fe, and Ti from regolith. Al and Mg are suitable for structural members. Ti alloys (e.g., Ti6Al4V) offer high strength/stiffness. Beryllium alloys offer highest stiffness but have toxicity concerns.\n\n## 4. Design Considerations\nLow gravity allows for larger spans and thinner members (e.g., scaling a 36,000-ton Earth telescope to 4,000 tons on the Moon). Structures should be shielded or buried under 1-6 m of regolith for radiation and impact protection.\n\n## 5. Conclusions\nSustainable infrastructure must utilize indigenous materials. Key challenges include understanding long-term material behavior in space and developing autonomous fabrication units.
