# Space-native construction materials for earth-independent and sustainable infrastructure

**Author:** M.Z. Naser
**Affiliation:** Glenn Department of Civil Engineering, Clemson University, Clemson, SC, 29634, USA
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 155 (2019) 264–273

## Abstract
A successful space exploration requires establishing permanent and earth-independent infrastructure that are not only resilient to the extreme environment of space but also preferably made of sustainable and indigenous “space-native” construction materials. This review covers feasibility of exploiting in-situ lunar and Martian resources as well as harvesting of elements and compounds, from near Earth objects (NEOs), to produce extra-terrestrial materials suitable for construction of space-based infrastructure. This review also details material features and characteristics required to withstand the unique, and harsh, effects of space environment.

## 1. Introduction
Transportation costs for construction materials from Earth are estimated at $5000 to $20,000 per kg to the Moon, and significantly higher for Mars. This necessitates the use of indigenous (space-native) resources. Analysis of lunar and Martian soil confirms the availability of minerals (SiO2, Al2O3, FeO, etc.) that can be processed into concrete-like materials and metals.

## 2. Space environment and resources
### 2.1. Space environment
Materials must withstand:
- **Radiation:** Solar and galactic cosmic radiation.
- **Vacuum:** Causes outgassing and moisture loss in traditional mortars.
- **Thermal Fluctuations:** -173 to 127 °C on the Moon; -60 to 125 °C on Mars.
- **Meteorite Impacts:** Speeds of 20–70 km/s can cause significant structural damage.

### 2.2. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU)
Regolith (surficial soil) is the primary resource. Lunar soil grain size averages 40-270 μm, while Martian soil ranges from 70-800 μm.

## 3. Properties of space-native construction materials
### 3.1. Concrete-like hybrids
- **Sulfur Concrete:** Water-free, formed by heating sulfur to 120–150 °C. Rapid curing. Concerns include sublimation in vacuum.
- **Polymer Concrete:** Uses resins (epoxy, thermoplastics) as binders. Compressive strengths reach 17-60 MPa.
- **Geopolymers:** Formed by aluminosilicate interaction with alkaline solutions. Compressive strength 16.6 to 33.1 MPa.

### 3.2. Metals and alloys
Extracted elements (Al, Mg, Fe, Ti) can be used for structural members. Magnesium offers electromagnetic shielding and damping properties suitable for meteorite impact protection.

## 4. Design considerations
Reduced gravity (17% Moon, 38% Mars) allows for structures with larger spans and thinner members (potentially 3 to 6 times the mass capacity of Earth-equivalent structures). Foundations must be placed below the thermal cycling depth to prevent settlement issues.

## 5. Summary and conclusions
Sustainable space exploration requires utilizing indigenous materials to minimize Earth-dependence. While regolith provides a strong base for concrete and metal production, further research is required to characterize behavior under combined vacuum and low gravity.
