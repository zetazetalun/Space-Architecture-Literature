## Abstract
A successful space exploration requires establishing permanent and earth-independent infrastructure that are not only resilient to the extreme environment of space but also preferably made of sustainable and indigenous “space-native” construction materials. This review covers feasibility of exploiting in-situ lunar and Martian resources as well as harvesting of elements and compounds, from near Earth objects (NEOs), to produce extra-terrestrial materials suitable for construction of space-based infrastructure. This review also details material features and characteristics required to withstand the unique, and harsh, effects of space environment.

## 1. Introduction
Serious attention was first focused towards space exploration in the beginning of the twentieth century. Transportation of raw materials from Earth costs between $5000 to $20,000 per kg to the Moon, and significantly more for Mars. Therefore, utilizing in-situ resources is critical for establishing economical space exploration programs. Analysis on collected soils from lunar and Martian landing sites has verified that both hold a wealth of materials that can be processed to yield terrestrial-like construction materials such as concrete and metals.

## 2. Space environment, and resources
### 2.1. Space environment
Space environments differ from Earth due to radiation (gamma rays, cosmic rays), vacuum (leading to dry-out and outgassing of materials), and low gravity. Meteorite impact is also a significant factor; particles reaching 20-70 km/s can cause significant damage to infrastructure.

### 2.2. Space resources
Two main concepts involve: 1) Direct use of in-situ raw materials available on the Moon and Mars, and 2) Mining and harvesting Near-Earth Objects (NEOs).

#### 2.2.1. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU)
Regolith covers the lunar and Martian surfaces. Lunar regolith consists of well-graded grains with sizes between 40 and 270 um. Martian soil has an average grain size between 70 and 800 um. Both contain high concentrations of SiO2, Al2O3, and FeO.

## 3. Properties of space-native construction materials
### 3.1. Concrete-like hybrids
* **Polymer Concrete:** Uses polymeric resin binders (epoxies) instead of water. Compressive strengths vary from 17 to 60 MPa.
* **Geopolymers:** Formed by interaction between aluminosilicate and an alkaline solution. Compressive strengths range from 16.6 to 33.1 MPa.
* **Sulfur Concrete:** Waterfree, made by heating sulfur to 120-150 C. Compressive strengths reach 39-75.7 MPa for lunar variants and 20-63 MPa for Martian variants.
* **Sintering:** Regolith can be sintered into bricks using solar or microwave energy. Sintered materials show compressive strengths between 0.84 and 20.9 MPa.

### 3.2. Metals and alloys
Lunar and Martian soils contain aluminum, magnesium, iron, and titanium. Aluminum and magnesium are suitable for structural members due to low density and low melting points. Magnesium also provides electromagnetic shielding.

## 4. Design considerations for sustainable space infrastructures
Design loads in space are significantly lower due to reduced gravity (17% on Moon, 38% on Mars), allowing for thinner structural members and larger spans. However, shielding is required for radiation and meteorite protection, often involving burying structures under 1-6 meters of regolith.

## 6. Summary and conclusions
1. Extreme space environments present unique challenges for extraterrestrial construction.
2. Abundance of SiO2 in regolith allows for cementitious material production.
3. Utilizing ISRU minimizes dependence on Earth-based resources.
4. Research is needed in the characterization of space-native materials under simulated extreme conditions.
