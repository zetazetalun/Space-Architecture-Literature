# Space-native construction materials for earth-independent and sustainable infrastructure

**Author:** M.Z. Naser  
**Publisher:** Acta Astronautica, Elsevier  
**Year:** 2019  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2018.12.014

## Abstract
A successful space exploration requires establishing permanent and earth-independent infrastructure that are not only resilient to the extreme environment of space but also preferably made of sustainable and indigenous “space-native” construction materials. This review covers feasibility of exploiting in-situ lunar and Martian resources as well as harvesting of elements and compounds from near Earth objects (NEOs). It details material features required to withstand harsh space environments and highlights design considerations for sustainable human settlements.

## 1. Introduction
Space infrastructure development is hindered by high transportation costs ($5,000 to $20,000 per kg). Utilizing in-situ resources (ISRU) to yield concrete and metals is essential for economical space exploration.

## 2. Space Resources
### 2.1 Regolith
Regolith consists of fine particles (SiO2, Al2O3, FeO). Martian regolith grain sizes range from 70 to 800 μm, while lunar samples typically vary between 40 and 270 μm.

## 3. Properties of Space-Native Materials
### 3.1 Concrete-like hybrids
*   **Sulfur Concrete:** Water-free alternative made by heating sulfur (120–150 °C) and mixing with regolith. Compressive strength: 5.5–17 MPa (can be increased by 45% with fiber reinforcement).
*   **Polymer/Epoxy Concrete:** Uses polymeric resins as binders. Compressive strength: 17–60 MPa.
*   **Geopolymers:** Formed by interaction between aluminosilicate and alkaline solutions. Average strength: 16.6–33.1 MPa.

### 3.2 Metals and Alloys
Elements like Al, Mg, Fe, and Ti are abundant. Magnesium is noted for its electromagnetic shielding and high damping effect.

## 4. Design Considerations
*   **Loading:** Thinner structural members are possible due to 1/6g (Moon) and 1/3g (Mars) gravity.
*   **Shielding:** Regolith burial (1–6 m depth) is proposed for radiation and meteorite protection.
*   **Foundations:** Must be positioned below the thermal cycling depth to control settlement.

## 5. Summary and Conclusions
1. Extreme environments pose unique challenges for construction.
2. High SiO2 content makes cementitious terrestrial-like materials feasible via ISRU.
3. Waterless construction (sulfur/polymers) is critical for sustainable development.
