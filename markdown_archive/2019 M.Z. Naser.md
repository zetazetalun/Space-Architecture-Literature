# Space-native construction materials for earth-independent and sustainable infrastructure

**Author:** M.Z. Naser
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 155 (2019) 264–273

## Abstract
A successful space exploration requires establishing permanent and earth-independent infrastructure that are not only resilient to the extreme environment of space but also preferably made of sustainable and indigenous "space-native" construction materials. This review covers feasibility of exploiting in-situ lunar and Martian resources as well as harvesting of elements and compounds, from near Earth objects (NEOs), to produce extra-terrestrial materials suitable for construction of space-based infrastructure.

## 1. Introduction
Transportation of construction materials from Earth is prohibitively expensive, costing between $5,000 to $20,000 per kg to the Moon, and significantly more to Mars. To realize Earth-independent exploration, infrastructure must be fabricated using indigenous resources. Analysis of lunar and Martian soils shows they contain minerals that can be processed into terrestrial-like construction materials such as concrete and metals.

## 3. Properties of Space-Native Materials
### 3.1 Concrete-like Hybrids
*   **Traditional Concrete:** Water accessibility is a major hurdle. Cullingford and Keller (1992) noted water loss in vacuum at a rate of 0.04% per day.
*   **Polymer Concrete:** Uses resins/epoxies as binders. Compressive strengths reach 17-60 MPa.
*   **Geopolymers:** Produced by interaction of aluminosilicates (regolith) and alkaline solutions. Strengths range from 16.6 to 33.1 MPa.
*   **Sulfur Concrete:** Water-free, rapid-curing. Strength gains are significant, but sublimation in vacuum is a concern. Wan et al. (2016) reported Martian sulfur concrete strengths between 20-63 MPa.

### 3.2 Metals and Alloys
Aluminum, Magnesium, and Iron are abundant in lunar/Martian regolith. Titanium is available in ilmenite (FeTiO₃).

## 4. Design Considerations
Low gravity (17% Moon, 38% Mars) allows for structures with 3 to 6 times larger spans or more slender members compared to Earth. Regolith shielding (1-6 meters deep) is recommended to protect against radiation, thermal cycles, and micrometeorite impacts.

## 6. Summary and Conclusions
Space exploration is dependent on the development of indigenous materials. The abundance of SiO₂ and metals in regolith supports the feasibility of cementitious hybrids and metal-based structures, provided energy and processing challenges are met.
