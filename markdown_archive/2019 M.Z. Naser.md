# Space-native construction materials for earth-independent and sustainable infrastructure

**Author:** M.Z. Naser  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 155 (2019) 264–273

## Abstract
A successful space exploration requires establishing permanent and earth-independent infrastructure that are not only resilient to the extreme environment of space but also preferably made of sustainable and indigenous “space-native” construction materials. This review covers feasibility of exploiting in-situ lunar and Martian resources as well as harvesting of elements and compounds, from near Earth objects (NEOs), to produce extra-terrestrial materials suitable for construction of space-based infrastructure. 

## 1. Introduction
The migration to Moon or Mars is highly dependent upon development of resilient, economical, and sustainable infrastructure. Since transportation of infrastructure (or raw/construction materials) can cost between $5000 to $20,000 to deliver 1 kg from Earth to the Moon, an alternative would be to build and fabricate such infrastructure on the Moon and Mars using indigenous resources.

## 2. Space environment, and resources
### 2.1. Space environment
Key factors affecting material behavior include radiation, vacuum conditions, and low gravitational forces. Lack of atmosphere on the Moon and thin atmosphere on Mars lead to significant thermal fluctuations and micrometeorite impact risks.

### 2.2. Space resources
#### 2.2.1. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU)
ISRU involves harnessing raw resources found on the surface or subsurface. Regolith (fine soil particles) is the primary material, containing high concentrations of silicon dioxide (SiO2), alumina (Al2O3), and iron oxide (FeO).

## 3. Properties of space-native construction materials
### 3.1. Concrete-like hybrids
- **Polymer concrete:** Uses inorganic aggregates with polymeric resin binders (epoxy/thermoplastic). Strengths vary from 17 to 60 MPa.
- **Geopolymers:** Formed by interaction between aluminosilicates and alkaline solutions. Compressive strength ranges 16.6 to 33.1 MPa.
- **Sulfur concrete:** Waterless concrete made by heating sulfur to 120–150 °C. It offers rapid curing times but is prone to sublimation in vacuum conditions.
- **Sintered Regolith:** Heating regolith below its melting point using solar energy, lasers, or microwaves to form bricks. Strengths range from 0.84 to 20.9 MPa.

### 3.2. Metals and alloys
Aluminum (Al), Magnesium (Mg), Iron (Fe), and Titanium (Ti) are available in regolith. Magnesium is noted for its electromagnetic shielding and high damping effects.

## 4. Design considerations for sustainable space infrastructures
Design loads must be scaled for local gravity (17% of Earth for Moon; 38% for Mars). Structural members can be significantly lighter and thinner than Earth counterparts. One major challenge is shielding, with proposed regolith layers varying from 1 to 6 m depth.

## 5. Current challenges and future research
1. Deficiency in understanding the behavior of space-native materials under vacuum/radiation.
2. Need for autonomous fabrication units.
3. Lack of constitutive material models for structural engineering integration.
