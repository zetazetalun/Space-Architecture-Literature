## Abstract
A successful space exploration requires establishing permanent and earth-independent infrastructure that are not only resilient to the extreme environment of space but also preferably made of sustainable and indigenous “space-native” construction materials. This review covers feasibility of exploiting in-situ lunar and Martian resources as well as harvesting of elements and compounds, from near Earth objects (NEOs), to produce extra-terrestrial materials suitable for construction of space-based infrastructure. This review also details material features and characteristics required to withstand the unique, and harsh, effects of space environment.

## 1. Introduction
Serious attention was first focused towards space exploration in the beginning of the twentieth century... development of such infrastructure continues to be a major challenge that hinders our ability to realize Earth-independent space exploration programs. Since transportation of infrastructure (or raw/construction materials) can cost between $5000 to $20,000 to deliver 1 kg from Earth to the Moon; a cost with the potential to scale up if transportation is to reach Mars, another alternative would be to build and fabricate such infrastructure on the Moon and Mars using their own (i.e. indigenous) resources.

## 2. Space environment, and resources
### 2.1. Space environment
Radiation occurs due to electromagnetism such as gamma rays and galactic cosmic radiation... The lack of atmosphere on the Moon and presence of a very thin atmosphere on Mars shapes a fragile shield against space debris including meteorites and/or micrometeorites.

### 2.2. Space resources
Two main concepts have been proposed to arrive at sustainable and Earth-independent space infrastructure:
1. Direct (or immediate) use of in-situ raw materials available on the Moon and Mars,
2. Mining and harvesting Near-Earth Objects (NEOs) for minerals and compounds.

## 3. Properties of space-native construction materials
### 3.1. Concrete-like hybrids
*   **Polymer concrete:** Comprises inorganic aggregates and polymeric resin binders. Strengths vary from 17 to 60 MPa.
*   **Sulfur concrete:** Waterless concrete made by heating sulfur (120–150 °C). It reaches 80-90% strength quickly but is susceptible to sublimation in vacuum.
*   **Geopolymers:** Formed by interaction between an aluminosilicate and an alkaline solution. Compressive strengths range from 16.6 to 33.1 MPa.

### 3.2. Metals and alloys
Lunar and Martian resources contain Al, Mg, Fe, and Ti. Aluminum and magnesium are suitable for interplanetary construction due to low density and low melting points.

## 4. Design considerations for sustainable space infrastructures
Design loads (dead/live) can be scaled to the equivalent gravity of Luna or Mars (17% and 38% of Earth). Hence, space infrastructure can theoretically have much larger spans and thinner members. Geotechnical considerations suggest foundations should be positioned beneath the depth of thermal cycling to control settlement. Habitat shielding depth is recommended between 1 and 6 m of regolith to protect against radiation and micrometeorites.

## 6. Summary and conclusions
*   The extreme environment presents a unique challenge to development.
*   High SiO₂ content in in-situ resources allows for cementitious materials.
*   In-situ materials reduce Earth-dependency costs significantly.
*   Serious efforts are needed for processing techniques and structural design models.
