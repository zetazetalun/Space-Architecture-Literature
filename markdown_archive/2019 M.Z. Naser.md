# Space-native construction materials for earth-independent and sustainable infrastructure

**Author:** M.Z. Naser  
**Affiliation:** Glenn Department of Civil Engineering, Clemson University, Clemson, SC, 29634, USA  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 155 (2019) 264–273

## Abstract
A successful space exploration requires establishing permanent and earth-independent infrastructure that are not only resilient to the extreme environment of space but also preferably made of sustainable and indigenous “space-native” construction materials. This review covers feasibility of exploiting in-situ lunar and Martian resources as well as harvesting of elements and compounds, from near Earth objects (NEOs), to produce extraterrestrial materials suitable for construction of space-based infrastructure. This review also details material features and characteristics required to withstand the unique, and harsh, effects of space environment.

## 1. Introduction
The development of resilient, economical, and sustainable infrastructure is a major challenge for space colonization. Transporting raw materials from Earth costs between $5,000 and $20,000 per kg to the Moon. Utilizing indigenous resources (ISRU) on the Moon and Mars is essential for Earth-independent exploration. Analysis of soils from landing sites confirms the presence of materials that can be processed into terrestrial-like construction materials such as concrete and metals.

## 2. Space Environment and Resources
### 2.1. Space Environment
The primary environmental challenges include:
- **Radiation:** Solar and galactic cosmic radiation.
- **Vacuum:** Leads to material dry-out, outgassing, and deterioration.
- **Thermal Fluctuations:** Range from -173 to 127 °C on the Moon and -60 to 125 °C on Mars.
- **Micrometeorites:** Impact speeds of 20–70 km/s can cause significant cratering in structural materials.

### 2.2. Space Resources
Two main concepts are proposed:
1. Direct use of in-situ raw materials (Moon and Mars).
2. Mining and harvesting Near-Earth Objects (NEOs).

## 3. Properties of Space-Native Construction Materials
### 3.1. Concrete-like Hybrids
- **Traditional Concrete:** Challenging due to water requirements. Curing in vacuum leads to water loss, though strength may remain stable if properly managed.
- **Polymer Concrete:** Uses inorganic aggregates with polymeric resin binders. Compressive strengths range from 17 to 60 MPa.
- **Sulfur Concrete:** Water-free; made by heating sulfur to 120–150 °C. It cures quickly but is susceptible to sublimation in vacuum conditions.
- **Geopolymers:** Produced by interaction between aluminosilicate and alkaline solutions. Offers radiation protection.
- **Sintered Regolith:** Using solar or microwave energy to bond regolith into bricks (compressive strengths up to 20.9 MPa).

### 3.2. Metals and Alloys
- **Aluminum & Magnesium:** Suitable for interplanetary construction due to low density and abundance in lunar highlands.
- **Iron:** Abundant; can be used to produce steel.
- **Titanium:** High mechanical properties but high reactivity with oxygen.

## 4. Design Considerations
- **Gravity Scaling:** Lower gravity (17% on Moon, 38% on Mars) allows for structures with much larger spans and thinner members compared to Earth.
- **Shielding:** Habitats may need to be buried under 1–6 meters of regolith to protect against radiation and micrometeorites.
- **Foundations:** Extreme diurnal temperatures cause regolith expansion/contraction, requiring deep foundations.

## 5. Summary and Conclusions
- The extreme space environment requires innovative extraterrestrial construction.
- High SiO₂ content enables the production of cementitious terrestrial-like materials.
- Indigenous production is vital for sustainable and Earth-independent infrastructure.
