# Review of research into bioregenerative life support system(s) which can support humans living in space

**Authors:** Hong Liu, Zhikai Yao, Yuming Fu, Jiajie Feng  
**Journal:** Life Sciences in Space Research 31 (2021) 113–120  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.lssr.2021.09.003

## Abstract
To travel beyond Earth and realize long-term survival in deep space, humans need to construct Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS), which reduce supply requirements by in situ regenerating oxygen, water, and food. This paper reviews the progress of BLSS research across the USSR/Russia, USA, Europe, Japan, and China, focusing on Earth-based experimental facilities like "Lunar Palace 365" and future prospects for extraterrestrial application.

## 1. Introduction
BLSS is an artificial closed ecosystem composed of humans, plants, animals, and microorganisms. It is a key technology for crewed deep space exploration, providing a stable habitat environment similar to Earth's ecosystem. 

## 2. Theoretical and Technological Framework
Theories and technologies of BLSS are multidisciplinary, involving biology, environmental engineering, ecology, materials science, and systems engineering. The development path involves studying individual units, followed by artificial ecosystem integration, and finally Earth-based and extraterrestrial simulation.

## 3. Research Progress of Earth-based BLSS

### 3.1 Soviet Union/Russia
*   **BIOS-3:** A 315 m³ system with four compartments. Achieved >90% closure for gas/water and 70% food production for 2-3 people. Record-holding 95% total closure for over 30 years.

### 3.2 United States
*   **CELSS/ALS:** NASA focused on advanced life support for lunar/Martian bases.
*   **LMLSTP:** Integrated biological and physical/chemical technologies; achieved 91-day survival for 4 crew members.
*   **Biosphere 2:** The largest closed system (1.3 hectares). Highlighted the complexity of carbon dioxide absorption and ecosystem stability.

### 3.3 Japan
*   **CEEF:** Focused on a "human-goat-higher plants" loop. Utilized physical/chemical methods for rapid waste treatment.

### 3.4 Europe
*   **MELiSSA:** ESA's project focusing on a five-compartment cycle, including waste decomposition, nitrification, and food production.
*   **DLR:** Studied optimal structural design for a lunar greenhouse module. Proposed a semi-cylindrical hybrid (rigid-inflatable) structure (20m length, 3m radius).

### 3.5 China
*   **Lunar Palace 1:** A 500 m³ facility. The "Lunar Palace 365" experiment achieved 98.2% material closure over 370 days with a 4-person crew. Achieved 100% O2/water regeneration and 83% food regeneration.

## 4. Prospect of Spatial BLSS Experimental Research
Future BLSS research must transition to the lunar surface. The lunar polar regions are ideal due to continuous illumination (up to 90% of the year) and potential water ice resources. ISRU will play a vital role in building large-scale ecosystems by mining water and using lunar soil for plant substrates.

## 5. Development Path and Evaluation
The transition to extraterrestrial BLSS follows a three-stage strategy:
1.  **Stage 1:** Hydroponics with limited ISRU for soil-like substrates.
2.  **Stage 2:** Expansion of ISRU-based substrates and integration of insect protein (e.g., mealworms).
3.  **Stage 3:** Large-scale bases with 100% biological regeneration and "artificial oceans" for fish cultivation.

### Three Key Conditions for Gas Balance (Updated per Corrigendum):
1.  Internal biosynthesis must produce sufficient oxygen for the crew's breathing.
2.  Outside organic matter input must be balanced by internal output of the same respiration quotient.
3.  Gas balance cannot be achieved by biological self-organization alone and requires human intervention.

## 6. Overall Prospect
BLSS will evolve towards lightweight, automated, real-time, and modular designs. New technologies like flexible cabins, nanoparticles, and plant probiotics will be integrated to reduce transportation costs and improve system reliability.
