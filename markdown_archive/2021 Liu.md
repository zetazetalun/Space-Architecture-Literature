# Review of research into bioregenerative life support system(s) which can support humans living in space

**Authors:** Hong Liu, Zhikai Yao, Yuming Fu, Jiajie Feng  
**Journal:** Life Sciences in Space Research 31 (2021) 113–120  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.lssr.2021.09.003

## Abstract
To travel beyond the Earth and realize long-term survival in deep space, humans need to construct Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS), which reduce the requirement for supplies from the Earth by in situ regenerating oxygen, water and food. This paper reviews global progress in BLSS systematic theories, design, construction, and operation, focusing on Earth-based simulations like China’s “Lunar Palace 365” and the transition toward extraterrestrial application.

## 1. Introduction
BLSS is an artificial closed ecosystem composed of humans, plants, animals, and microorganisms. It aims to recycle oxygen, water, and food for long-term autonomous existence in deep-space exploration, such as lunar research stations and landing on Mars.

## 2. BLSS Theoretical and Technological Framework
Theories and technologies are multidisciplinary, including biology, environmental engineering, ecology, materials science, and computer science. The framework is guided by actual design, construction, and operation requirements.

## 3. Research Progress of Earth-based BLSS

### 3.1. Soviet Union/Russia
*   **BIOS-3:** A 315 m³ facility with four compartments. It achieved 95% closure in some experiments, with a 63 m² planting area meeting 40-70% of food needs for a crew of 2-3.

### 3.2. United States
*   **NASA CELSS & ALS:** Developed for lunar and Martian bases.
*   **LMLSTP:** Integrated biological and physical/chemical technologies; achieved 91-day survival for 4 people.
*   **HDU-DSH:** Habitat Demonstration Unit in the Arizona desert.
*   **Biosphere 2:** The largest closed ecosystem (1.3 hectares). The second experiment achieved 100% food production.

### 3.3. Japan
*   **CEEF:** Introduced the first animal unit (goat) and used physical/chemical equipment for waste treatment.

### 3.4. Europe
*   **MELiSSA:** ESA's micro-ecological alternative involving interconnected compartments (waste decomposition, nitrification, photosynthetic food production).
*   **DLR Lunar Greenhouse:** Designed a semi-cylindrical hybrid structure (20 m length, 3 m radius) yielding 22 kg/month of biomass.

### 3.5. China
*   **Lunar Palace 1:** A large-scale crewed BLSS with 100 m² area and 300 m³ volume.
*   **Lunar Palace 365:** A 370-day experiment achieving 100% oxygen/water regeneration and 98.2% total material closure with a 4-person crew.

## 4. Prospect of Spatial BLSS Experimental Research
The transition from Earth to space requires testing under different gravity, magnetic fields, and radiation. The lunar polar region is identified as ideal due to continuous illumination and potential water ice resources.

## 5. Development Path of Extraterrestrial BLSS

### Three-Stage Strategy:
1.  **First Stage:** Hydroponics and processing in situ resources (lunar/Martian soil) with waste to prepare soil-like substrates.
2.  **Second Stage:** Expansion using large amounts of soil-like substrates for plant cultivation and insects (mealworms) for protein.
3.  **Third Stage:** Recycling inorganic salts and purifying organic waste to support large-scale bases with artificial oceans for fish cultivation.

### Evaluation Indices:
1. Regeneration rates and material closure.
2. Stability and reliability.
3. Energy consumption and space requirements.
4. Equipment weight.
5. Labor intensity.

## 6. Conclusion
Future BLSS development will integrate modular, automated, and lightweight designs, utilizing in situ resources to support permanent human presence on the Moon and Mars.
