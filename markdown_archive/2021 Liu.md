# Review of research into bioregenerative life support system(s) which can support humans living in space

**Authors:** Hong Liu, Zhikai Yao, Yuming Fu, Jiajie Feng  
**Journal:** Life Sciences in Space Research  
**Volume:** 31 (2021) 113–120  
**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lssr.2021.09.003](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lssr.2021.09.003)

## Abstract
To travel beyond the Earth and realize long-term survival in deep space, humans need to construct Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS), which reduces the requirement for supplies from the Earth by in situ regenerating oxygen, water and food, and prevents pollution to extraterrestrial bodies by recycling waste. Since the 1960s, the USSR/Russia, the United States, Europe, Japan, and China carried out studies in BLSS systematic theories and long-term operation. China's "Lunar Palace 365" experiment realized Earth-based closed human survival for a year with material closure of >98%. Future research will focus on uncrewed closed ecosystems in space to clarify the impact of space environmental conditions, providing theoretical and technological support for BLSS application in crewed deep space exploration.

## 1. Introduction
Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS) are artificial closed ecosystems composed of humans, plants, animals, and microorganisms. They function based on ecological principles, utilizing producers (plants), consumers (humans/animals), and decomposers (microorganisms) to recycle oxygen, water, and food. BLSS is a key technology for crewed deep space exploration, supporting long-term autonomous existence.

## 2. BLSS Theoretical and Technological Framework
The framework involves multidisciplinary fields including biology, environmental engineering, ecology, materials science, and computer science. Research follows a path from individual units to artificial ecosystems, culminating in Earth-based experimental BLSS with human participation.

## 3. Research Progress of Earth-based BLSS

### 3.1 Soviet Union/Russia
*   **BIOS-1, 2, 3:** Initiated in the 1960s. BIOS-3 (315 m³) achieved 95% closure for gas and water circulation, meeting 40–45% of food needs for a three-person crew.

### 3.2 United States
*   **CELSS & ECLSS:** Developed theories for lunar and Martian bases.
*   **LMLSTP:** Integrated biological and physical/chemical technologies; achieved 91-day survival.
*   **Biosphere 2:** The largest closed ecosystem (1.3 hectares). While it faced challenges like CO2 absorption into concrete, it provided critical data for multi-planetary facilities.
*   **Lunar Greenhouse (LGH):** Prototype deployable units for the Moon/Mars.

### 3.3 Japan
*   **CEEF:** Included an animal unit (goats) and focused on rapid substance circulation via physical and chemical methods.

### 3.4 Europe
*   **MELiSSA:** ESA's project focusing on a five-compartment cycle, aiming for complete closure of water and air.
*   **DLR Greenhouse:** Studied optimal structural design for lunar greenhouse modules, proposing a semi-cylindrical hybrid structure (20 m length, 3 m radius).

### 3.5 China
*   **Lunar Palace 1:** A large-scale system including plant and integrated cabins (500 m³). 
*   **Lunar Palace 365:** A 370-day experiment achieved 100% oxygen/water regeneration and 83% food regeneration (98.2% total material closure).

## 4. Prospect of Spatial BLSS Research
Future focus is on applying these systems to the Moon and Mars. Lunar polar regions are considered ideal due to continuous illumination (up to 90% in some craters), which provides stable energy and reduces thermal control requirements. Use of in situ resources (lunar soil, water ice) is critical for reducing construction costs.

## 5. Development Path of Extraterrestrial BLSS
1.  **First Stage:** Hydroponics-based systems; physical/chemical methods as redundancy; partial use of processed lunar/Martian soil.
2.  **Second Stage:** Expansion using large amounts of in situ soil-based substrates; inclusion of insects (mealworms) for protein.
3.  **Third Stage:** Large-scale bases with 100% biological regeneration, including salt recovery and artificial oceans for fish cultivation.

### Key Conditions for Gas Balance (Corrected via Corrigendum)
1.  Oxygen production must meet crew breathing needs if food is biosynthesized.
2.  Organic matter input from outside must be balanced by internal output.
3.  Gas balance requires human intervention and cannot be achieved by biological self-organization alone.

## 6. Overall Prospect
Future BLSS will utilize flexible cabin technologies, nanotechnology for plant growth, and in situ resource utilization to reduce launch mass. It will evolve towards lightweight, automated, and modular designs.
