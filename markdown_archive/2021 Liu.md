# Review of research into bioregenerative life support system(s) which can support humans living in space\n\n## Abstract\nTo travel beyond the Earth and realize long-term survival in deep space, humans need to construct Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS), which reduce the requirement for supplies from Earth by in situ regenerating oxygen, water, and food. This review summarizes global research achievements from the USSR/Russia, the United States, Europe, Japan, and China. China’s 'Lunar Palace 365' experiment realized Earth-based closed human survival for a year with >98% material closure. Future research will focus on lunar probe payloads to clarify the impact of space environmental conditions on ecosystems to calibrate Earth-based parameters.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nA BLSS is an artificial closed ecosystem composed of humans, plants, animals, and microorganisms. It follows the ecological structure of producers, consumers, and decomposers to recycle essential substances and provide a habitable environment. It is a key technology for autonomous existence during crewed deep-space exploration.\n\n## 2. BLSS's Theoretical and Technological Framework\nBLSS development is multidisciplinary, involving biology, ecology, environmental engineering, nutrition, and materials science. Research paths transition from individual unit studies to integrated Earth-based experiments, followed by extraterrestrial calibration.\n\n## 3. Research Progress of Earth-based BLSS\n### 3.1. Soviet Union/Russia\n- **BIOS-1/2/3:** Developed by the Institute of Biophysics. BIOS-3 (315 m³) achieved 95% closure in some experiments, supporting three people for 4-6 months with >90% water/gas circulation and 40-45% food production.\n\n### 3.2. United States\n- **CELSS & ALS:** NASA programs focused on missions like lunar/Martian bases.\n- **LMLSTP:** Integrated biological and physical/chemical technologies; achieved 91-day four-person survival.\n- **Biosphere 2:** The largest facility (1.3 hectares) encountered O2/CO2 imbalances but provided valuable data on large-scale ecosystem dynamics.\n- **Lunar Greenhouse (LGH):** Development of prototype deployable structures for Moon/Mars.\n\n### 3.3. Japan\n- **CEEF:** Included the first animal unit (goats). Focused on rapid circulation using physical/chemical methods.\n\n### 3.4. Europe\n- **MELiSSA:** Managed by ESA, focusing on a five-compartment cycle involving thermophilic bacteria and photoheterotrophic reactors.\n- **DLR Design:** Studied optimal greenhouse structures, proposing a semi-cylindrical hybrid structure (20m length, 3m radius) for lunar applications.\n\n### 3.5. China\n- **Lunar Palace 1:** A 500 m³ facility that supported four crew members for 370 days (Lunar Palace 365) with 98.2% total material closure. Cultivated 35 kinds of plants and used yellow mealworms for animal protein.\n\n## 4. Prospect of Spatial BLSS Experimental Research\nFuture focus shifts to lunar polar regions due to continuous illumination and potential water ice. ISRU is highlighted: mining resources in situ will reduce construction and operation costs for large-scale closed ecosystems.\n\n## 5. Development Path and Evaluation Indices\nA 'three-stage strategy' for extraterrestrial BLSS is proposed:\n1. **First Stage:** Hydroponics with limited ISRU (soil-like substrates from lunar/Martian regolith mixed with waste).\n2. **Second Stage:** Expansion using large amounts of soil-like substrates and increased animal protein (insects).\n3. **Third Stage:** Large-scale bases with full biological regeneration and inorganic salt cycling.\n\n## 6. Corrigendum (2022)\nCorrection to the 'three key conditions' for gas balance:\n1. If all food comes from internal biosynthesis, O2 produced is sufficient for breathing.\n2. Organic matter input must be balanced by organic matter output of the same respiration quotient.\n3. Gas balance cannot be achieved by biological self-organization alone and requires human intervention.
