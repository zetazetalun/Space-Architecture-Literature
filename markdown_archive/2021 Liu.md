# Review of research into bioregenerative life support system(s) which can support humans living in space

**Journal:** Life Sciences in Space Research 31 (2021) 113–120  
**Authors:** Hong Liu, Zhikai Yao, Yuming Fu, Jiajie Feng  
**Published:** September 12, 2021

## Abstract
To travel beyond Earth and realize long-term survival in deep space, humans need to construct Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS). These systems reduce Earth-supply requirements by in situ regenerating oxygen, water, and food, and recycling waste. This paper reviews the history of BLSS research in the USSR/Russia (BIOS series), the USA (CELSS, ALS, Biosphere 2), Europe (MELiSSA, DLR Greenhouse), Japan (CEEF), and China (Lunar Palace 365). It highlights that while Earth-based tests have achieved over 98% material closure, future research must address the impact of space environmental conditions (gravity, radiation) on these ecosystems.

## 1. Introduction
BLSS is an artificial closed ecosystem composed of humans, plants, animals, and microorganisms. It follows the ecological structure of producers, consumers, and decomposers to recycle essential survival substances. It is a key technology for crewed deep space exploration (Lunar research stations, Mars landings).

## 2. BLSS Framework and Development Path
BLSS development involves a multidisciplinary approach including biology, environmental engineering, materials science, and systems engineering. The development path moves from individual units to artificial ecosystems, then to Earth-based experimental facilities, and finally to extraterrestrial application.

## 3. Research Progress of Earth-based BLSS

### 3.1 Soviet Union/Russia
*   **BIOS-3 (1972):** 315 m³ volume, 4 compartments. Achieved >90% closure for gas and water; met 40–70% of food needs for a crew of 2-3.

### 3.2 United States
*   **CELSS & ALS:** NASA programs focusing on integrated biological and physical-chemical technologies.
*   **Biosphere 2 (1987-1991):** 1.3 hectares, 1.8 × 10⁵ m³. Proved 100% food production was possible in the second experiment, despite carbon dioxide absorption issues in the first.
*   **Lunar Greenhouse (LGH):** Prototype deployable greenhouse for Moon/Mars missions.

### 3.3 Europe (ESA)
*   **MELiSSA:** Five-compartment system (waste decomposition, nitrification, photosynthetic food production, crew). 
*   **DLR Greenhouse:** Structural design of a semi-cylindrical hybrid structure (rigid end-caps, rigid-inflatable) with a yield of 22 kg/month of edible biomass.

### 3.4 China
*   **Lunar Palace 1:** 500 m³, 120 m² planting area. 
*   **Lunar Palace 365 (2017-2018):** 370-day experiment with 4 crew members. Achieved 100% O2/water regeneration, 83% food regeneration, and 98.2% overall closure.

## 4. Development Path and Evaluation Indices
1.  **Regeneration rate:** Material closure (target 100%).
2.  **Stability:** Reliability of operation.
3.  **Efficiency:** Energy consumption and space required (kWh·m³/person).
4.  **Logistics:** Weight of equipment and labor intensity.

## 5. Three-Stage Strategy for Extraterrestrial BLSS
*   **First Stage:** Hydroponics and ISRU (processing lunar/Martian soil into substrates with waste). Provides all plant food, O2, and water.
*   **Second Stage:** Expansion to large-scale soil-like substrates and animal protein (e.g., mealworms).
*   **Third Stage:** Recycling of inorganic salts; construction of artificial oceans for fish cultivation. 100% biological regeneration of all resources.

## 6. Key Conditions for Gas Balance
1.  Food must come from biosynthesis to ensure oxygen production meets breathing needs.
2.  External organic input must be balanced by internal respiration quotients.
3.  Gas balance requires human intervention and cannot rely solely on biological self-organization.
