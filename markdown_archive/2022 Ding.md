# Karst Cave as Terrestrial Simulation Platform to Test and Design Human Base in Lunar Lava Tube

## Abstract
Developing efficient approaches to building a suitable environment for humans on the moon play a key role in future long-term sustainable lunar exploration activities. Lunar lava tubes offer potential sites to host bases due to their stable structures, suitable temperatures, low radiation doses, and low meteorite impact rates. This paper proposes using Earth’s karst caves as simulation sites for extraterrestrial lava tubes, focusing on three research directions: cave sealing technology, efficient daylight systems, and internal circulation of artificial ecosystems.

## 1. Introduction
Extraterrestrial environments are hostile (extreme temperature, vacuum, low gravity, radiation). Caves provide natural protection. Lunar lava tubes are hollow pipe-shaped caves formed by volcanic activity, offering a hard basalt roof and a stable internal environment.

### 2.2. Safety Assessment
- **Radiation**: Lava tubes deeper than 6m are protected from cosmic rays.
- **Meteorite Impact**: Burial depth protects against most impacts.
- **Moon Dust**: Lava tubes are permanent shadow zones where charged dust is hardly produced.
- **Moon Quakes**: Lava tubes have survived moonquakes for millions of years.

## 3. Using a Karst Cave to Simulate Lunar Lava Tubes

### 3.2. Feasibility of Chongqing Karst Caves Reconstruction
Chongqing karst caves provide comparable size, mild internal temperatures (14-21°C), and insulation. 

### 3.2.3. Insulation and Sealing
Unlike lunar tubes, Earth caves have air convection and must be sealed. Preliminary work suggests that **extruded lunar soil** (simulant) has good sealing properties.

## 4. Significance and Roadmap
The proposed roadmap includes:
1. **Preparatory stage**: Site selection on Earth.
2. **Training**: Training in Karst caves.
3. **Construction**: Automatic construction systems and long-term artificial ecosystems.
4. **Autonomous stage**: Use of in-situ resources to build bases.

## 5. Conclusion
Lunar lava tubes are good candidates for human bases. Energy consumption is significantly lower than on the surface (145.5 kW for 8 astronauts). Karst caves offer a natural simulation platform to test sealing, lighting, and ecosystem technologies.
