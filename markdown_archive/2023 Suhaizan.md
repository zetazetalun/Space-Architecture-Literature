# Regolith sintering and 3D printing for lunar construction: An extensive review on recent progress

## Abstract
In-Situ Resource Utilisation (ISRU) is increasingly being seen as a viable and essential approach to constructing infrastructure for human habitation on the moon. This review presents a comprehensive study on the development of several sintering techniques of lunar regolith simulant to demonstrate its feasibility for ISRU on the moon. Various critical processing parameters are evaluated in pursuit of creating a structural material that can withstand the extreme lunar environment. Key outcomes are summarised and assessed to provide insight into their viability. Finally, current challenges are addressed and potential improvements, and avenues for further research, suggested.

## 1 Background of lunar construction
Recently, national space organisations and aerospace companies have begun working towards exploring the possibility of building a safe and secure human habitat on the Moon. One viable location is the Moon’s South Pole, which contains significant ice deposits. A lunar base must shield humans from extreme temperature fluctuations (-248 °C to 77 °C), solar and cosmic radiation, micrometeoroid impacts, and lunar dust.

## 3 Bulk Sintering

### 3.2 Radiant heat sintering
Radiant sintering involves using heating elements to irradiate and heat a large sample volume. Liquid-phase sintering of lunar soil is more feasible than solid-state sintering. Highland simulants had higher sintering temperatures and lower densities than mare simulants.

### 3.3 Microwave sintering
Microwave sintering (300 MHz-300 GHz) heats powder through dielectric absorption. It is sensitive to material properties, compaction, and high-temperature behavior. Susceptors were found to reduce the total energy required and provide stability, preventing microwave 'runaway'.

### 3.4 Spark plasma sintering (SPS)
SPS utilizes pulsed electric current, external pressure, and rapid heating rates in a vacuum. It produces higher density samples at lower sintering temperatures compared to radiant sintering. Densities as high as 2.9 g/cm³ can be achieved at 1050 °C under 100 MPa pressure.

## 4 Focused Sintering

### 4.1 Laser sintering
Selective laser sintering (SLS) involves rapidly heating a small volume of powder using a high-power laser. Lunar soil is an excellent absorber in the 1000 nm range. SLS achieved compressive strengths up to 30 MPa, but often resulted in high porosity (35-65%).

### 4.2 Solar sintering
Solar sintering focuses sunlight to heat regolith directly. The RegoLight project utilized Xenon arc lamps (simulating solar flux) to print layer-by-layer. Results showed average compressive strength of 2.31 ± 0.30 MPa.

## 5 Additive Manufacturing Applications

### 5.4 Direct Ink Writing (DIW)
DIW creates complex structures using a paste/slurry. Taylor et al. developed a 'soil ink' using JSC-1A simulant and polylactic acid/glycolic acid polymer. Compressive strengths of micro-trusses ranged from 1 to 19 MPa.

## 6 Conclusion
Exploiting local materials (ISRU) is the most pragmatic approach to lunar construction. AM technologies like solar sintering and SLM are capable of high precision, while DIW is suited for larger architectural constructions despite limited mechanical strength. Future work must verify high-temperature behavior of actual lunar soil versus simulants.
