# Exploring Multi-Directional 3D Printing for Enclosures on Earth and Beyond: Sensitivity Analysis of Buildability\n\n**Authors:** Nusrat Tabassum, José Pinto Duarte\n**Affiliation:** Pennsylvania State University\n**Conference:** Earth and Space 2024 (ASCE)\n**DOI:** 10.1061/9780784485736.073\n\n## Abstract\nArchitecture in space demands innovative solutions for constructing robust, airtight habitats. Additive manufacturing (AM) is a promising technology for Mars due to its potential for automated construction. This study aims to lower the printable overhang angle constraints (currently limited to 60 degrees) by exploring multi-directional printing with corbelling, radial, and inclined slicing techniques. A sensitivity analysis study evaluates the impact of geometric variables on the buildability of barrel vaults.\n\n## Introduction\n3D printing in space addresses the challenges of harsh environments. Previous designs, such as Penn State's entry in NASA's 3D-Printed Habitat Challenge, required pointed arches to maintain overhang angles above 60 degrees to avoid formwork. This study explores combining different slicing techniques to enable lower structures and greater design flexibility.\n\n## Methodology\nThe research follows a four-stage workflow: \n1. **Form Generation and Decomposition:** Using shape grammars to create printable patches.\n2. **Sensitivity Analysis:** Using a simulator to predict the number of printed layers until collapse based on geometric variables.\n3. **Transition Strategies:** Choosing infill patterns and toolpaths.\n4. **Optimization and Verification:** Validating the designed toolpaths.\n\n## Sensitivity Analysis Parameters\n- **Constant Variables:** Overhang angle of base structure (V1), Room width/Span (V6).\n- **Independent Variables:** Wall height (V4), Room length (V5), Wall thickness (V7).\n- **Dependent Variables:** Total height (V2), Pitch height (V3), Number of printable layers (V8).\n\n## Results and Findings\n- **Printability:** The 30° printing angle exhibited the highest level of printability, reaching 91% of the total necessary layers (132 out of 146 layers).\n- **Geometric Impact:** Longer room lengths generally contribute to increased potential for printing more layers, though this effect plateaus.\n- **Tradeoffs:** To maximize printable layers, designers should decrease wall height (H-h) while increasing room length (L), or increase room length while decreasing the printing angle.\n- **Max Layers:** The highest number of printable layers achieved for 60°, 45°, and 30° angles were 134, 132, and 132 respectively.\n\n## Conclusion\nSensitivity analysis serves as a crucial tool for predicting structural collapse in 3DCP. Future work involves physical validation through test prints and exploring vaults enclosed by walls on both ends.
