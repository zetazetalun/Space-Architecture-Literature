# Solar 3D printing of lunar regolith

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering of individual layers of lunar regolith simulant, due to the significant flux density variations caused by atmospheric fluctuations. Tests focusing Xenon light demonstrated however the concept with the manufacturing of the first solar 3D printed brick made of lunar regolith simulant. The use of artificial light enabled steady illumination conditions over several hours as required to sinter lunar regolith layer-by-layer. Actual compressive strength of the sintered bricks, below 5 MPa, might be currently too low for a direct lunar application. High porosity levels and weak layer-to-layer bonding were observed on the micro-scale, but ways of improving the technology were found by reducing the thermal gradient and the cooling time between consecutive layers, thus showing the potential of the process.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can be used to reduce the risk and the cost of lunar missions. The use of local resources to build up the lunar habitats would indeed reduce the quantity of material launched from Earth. Additive manufacturing (AM) technologies, commonly called 3D printing, would fit in this perspective. Until today, no 3D printing process, using solely lunar soil, exists for building large structure. Compared to other technologies, the use of concentrated sunlight is the only one using an unlimited source of energy, the Sun, thus reducing the risk of process failure.

## 2. Experimental set-up
Sintering experiments were performed at the solar furnace facility of DLR-Köln. The High-Flux Solar Furnace consists of a 52 m2 flat mirror (heliostat) and a concentrator made of 147 mirror facets. Total power collected can be up to 20 kW with peak flux densities of 4 MW/m2. For long-term stable experiments, a Xenon High-Flux Solar Simulator providing 21 kW (flux density 4.3 MW/m2) was used. A 3-axis table moved the testbed under the beam, synchronized with a powder deposition system dropping 0.1 mm layers.

## 3. As-received material
Lunar regolith simulants JSC-1A and JSC-2A (mare soil simulants) were used. Their mineral composition is close to Apollo samples, containing anorthite feldspath, plagioclase, pyroxene, and olivine.

## 4. Solar additive manufacturing
Initial 1D and 2D sintering tests at the solar oven showed that atmospheric fluctuations (clouds) caused inhomogeneous melting. Stable Xenon light allowed the creation of a 5-mm-thick 3D part in 30 minutes and a larger brick in 3 hours 45 minutes.

## 5. Analysis and discussion
The objective was to demonstrate the feasibility of solar 3D printing. Compression strength was measured on 20 mm cubes.

### Table 2: Compression test results (Average of 12 samples)
| Material | Compression strength (MPa) | Young's Modulus (GPa) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Solar 3D printed regolith | 2.31 ± 0.30 | 0.14 ± 0.06 |

Tomography revealed a layered structure with 0.20 to 0.60 mm thick visible layers. High porosity (pores up to 0.60 mm) and poor bonding between layers were identified as primary weaknesses. Most minerals reached a melt fraction, except plagioclase (melting point up to 1500°C).

## 7. Conclusion
This work demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer using concentrated sunlight. While current strength (2.3 MPa) is insufficient for primary structures, reducing thermal gradients by decreasing cooling time between layers significantly improved strength. Future work includes testing in a vacuum environment and developing mobile printing heads for precursor missions to build roads and habitat shielding.
