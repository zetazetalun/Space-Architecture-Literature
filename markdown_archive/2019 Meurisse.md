# Solar 3D printing of lunar regolith

**Authors:** A. Meurisse, A. Makaya, C. Willsch, M. Sperl
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 152 (2018) 800–810

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering of individual layers of lunar regolith simulant, due to the significant flux density variations caused by atmospheric fluctuations. Tests focusing Xenon light demonstrated however the concept with the manufacturing of the first solar 3D printed brick made of lunar regolith simulant. The use of artificial light enabled steady illumination conditions over several hours as required to sinter lunar regolith layer-by-layer. Actual compressive strength of the sintered bricks, below 5 MPa, might be currently too low for a direct lunar application. High porosity levels and weak layer-to-layer bonding were observed on the micro-scale, but ways of improving the technology were found by reducing the thermal gradient and the cooling time between consecutive layers, thus showing the potential of the process.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can be used to reduce the risk and the cost of lunar missions. Building layer by layer leads to the realization of complex shape parts, with minimal material waste. Until today, no 3D printing process, using solely lunar soil, exists for building large structure. In this work, the authors combined concentrated sunlight with a 3D printing process to create the first 3D printer capable of creating three-dimensional objects made of lunar regolith simulant sintered layer by layer.

## 2. Experimental set-up
Sintering experiments were performed at the solar furnace facility of DLR-Köln. The High-Flux Solar Furnace consists of a 52 m² flat mirror (heliostat) and a concentrator of 147 mirror facets. For stable long-term tests, a Xenon High-Flux Solar Simulator was used. A 3-axis table moved a testbed under the concentrated beam, synchronized with a powder deposition system that dropped off new layers of lunar simulant.

## 5. Analysis and discussion
The objective was to demonstrate feasibility. Results in Table 2 show current mechanical properties. The compression strength is still far from the 150 MPa achieved with traditional sintering. Tomography showed a manufactured layered structure with layers 0.20 to 0.60 mm thick. Open pores up to 0.60 mm were revealed, contributing to weak bonding.

## 7. Conclusion
The feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight was demonstrated. Depositing layers of 0.1 mm, a brick-shape part could be sintered in about 5 h. Improvement of strength was obtained by reducing the time between sintering successive layers, thereby reducing the thermal gradient and improving layer-to-layer bonding. Sintering in a vacuum environment and the use of a mobile printing head need to be tested next.
