# Solar 3D printing of lunar regolith

**Authors:** A. Meurisse, A. Makaya, C. Willsch, M. Sperl
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 152 (2018) 800–810
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2018.06.063

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering of individual layers of lunar regolith simulant, due to the significant flux density variations caused by atmospheric fluctuations. Tests focusing Xenon light demonstrated however the concept with the manufacturing of the first solar 3D printed brick made of lunar regolith simulant. The use of artificial light enabled steady illumination conditions over several hours as required to sinter lunar regolith layer-by-layer. Actual compressive strength of the sintered bricks, below 5 MPa, might be currently too low for a direct lunar application. High porosity levels and weak layer-to-layer bonding were observed on the micro-scale, but ways of improving the technology were found by reducing the thermal gradient and the cooling time between consecutive layers, thus showing the potential of the process.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can be used to reduce the risk and the cost of lunar missions. Use of local materials to build habitats reduces the quantity of material launched from Earth. Additive manufacturing (AM) technologies allow for complex shape realization with minimal waste. This work combines concentrated sunlight with 3D printing to create three-dimensional objects made of sintered lunar regolith layer by layer.

## 2. Experimental Set-up
Sintering experiments were performed at the DLR-Köln solar furnace facility. It utilizes a 52 m² heliostat and a concentrator of 147 mirror facets. A Xenon High-Flux Solar Simulator was also used for stable long-term experiments. A 3-axis table was used to move the testbed under the beam, synchronized with a powder deposition system.

## 3. As-received Material
JSC-1A and JSC-2A mare soil simulants were used. The mineral composition is close to Apollo samples, containing anorthite, plagioclase, pyroxene, and olivine.

## 4. Solar Additive Manufacturing
Testing progressed from 0D (dots) to 1D (lines) and 2D (layers). For 3D printing, a 10g layer of simulant was spread over a sintered 2D part, and the process was repeated. A 5-mm-thick 3D printed part was formed over 30 minutes. Larger bricks (240 x 120 mm²) were produced in approximately 3.75 hours.

## 5. Analysis and Discussion
Compression tests were performed on 20 mm³ cubes. Structural analysis through tomography revealed high porosity (up to 0.60 mm pores) and poor bonding between layers.

## 6. Lunar Application
The technology is envisioned for precursor missions to build infrastructure like shielding, roads, or launch pads prior to human arrival. Swarm robots could be used to assemble the 3D-printed bricks around inflatable habitats.

## 7. Conclusion
The feasibility of layer-by-layer solar sintering was demonstrated. Future improvements require sintering in a vacuum to reduce thermal stresses and enhance mechanical properties.
