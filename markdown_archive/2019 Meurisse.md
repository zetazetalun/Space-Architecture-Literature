# Solar 3D printing of lunar regolith

**Authors:** A. Meurisse, A. Makaya, C. Willsch, M. Sperl
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 152 (2018) 800–810

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering of individual layers of lunar regolith simulant, due to the significant flux density variations caused by atmospheric fluctuations. Tests focusing Xenon light demonstrated however the concept with the manufacturing of the first solar 3D printed brick made of lunar regolith simulant. Actual compressive strength of the sintered bricks, below 5 MPa, might be currently too low for a direct lunar application. High porosity levels and weak layer-to-layer bonding were observed on the micro-scale, but ways of improving the technology were found by reducing the thermal gradient and the cooling time between consecutive layers.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can be used to reduce the risk and the cost of lunar missions. This paper explores the combination of concentrated sunlight with a 3D printing process to create three-dimensional objects made of lunar regolith simulant sintered layer by layer.

## 2. Experimental set-up
Experiments were performed at the DLR High-Flux Solar Furnace and using a Xenon High-Flux Solar Simulator. A 3-axis table was used to move a testbed under the concentrated beam, synchronized with a powder deposition system that dropped layers of lunar simulant.

## 4. Solar additive manufacturing
Initial tests at the solar oven showed that atmospheric fluctuations (clouds) caused inhomogeneous sintering. Subsequent tests in the solar simulator (Xenon lamps) provided stable conditions for 3D printing. Bricks were printed in approximately 5 hours using 0.1 mm thick layers.

## 5. Analysis and Discussion
Mechanical testing and tomography revealed challenges with porosity and interlayer bonding. Higher temperatures and reduced cooling times between layers were found to improve bonding.

## 6. Conclusion
The study successfully demonstrated solar sintering of regolith bricks. While current strengths are low (~2.3 MPa), improvements can be made by sintering in vacuum and optimizing thermal management. The technology is envisioned for precursor missions to build shielding and roads.
