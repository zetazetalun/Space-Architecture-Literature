# Solar 3D printing of lunar regolith

## Metadata
- **Journal:** Acta Astronautica 152 (2018) 800–810
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2018.06.063
- **Authors:** A. Meurisse, A. Makaya, C. Willsch, M. Sperl

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering of individual layers of lunar regolith simulant, due to the significant flux density variations caused by atmospheric fluctuations. Tests focusing Xenon light demonstrated however the concept with the manufacturing of the first solar 3D printed brick made of lunar regolith simulant. The use of artificial light enabled steady illumination conditions over several hours as required to sinter lunar regolith layer-by-layer. Actual compressive strength of the sintered bricks, below 5 MPa, might be currently too low for a direct lunar application. High porosity levels and weak layer-to-layer bonding were observed on the micro-scale, but ways of improving the technology were found by reducing the thermal gradient and the cooling time between consecutive layers, thus showing the potential of the process.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can reduce the risk and cost of lunar missions. Building elements from local soil to create roads, launchpads, and habitat shelters is essential. Previous studies used binders, microwave, or laser sintering. This work focuses on using concentrated sunlight—an unlimited energy source—to 3D print structures layer-by-layer.

## 2. Experimental Set-up
- **Solar Oven:** High-Flux Solar Furnace at DLR-Köln (52 m² heliostat, 147 mirror concentrator).
- **Solar Simulator:** Xenon short arc lamps (21 kW total power) used to simulate stable lunar sunlight without atmospheric interference.
- **Additive System:** A 3-axis table moved a testbed under the beam, synchronized with a powder deposition system to add 0.1 mm layers.

## 4. Results and Discussion
- **Feasibility:** Successfully printed the first 3D solar-sintered brick.
- **Sintering Parameters:** Scanning speed of 48-50 mm/s with 14-20 mm line spacing produced a 5-mm-thick part in 30 minutes. A full brick (240 x 120 mm) took ~3h 45m.
- **Mechanical Properties:**

- **Structural Issues:** High porosity and poor interlayer bonding were observed via CT scans and SEM. These were attributed to rapid cooling and thermal gradients.

## 5. Conclusion
The study proves that solar 3D printing of lunar regolith is possible. Future improvements involve performing the process in a vacuum to eliminate convection cooling and using mobile printing heads (rovers) to build large-scale infrastructure like shielding shells and roads.
