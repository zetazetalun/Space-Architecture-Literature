# Solar 3D Printing of Lunar Regolith

**Authors:** A. Meurisse, A. Makaya, C. Willsch, M. Sperl  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 152 (2018) 800–810  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2018.06.063

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering of individual layers due to atmospheric fluctuations. Tests using a Xenon solar simulator successfully manufactured the first solar 3D printed brick made of lunar regolith simulant. Compressive strength was found to be below 5 MPa, currently low for direct lunar application, but improvements were identified by reducing thermal gradients and cooling times between layers. This technology is suitable for shielding habitats and building roads prior to the arrival of astronauts.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) reduces the risk and cost of lunar missions by using local materials for habitat construction. Additive Manufacturing (AM) fits this perspective by allowing automated construction of complex shapes with minimal waste. While previous research used binders or microwave/laser sintering, solar sintering uses the Sun as an unlimited energy source.

## 2. Experimental Set-up
### 2.1. Solar Furnace
Experiments were performed at the DLR-Köln High-Flux Solar Furnace. The setup uses a 52 m² heliostat reflecting light onto a 147-mirror concentrator, providing up to 20 kW power with peak flux densities of 4 MW/m².

### 2.2. Solar Simulator
For stable conditions, a Xenon High-Flux Solar Simulator was used, providing 4.1 kW power and 1.2 MW/m² flux density at a 20 mm focal spot. A 3-axis table moved the testbed under the beam, synchronized with a powder deposition system.

## 3. Materials
JSC-1A and JSC-2A lunar regolith simulants were used. JSC-2A contains a glassy phase with a glass transition at 620 °C and melts completely at 1070 °C.

## 4. Solar Additive Manufacturing
Layers of 0.1 mm regolith were deposited and sintered. Sintering at the solar furnace (Earth sunlight) was challenging due to clouds. However, the solar simulator allowed for a continuous process. A 5 mm thick 3D printed part was formed in 30 minutes, and a large brick (240 x 120 mm) was printed in 3 hours and 45 minutes.

## 5. Analysis and Results
Mechanical tests on 20 mm³ cubes cut from the printed bricks showed:

| Material | Compression strength (MPa) | Young's Modulus (GPa) |
|---|---|---|
| Solar 3D printed regolith | 2.31 ± 0.30 | 0.14 ± 0.06 |

Tomography revealed high porosity and poor bonding between layers. SEM images showed that plagioclase minerals often remained un-melted at the interface, acting as a barrier to fusion. Strengthening was achieved by reducing the time between layer deposition to 20 seconds, maintaining a higher surface temperature (~525 °C) to facilitate better interlayer bonding.

## 6. Lunar Application
On the Moon, a heliostat could track the sun and reflect light onto a rover-mounted concentrator or Fresnel lens. Swarm robots could then assemble the 3D printed bricks around inflatable (Class II) habitats for radiation and micrometeoroid shielding.

## 7. Conclusion
The feasibility of layer-by-layer solar sintering of lunar regolith was proven. While strength is currently low, improving thermal management and printing in a vacuum (to eliminate convection) are expected to enhance the mechanical properties of the final structures.
