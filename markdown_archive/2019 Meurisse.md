# Solar 3D printing of lunar regolith

**Authors:** A. Meurisse, A. Makaya, C. Willsch, M. Sperl  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 152 (2018) 800–810  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2018.06.063

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering of individual layers of lunar regolith simulant, due to the significant flux density variations caused by atmospheric fluctuations. Tests focusing Xenon light demonstrated however the concept with the manufacturing of the first solar 3D printed brick made of lunar regolith simulant. Actual compressive strength of the sintered bricks, below 5 MPa, might be currently too low for a direct lunar application. High porosity levels and weak layer-to-layer bonding were observed on the micro-scale, but ways of improving the technology were found by reducing the thermal gradient and the cooling time between consecutive layers. Adaptation of the technology on the Moon is presented to illustrate the suitability of solar additive manufacturing on the lunar surface, as a possible technique for shielding habitats and building roads prior to arrival of the astronauts.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can be used to reduce the risk and the cost of lunar missions. Building technologies should be flexible and able to run with minimal human assistance. This work combines concentrated sunlight with a 3D printing process to create the first 3D printer capable of creating three-dimensional objects made of lunar regolith simulant sintered layer by layer.

## 2. Experimental Setup
- **Solar Oven:** DLR-Köln High-Flux Solar Furnace (52 m² heliostat, 147 mirror facets). Peak flux: 4 MW/m².
- **Solar Simulator:** Xenon High-Flux Solar Simulator (ten Xenon short arc lamps). Provided 4.1 kW and 1.2 MW/m² flux density on a 20 mm spot.
- **3D Printing System:** A 3-axis table moved the testbed under the beam, synchronized with a powder deposition system.

## 4. Results
### 4.1 Mechanical Properties
Compression tests were performed on 20x20x20 mm³ cubes cut from the sintered bricks.

### 4.2 Microstructure
Tomography and SEM analysis revealed:
- **Layer Thickness:** deposited at 0.1 mm, but sintered layers varied from 0.20 to 0.60 mm.
- **Porosity:** Open pores up to 0.60 mm and closed pores up to 0.45 mm were observed.
- **Bonding:** Poor bonding between layers was attributed to high thermal gradients and rapid cooling on the edges.

## 5. Conclusion
The feasibility of solar 3D printing for lunar infrastructure (shielding, roads) was demonstrated. Future improvements require sintering in a vacuum environment and optimizing scanning strategies to reduce thermal stresses and improve interlayer bonding.
