# Solar 3D printing of lunar regolith

**Authors:** A. Meurisse, A. Makaya, C. Willsch, M. Sperl  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 152 (2018) 800–810  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.actaastro.2018.06.063

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering of individual layers... Tests focusing Xenon light demonstrated however the concept with the manufacturing of the first solar 3D printed brick made of lunar regolith simulant. Actual compressive strength of the sintered bricks, below 5 MPa, might be currently too low for a direct lunar application. Adaptation of the technology on the Moon is presented to illustrate the suitability of solar additive manufacturing on the lunar surface, as a possible technique for shielding habitats and building roads prior to arrival of the astronauts.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can be used to reduce the risk and the cost of lunar missions. Building layer by layer leads to the realization of complex shape parts, with minimal material waste and post-processing work. Compared to other technologies (microwave, laser), the use of concentrated sunlight is the only one using an unlimited source of energy, the Sun, thus reducing the risk of process failure.

## 2. Experimental set-up
Sintering experiments were performed at the solar furnace facility of DLR-Köln. The High-Flux Solar Furnace consists of a 52 m² flat mirror (heliostat) and a concentrator made of 147 mirror facets. Experiments using Xenon light were carried out with the Xenon High-Flux Solar Simulator, providing a power of 21 kW with a flux density of 4.3 MW/m².

## 3. As-received material
Lunar soil is 85% anorthositic in highlands and 15% basaltic in maria. JSC-1A and JSC-2A simulants were used in this research work. JSC-2A contains a glassy phase with a glass transition at 620 °C and melting of all constituents at 1070 °C.

## 5. Analysis and discussion
The objective was to demonstrate the feasibility of solar 3D printing of a lunar regolith brick. Compression strength was measured on 20x20x20 mm³ cubes. Non-destructive structural analysis (Tomography) showed a manufactured layered structure with thickness variations (0.20 to 0.60 mm). Open pores (up to 0.60 mm) revealed poor bonding between layers.

## 7. Conclusion
In this work, the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer by layer solely using concentrated sunlight was demonstrated. Improvement of the sintered material's strength was obtained by reducing the time between sintering of successive layers, thereby reducing thermal gradient and thermal stresses and improving layer-to-layer bonding. Sintering in a vacuum environment and the use of a mobile printing head need to be tested to increase the TRL.
