# Solar 3D printing of lunar regolith

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering of individual layers of lunar regolith simulant, due to the significant flux density variations caused by atmospheric fluctuations. Tests focusing Xenon light demonstrated however the concept with the manufacturing of the first solar 3D printed brick made of lunar regolith simulant. The use of artificial light enabled steady illumination conditions over several hours as required to sinter lunar regolith layer-by-layer. Actual compressive strength of the sintered bricks, below 5 MPa, might be currently too low for a direct lunar application. High porosity levels and weak layer-to-layer bonding were observed on the micro-scale, but ways of improving the technology were found by reducing the thermal gradient and the cooling time between consecutive layers, thus showing the potential of the process. Adaptation of the technology on the Moon is presented to illustrate the suitability of solar additive manufacturing on the lunar surface, as a possible technique for shielding habitats and building roads prior to arrival of the astronauts.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can be used to reduce the risk and the cost of lunar missions. Additive manufacturing (AM) technologies, commonly called 3D printing, would fit in this perspective. Research on sintering lunar soil simulant were carried out using microwave, laser, classic resistive radial elements and concentrated solar light. Compared to other technologies, the use of concentrated sunlight is the only one using an unlimited source of energy, the Sun, thus reducing the risk of process failure.

## 2. Experimental set-up
Sintering experiments were performed at the solar furnace facility of DLR-Köln. The High-Flux Solar Furnace consists of a 52 m² flat mirror (heliostat) reflecting light onto a concentrator made of 147 mirror facets. For stable long-term experiments, the Xenon High-Flux Solar Simulator was used, providing a power of 4.1 kW and a flux density of 1.2 MW/m² focused on a Ø20 mm spot. A 3-axis table was used to move a testbed under the concentrated solar beam, synchronized with a powder deposition system.

## 3. As-received material
Lunar regolith simulants JSC-1A and JSC-2A were used. These mare soil simulants have a low titanium content and mineral compositions close to Apollo samples (anorthite feldspath plagioclase, pyroxene, and olivine minerals).

## 4. Solar additive manufacturing
Initial tests at the solar oven showed that atmospheric variations (clouds) caused inhomogeneous sintering. Subsequent tests in the solar simulator used a scanning speed of 48 mm/s and a layer thickness of approximately 0.1 mm. A 3D printed part of 5-mm thickness was produced in 30 min, while a larger brick (240 x 120 mm) was produced in 3 h 45 min.

## 5. Analysis and discussion
The objective was to demonstrate the feasibility of solar 3D printing of a lunar regolith brick. Compression strength was measured on cubes cut from the bricks.

| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Solar 3D printed regolith | 2.31 ± 0.30 | 0.14 ± 0.06 |

Tomography revealed open pores up to 0.60 mm and closed porosity up to 0.45 mm. Weak bonding between layers was attributed to high thermal gradients and fast cooling. Improvement was observed when reducing the time between layer depositions (e.g., from 60s to 20s), which kept the surface temperature higher (approx. 525°C) and promoted better layer merging.

## 6. Lunar application
Two main implementation options are proposed for the Moon: 1) A fixed heliostat reflecting light to a fixed 3D printer creating bricks for swarm robots to assemble, or 2) A mobile printer (rover-mounted) using a Fresnel lens to build continuous shells or roads directly on the surface.

## 7. Conclusion
The feasibility of layer-by-layer solar sintering of lunar regolith was demonstrated. A brick-shaped part was sintered in about 5 hours using 0.1 mm layers. While current strengths are low, reducing the thermal gradient and operating in a vacuum are identified as pathways to increase mechanical properties and Technology Readiness Level (TRL).
