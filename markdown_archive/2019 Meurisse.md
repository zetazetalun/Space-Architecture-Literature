# Solar 3D printing of lunar regolith

**Authors:** A. Meurisse, A. Makaya, C. Willsch, M. Sperl  
**Journal:** Acta Astronautica 152 (2018) 800–810  
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.actaastro.2018.06.063

## Abstract
The authors demonstrated the feasibility of sintering lunar regolith layer-by-layer solely using concentrated sunlight. First trials using actual concentrated sunlight led to an inhomogeneous sintering... The use of artificial light enabled steady illumination conditions over several hours as required to sinter lunar regolith layer-by-layer. Actual compressive strength of the sintered bricks, below 5 MPa, might be currently too low for a direct lunar application. Adaptation of the technology on the Moon is presented as a possible technique for shielding habitats and building roads prior to arrival of the astronauts.

## 1. Introduction
In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can reduce the risk and cost of lunar missions. Building elements from lunar soil can create roads, launchpads, and habitat shelters. Additive manufacturing (AM) allows for complex shape realization with minimal material waste. While microwave and laser sintering have been studied, concentrated sunlight uses an unlimited energy source (the Sun).

## 2. Experimental Set-up
Experiments were performed at the solar furnace facility of DLR-Köln. 
- **Solar Oven:** 52 m² flat mirror (heliostat) reflecting onto a concentrator with 147 facets.
- **Solar Simulator:** Ten Xenon short arc lamps providing 21 kW power with flux density of 4.3 MW/m².
- **3D Printing System:** A 3-axis table moving a testbed under a fixed concentrated beam, synchronized with a powder deposition system.

## 3. Materials
- **JSC-1A and JSC-2A:** Lunar mare soil simulants with low titanium content.

## 4. Key Results
- **1D/2D Sintering:** Successful sintering of lines and squares. 
- **3D Sintering:** First 3D printed brick produced in approximately 5 hours (at solar oven) or 3.75 hours (at solar simulator).
- **Mechanical Properties:** Compressive strength averaged 2.31 ± 0.30 MPa; Young's Modulus averaged 0.14 ± 0.06 GPa.

## 5. Analysis
Tomography revealed a manufactured layered structure with high porosity (open pores up to 0.60 mm). Weak bonding between layers was attributed to thermal gradients and rapid cooling. Improvements were found by reducing the time between depositing layers to maintain higher surface temperatures (e.g., 525°C), facilitating better interlayer diffusion.

## 6. Lunar Application
Proposes mobile rovers equipped with Fresnel lenses or heliostats to build habitat shells and roads. Precursor missions could prepare infrastructure before crew arrival.
