# Influence of in-situ lunar temperature on the pore structure and solid phases of alkali-activated lunar regolith simulant

**Journal:** Journal of Building Engineering 98 (2024) 111162

**Authors:** Yizhou Yao, Chao Liu, Wei Zhang, Huawei Liu, Chao Zhu

## Abstract
The construction of extraterrestrial bases has become an indispensable step in the active exploration of deep space. In this study, alkali-activated lunar regolith simulant (AALRS) activated by sodium hydroxide (SH) and sodium silicate (SS) were prepared and then cured at various lunar temperatures. The compressive strength was tested, and the pore structure characteristics were characterized using mercury intrusion porosimetry (MIP), followed by further in-depth quantitative analysis through fractal theory. The solid phases were characterized using thermogravimetry analysis (TGA) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM). It is found that all the fractal curves are divided into three regions, and the fractal dimensions in all regions are between 2 and 3, suggesting the pores of AALRS paste always have self-similar physical significance. The fractal dimension D1 of the small pore is the largest, followed by fractal dimension D3 of the great pore and fractal dimension D2 of the middle pore. The increase in modulus and temperature leads to an increase in the pore surface area, fractal dimension, amount of N-A-S-H gel and compressive strength and a decrease in total pore volume and characteristic pore diameter, which is mainly attributed to the nucleation effect of soluble Si. The turning pore diameter at the micrometer scale and excessive porosity of AALRS paste, which is distinctly different from that of common cementitious materials, demonstrates looser pore structure of AALRS paste. Based on these results, the influence mechanism of in-situ lunar temperature on the performance of AALRS paste is profoundly discussed and elucidated, which provides a deep understanding of designing and tailoring the various properties of AALRS material.

## 1. Introduction
The establishment of a lunar infrastructure is a critical and indispensable step towards achieving extraterrestrial habitats. Recent missions like Chang’E-6 and programs like Artemis aim to establish permanent lunar bases. Cement-based materials used on Earth are unsuitable due to transportation costs; thus, lunar regolith is viewed as a vital in-situ resource. Alkali-activated lunar regolith (lunar regolith geopolymer) is considered a promising solution due to high strength, durability, and low energy requirements.

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.2. Raw materials
- **Lunar regolith simulant:** LRS-XJ produced in Huinan town, Jilin Province.
- **Basalt aggregate:** Coarse (BCA) and fine (BFA).
- **Alkaline activator:** Sodium hydroxide (SH) and sodium silicate (SS) with an initial modulus of 2.25.

### 2.3. Design of curing schemes
Four curing schemes were designed based on actual lunar temperature variations at 30° latitude:
- **NM:** Normal temperature (20 ± 2 °C).
- **LH:** Low to high temperature.
- **HL:** High to low temperature.
- **HH:** High to high temperature.

## 4. Results and discussion

### 4.6. Compressive strength
The increase in silicate modulus and temperature leads to rapid increases in compressive strength. Under the HH scheme, the compressive strength of AALRS paste increased significantly. When the modulus is 1, SSP1.0-HH achieved the highest value of 18.46 MPa. High temperatures promote the dissolution of the original structure and the diffusion of ions, leading to more nucleation sites and denser N-A-S-H gel precipitation.

## 5. Conclusions
1. Increased silicate modulus decreases total pore volume and diameter, densifying the gel matrix.
2. AALRS paste lacks gel pores (≤10 nm) and has higher porosity than terrestrial cementitious materials.
3. High temperatures (HH scheme) are beneficial for strength development, reaching 18.46 MPa.
4. Early exposure to cryogenic temperatures causes irreversible damage and inhibits hydration, but high-temperature pre-curing provides durability against subsequent cold cycles.
5. Low-latitude lunar regions (within 30°) provide sufficient high-temperature windows for optimal curing.
