# 3D printed sulfur-regolith concrete performance evaluation for waterless extraterrestrial robotic construction

**Authors:** Ilerioluwa Giwa, Mary Dempsey, Michael Fiske, Ali Kazemian  
**Journal:** Automation in Construction 165 (2024) 105571  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.autcon.2024.105571

## Abstract
By leveraging the capabilities of construction 3D printing, building structures in harsh extraterrestrial environments is conceivable. Sulfur concrete is a waterless construction material that offers great potential to replace Portland cement concrete (PCC) in extraterrestrial construction. This study evaluates Martian sulfur-regolith concrete (SRC) for 3D printing, investigating shape stability, mechanical properties, and performance under vacuum conditions.

## 1. Introduction
Conventional construction methods relying on manual efforts are unsuitable for the Moon and Mars due to limited human workforce and hazardous conditions. Extrusion-based Construction 3D Printing (C3DP) and In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) are essential. Sulfur concrete (SC) is a waterless alternative to hydraulic cement-based concretes which require precious water resources.

## 3. Methodology
### 3.1 Materials and Mixture Proportions

- **Aggregate:** Mars Global Simulant (MGS-1).

## 4. Results and Discussion
### 4.2.1 Shape Stability
- Layer deformations occurred only at the shortest interlayer time gap (45 s).
- At 130°C and 150°C, 45 s delay caused 10% and 23% deformation, respectively.
- Longer gaps (120 s, 180 s) showed 0% deformation due to rapid solidification.

### 4.3.1 Strength Development
- **SRC (12h):** Gained 85% of ultimate strength (8 MPa).
- **SRC (7-day):** 9.4 MPa flexural strength.
- **PCC (12h):** Gained only 19% of its ultimate strength (1.1 MPa).

### 4.3.2 Vacuum Performance (55°C)
- **SRC weight loss:** 0.92% after 7 days (sublimation).
- **MSRC weight loss:** Significantly lower than SRC (up to 85% reduction compared to SRC).
- **PCC weight loss:** Significant loss of free moisture, leading to a 78% reduction in flexural strength compared to controls.

## 5. Conclusions
1. Martian SRC was successfully 3D printed, showing feasibility for off-world construction.
2. SRC exhibits rapid strength development (85% in 12 hours) compared to PCC.
3. Printed SRC can be remelted and recycled without loss of strength.
4. DCPD modification enhances strength by 44% and dramatically reduces sublimation in vacuum.
