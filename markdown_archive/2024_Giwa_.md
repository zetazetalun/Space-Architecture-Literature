# 3D printed sulfur-regolith concrete performance evaluation for waterless extraterrestrial robotic construction

## Abstract
By leveraging the capabilities of construction 3D printing, building structures in harsh extraterrestrial environments is conceivable. Sulfur concrete is a waterless construction material that offers great potential to replace Portland cement concrete (PCC) in extraterrestrial construction. The shape stability of 3D printed Martian sulfur-regolith concrete (SRC) was found to benefit from a lower substrate layer temperature. However, this comes at the cost of flexural strength, resulting in up to 53% strength loss. The printed SRC specimens demonstrated a significantly faster strength development rate (gaining about 85% of the ultimate strength after only 12 h) compared to the printed PCC. The printed SRC specimens also outperformed the PCC specimens in vacuum conditions at higher temperatures. Furthermore, modifying the SRC materials with Dicyclopentadiene resulted in up to 44% strength increase and minimized the sublimation rate of the printed specimens in vacuum, especially at an elevated temperature.

## 1. Introduction
NASA’s Artemis program aims to establish a sustained human presence on the Moon as a precursor for missions to Mars. Infrastructure such as landing pads, habitats, and protective shields are needed to protect assets from radiation and micrometeoroids. Extrusion-based Construction 3D Printing (C3DP) holds significant potential for off-world construction due to its robotic and autonomous capabilities.

## 2. Background
Sulfur concrete (SC) is a waterless alternative to Portland cement concrete (PCC). Elemental sulfur can be extracted from Martian or Lunar surface deposits using in-situ resource utilization (ISRU). SC possesses superior mechanical strength and rapid hardening properties compared to conventional concrete.

### 2.1. Properties of sulfur concrete for terrestrial applications
Sulfur binder is typically modified with additives like dicyclopentadiene (DCPD) to prevent crystallization into unstable allotropes that lead to shrinkage and cracking.

### 2.2. Properties of sulfur concrete for extraterrestrial applications
SC is suitable for locations where temperatures remain below 110°C, such as the Moon's permanently shadowed regions or most Martian locales. A primary concern is sulfur sublimation under vacuum conditions.

Two custom extrusion-based systems were used: a gantry-type system for high-temperature SRC and a separate system for PCC. Nozzles were 40mm x 20mm.

## 4. Results and Discussion

### 4.1. Fresh-state properties
SRC showed higher density (2.310 g/cm³) than PCC (2.096 g/cm³). SRC flowability and slump increased with temperature (130°C to 150°C).

### 4.2. Performance of printed SRC specimens
*   **Shape Stability:** Layer deformations (up to 23%) occurred at short interlayer gaps (45s) when the bottom layer was still molten. Longer delays (120s+) ensured stability.
*   **Flexural Strength:** Higher printing temperatures (150°C) improved interlayer adhesion. 8-layer specimens showed up to 53% strength loss if substrate temperatures were too low (cold joints).
*   **DCPD Modification:** Modification with 10% DCPD increased compressive strength by 44% and flexural strength by 34%.
*   **Recyclability:** Printed SRC can be remelted and reprinted without loss of mechanical strength, supporting a cradle-to-cradle sustainability approach.

### 4.3. Performance comparison: SRC vs PCC
*   **Strength Development:** SRC reached 85% of ultimate strength in 12h, whereas PCC reached only 19% in the same period.
*   **Vacuum Performance:** PCC suffered significant strength loss (up to 78%) in vacuum due to moisture evaporation. SRC weight loss in vacuum was negligible at room temperature but increased at 55°C. DCPD-modified SRC reduced sublimation by 85% at elevated temperatures.

## 5. Conclusions
1. SRC is feasible for waterless robotic C3DP using Martian/Lunar resources.
2. SRC exhibits rapid strength development (12h) compared to PCC (28 days).
3. DCPD modification enhances strength and limits sublimation in vacuum.
4. SRC is fully recyclable through reheating.
