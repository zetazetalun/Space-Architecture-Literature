# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 449 (2024) 138517  
**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Jiale Huang, Xiangyu Wang, Ming Jen Tan, Wei She, Hao Zhou, Guoqing Geng

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial exploration, represented by Lunar base construction, has been promoted worldwide as a multi-disciplinary, cutting-edge and strategic issue. Herein we proposed a novel extraterrestrial construction material composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and only 4 wt% of silicate adhesive. This silicate-regolith composite can be directly self-hardened in vacuum within 4.5 h without any curing to achieve 29 MPa of hardening strength. Meanwhile, it shows comprehensive durability with stable microstructures and >70 % of residual strength against long-term vacuum exposure, huge temperature difference and intense γ-ray and proton radiations. Its in-situ solidification and durability originate from vacuum dehydration, where silica clusters in solution spontaneously cohere into a solid network with strong connections of silica tetrahedra and bound water. A moderate silicate modulus around 2.5 and appropriate modifications can disperse finer silica clusters to increase oligomer silica units in solution and enhance hardening strength. Compared with lunar geopolymer and other existing extraterrestrial construction materials, this vacuum self-hardening composite allows a cryogenic preparation even below −40 °C using diverse regolith with a wide range of compositions and sizes, proposing a facile, durable and versatile solution towards in-situ extraterrestrial constructions.

## 1. Introduction
Infrastructure on the Moon faces rigorous environments: temperature differences (-200 °C to 120 °C), high vacuum (10⁻¹⁰ to 10⁻⁹ Pa), radiation, moonquakes, and meteoroid impacts. Transportation costs make ISRU (In-Situ Resource Utilization) essential. Existing materials like sintered regolith (low strength, high energy), polymers/sulfur (durability issues), or geopolymers (requiring sealed heat curing) have significant drawbacks.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1. Materials
- **Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS):** LRS-1 (Apollo-17 simulant), LRS-2 (Apollo-14), LRS-3 (Apollo-12).
- **Martian Regolith Simulant (MRS):** Spirit-355 simulant.
- **Binders:** Sodium silicate (SS) with adjusted modulus (2.0, 2.5, 3.0) and modifying agents (sodium pyrophosphate, sodium dodecyl sulfate, hydrothermal treatment).

### 2.2. Sample Preparation
1. Regolith and silicate solution mixed (Mass ratio 0.14:1).
2. Cold-pressed at 9.5 MPa into 40x10x10 mm³ green bodies.
3. Exposed to high vacuum (<10 Pa).
4. Simulated lunar environment: Temperature cycling between -196 °C and 78 °C.

## 3. Results and Discussion
### 3.1. Self-hardening and Durability
- **Hardening:** Rapid water loss in vacuum leads to self-hardening within 4.5 h.
- **Strength:** SS2.5 modulus produced optimal results (Flexural: 5.48 MPa, Compressive: 18.19 MPa). Modified binders (SS2.5HT) reached flexural strength of 6.54 MPa and compressive strength of 20.34 MPa within 4.5 h.
- **Long-term performance:** After 4 lunar cycles (108 days), SLRC reserved 71-81% of flexural strength and 88-98% of compressive strength compared to initial hardening.

### 3.2. Microstructure
SEM analysis shows regolith particles covered with membranous silicate binders bridging the particles. Porosity remains stable around 34.7 vol% (hardening stage) to 37.4 vol% (after first midday), becoming invariant after the first lunar cycle.

## 5. Conclusion
The Silicate-Lunar Regolith Composite (SLRC) offers a unique vacuum self-hardening mechanism that requires no external heat, making it suitable for cryogenic regions (poles) or sunless lava tubes. It utilizes low binder percentages (~4 wt%) and maintains high durability against radiation and extreme thermal cycles.
