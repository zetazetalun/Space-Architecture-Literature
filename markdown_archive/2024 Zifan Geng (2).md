# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material ## Abstract This paper proposes a novel extraterrestrial construction material (SLRC) composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and 4 wt% silicate adhesive. The material self-hardens in vacuum within 4.5 hours, reaching 29 MPa compressive strength. It demonstrates durability against vacuum exposure, thermal cycling (-196 to 78 C), and radiation. The hardening mechanism is vacuum dehydration, which causes silica clusters to form a solid network. ## 1. Introduction Construction on the Moon or Mars requires in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) to reduce transport costs. Current methods like sintering (high energy) or polymer binders (poor durability) have limitations. SLRC offers a low-energy, durable alternative. ## 2. Materials and Methods Researchers used lunar regolith simulants (LRS-1/2/3) and Martian simulant (MRS). Sodium silicate solutions were used as binders. Samples were cold-pressed (9.5 MPa) and exposed to vacuum (<10 Pa) for dehydration. ## 3. Results and Discussion ### 3.1 Self-hardening SLRC achieved 29 MPa compressive strength and 5.48 MPa flexural strength within 4.5 hours of vacuum exposure. ### 3.2 Durability Samples retained >70% flexural and 88-98% compressive strength after 4 months of simulated lunar environment cycles. Radiation tests (gamma and proton) showed no significant degradation. ### 3.4 Comparison with other materials Table 1 compares SLRC with sintering, thermite welding, and geopolymers. SLRC requires less energy than sintering and is more stable than sulfur or polymer concrete. ## 4. Conclusion SLRC is a versatile solution for lunar polar regions and lava tubes, requiring only cold pressing and vacuum exposure to achieve structural integrity.
