# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material

**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Jiale Huang, Xiangyu Wang, Ming Jen Tan, Wei She, Hao Zhou, Guoqing Geng  
**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 449 (2024) 138517  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.conbuildmat.2024.138517

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial exploration, represented by Lunar base construction, has been promoted worldwide as a multi-disciplinary, cutting-edge and strategic issue. Herein we proposed a novel extraterrestrial construction material composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and only 4 wt% of silicate adhesive. This silicate-regolith composite can be directly self-hardened in vacuum within 4.5 h without any curing to achieve 29 MPa of hardening strength. Meanwhile, it shows comprehensive durability with stable microstructures and >70 % of residual strength against long-term vacuum exposure, huge temperature difference and intense γ-ray and proton radiations. Its in-situ solidification and durability originate from vacuum dehydration, where silica clusters in solution spontaneously cohere into a solid network with strong connections of silica tetrahedra and bound water. A moderate silicate modulus around 2.5 and appropriate modifications can disperse finer silica clusters to increase oligomer silica units in solution and enhance hardening strength. Compared with lunar geopolymer and other existing extraterrestrial construction materials, this vacuum self-hardening composite allows a cryogenic preparation even below − 40 ℃ using diverse regolith with a wide range of compositions and sizes, proposing a facile, durable and versatile solution towards in-situ extraterrestrial constructions.

## 1. Introduction
The infrastructure on the Moon will face rigorous environments including huge temperature differences (-200 °C to 120 °C), high vacuum (10⁻¹⁰ to 10⁻⁹ Pa), intense radiation, and meteoroid impact. Transporting materials is costly, necessitating in-situ resource utilization (ISRU). Existing materials like sintered regolith (low strength), polymers (radiation aging), and geopolymers (require high-temperature curing) have limitations. This study proposes Silicate-Lunar Regolith Composite (SLRC) which utilizes vacuum dehydration for self-hardening.

## 2. Materials and Methods
- **Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS):** LRS-1 (Apollo-17 simulant), LRS-2 (Apollo-14), LRS-3 (Apollo-12), and Martian Regolith Simulant (MRS).
- **Silicate Binder:** Sodium silicate with varied moduli (2.0 to 3.0) and modification agents.
- **Preparation:** Mixing silicate solution with regolith (0.14:1 ratio), cold-pressing (9.5 MPa), and immediate vacuum exposure (<10 Pa).
- **Testing:** Flexural and compressive strength tests, SEM, XRD, NMR, and molecular dynamic (MD) simulations.

## 3. Results and Discussion
- **Hardening:** SS2.5 achieved 18.19 MPa compressive strength in 4.5h of vacuum exposure. Modified SS2.5HT reached 20.34 MPa.
- **Cryogenic Processing:** SLRC was successfully prepared at -40 °C, achieving ~19.5 MPa compressive strength after 15h in vacuum.
- **Durability:** After long-term exposure (108 days) and thermal cycles (-196 to 78 °C), SLRC retained 71-81% of flexural and 88-98% of compressive strength.
- **Radiation Resistance:** Maintained ~23.3 MPa compressive strength after intense γ-ray and proton attacks.

## 4. Conclusion
The SLRC offers a versatile solution for in-situ extraterrestrial construction. It requires minimal terrestrial materials (4 wt%), works across a wide range of regolith types, and can be processed in cryogenic/shadowed regions without artificial heating by leveraging the vacuum environment for solidification.
