# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 449 (2024) 138517  
**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Jiale Huang, Xiangyu Wang, Ming Jen Tan, Wei She, Hao Zhou, Guoqing Geng

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial exploration, represented by Lunar base construction, has been promoted worldwide as a multidisciplinary, cutting-edge and strategic issue. Herein we proposed a novel extraterrestrial construction material composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and only 4 wt% of silicate adhesive. This silicate-regolith composite can be directly self-hardened in vacuum within 4.5 h without any curing to achieve 29 MPa of hardening strength. Meanwhile, it shows comprehensive durability with stable microstructures and >70 % of residual strength against long-term vacuum exposure, huge temperature difference and intense γ-ray and proton radiations. Its in-situ solidification and durability originate from vacuum dehydration, where silica clusters in solution spontaneously cohere into a solid network with strong connections of silica tetrahedra and bound water. A moderate silicate modulus around 2.5 and appropriate modifications can disperse finer silica clusters to increase oligomer silica units in solution and enhance hardening strength. Compared with lunar geopolymer and other existing extraterrestrial construction materials, this vacuum self-hardening composite allows a cryogenic preparation even below − 40 ℃ using diverse regolith with a wide range of compositions and sizes, proposing a facile, durable and versatile solution towards in-situ extraterrestrial constructions.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar construction has become a multi-disciplinary, cutting-edge and strategic research issue. Infrastructure on the Moon, especially the outer shell for loading and/or shielding, will face rigorous environments including huge temperature differences (-200 °C to 120 °C), high vacuum ($10^{-10}$ to $10^{-9}$ Pa), intense radiation, and meteoroid impacts. Transportation costs remain a significant barrier, necessitating In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU).

## 2. Materials and Methods
- **Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS):** LRS-1 (Apollo-17 simulant), LRS-2, LRS-3, and MRS (Martian regolith simulant).
- **Binder:** Sodium silicate (SS) solution with varying moduli (2.0, 2.5, 3.0) and modifiers (sodium pyrophosphate, SDS, hydrothermal treatment).
- **Sample Preparation:** Regolith mixed with silicate solution (ratio 0.14:1). Cold-pressed at 9.5 MPa and placed in a vacuum chamber (<10 Pa) to simulate lunar conditions.

## 4. Conclusion
The research proposes a novel SLRC that self-hardens in vacuum via dehydration without curing. A silicate modulus of 2.5 was found to be optimal. The material retains over 72% flexural and 87% compressive strength after long-term vacuum exposure and extreme thermal cycling, proving its viability for lunar and Martian surface construction.
