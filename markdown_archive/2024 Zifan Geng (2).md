# Silicate-lunar regolith composite: A vacuum self-hardened and comprehensively durable extraterrestrial construction material

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 449 (2024) 138517  
**Authors:** Zifan Geng, Lizhi Zhang, Zhiwen Wu, Jiale Huang, Xiangyu Wang, Ming Jen Tan, Wei She, Hao Zhou, Guoqing Geng

## Abstract
Extraterrestrial exploration, represented by Lunar base construction, has been promoted worldwide. This paper proposes a novel material composed of Lunar/Martian regolith simulants and 4 wt% silicate adhesive. This composite can self-harden in vacuum within 4.5 h without curing, achieving 29 MPa strength. It shows high durability (>70% residual strength) against vacuum exposure, extreme temperature differences (-196 to 78 °C), and γ-ray/proton radiation. The mechanism relies on vacuum dehydration, where silica clusters cohere into a solid network. The material allows cryogenic preparation even below -40 °C.

## 1. Introduction
Lunar construction faces extreme environments: temperature differences (-200 °C to 120 °C), high vacuum (10⁻⁹ Pa), and radiation. Transporting materials is costly; thus, in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) and automated construction are essential.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1 Materials
- **Regolith Simulants:** LRS-1 (Apollo-17 type), LRS-2 (Apollo-14), LRS-3 (Apollo-12), and MRS (Martian regolith simulant).
- **Binder:** Sodium silicate (SS) solution (30 wt% in water), with modulus adjusted to 2.0, 2.5, and 3.0.

### 2.2 Sample preparation
Regolith and silicate solution (mass ratio 1:0.14) were mixed, cold-pressed (9.5 MPa) into 40x10x10 mm³ green bodies, and immediately placed in a vacuum tank (<10 Pa).

## 3. Results and Discussion
### 3.1 Self-hardening and durability of SLRC
- **Hardening:** Rapid water loss in vacuum leads to solidification without heat. SS2.5 generated 5.48 MPa flexural and 18.19 MPa compressive strength in 4.5 h.
- **Durability:** After 108 days (4 lunar days) of cycling between 78 °C and -196 °C, the material reserved 71-81% of flexural and 88-98% of compressive strength.
- **Microstructure:** SEM shows regolith particles bridged by membranous silicate binders. Porosity slightly increases during the first lunar day but stabilizes thereafter.

## 4. Conclusion
The SLRC material utilizes low amounts of terrestrial binder (4 wt%), requires no curing heat, and is compatible with diverse regolith types. It is particularly suitable for cryogenic regions (lunar poles) or sunless lava tubes. Water used as a dispersant can potentially be recycled through vapor condensation modules.
