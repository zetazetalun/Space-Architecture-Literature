# Regolith sintering and 3D printing for lunar construction: An extensive review on recent progress

**Authors:** Muhammad Shazwan Suhaizan, Phuong Tran, Ash Exner, Brian G. Falzon
**Journal:** Progress in Additive Manufacturing (2024) 9:1715–1736
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s40964-023-00537-1

## Abstract
In-Situ Resource Utilisation (ISRU) is increasingly being seen as a viable and essential approach to constructing infrastructure for human habitation on the moon. Transporting materials and resources, from Earth to the Moon, is prohibitively expensive and not sustainable for long-term, large-scale development. Various fabrication technologies have been investigated in recent years, designed for extra-terrestrial exploration and settlement. This review presents a comprehensive study on the development of several sintering techniques of lunar regolith simulant to demonstrate its feasibility for ISRU on the moon. Various critical processing parameters are evaluated in pursuit of creating a structural material that can withstand the extreme lunar environment.

## 1. Background of lunar construction
A lunar base must shield humans from extreme temperature fluctuations (-248 °C to 77 °C), radiation, micrometeoroids, and lunar dust. Current launch costs (SpaceX Falcon 9) are ~$3k/kg, making Earth-supplied construction unsustainable. ISRU using regolith (lunar soil) is the primary solution for building habitats and landing pads.

## 2. Regolith Simulants and Soils
### 2.1 Characteristics
- **Composition:** >40% Silicon, with Ti, Al, Fe. High alumina (highlands) vs high FeO (mare).
- **Bulk Density:** 1.5–1.66 g/cm³.
- **Thermal Conductivity:** ~0.001 W/mK in vacuum.
- **Particle Size:** Ranges from 0.002 to 2 mm.

## 3. Bulk Sintering Methods
### 3.1 Radiant Heat Sintering
Uses infrared heat to create liquid-phase sintering. 
- **Working Temperature:** 1100–1380 °C.
- **Properties:** Compressive strengths of 30–200 MPa; porosity below 1% for fine powders.

### 3.2 Microwave Sintering (2.45 GHz)
- **Mechanism:** Couplings with defects and iron-rich phases (FeO, TiO2).
- **Pros:** Fast heating, potential energy efficiency.
- **Cons:** Penetration depth limit (~1 cm), 'runaway' heating risk, thermal cracking.
- **Properties:** Compressive strength 10–70 MPa.

### 3.3 Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS)
Uses pulsed electric current and pressure in vacuum.
- **Pros:** High heating/cooling rates (1000 °C/min), produces higher density samples than radiant sintering.
- **Results:** Density as high as 2.9 g/cm³ achieved at 1050 °C/100 MPa.

## 4. Focused Sintering
### 4.1 Laser Sintering (SLS/SLM)
- **Pros:** High geometrical accuracy, precision parts.
- **Cons:** High porosity due to poor regolith packing density; energy-intensive.
- **Results:** Compressive strengths up to 30 MPa; porosity can reach 65%.

### 4.2 Solar Sintering
- **Mechanism:** Direct utilization of concentrated sunlight (unlimited energy source).
- **Results:** 3D-printed interlocking bricks achieved ~2.31 MPa compressive strength. Suited for bulk radiation shielding.

## 5. Additive Manufacturing Applications
- **Direct Ink Writing (DIW):** Uses regolith mixed with organic binders (e.g., PLGA) to form a paste. Produces complex shapes but requires post-processing and earth-supplied binders.

## 6. Conclusion
- **Bulk Sintering:** Immobile, high energy, but high strength.
- **Focused Sintering:** Suited for in-situ surfacing or detailed parts.
- **DIW:** High compatibility but limited by binder storage.
- **Future Work:** Verification of high-temp behavior of real lunar soil vs simulants; non-destructive testing development.
