# Designing sustainable built environments for Mars habitation: Integrating innovations in architecture, systems, and human well-being

**Authors:** Hongli Sun, Mengfan Duan, Yifan Wu, Yunyi Zeng, Hengxin Zhao, Shuangdui Wu, Borong Lin, Ronggui Yang, and Gang Tan.
**Published in:** Nexus (Cell Press), 2024.
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.ynexs.2024.100030

## Summary
Migration to Mars and building livable habitation gradually become prosperous and arduous roads for human progress. Although many technical breakthroughs have been achieved for supporting migration, sustainable survival on Mars needs further attention. The Mars habitation module serves as humans' first barrier from the harsh extraterrestrial conditions. This review provides a systemic outlook of promising Mars habitation environment control technologies for the sustainability of both habitation resources and occupant health.

## Introduction
Mars presents a much harsher environment than Earth (colder, no oxygen, higher radiation), but offers better in situ resources than the Moon or the ISS. Mars dwelling involves two key aspects: resource sustainability (efficient construction/operation) and human sustainability (healthy survival). Experiences from habitats in extreme environments like the poles and deep sea provide valuable insights for Mars construction.

## Mars Habitation Technologies: From Design to Operation

### Design of Mars Habitation Module
Habitation designs are categorized into:
1. **Above-ground:** Prefab pressurized (often inflatable) cores with outer shells to resist radiation (e.g., Mars Ice House, Marsha).
2. **Underground/Semi-underground:** Leveraging Martian lava tubes and caverns to withstand space risks.

### Materials and ISRU
- **Martian Basalt:** High radiation resistance and low permeability.
- **Sulfur-based Concrete:** Suitable due to Martian soil containing ~6-37 wt% sulfur; offers fast solidification and recyclability.
- **Regolith-based Materials:** Elemental iron minerals (15 wt%) can be mixed with sulfur for advanced materials.
- **Ice:** Used as water domes to protect against solar radiation.

### Construction Techniques
- **3D Printing:** Offers possibilities for utilizing in situ materials.
- **Robotic Construction:** Systems like 'Team Zopherus' or 'Chinese Super Mason' utilize autonomous robotics for 3D printing and brick assembly.

### Operation and Life Support
- **Thermal Control:** Multi-layer insulation, PU polyfoam, and aerogels. Use of heat pipes and phase change materials.
- **Atmosphere:** Air generation via electrolyzed water and pressurized oxygen tanks. Waste air recovery systems are essential.
- **Lighting:** Artificial rhythmic lighting to maintain human circadian rhythms due to the lack of natural atmosphere.

## Performance-Centered Design Paths

### Building Structure and Envelope
- **Parametric Design:** Utilizing AI and genetic algorithms to optimize building forms for energy efficiency (e.g., 15.8% reduction in energy consumption in Earth analogs).
- **High-Performance Envelopes:** Using basalt fiber (thermal conductivity 0.031–0.038 W/(m·K)) and translucent aerogels for insulation and natural light transmission.
- **BIPV:** Building-integrated photovoltaics can generate significant power despite the Martian solar constant being only ~20% of Earth's.

## Challenges and Future Opportunities
Five critical issues for Mars migration:
1. Module appearance/form.
2. Construction methodology.
3. Material selection (ISRU focus).
4. Energy sourcing (Solar, Wind, Nuclear).
5. Human health and well-being via bionic indoor environments.

## Conclusion
Achieving a livable Mars module requires multidisciplinary support across architecture, engineering, physiology, and psychology. The integration of ergonomic and AI technologies in habitation design, combined with advanced ISRU construction, is crucial for long-term sustainability.
