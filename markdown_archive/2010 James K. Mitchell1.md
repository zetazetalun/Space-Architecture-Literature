# Geotechnical Engineering Considerations for Extraterrestrial Infrastructure Construction

James K. Mitchell, Sc.D., P.E., Dist. M. ASCE and W. David Carrier III, Sc.D., P.E., Fellow, Life M. ASCE

## ABSTRACT
It is only a matter of time until space exploration enters a new phase that will include additional manned and unmanned landings and explorations on the Moon, Mars, and perhaps on asteroids. Detailed knowledge of the soils and rocks will be needed for the design of foundations for structures, for excavation and underground space utilization, for design of surface vehicles and construction of transportation systems, for evaluation of their suitability as construction materials and development of construction methods. In-situ resource utilization can be expected to play a major role in these activities owing to the high cost and difficulty in transporting needed materials from Earth. The extreme environments in which the work is to be carried out will complicate virtually every activity to be undertaken.

Current knowledge about the rocks and soils on the Moon and Mars is summarized, with particular emphases on their compositions, geotechnical properties and in-situ states. Some implications for geotechnical engineering and foundation design are stated. Were it not for the unique and harsh environmental conditions, in addition to the great distance from Earth and its impacts on material transport and communications, engineering and construction on, in, and with the silty fine sandy soils of the Moon and Mars would be straightforward.

**Keywords:** dust, extra-terrestrial soils, geotechnical engineering, infrastructure, lunar soil mechanics, Mars, Moon, soil composition, soil properties, space exploration

## INTRODUCTION

> The surface is fine and powdery. I can pick it up loosely with my toe. It does adhere in fine layers like powdered charcoal to the sole and insides of my boots. I only go in a small fraction of an inch – maybe an eighth of an inch – but I can see the footprints of my boots and the treads in the fine sandy particles.

These words by Neil A. Armstrong on July 20, 1969, immediately following his first step onto the Moon and his memorable statement: “That’s one small step for (a) man; one giant leap for mankind”, marked the beginning of a new era in geotechnical engineering. Now, forty years later, after a significant hiatus in manned space exploration, planning is well underway for a new generation of missions leading to further exploration of the moon and near solar system. NASA’s Constellation Program calls for a return to the Moon by 2020 and then on to Mars sometime after 2030.

As with all high cost, high risk ventures such as this, many ask “WHY?” Some of the answers with respect to the Moon are the following:
* To pursue scientific questions about the origin and history of the Earth, solar system, and universe
* To test space technologies, systems, flight operations
* To obtain experience in extraterrestrial living
* To develop strategic (military) advantages
* To establish an observatory
* To provide a platform for Earth monitoring
* To establish a jumping off place for further space exploration

Similar reasons apply with respect to Mars and inevitably to other planets and asteroids. For some, sufficient justification is simply that (1) the Moon and Mars are there, and (2) we humans want to know, and when we no longer want to know, we are no longer human.

No matter what the activity to be done on the Moon, Mars, or other extraterrestrial bodies, it will be done on, in, or with the “soils” that are there. The purposes of this paper are to summarize current knowledge and understanding of the types and geotechnical properties of the soils on the Moon and Mars, and to identify some important considerations in geotechnical engineering for safe operations and facility design and construction in the unique and harsh environmental conditions encountered there.

## PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS AND ENVIRONMENTS OF THE EARTH, MOON, AND MARS

Several physical and environmental characteristics and properties of the Earth, Moon, and Mars are listed in Table 1. The most important of these as they affect the geotechnical properties and mechanical behavior of soils on these three bodies are gravity, temperature, and atmospheric composition and pressure.

Weights and body forces are in proportion to the gravitational field; i.e., 1 to 6 in the case of the Moon to the Earth, and about 1 to 2.64 in the case of Mars to the Earth. Materials such as granular soil that derive much of their strength from friction are correspondingly weaker on the Moon and Mars than on Earth. However, the forces that they will be called upon to carry under static loading are also proportionately less for support of bodies of the same mass. As cohesive forces are independent of gravity, a soil possessing some given amount of cohesion will be disproportionately stronger on the Moon and Mars than on the Earth. These gravitational differences also mean that dynamic forces developed by a given moving mass; e.g. accelerating vehicle, active astronaut, impacting hammer, will be the same for given motions on the Earth, the Moon, and Mars. In effect, the dynamic forces will be significantly greater relative to gravity (body) forces on the Moon and Mars than on Earth.

The very high and very low temperatures encountered on the Moon and Mars impact both material behavior and the ease of working. The near absence of any atmosphere (a nearly hard vacuum) on the Moon precludes significant gas adsorption on lunar soil and rock particles.

## THE NATURE AND GEOTECHNICAL PROPERTIES OF THE SOILS ON THE MOON AND MARS

### Moon

**Lunar Rock and Soil Types and Composition**
Consolidated rocks of the Moon fall into three categories (Taylor, et al. 1991):
1. *Basaltic volcanic* rocks that include lava flows and pyroclastic (volcanic ash) rocks
2. *Pristine rocks* from the lunar highlands that have original compositions uncontaminated by impact mixing
3. *Complex polymict breccias* formed by impacts that shatter, mix, and recompact the lunar surface and impact melts formed by melting of older rocks during meteoroid impacts.

All of these rocks were originally igneous rocks formed by cooling of molten material. Soil, also referred to as the regolith, covers virtually the Moon’s entire surface to a depth of at least 2 m up to as much as 20 m or more (Carrier and Mitchell, 1989). Meteorite impact is the dominant soil forming process. Individual soil particles consist of rock fragments, mineral grains, bits of glass, and agglutinates, which are composed of all the other particle types stuck together.

**Lunar Soil Mechanical Properties**
Given its gradation, the limited number of minerals, and the absence of water and air (or other gases), lunar soil has engineering properties that depend primarily on relative density. The relative density is 0 to 30 percent (very loose to loose) at the surface, increasing to more than 65 percent (medium to dense) at a depth of 5 cm, greater than 80 percent (dense) at 10 cm depth, and more than 90 percent (very dense) below 30 cm. The bulk density ranges from about 1.3 g/cm^3 to slightly greater than 1.8 g/cm^3 over this range.

### Mars

**Martian Rock and Soil Types and Composition**
Six geologic processes have shaped the Martian surface and the materials that are encountered there. These are: (1) tectonic, (2) volcanic, (3) cratering from meteor impact, (4) hydro; i.e., drainage systems, floods, polar ice caps, (5) mass movements; e.g., landslides and debris flows, and (6) aeolian; i.e., wind erosion and dust transport.

**Martian Soil Mechanical Properties**
The mechanical properties of the surface and near-surface soils of Mars have been inferred and deduced from such sources as photographs, interactions of landers and rovers with the surface, remote surface sampling, and measurements of motor currents, temperature and vehicle kinematics during rover movements.

## SOME GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING AND FOUNDATION DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS

Information obtained from unmanned and manned missions to the Moon and unmanned missions to Mars have provided sufficient knowledge of the soil and foundation conditions to support several general conclusions relevant to design and construction of the infrastructure needed to support future space exploration and development.
* Dust problems are likely on both the Moon and Mars.
* The silty fine sand soil and its high density reached at shallow depth on the Moon indicate that it will be capable of supporting high foundation loads on shallow foundations. Bearing capacity is likely to be more site-specific on Mars than on the Moon.
* Core tube and drill holes on the Moon remain open. Therefore, it should be possible to excavate with stable vertical walls to a depth of 2 m or more.
* Owing to the high density of lunar soil at depths greater than a few cm, excavated soil will exhibit bulking when recompacted.

## IN-SITU RESOURCE UTILIZATION

The objective of In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) is to extract elements and minerals from the land and/or atmospheric materials that are present on the Moon and Mars. At a cost of about $100,000 per kg to transport materials to the Moon, considerable savings would be possible if local materials could be used. NASA’s near-term goal is to produce oxygen from the lunar regolith for life support at 1 ton per year. Concurrently, a number of studies are being made to enable utilization of lunar soils and rock as construction materials.

## CONCLUSIONS

Reliable knowledge of the soils and their properties will be an essential part of successful further manned exploration and development on the Moon and Mars. The finer grained surficial soils on Mars appear somewhat similar to the silty fine sands that cover the Moon, however, some areas are populated with gravel, cobble, and boulder-size rocks. Dust is ubiquitous on both the Moon and Mars, and it adversely impacts both equipment and astronaut activities.

The surfaces of both the Moon and Mars appear capable of supporting vehicles and structures of the types presently envisioned for the next generation of exploration and development, although soft zones on Mars may require special care.
