# Geotechnical Engineering Considerations for Extraterrestrial Infrastructure Construction

**Authors:** James K. Mitchell, W. David Carrier III
**Publication:** The Art of Foundation Engineering Practice Congress 2010 (ASCE)

## Abstract
It is only a matter of time until space exploration enters a new phase that will include additional manned and unmanned landings and explorations on the Moon, Mars, and perhaps on asteroids. Detailed knowledge of the soils and rocks will be needed for the design of foundations for structures, for excavation and underground space utilization, for design of surface vehicles and construction of transportation systems, for evaluation of their suitability as construction materials and development of construction methods. In-situ resource utilization can be expected to play a major role in these activities owing to the high cost and difficulty in transporting needed materials from Earth. The extreme environments in which the work is to be carried out will complicate virtually every activity to be undertaken.

## Introduction
NASA’s Constellation Program calls for a return to the Moon by 2020 and then on to Mars sometime after 2030. No matter what the activity to be done on the Moon, Mars, or other extraterrestrial bodies, it will be done on, in, or with the “soils” that are there.

## Geotechnical Engineering Considerations
- **Trafficability:** Vehicle mobility is generally feasible, but rocky areas and soft zones must be avoided.
- **Dust:** Creates health risks and equipment damage; electrostatic adhesion is a major challenge.
- **Foundations:** Lunar soil can support high foundation loads due to rapid density increase with depth. Vertical excavation walls may be stable up to 2m or more.

## In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)
ISRU aims to extract elements like Oxygen and metals for parts fabrication. Transporting material from Earth costs approximately $100,000 per kg. Utilizing local soils as construction materials is essential for long-term viability.

## Conclusions
Successful exploration requires knowledge of soil mechanics for construction on, in, and with extraterrestrial materials. The surfaces of both the Moon and Mars appear capable of supporting the next generation of structures and vehicles.
