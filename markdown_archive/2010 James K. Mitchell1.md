# Geotechnical Engineering Considerations for Extraterrestrial Infrastructure Construction

**James K. Mitchell**, Sc.D., P.E., Dist. M. ASCE and **W. David Carrier III**, Sc.D., P.E., Fellow, Life M. ASCE

**ABSTRACT:** It is only a matter of time until space exploration enters a new phase that will include additional manned and unmanned landings and explorations on the Moon, Mars, and perhaps on asteroids. Detailed knowledge of the soils and rocks will be needed for the design of foundations for structures, for excavation and underground space utilization, for design of surface vehicles and construction of transportation systems, for evaluation of their suitability as construction materials and development of construction methods. In-situ resource utilization can be expected to play a major role in these activities owing to the high cost and difficulty in transporting needed materials from Earth. The extreme environments in which the work is to be carried out will complicate virtually every activity to be undertaken.
Current knowledge about the rocks and soils on the Moon and Mars is summarized, with particular emphases on their compositions, geotechnical properties and in-situ states. Some implications for geotechnical engineering and foundation design are stated. Were it not for the unique and harsh environmental conditions, in addition to the great distance from Earth and its impacts on material transport and communications, engineering and construction on, in, and with the silty fine sandy soils of the Moon and Mars would be straightforward.

**Keywords:** dust, extra-terrestrial soils, geotechnical engineering, infrastructure, lunar soil mechanics, Mars, Moon, soil composition, soil properties, space exploration

## INTRODUCTION

> *The surface is fine and powdery. I can pick it up loosely with my toe. It does adhere in fine layers like powdered charcoal to the sole and insides of my boots. I only go in a small fraction of an inch – maybe an eighth of an inch – but I can see the footprints of my boots and the treads in the fine sandy particles.*

These words by Neil A. Armstrong on July 20, 1969, immediately following his first step onto the Moon and his memorable statement: "That's one small step for (a) man; one giant leap for mankind", marked the beginning of a new era in geotechnical engineering. Now, forty years later, after a significant hiatus in manned space exploration, planning is well underway for a new generation of missions leading to further exploration of the moon and near solar system. NASA's Constellation Program calls for a return to the Moon by 2020 and then on to Mars sometime after 2030.

As with all high cost, high risk ventures such as this, many ask "WHY?" Some of the answers with respect to the Moon are the following:
* To pursue scientific questions about the origin and history of the Earth, solar system, and universe
* To test space technologies, systems, flight operations
* To obtain experience in extraterrestrial living
* To develop strategic (military) advantages
* To establish an observatory
* To provide a platform for Earth monitoring
* To establish a jumping off place for further space exploration

Similar reasons apply with respect to Mars and inevitably to other planets and asteroids. For some, sufficient justification is simply that (1) the Moon and Mars are there, and (2) we humans want to know, and when we no longer want to know, we are no longer human.

No matter what the activity to be done on the Moon, Mars, or other extraterrestrial bodies, it will be done on, in, or with the "soils" that are there. The purposes of this paper are to summarize current knowledge and understanding of the types and geotechnical properties of the soils on the Moon and Mars, and to identify some important considerations in geotechnical engineering for safe operations and facility design and construction in the unique and harsh environmental conditions encountered there.

## THE NATURE AND GEOTECHNICAL PROPERTIES OF THE SOILS ON THE MOON AND MARS

### Moon
**Lunar Rock and Soil Types and Composition**
Consolidated rocks of the Moon fall into three categories:
1. Basaltic volcanic rocks that include lava flows and pyroclastic rocks
2. Pristine rocks from the lunar highlands that have original compositions uncontaminated by impact mixing
3. Complex polymict breccias formed by impacts

Soil, also referred to as the regolith, covers virtually the Moon's entire surface to a depth of at least 2 m up to as much as 20 m or more. Meteorite impact is the dominant soil forming process... The gradational characteristics show the soil to be broadly-graded silty fine sand to sandy silt (SM) or (ML) in the Unified Soil Classification System.

**Lunar Soil Mechanical Properties**
Given its gradation, the limited number of minerals, and the absence of water and air, lunar soil has engineering properties that depend primarily on relative density. The relative density is 0 to 30 percent at the surface, increasing to more than 65 percent at a depth of 5 cm, greater than 80 percent at 10 cm depth, and more than 90 percent below 30 cm. The bulk density ranges from about 1.3 g/cm^3 to slightly greater than 1.8 g/cm^3 over this range.

### Mars
**Martian Rock and Soil Types and Composition**
Six geologic processes have shaped the Martian surface... (1) tectonic, (2) volcanic, (3) cratering, (4) hydro, (5) mass movements, and (6) aeolian. The regolith on Mars is much more complex than that of the Moon. The dominant migrating material is windblown dust.

**Martian Soil Mechanical Properties**
The mechanical properties of the surface and near-surface soils of Mars have been inferred and deduced from photographs, interactions of landers and rovers with the surface, remote surface sampling, and measurements of motor currents... The Pathfinder Mission Rover Team (1997) describes the Martian soil as "similar to moderately dense soils on Earth, such as clayey silt with embedded sands, granules, and pebbles."

## SOME GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING AND FOUNDATION DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS
* Trafficability and vehicle mobility on the lunar surface are not likely to be major problems, although some soft zones exist.
* Dust problems are likely on both the Moon and Mars, creating health and mission risks.
* The silty fine sand soil and its high density reached at shallow depth on the Moon indicate that it will be capable of supporting high foundation loads on shallow foundations.
* Core tube and drill holes on the Moon remain open. Therefore, it should be possible to excavate with stable vertical walls to a depth of 2 m or more.

## IN-SITU RESOURCE UTILIZATION
The objective of In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) is to extract elements and minerals from the land and/or atmospheric materials that are present on the Moon and Mars (National Research Council, 2008). At a cost of about $100,000 per kg to transport materials to the Moon, considerable savings would be possible if local materials could be used. NASA's near-term goal is to produce oxygen from the lunar regolith for life support at 1 ton per year... None of these goals are likely to be achievable without consideration of important geotechnical issues; e.g., drilling, sampling and testing of soils and rocks, dust control, excavation, vehicle mobility and trafficability, tunneling, and foundation engineering.

## CONCLUSIONS
Reliable knowledge of the soils and their properties will be an essential part of successful further manned exploration and development on the Moon and Mars. All activities on these bodies will be on, in, and/or with the soils and rocks that are there. Knowledge of soil conditions on the Moon is relatively good. On the average, the soil is a silty fine sand composed of grains of several known types formed by meteoroid impact, increasing to a relative density of 90 percent or more at a depth of 30 cm... The surfaces of both the Moon and Mars appear capable of supporting vehicles and structures of the types presently envisioned for the next generation of exploration and development.
