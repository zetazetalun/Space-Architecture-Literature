# Geotechnical Engineering Considerations for Extraterrestrial Infrastructure Construction

**James K. Mitchell**, Sc.D., P.E., Dist. M. ASCE and **W. David Carrier III**, Sc.D., P.E., Fellow, Life M. ASCE

## Abstract
It is only a matter of time until space exploration enters a new phase that will include additional manned and unmanned landings and explorations on the Moon, Mars, and perhaps on asteroids. Detailed knowledge of the soils and rocks will be needed for the design of foundations for structures, for excavation and underground space utilization, for design of surface vehicles and construction of transportation systems, for evaluation of their suitability as construction materials and development of construction methods. In-situ resource utilization can be expected to play a major role in these activities owing to the high cost and difficulty in transporting needed materials from Earth.

## Geotechnical Properties of Lunar Soil
Lunar soil (regolith) covers the Moon's surface to depths of 2m to over 20m. It is characterized as broadly-graded silty fine sand to sandy silt (SM or ML).

### Lunar Soil Mechanical Properties
Relative density increases rapidly with depth due to meteorite impact processes:
- **Surface (0-5 cm):** 0 to 30% (Very loose)
- **10 cm depth:** > 80% (Dense)
- **Below 30 cm:** > 90% (Very dense)
- **Bulk Density:** 1.3 to 1.8 g/cm³

## Geotechnical Properties of Martian Soil
Martian soil is more complex than lunar soil due to tectonic, volcanic, and aeolian processes, as well as the historical presence of liquid water. The soil contains silicon, iron, aluminum, magnesium, calcium, titanium, sulfur, and chlorine.

## In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)
ISRU aims to extract elements and minerals from local materials to reduce the ~$100,000/kg transport cost from Earth. Long-range goals include extracting metals for parts fabrication and utilizing lunar soils/rocks as construction materials. Achieving these goals requires geotechnical expertise in drilling, excavation, and foundation engineering.

## Conclusions
Reliable knowledge of soil properties is essential for extraterrestrial infrastructure. While lunar soil conditions are relatively well-understood as silty fine sand with high density at shallow depths, Martian conditions are more variable and require site-specific investigation. Both surfaces are capable of supporting envisioned vehicles and structures, provided unique environmental hazards (dust, vacuum, temperature) are addressed.
