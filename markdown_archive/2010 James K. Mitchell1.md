# Geotechnical Engineering Considerations for Extraterrestrial Infrastructure Construction

**Authors:** James K. Mitchell and W. David Carrier III
**Publication:** ASCE Geo Institute Geotechnical Special Publication, 2010

## Abstract
It is only a matter of time until space exploration enters a new phase that will include additional manned and unmanned landings and explorations on the Moon, Mars, and perhaps on asteroids. Detailed knowledge of the soils and rocks will be needed for the design of foundations for structures, for excavation and underground space utilization, for design of surface vehicles and construction of transportation systems, for evaluation of their suitability as construction materials and development of construction methods. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can be expected to play a major role in these activities owing to the high cost and difficulty in transporting needed materials from Earth. Current knowledge about the rocks and soils on the Moon and Mars is summarized, with particular emphases on their compositions, geotechnical properties and in-situ states.

## Introduction
NASA’s Constellation Program (at the time of writing) called for a return to the Moon and missions to Mars. The paper establishes that all activity on extraterrestrial bodies will involve interactions with local 'soils'. Geotechnical engineering is required for safe operations and facility design in harsh environmental conditions.

## Geotechnical Properties of Lunar Soils
Lunar regolith is a broadly-graded silty fine sand to sandy silt (SM or ML). Properties depend heavily on relative density, which increases rapidly with depth (from ~30% at surface to >90% below 30 cm).

## Geotechnical Properties of Martian Soils
Martian soil is more complex due to volcanic, hydro, and aeolian processes. It is generally described as clayey silt with embedded sands and granules. Evidence of soil cohesion and densification is observed in rover tracks.

## Geotechnical Engineering Considerations
- **Trafficability:** Generally manageable on the Moon, but rocky and soft zones on Mars pose risks.
- **Dust:** Creates health and mission risks; electrostatic and magnetic (lunar) or complex (Martian).
- **Foundations:** Lunar soil can support high loads on shallow foundations due to its density; Mars requires more site-specific data.
- **Excavation:** Lunar core tubes stay open; vertical walls stable up to 2m deep. Excavated soil will bulk when recompacted.

## In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)
ISRU is essential to reduce transport costs ($100k/kg). Near-term goals include oxygen extraction; long-term goals involve using lunar soil as construction material for shelters and manufacturing metals for parts. Geotechnical expertise in drilling and tunneling is vital.

## Conclusions
Knowledge of lunar soil is relatively good compared to Martian soil. Both surfaces can support envisioned infrastructure, but harsh environments and long distances complicate engineering efforts.
