# Geotechnical Engineering Considerations for Extraterrestrial Infrastructure Construction

**Authors:** James K. Mitchell, W. David Carrier III
**Publication:** The Art of Foundation Engineering Practice Congress 2010 (ASCE)

## Abstract
It is only a matter of time until space exploration enters a new phase that will include additional manned and unmanned landings and explorations on the Moon, Mars, and perhaps on asteroids. Detailed knowledge of the soils and rocks will be needed for the design of foundations for structures, for excavation and underground space utilization, for design of surface vehicles and construction of transportation systems, for evaluation of their suitability as construction materials and development of construction methods. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can be expected to play a major role in these activities owing to the high cost and difficulty in transporting needed materials from Earth.

## Lunar Geotechnical Properties

### Lunar Rock and Soil Types
Consolidated rocks fall into three categories:
1. Basaltic volcanic rocks (lava flows and pyroclastic).
2. Pristine rocks from lunar highlands.
3. Complex polymict breccias formed by impacts.

### Lunar Soil Mechanical Properties
Lunar soil (regolith) is a broadly-graded silty fine sand (SM) or sandy silt (ML). Its engineering properties depend primarily on relative density, which increases rapidly with depth:
- **0-5 cm:** 0 to 30% (very loose to loose)
- **10 cm:** >80% (dense)
- **Below 30 cm:** >90% (very dense)

## Martian Geotechnical Properties
Martian soil is described as similar to moderately dense soils on Earth, such as clayey silt with embedded sands and granules. The geology is more complex than the Moon's due to tectonic, volcanic, hydro, and aeolian processes.

## Geotechnical Engineering Considerations
- **Trafficability:** Generally good on the Moon, but soft zones exist. Mars presents more challenges due to rocks and varied soil types.
- **Dust:** Creates health and mission risks on both bodies; adheres via electrostatics (Moon) and is ubiquitous.
- **Foundations:** Lunar soil can support high loads on shallow foundations due to high density at shallow depths.
- **Excavation:** Lunar core tubes and drill holes remain open, allowing for stable vertical walls to 2m+ depth.
- **ISRU:** Essential for reducing transport costs ($100,000/kg). Focus includes producing oxygen and utilizing soil as construction material.

## Conclusions
Successful exploration requires reliable soil knowledge. While Lunar soil is relatively predictable (silty fine sand), Martian conditions are more variable due to its atmosphere and complex geological history. Surfaces on both are capable of supporting structures, provided environmental and distance-related logistical challenges are managed.
