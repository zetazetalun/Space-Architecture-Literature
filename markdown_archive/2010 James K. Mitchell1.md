# Geotechnical Engineering Considerations for Extraterrestrial Infrastructure Construction

**Authors:** James K. Mitchell, Sc.D., P.E., Dist. M. ASCE and W. David Carrier III, Sc.D., P.E., Fellow, Life M. ASCE
**Source:** The Art of Foundation Engineering Practice Congress 2010 (ASCE)

### Abstract
It is only a matter of time until space exploration enters a new phase that will include additional manned and unmanned landings and explorations on the Moon, Mars, and perhaps on asteroids. Detailed knowledge of the soils and rocks will be needed for the design of foundations for structures, for excavation and underground space utilization, for design of surface vehicles and construction of transportation systems, for evaluation of their suitability as construction materials and development of construction methods. In-situ resource utilization can be expected to play a major role in these activities owing to the high cost and difficulty in transporting needed materials from Earth. The extreme environments in which the work is to be carried out will complicate virtually every activity to be undertaken.

### Lunar Geotechnical Properties
Lunar soil (regolith) covers the surface to depths of 2 m to over 20 m. It is categorized as broadly-graded silty fine sand to sandy silt (SM or ML).

**Relative Density:**
- Surface: 0 to 30% (very loose)
- 5 cm depth: >65% (medium to dense)
- 30 cm depth: >90% (very dense)

**Shear Strength (Table 3):**
- 0-15 cm: Friction Angle 41-43°, Cohesion 0.4-0.6 kPa
- 30-60 cm: Friction Angle 52-55°, Cohesion 2.3-3.8 kPa

### Martian Geotechnical Properties
Martian soil is more complex due to atmospheric and hydro processes. Descriptions suggest it is similar to moderately dense soils on Earth, such as clayey silt with embedded sands, granules, and pebbles.

**Soil Properties (Table 4):**
- Bulk Density: 1.0 - 1.8 g/cm³
- Internal Friction Angle: 20° - 45°
- Cohesion: 0.1 - 1.0 kPa

### In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)
ISRU aims to extract elements from the land/atmosphere. Transporting material to the Moon costs ~$100,000/kg. Near-term goals include oxygen production (1 ton/year) and long-term goals involve metal extraction for parts fabrication and using regolith as construction material.

### Geotechnical Engineering Considerations
- **Trafficability:** Generally manageable on the Moon; more complex on Mars due to varied terrain and ices.
- **Dust:** Creates health risks and equipment damage; adheres due to electrostatics.
- **Foundation Design:** High relative density at shallow depths on the Moon supports high foundation loads. Martian bearing capacity is site-specific.
- **Excavation:** Vertical walls up to 2m can remain stable on the Moon. Soil exhibits "bulking" when recompacted.
