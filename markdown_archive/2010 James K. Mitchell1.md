# Geotechnical Engineering Considerations for Extraterrestrial Infrastructure Construction

James K. Mitchell1, Sc.D., P.E., Dist. M. ASCE and W. David Carrier III2, Sc.D., P.E., Fellow, Life M. ASCE

1University Distinguished Professor Emeritus, Virginia Tech, Blacksburg, VA 24061-0105; jkm@vt.edu
2Director, Lunar Geotechnical Institute, Lakeland, FL 33807-5056; wdcarrier@verizon.net

**ABSTRACT:** It is only a matter of time until space exploration enters a new phase that will include additional manned and unmanned landings and explorations on the Moon, Mars, and perhaps on asteroids. Detailed knowledge of the soils and rocks will be needed for the design of foundations for structures, for excavation and underground space utilization, for design of surface vehicles and construction of transportation systems, for evaluation of their suitability as construction materials and development of construction methods. In-situ resource utilization can be expected to play a major role in these activities owing to the high cost and difficulty in transporting needed materials from Earth. The extreme environments in which the work is to be carried out will complicate virtually every activity to be undertaken.

Current knowledge about the rocks and soils on the Moon and Mars is summarized, with particular emphases on their compositions, geotechnical properties and in-situ states. Some implications for geotechnical engineering and foundation design are stated. Were it not for the unique and harsh environmental conditions, in addition to the great distance from Earth and its impacts on material transport and communications, engineering and construction on, in, and with the silty fine sandy soils of the Moon and Mars would be straightforward.

**Keywords:** dust, extra-terrestrial soils, geotechnical engineering, infrastructure, lunar soil mechanics, Mars, Moon, soil composition, soil properties, space exploration

## INTRODUCTION

> The surface is fine and powdery. I can pick it up loosely with my toe. It does adhere in fine layers like powdered charcoal to the sole and insides of my boots. I only go in a small fraction of an inch – maybe an eighth of an inch – but I can see the footprints of my boots and the treads in the fine sandy particles.

These words by Neil A. Armstrong on July 20, 1969, immediately following his first step onto the Moon and his memorable statement: “That’s one small step for (a) man; one giant leap for mankind”, marked the beginning of a new era in geotechnical engineering. Now, forty years later, after a significant hiatus in manned space exploration, planning is well underway for a new generation of missions leading to further exploration of the moon and near solar system. NASA’s Constellation Program calls for a return to the Moon by 2020 and then on to Mars sometime after 2030.

As with all high cost, high risk ventures such as this, many ask “WHY?” Some of the answers with respect to the Moon are the following:
*   To pursue scientific questions about the origin and history of the Earth, solar system, and universe
*   To test space technologies, systems, flight operations
*   To obtain experience in extraterrestrial living
*   To develop strategic (military) advantages
*   To establish an observatory
*   To provide a platform for Earth monitoring
*   To establish a jumping off place for further space exploration

Similar reasons apply with respect to Mars and inevitably to other planets and asteroids. For some, sufficient justification is simply that (1) the Moon and Mars are there, and (2) we humans want to know, and when we no longer want to know, we are no longer human.

No matter what the activity to be done on the Moon, Mars, or other extraterrestrial bodies, it will be done on, in, or with the “soils” that are there. The purposes of this paper are to summarize current knowledge and understanding of the types and geotechnical properties of the soils on the Moon and Mars, and to identify some important considerations in geotechnical engineering for safe operations and facility design and construction in the unique and harsh environmental conditions encountered there.

This paper has been prepared for inclusion in the ASCE Geo Institute Geotechnical Special Publication honoring our distinguished colleague, Clyde N. Baker, Jr. and his many monumental contributions to foundation engineering, especially deep foundations for most of the world’s tallest structures. Although similar structures are not likely soon for the faraway sites and alien conditions considered herein, building the needed infrastructure to support activities on the Moon and Mars will require geotechnical engineering of the highest quality done by imaginative engineers, such as Clyde Baker.

## PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS AND ENVIRONMENTS OF THE EARTH, MOON, AND MARS

Several physical and environmental characteristics and properties of the Earth, Moon, and Mars are listed in Table 1. The most important of these as they affect the geotechnical properties and mechanical behavior of soils on these three bodies are gravity, temperature, and atmospheric composition and pressure.

Weights and body forces are in proportion to the gravitational field; i.e., 1 to 6 in the case of the Moon to the Earth, and about 1 to 2.64 in the case of Mars to the Earth. Materials such as granular soil that derive much of their strength from friction are correspondingly weaker on the Moon and Mars than on Earth. However, the forces that they will be called upon to carry under static loading are also proportionately less for support of bodies of the same mass. As cohesive forces are independent of gravity, a soil possessing some given amount of cohesion will be disproportionately stronger on the Moon and Mars than on the Earth. These gravitational differences also mean that dynamic forces developed by a given moving mass; e.g. accelerating vehicle, active astronaut, impacting hammer, will be the same for given motions on the Earth, the Moon, and Mars. In effect, the dynamic forces will be significantly greater relative to gravity (body) forces on the Moon and Mars than on Earth.

Both the mean surface temperatures and the temperature extremes differ significantly among the Earth, the Moon, and Mars, as indicated in Table 1. The very high and very low temperatures encountered on the Moon and Mars impact both material behavior and the ease of working. To date no liquid water or ice has been discovered on the Moon, so water interactions with the soil and phase changes are not problems in the regions that have been explored so far. Although its geology indicates that liquid water did exist on Mars in the past, evidence of its presence today, except as a polar ice cap in the northern hemisphere, has not yet been obtained. The possibility that there is groundwater is still being studied.

The near absence of any atmosphere (a nearly hard vacuum) on the Moon precludes significant gas adsorption on lunar soil and rock particles, whereas the abundance of oxygen and nitrogen surrounding the Earth provides opportunities for surface reactions that can contribute to both weathering and alterations in physical properties. Although the Martian atmosphere is very sparse compared with that of the Earth; i.e., a surface pressure of 4.0 to 8.7 mb on Mars compared to 1013.25 mb on Earth, the Martian winds are capable of surface erosion and small particle transport. The atmosphere on Mars contains about 95.7 percent CO2, 2.7 percent Nitrogen, 1.6 percent Argon and 0.2 percent Oxygen.

## THE NATURE AND GEOTECHNICAL PROPERTIES OF THE SOILS ON THE MOON AND MARS

### Moon

**Lunar Rock and Soil Types and Composition**
Consolidated rocks of the Moon fall into three categories (Taylor, et al. 1991):
*   Basaltic volcanic rocks that include lava flows and pyroclastic (volcanic ash) rocks
*   Pristine rocks from the lunar highlands that have original compositions uncontaminated by impact mixing
*   Complex polymict breccias formed by impacts that shatter, mix, and recompact the lunar surface and impact melts formed by melting of older rocks during meteoroid impacts.

All of these rocks were originally igneous rocks formed by cooling of molten material (Taylor, et al., 1991). The rocks are composed primarily of pyroxenes, olivine, plagioclase feldspar, ilmenite, and silica minerals in the form of tridymite or cristobalite. Whereas nearly 4000 minerals have been identified in the rocks and soil on Earth, less than 100 have been found on the Moon. This is primarily the result of the absence of atmosphere and water, which are necessary conditions for chemical weathering processes.

Soil, also referred to as the regolith, covers virtually the Moon’s entire surface to a depth of at least 2 m up to as much as 20 m or more (Carrier and Mitchell, 1989). Meteorite impact, at velocities greater than 20 km/s, which breaks big rocks into small rocks, is the dominant soil forming process. Furthermore, the energy of impact melts small particles back into larger particles. In addition, the meteorites strike the Moon isotropically, and thus the soil-forming process is ubiquitous on the lunar surface. This process is estimated to reach steady state after about 100 million years and the result is the rather narrow range of soil particle sizes. Individual soil particles consist of rock fragments, mineral grains, bits of glass, and agglutinates, which are composed of all the other particle types stuck together. The gradational characteristics show the soil to be broadly-graded silty fine sand to sandy silt, (SM) or (ML) in the Unified Soil Classification System. Owing to the irregular particle shapes, with reentrant surfaces and enclosed voids in the agglutinates (Carrier and Mitchell, 1989), both the specific surface area and particle crushability are higher than for terrestrial materials of comparable gradation but composed mainly of quartz.

**Lunar Soil Mechanical Properties**
Given its gradation, the limited number of minerals, and the absence of water and air (or other gases), lunar soil has engineering properties that depend primarily on relative density. The relative density is 0 to 30 percent (very loose to loose) at the surface, increasing to more than 65 percent (medium to dense) at a depth of 5 cm, greater than 80 percent (dense) at 10 cm depth, and more than 90 percent (very dense) below 30 cm (Carrier and Mitchell, 1989). The bulk density ranges from about 1.3 g/cm3 to slightly greater than 1.8 g/cm3 over this range. The average relative density in the upper 60 cm is 83±3 percent (Mitchell, et al., 1974; Houston, et al., 1974). This density profile is attributed to meteorite impacts that stir and loosen the uppermost few cm but shake and densify the material below (Carrier, et al., 1973).

### Mars

**Martian Rock and Soil Types and Composition**
Six geologic processes have shaped the Martian surface and the materials that are encountered there. These are: (1) tectonic, (2) volcanic, (3) cratering from meteor impact, (4) hydro; i.e., drainage systems, floods, polar ice caps, (5) mass movements; e.g., landslides and debris flows, and (6) aeolian; i.e., wind erosion and dust transport. As noted by Schmitt (2009), the surface is not covered by impact-generated material as on the Moon, and the regolith on Mars is much more complex than that of the Moon. 

The thickness of the unconsolidated soil layer and depth to bedrock are not reliably known from the evidence so far obtained. The soil contains silicon, iron, aluminum, magnesium, calcium, titanium, sulfur, and chlorine, but no organic matter has so far been found (Banin, 2005). Oxidation of the iron is responsible for the red color of Mars.

**Martian Soil Mechanical Properties**
The mechanical properties of the surface and near-surface soils of Mars have been inferred and deduced from such sources as photographs, interactions of landers and rovers with the surface, remote surface sampling, and measurements of motor currents, temperature and vehicle kinematics during rover movements. The Pathfinder Mission Rover Team (1997) describes the Martian soil as “similar to moderately dense soils on Earth, such as clayey silt with embedded sands, granules, and pebbles.”

## SOME GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING AND FOUNDATION DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS

Among the significant findings and expectations are the following:
*   Trafficability and vehicle mobility on the lunar surface are not likely to be major problems. However, greater difficulties could be encountered on Mars owing to the more complex geology, greater variety of soil types, and the existence of both water ice and CO2 ice in the polar regions.
*   Dust problems are likely on both the Moon and Mars. Dust creates both health risks and mission risks, from obscuring landing sites, equipment overheating, and covering solar arrays.
*   The silty fine sand soil and its high density reached at shallow depth on the Moon indicate that it will be capable of supporting high foundation loads on shallow foundations. Bearing capacity is likely to be more site-specific on Mars than on the Moon.
*   Core tube and drill holes on the Moon remain open. Therefore, it should be possible to excavate with stable vertical walls to a depth of 2 m or more. Qualitatively similar behavior can be expected on Mars.
*   Owing to the high density of lunar soil at depths greater than a few cm, excavated soil will exhibit bulking when recompacted.

## IN-SITU RESOURCE UTILIZATION

The objective of In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) is to extract elements and minerals from the land and/or atmospheric materials that are present on the Moon and Mars (National Research Council, 2008). Concurrently, a number of studies are being made to enable utilization of lunar soils and rock as construction materials. None of these goals are likely to be achievable without consideration of important geotechnical issues; e.g., drilling, sampling and testing of soils and rocks, dust control, excavation, vehicle mobility and trafficability, tunneling, and foundation engineering.

## CONCLUSIONS

Reliable knowledge of the soils and their properties will be an essential part of successful further manned exploration and development on the Moon and Mars. All activities on these bodies will be on, in, and/or with the soils and rocks that are there. Knowledge of soil conditions on the Moon is relatively good. On the average, the soil is a silty fine sand formed by meteoroid impact, increasing to a relative density of 90 percent or more at a depth of 30 cm. 

On the other hand, the conditions on Mars are much more variable and complex owing to the presence of a gaseous atmosphere, and a history that has involved hydro, volcanic and aeolian processes. Dust is ubiquitous on both the Moon and Mars, and it adversely impacts both equipment and astronaut activities.

The surfaces of both the Moon and Mars appear capable of supporting vehicles and structures of the types presently envisioned for the next generation of exploration and development. Were it not for the unique and harsh environmental conditions, in addition to the great distances and their impacts on material transport and communications, engineering and construction on, in, and with the silty fine sandy soils of the Moon and Mars would be straightforward.
