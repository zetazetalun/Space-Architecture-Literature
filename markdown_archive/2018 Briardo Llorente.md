# The Multiplanetary Future of Plant Synthetic Biology

**Authors:** Briardo Llorente, Thomas C. Williams, and Hugh D. Goold
**Journal:** Genes (MDPI)
**Received:** 30 May 2018; **Accepted:** 9 July 2018; **Published:** 10 July 2018

## Abstract
The interest in human space journeys to distant planets and moons has been re-ignited in recent times and there are ongoing plans for sending the first manned missions to Mars in the near future. In addition to generating oxygen, fixing carbon, and recycling waste and water, plants could play a critical role in producing food and biomass feedstock for the microbial manufacture of materials, chemicals, and medicines in long-term interplanetary outposts. However, because life on Earth evolved under the conditions of the terrestrial biosphere, plants will not perform optimally in different planetary habitats. The construction or transportation of plant growth facilities and the availability of resources, such as sunlight and liquid water, may also be limiting factors, and would thus impose additional challenges to efficient farming in an extraterrestrial destination. Using the framework of the forthcoming human missions to Mars, here we discuss a series of bioengineering endeavors that will enable us to take full advantage of plants in the context of a Martian greenhouse. We also propose a roadmap for research on adapting life to Mars and outline our opinion that synthetic biology efforts towards this goal will contribute to solving some of the main agricultural and industrial challenges here on Earth.

## 1. Taking Full Advantage of Plants on Extraterrestrial Human Outposts
The exploration of space is a major driver of technological innovation. A major factor limiting the expansion of human space exploration is the enormous logistical costs of resupplying resources from Earth. Therefore, developing robust technologies to enable sustainable long-duration human operations in space is paramount. Plants could contribute to sustaining bioregenerative life support systems by supplying O2, fixing CO2, and recycling waste/water, while providing food and precursors for manufacturing medicines and materials on the Moon or Mars.

## 2. Refactoring Plants for Enhanced Performance on Mars
Mars is the next step for human planetary exploration. To achieve long-duration habitation, missions must depart from reliance on shipped cargo. 

### 2.1. Enhancing Photosynthesis and Photoprotection
Sunlight intensity on Mars is significantly lower than on Earth (~43%). Improved photosynthesis will save considerable power resources otherwise used for artificial lighting. Engineering light-harvesting antennae to use UV or infrared regions could be effective since Mars has a high surface flux of UV radiation due to its thin atmosphere.

### 2.2. Improving Drought and Cold Tolerance
Water on Mars is mostly available as ice. Mitigating low average temperatures and diurnal thermal variations is key. Engineering cold-hardy plants reduces the energy needed for greenhouse thermal maintenance.

### 2.3. Engineering High Yield and Functional Food
Martian greenhouses will be limited in size. Ideal plants should have high biomass productivity, high harvest indices, and minimum horticultural requirements.

## 3. Tailoring Microorganisms to Complement and Facilitate Plant Life on Mars
Engineered microbes are necessary for soil conditioning and removing toxic compounds like perchlorates from Martian regolith.

### 3.1. Conditioning Martian Soil for Plant Growth Using Microbes
Martian soil contains high levels of toxic perchlorates. Biological removal using engineering CO2-utilizing bacteria to express perchlorate reduction enzymes offers an alternative to water-intensive washing. Microbes could also produce extracellular polysaccharides to bind soil particles and mitigate desiccation.

### 3.2. Microbes for Metabolite and Protein Production from Plant Material
Using plant-derived sugars and biomass for aerobic heterotrophic catabolism in microbes can circumvent limitations in production rates of medicines and materials on Mars.

## 4. A Roadmap for Research on Adapting Life to Mars
The authors propose the 'Mars Biofoundry': an automated platform for high-throughput phenotyping of biological systems adapted to Martian conditions. This involves a design-build-test cycle using Earth-based simulations and eventually miniature growth facilities deployed on Mars for remote monitoring.

## 5. From Earth to Mars and Back to Earth
Advances in space-based plant and microbial technology will have far-reaching implications for terrestrial agriculture, food security, and environmental protection.
