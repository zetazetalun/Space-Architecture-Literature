# The Multiplanetary Future of Plant Synthetic Biology

**Authors:** Briardo Llorente, Thomas C. Williams, and Hugh D. Goold  
**Journal:** Genes  
**Date:** July 2018  
**DOI:** 10.3390/genes9070348

## Abstract
The interest in human space journeys to distant planets and moons has been re-ignited in recent times and there are ongoing plans for sending the first manned missions to Mars in the near future. In addition to generating oxygen, fixing carbon, and recycling waste and water, plants could play a critical role in producing food and biomass feedstock for the microbial manufacture of materials, chemicals, and medicines in long-term interplanetary outposts. However, because life on Earth evolved under the conditions of the terrestrial biosphere, plants will not perform optimally in different planetary habitats. The construction or transportation of plant growth facilities and the availability of resources, such as sunlight and liquid water, may also be limiting factors, and would thus impose additional challenges to efficient farming in an extraterrestrial destination. Using the framework of the forthcoming human missions to Mars, here we discuss a series of bioengineering endeavors that will enable us to take full advantage of plants in the context of a Martian greenhouse. We also propose a roadmap for research on adapting life to Mars and outline our opinion that synthetic biology efforts towards this goal will contribute to solving some of the main agricultural and industrial challenges here on Earth.

## 1. Taking Full Advantage of Plants on Extraterrestrial Human Outposts
Space agencies (NASA, ESA) and private companies (SpaceX) are moving toward deep space exploration. A major limiting factor is the logistical cost of resupplying from Earth. Sustaining long-duration human operations requires Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS). Plants provide O2, fix CO2, recycle waste/water, and serve as precursors for manufacturing medicines and materials on the Moon or Mars. To achieve efficiency, greenhouses must shield plants from UV/cosmic radiation and maintain controlled temperature, humidity, and pressure.

## 2. Refactoring Plants for Enhanced Performance on Mars
Achieving self-sufficiency on Mars requires plants to survive the harsh environment. Modifications at multiple levels are needed to optimize benefits in energy, water, and habitat-space use.

### 2.1. Enhancing Photosynthesis and Photoprotection
Sunlight on Mars is ~43% of Earth's intensity. Greenhouses will further reduce light levels. Synthetic biology targets include:
- Expanding light-harvesting spectrum to UV/infrared regions.
- Engineering improved photoprotection mechanisms to handle higher surface flux of UV radiation.
- Building "new-to-nature" CO2-fixing pathways to improve photosynthetic efficiency.

### 2.2. Improving Drought and Cold Tolerance
- **Water Management:** Engineering plants with increased water-use efficiency (e.g., CAM metabolism or programmable stomatal closure) to minimize energy spent on water extraction from ice.
- **Thermal Mitigation:** Engineering cold-hardy plants with ice-binding proteins and synthetic circadian regulation to survive large diurnal temperature drops (Martian night).

### 2.3. Engineering High Yield and Functional Food
- Optimization of plant density and architecture (e.g., shade avoidance response manipulation).
- Biofortification to produce antioxidant-rich food (carotenoids) to protect human health in space.

## 3. Tailoring Microorganisms to Complement Plant Life
### 3.1. Conditioning Martian Soil Using Microbes
Martian regolith contains toxic perchlorates. Biological removal using engineered CO2-utilizing bacteria can detoxify soil and release water from hydrated perchlorate salts. Microbes can also be engineered to produce polysaccharides to bind soil particles and improve moisture retention.

### 3.2. Microbial Manufacture
Microbes can convert plant biomass (sugars/inedible parts) into proteins, metabolites, materials, and medicines using aerobic heterotrophic catabolism or growth on methanol.

## 4. A Roadmap for Research: The Mars Biofoundry
The authors propose a "Mars Biofoundry"—an automated platform on Earth for high-throughput phenotyping of biological systems under simulated Martian conditions. Best-performing solutions would be iterated through a design-build-test cycle before transport to miniature growth facilities on Mars.

## 5. Conclusion
Developing technology for sustaining humans on Mars will advance Earth's agriculture, industry, and environmental protection by improving soil conditioning and crop resilience.
