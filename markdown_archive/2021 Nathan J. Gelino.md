# Off Earth Landing and Launch Pad Construction—A Critical Technology for Establishing a Long-Term Presence on Extraterrestrial Surfaces

**Authors:** Nathan J. Gelino, Robert P. Mueller, Robert W. Moses, James G. Mantovani, Philip T. Metzger, Brad C. Buckles, and Laurent Sibille

**Published in:** Earth and Space 2021, ASCE

## ABSTRACT
In 2017, Space Policy Directive–1 was issued to ensure that the United States will lead the return of humans to the Moon for long-term exploration and utilization. A long-term presence on the Moon will require numerous lunar landings and launches. Interactions between rocket engine plumes and the unprepared lunar surface lead to erosion and ejection of regolith particles at high velocities, risking sensors, hardware, and visibility. The construction of a reusable landing/launch pad (LLP) infrastructure is a method to mitigate such risks. This paper summarizes previous work towards construction of off-Earth LLP infrastructure, identifies key technology gaps, and establishes metrics for comparison of pad construction technologies.

## INTRODUCTION
A Launch & Landing Complex (LLC) on Earth is a well-defined discipline. For repeated missions to the Moon and Mars, functionally equivalent LLCs must be developed for safe operations at reasonable life cycle costs. This paper focuses on the technologies needed to construct the Launch & Landing Pad (LLP) and associated blast barriers.

## PRELIMINARY ENGINEERING ASSESSMENT
Large-scale non-human HLS landers are expected to require descent engines with 100 kN of thrust. By contrast, the Apollo lunar lander module descent engine had a thrust of 16 kN. Analysis suggests:
- Apollo displaced ~2.6 metric tons (t) of regolith.
- Erosion rate scales as thrust to the 2.5 power (Lane and Metzger, 2015).
- A 100 kN HLS lander may displace a total mass of 16 t of lunar regolith during landing on unmodified surfaces.
- Ejecta can exceed lunar escape velocity and travel at velocities >2,000 m/s.

## STATE OF THE ART AND PAST WORK
The overall TRL of LLP technology is approximately TRL-2. Key projects include:
- **PISCES VTVL Pad:** Robotically constructed from sintered basalt pavers in Hawaii (Kelso, 2016).
- **Soil Stabilization:** Investigation of rocks and pavers to mitigate plume effects (Van Susante and Metzger, 2016).
- **Lunar Concrete:** Pads made with KOHLS-1 simulant, polymer binders, and glass fiber (Lee, 2012).

## CONCLUSION
Launch/Landing Pad infrastructure is necessary to reduce mission risks to acceptable levels for sustained lunar activities. This paper establishes a baseline of the current state of the art, provides a listing of known LLP concepts, and presents criteria to trade concepts and identify gaps for future technology development.
