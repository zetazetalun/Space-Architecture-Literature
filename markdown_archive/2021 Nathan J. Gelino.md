# Off Earth Landing and Launch Pad Construction—A Critical Technology for Establishing a Long-Term Presence on Extraterrestrial Surfaces

**Nathan J. Gelino, Robert P. Mueller, Robert W. Moses, James G. Mantovani, Philip T. Metzger, Brad C. Buckles, and Laurent Sibille**

## ABSTRACT
In 2017, Space Policy Directive–1 was issued to ensure that “the United States will lead the return of humans to the Moon for long-term exploration and utilization”. A long-term presence on the Moon will require numerous lunar landings and launches. Rocket engine plumes interacting with unprepared lunar surface lead to erosion and ejection of regolith particles (ejecta) at high velocities, risking sensors, hardware, and surrounding assets. The construction of a reusable landing/launch pad (LLP) is a method to mitigate such risks. This paper summarizes previous work towards construction of off-Earth LLP infrastructure, establishes metrics for comparison of pad construction technologies, and identifies key technology gaps. 

## INTRODUCTION
Terrestrial Launch & Landing Complexes (LLC) include functions like structural support, plume management, and Foreign Object Debris (FOD) mitigation. Functionally equivalent LLCs will have to be developed for repeated returns to the Moon and Mars. Lunar regolith blast ejecta travels at velocities >2,000 m/s for kilometers in a vacuum. Ejecta can damage engines, sensors, and even enter orbit. Furthermore, cratering under landing gear can affect soil stability and tip the lander.

## PRELIMINARY ENGINEERING ASSESSMENT
- **Artemis Program:** Targets 2024 for human return; sustained infrastructure by 2028.
- **CLPS:** Early missions (300 kg payloads) with engines <4000 N thrust.
- **HLS:** Human Landing System landers deliver ~12 t payload with ~100 kN thrust engines.
- **Erosion:** Apollo descent engines (16 kN) displaced ~2.6 metric tons of regolith. HLS landers may displace ~16 t of regolith during landing on unmodified surfaces.

## STATE OF THE ART AND PAST WORK
The overall TRL level of LLP technology is approximately TRL-2. 
- **Sintered Basalt Pavers:** Swamp Works and PISCES robotically constructed a pad in Hawaii from sintered basalt. It withstood a Class M solid propellant engine, though some pavers lifted.
- **Soil Stabilization:** Rocks and rock beds were found somewhat effective; loose or compacted regolith was ineffective (van Susante and Metzger, 2016).
- **Lunar Concrete:** A pad using KOHLS-1 Korean Lunar Simulants and polymer binders was tested for compression strength (Lee, 2012).

## CONCLUSION
LLP infrastructure is necessary to reduce mission risks to acceptable levels for sustained lunar and Martian activity. The paper baselines current LLP concepts and presents criteria to guide future development. Currently, most concepts remain at low TRL and require prototyping in simulated lunar environments (thermal vacuum, radiation, reduced gravity).
