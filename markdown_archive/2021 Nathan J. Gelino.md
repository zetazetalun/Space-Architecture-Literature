## ABSTRACT
A long-term presence on the Moon will require numerous lunar landings and launches. Rocket engine plumes interacting with unprepared lunar surfaces lead to regolith erosion and ejecta at high velocities, risking sensors, vehicles, and surrounding assets. This paper summarizes previous work towards construction of off-Earth landing/launch pad (LLP) infrastructure, identifies technology gaps, and establishes metrics for comparing construction technologies. The goal is to baseline the state of the art for LLP construction.

## INTRODUCTION
A Launch & Landing Complex (LLC) on Earth is well-defined, but off-Earth sites require equivalent functionality for sustained presence. This paper focuses on technologies to construct the Launch & Landing Pad (LLP) and blast barriers for vertical landing and takeoff vehicles.

### Preliminary Engineering Assessment
Future large-scale non-human HLS landers (12 t payload) require descent engines with 100 kN of thrust. Erosion rate scales with thrust; while the Apollo module (16 kN thrust) displaced ~2.6 metric tons (t) of regolith, an HLS lander might displace 16 t. This ejecta travels at velocities exceeding lunar escape velocity (>2,000 m/s), potentially causing severe hardware damage.

## STATE OF THE ART AND PAST WORK
The current Technology Readiness Level (TRL) for LLP construction is approximately TRL-2. Key research includes:
- **Sintered Basalt Pavers:** Robotically constructed by Swamp Works and PISCES in Hawaii. Sintered pavers withstood engine firing but some individual pavers lifted due to seam weakness.
- **Soil Stabilization:** Van Susante and Metzger (2016) found loose/compacted regolith ineffective against plumes, while rock beds were more effective.
- **Lunar Concrete:** Pads using polymer binders and glass fiber were tested for compression strength but not plume interaction.

## TECHNOLOGY GAPS
Significant gaps exist in emplacement systems, processing strategies, and material performance under lunar environmental conditions (thermal vacuum, radiation, reduced gravity). Prototypes must be exposed to these conditions to raise TRL levels.

## CONCLUSION
LLP infrastructure is necessary to reduce mission risks to acceptable levels as NASA moves beyond initial landings toward a vibrant scientific endeavor. This paper establishes a baseline of the current state of the art and trade criteria for future development.
