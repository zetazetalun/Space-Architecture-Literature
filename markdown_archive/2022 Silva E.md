# Eco-friendly additive construction: Analysis of the printability of earthen-based matrices stabilized with potato starch gel and sisal fibers

**Journal:** Construction and Building Materials 347 (2022) 128556  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.conbuildmat.2022.128556  
**Authors:** Guido Silva, Robert Ñañez, Diana Zavaleta, Valeria Burgos, Suyeon Kim, Gaby Ruiz, Miguel A. Pando, Rafael Aguilar, Javier Nakamatsu

## Abstract
3D printing for construction purposes is a disruptive technology with the potential for rapid and massive applications making it a feasible alternative for social housing, temporary shelters after disasters, and, recently, for extraterrestrial habitats. This article studies the printable capabilities of eco-friendly earthen-based matrices which uses potato starch as a natural stabilizer for raw soil mortars. The aqueous starch gel was combined with sisal fibers to control shrinkage cracking during hardening. Results indicate that printable earthen-based matrices reinforced with 1% of sisal fibers and stabilized with aqueous starch gels (up to 5% w/w) showed improved workability and minor cracking.

## 1. Introduction
3D printing in construction offers socio-environmental and economic benefits by reducing waste and improving safety. The process involves delivery (pumping), deposition (print head/nozzle), motion (gantry/robotic), and control systems. Earthen-based materials are gaining attention as they require significantly less energy (18–38% of conventional masonry) and reduce climate impact by 75–82%.

## 2. Additive construction using earthen-based matrices
### 2.1. Summary of recent advances
Literature on local soil for 3D printing is categorized into:
1.  **Earthen-based 3D printing:** Clay soil matrices.
2.  **Regolith-based 3D printing:** Matrices composed of lunar and martian rocks and sand simulants for extraterrestrial construction (46% of recent publications).

### 2.2. Colibri: A prototype 3D printing system
The system includes:
- **Supporting/Movable subsystem:** Steel gantry frame (1x1x1m volume) with XYZ translations.
- **Controlling subsystem:** Arduino Mega 2560 (G-code).
- **Feeding subsystem:** Progressive cavity pump (MAI®2PUMP-PICTOR), mortar mixer, and 25mm diameter nozzle.

## 3. Analysis of printability
### 3.1. Materials
- **Base soil:** Low plasticity sandy clay (CL) from Carabayllo, Peru (58.6% SiO2, 15.6% Al2O3).
- **Stabilizer:** Food-grade potato starch (gelatinized at 90°C).
- **Reinforcement:** Sisal fibers (10mm length, 137μm diameter, aspect ratio 73).

## 4. Results and discussions
### 4.1. Fresh state
- **Yield Strength:** Ranges from 2.3 to 6.8 kPa for printable matrices.
- **Viscosity:** Starch gel increases kinematic viscosity, while fibers appear to lower mixture viscosity.
- **Printing Speed:** Optimum range 15–30 mm/s for consistent filaments (>30mm width).

### 4.2. Hardened state
- **Shrinkage Cracking:** Control soil (EW) failed with 8.49mm cracks. EFS5.0%(27) passed with only 0.60mm cracks due to fiber reinforcement.
- **Buildability:** Up to 16 layers for control soil; 12 layers for EFS5.0%(27).
- **Compressive Strength:** EFS5.0%(27) reached 2.2 MPa at 28 days (a 157% increase over the control).
- **Capillarity Absorption:** Starch gel significantly reduced water absorption from 0.611 to 0.0903 kg/m²min⁰·⁵.

## 5. Conclusions
The study validates the Colibri system for printing earthen composites. A composite of soil with 5% starch gel and 1% sisal fibers showed the best characteristics for construction: 4.3 kPa yield strength, 2.2 MPa compressive strength, and high resistance to shrinkage cracking and water absorption.
