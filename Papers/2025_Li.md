A R T I C L E I N F O A B S T R A C T

_Keywords:_

Lunar construction Robotic assembly control

Deep reinforcement learning Hybrid perception

The moon, as the closest celestial body to Earth, is currently the only deep space celestial body that humans can conveniently travel back and forth using chemical rocket. It can serve as an outpost for humans to move further towards other deep space celestial bodies and become an extraterrestrial resource gathering area that humans can utilize. Lunar construction is necessary for the long-term deep space exploration and resource utilization in the future. The lunar robotic assembly is an indispensable technology that must be broken through in long-term lunar surface exploration. To address the challenges brought by the lunar open-environment scene and the features of the lunar construction tasks, this paper proposes a two-stage DRL control method with the hybrid perception of visual and force feedback for lunar construction robot to perform assembly operations end-to-end rather than model-base. A staged reward function based on the visual pose norm and the first-order derivative of the piecewise force is designed for policy learning. For a large amount of interactive training of our lunar construction robot, this paper establishes a 1:1 simulation scenario covering dynamics and kinematics. A ground real robot prototype is designed to conduct ground experiments, and a sim-to- real pipeline using multi-Aruco visual calibration is provided to eliminate reality gaps. The effectiveness and feasibility of the proposed control method have been verified through simulation experiments and ground real-robot experiments. The application of DRL and cross-disciplinary integration helps lunar robotic systems to embody intelligence and perform complex assembly operations autonomously in open environments.

# Introduction

Deep space exploration is an inevitable trend in aerospace devel- opment, which can utilize space resources and promote the progress of human civilization \[[1](#_bookmark55)\]. The moon, as the closest celestial body to Earth, is currently the only deep space celestial body that humans can conveniently travel back and forth using chemical rocket. The moon is an ideal space staging base because of its low gravity and lack of atmosphere \[[2](#_bookmark56)\]. It can serve as an outpost for humans to move further towards other deep space celestial bodies and also become an extrater- restrial resource gathering area that humans can utilize \[[3](#_bookmark57)\]. Future deep space exploration support can be obtained by the huge potential in terms of the lunar base construction and the space resources develop- ment. With the major progress of the Artemis program, the number of signatories of the Artemis Accords has expanded to 33 as of December 15,2023 \[[4](#_bookmark58),[5](#_bookmark59)\]. Many plans for the lunar outposts/base construction have been proposed for the further development and utilization of planetary resources. NASA plans to send American astronauts back to the Moon and to develop the capabilities for long term presence on the

lunar surface \[[6](#_bookmark60)\]. Marshall Space Flight Center formulated the Moon- to-Mars Planetary Autonomous Construction Technology (MMPACT) project to spur the creation of novel technologies that will be needed for lunar surface exploration \[[7](#_bookmark61)\]. China has launched the lunar landing phase of China’s manned lunar exploration project in May 2023, and plans to land astronauts on the moon by 2030. The lunar surface con- struction will become the focus of the lunar exploration. Future lunar surface exploration and construction need to perform tasks such as remote exploration, autonomous robotic assembly, robotic mining, In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU), and infrastructure construction. Due to the special lunar environment and the scarcity of manpower, robots are the main force in lunar construction. The autonomous robotic as- sembly is the primary issue that needs to be studied first. Lunar surface infrastructure and equipment will need to be assembled independent of whether crew are present or not \[[8](#_bookmark62)\]. The assembly control of lunar construction robots is an indispensable technology that must be broken through in long-term lunar surface exploration.

Robotic assembly have been widely used in modern industrial au- tomated assembly tasks to perform component assembly and mounting

The robotic assembly control have also been widely studied and developed, including many methods such as compliant assembly control methods based on force-position \[[9](#_bookmark63),[10](#_bookmark64)\], industrial robotic assembly methods based on machine vision \[[11](#_bookmark65)\], and assembly planning control based on environmental attraction domain \[[12](#_bookmark66)\]. The above researches are model-based and aimed at ground industrial scenarios, which are in the static and structured environment. The relative posture relation- ships among the robot, assembly object, and scene environment are known and fixed. There are also some applications of robotic assem- bly in the aerospace field, with the typical example being on-orbit servicing. Space manipulators have been utilized for assembly tasks in the construction of space stations, where their operation relies on remote control by astronauts. Research in this field currently focuses primarily on dynamic modeling and trajectory planning for space ma- nipulators \[[13](#_bookmark67),[14](#_bookmark68)\], rather than on assembly control strategies and methods.

However, unlike the industrial closed-environment scenarios, the lunar construction is in an open-environment scenario \[[15](#_bookmark69)\]. For lunar construction robotic assembly, the distinctiveness of the lunar environ- ment, the diversity of lunar assembly objects and the autonomy of lunar assembly operations have brought challenges. (1) The lunar surface environment is unstructured and in state of flux. Compared with the single fixed industrial assembly environment, the lunar construction environment is often complex and unstructured. There is no fixed and known spatial posture relationship between the robot and the assembly objects, and the lunar assembly scene is always in state of flux. (2) The lunar assembly objects are diverse and irregular in shape. The lunar robotic assembly control method must be generalized to adapt to a variety of complex shapes. (3) It is impossible to establish an accurate assembly and mounting contact and friction model. In order to avoid jamming and assembly objects damage, an accurate assembly control method that is not based on accurate modeling should be studied.

(4) The robotic assembly operations cannot be remotely controlled. Due to the large delay in Earth-Moon communication and the scarcity of manpower, it is almost impossible for robots to perform assembly operations through remote control. Lunar construction robots need to embody intelligence and complete assembly operations autonomously through the proprioception obtained by physical sensors.

The development of artificial intelligence and robotics in recent years has provided new ideas. With the rapid development of Deep Re- inforcement Learning(DRL) algorithms and physical sensors, artificial intelligence technologies have been effectively applied to many robotic operation tasks, which enable the robot system to have capabilities like humans by feature extraction from sensor data. Lee M A et al. \[[16](#_bookmark70)\] made sense of vision and touch to learn a compact and multimodal representation of sensory inputs for contact-rich tasks, which can be used to improve the sample efficiency of policy learning. It was verified through a typical robotic single peg-in-hole assembly experiment. A model-driven reinforcement learning algorithm for industrial assembly tasks was proposed by \[[17](#_bookmark71)\] to accomplish the dual peg-in-hole assem- bly through the learned policy without analyzing the contact states. In order to solve the difficulty of obtaining dense reward signals, a deep reinforcement learning method for industrial assembly tasks with visual inputs and natural rewards was proposed \[[18](#_bookmark72)\]. DRL algorithms have been effectively applied to robotic assembly tasks to avoid a complex analysis of the contact model. The research and development of the above methods provides the possibility and inspiration for the realization lunar construction and assembly tasks by lunar robots.

Lunar surface construction involves the assembly and mounting tasks of various related launch devices, locking and release mecha- nisms, and other components. During the assembly process, frequent changes occur in the pose and force characteristics of these com- ponents. It is necessary to investigate how to utilize DRL methods to extract effective observational features from multi-modal sensory

data, such as images and force measurements, in order to assess the success of the assembly tasks. As a result, the research on deep rein- forcement learning-based assembly control methods tailored for lunar construction robots is of significant importance.

To deal with the above challenges of lunar construction robotic assembly, this paper studies the lunar construction robotic assembly control method based on deep reinforcement learning. We proposed a two-stage DRL control method with the hybrid perception of visual and force feedback to control the lunar robot to perform assembly opera- tions end-to-end rather than model-based. The application of DRL and cross-disciplinary integration helps lunar robotic systems to embody intelligence and perform complex assembly operations autonomously in lunar environments.

# Mission overview and problem statement

In order to describe the lunar construction assembly tasks and robot operation modes more specifically and clearly, this paper designs an assembly task scenarios of the lunar construction robots. The robotic assembly control process and principle is analyzed specifically through the lunar robot we designed, and is also applicable to other types of lunar robots.

- 1.  _Mission overview_

The mission scenario of lunar surface construction planned in this paper is shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark2) [1](#_bookmark2), which consists of lunar construction robots,assembly objects, and background lunar open-environment.

We have designed a Lunar Construction Smart Robot (LCSR) that can be used for high-precision and autonomous assembly operations. The designed lunar construction robot consists of an omnidirectional mobile chassis, a lifting mechanism, a 6-DOF high-precision end ef- fector. The end effector is fixedly connected to the assembly part through four positioning holes on its upper surface. A 6-dimensional force sensor and two RGB-D cameras are deployed on the end effector. The 6-dimensional force sensor can directly measure the force and torque during the assembly and docking of components in real time. The RGB-D camera is also fixed to the end effector as an eye-in-hand camera.

The docking interface of the assembly object studied in this paper is a universal form that can be assembled and locked, as shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark3) [2](#_bookmark3). The assembly interface form is two-point docking with two locking/release mechanisms. The two assembly objects will be locked and firmly connected after successful assembly. This interface form is more in line with future practical applications. It has technical principle compatibility and method portability due to the rich contact and locking forms of the two points \[[19](#_bookmark73),[20](#_bookmark74)\]. The method studied on this assembly form can be applied to most lunar robotic assembly tasks.

# Control method based on two-stage deep reinforcement learn- ing with hybrid perception

Deep neural networks have a powerful ability to extract features and learn representations from high-dimensional data such as visual images, but require a substantial amount of training data \[[21](#_bookmark75)\]. The reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm relies on the interactive rewards between the agents and the environment to learn action policy, but the agents are generally unable to learn high-dimensional features of the environment. The deep reinforcement learning (DRL) combines deep networks and reinforcement learning to improve the performance of robots in completing complex real-world tasks, such as deep Q network (DQN) \[[22](#_bookmark76)\]. However, DQN can only handle discrete and low-dimensional action spaces. Most physical robotic control tasks, including lunar robotic assembly, have continuous and high dimen- sional action spaces. The Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) algorithm was proposed to perform stably and efficiently on many continuous and high-dimensional action control tasks \[[23](#_bookmark77)\]. The DDPG algorithm has been effectively applied in grasping tasks of unknown irregular objects and has already been proven to have good perfor- mance for continuous and high-dimensional action control tasks \[[24](#_bookmark78)\]. While DDPG can achieve great performance sometimes, it is frequently brittle with respect to hyperparameters and dramatically overestimates Q-values, which then leads to the policy breaking. Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (TD3), as an improved DDPG algorithm, is proposed to addresses this issue and greatly improves both the learning speed and performance of DDPG in the continuous control tasks \[[25](#_bookmark79)\]. Therefore, we propose a two-stage deep reinforcement learning control strategy for lunar robotic assembly based on the TD3 algorithm network.

In this section, we first describe the framework of the proposed two-stage deep reinforcement learning control strategy. Then, we de- scribe the network setup and the staged reward function with hybrid perception and the network training.

- 1.  _Two-stage deep reinforcement learning control strategy_

The process of the lunar construction robotic assembly can be divided into two stages according to the contact and interaction rela- tionship between the assembly part and target part, as shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark7) [4](#_bookmark7). The first stage is the free spatial posture exploration stage, during which

the components are free and non-contacting. The lunar construction robot gradually adjusts the end effector to align the assembly part toward the target part according to the RGB-D images obtained by the eye-in-hand camera . It is necessary to ensure that no collision occurs during the first stage, and the force sensor is only affected by the weight and inertia of the components. The second stage is the constrained contact interaction and locking stage, during which contact and interaction occur between the components. The lunar construction robot inserts the two-point docking interface under the constraints of the locking/release mechanism. The lunar construction robot performs the precise interactive control based on the force-proprioception in- formation. The end force sensor can monitor The force and torque during the interactive process can be monitored by the six-dimensional force sensor in real time to judge the assembly status and whether it is completed. The locking/release mechanism will be locked after the assembly is completed, resulting in a sudden force response. Different assembly statuses have different learning policies and reward feedback, which will be described in detail in Section [3.3](#_bookmark15).

Deep reinforcement learning algorithms require a lot of interactive training with the environment to extract high-dimensional features of the environment and make action predictions. For the lunar construc- tion robot with multi-modal perception, directly inputting all percep- tion information into the network for training will cost a lot of time, and the control accuracy and success rate of action prediction cannot be guaranteed \[[26](#_bookmark80)\]. In order to effectively improve the training efficiency, control accuracy and success rate of the method, this paper proposes a two-stage DRL control strategy with hybrid perception of vision and force feedback according to the process characteristics of the lunar construction robotic assembly. We use the idea of staged fusion and design a virtual space target point as a sign of the two-stage transition. The overall architecture of the proposed control strategy is shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark8) [5](#_bookmark8).

The Vision-TD3 network is designed to adjust the pose of the assembly part during the first stage according to the observation input of RGB-D images obtained by the multi-angle hand-in-eye cameras. The goal of the first stage is to align the spatial six-dimensional pose of the robot end effector with the virtual target point. The virtual target point is the critical spatial point for the transition between the two stages, which can provide reward feedback for the exploration training of the Vision-TD3 network. During the second stage, the Force-TD3 network is designed to perform precise fine-tuning of the assembly part posture under the constraint of locking/release mechanism according to the six-dimensional force sensor information and the proprioception information. The goal is to control the assembly part reach the real expected posture and be locked by the locking/release mechanism, so that the part can be successfully assembled and fixed. The advantage of the proposed control strategy is that the Vision-TD3 network and the Force-TD3 network can be trained at the same time, and it is convenient

The lunar robotic assembly is a continuous and high-dimensional action control task. When building the deep reinforcement learning architecture, we use the TD3 network as the basic framework because of its excellent performance in high-dimensional and continuous con- trol. As an improvement of DDPG network, TD3 also draws on the experience replay mechanism and constructs a twin delayed network structure for learning and training based on the Actor-Critic (AC) framework. TD3 adds a pair of Q-value online-target networks in the critic framework

compared to DDPG. # Simulation

DRL algorithms require a large amount of interactive training with the environment to extract high-dimensional features and make ac- tion predictions. For lunar construction robotic assembly tasks, it is impossible to conduct a large number of experiments and training due to realistic task constraints, time cost and assembly wear. It is necessary to establish a simulation environment as realistic as possible for learning control polices. To test the effectiveness of the proposed control method and learn the policy on the lunar construction robot, this paper establishes a 1:1 simulation scenario covering dynamics

kinematics, dynamic characteristics and physical sensor information. We deploy the proposed two-stage DRL algorithm through the remote API of CoppeliaSim-python, and use the simulation mission scenario for network training and policy learning, which is conducive to al- gorithm transplantation on real lunar construction robots and ground verification.

The LCSR we designed is a serial–parallel hybrid mechanism with redundant multi-DOF. When building the 1:1 simulation prototype, the lunar construction robot can be divided into a 3-DOF motion chassis, a 1-DOF lifting arm, a 1-DOF end lateral movement mechanism, a 1- DOF end longitudinal movement mechanism, a 2-DOF end roll-pitch mechanism, and a 1-DOF end rotation mechanism, as shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark27) [11](#_bookmark27). The assembly part is fixed with end effector. The parameters of the lunar construction robot, the assembly part and target part are shown in [Table](#_bookmark37) [1](#_bookmark37), which shows the robot’s motion range, accuracy and physical parameters of the components. The end effector of lunar construction robot is equipped with a 6-axis force sensor and multi eye-in-hand RGB-D cameras, which are deployed in the simulation scenario and can generate observation images of the scene and force information for each

step. The parameters of the physical sensors are shown in [Table](#_bookmark39) [2](#_bookmark39).

The LCSR and the assembly components are combined to finally build a 1:1 digital prototype environment as shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark28) [12](#_bookmark28).

# Ground real-robot experiment

In order to verify the deployment and application of the algorithm on real robots, this paper conducted experiments for the lunar robotic assembly task through a ground real robot prototype, as shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark47) [22](#_bookmark47). An assembly part in with two-point docking interface and the matching target part with two-point locking/release mechanism are designed as experimental toolings. The trained network parameters are transplanted and deployed to the ground real robot, and the physical parameters of the parts used are exactly the same as those in the simulation scene.

- 1.  _Sim-to-real pipeline_

In the application of deep reinforcement learning for robotic con- trol, significant reality gaps exist between simulation and real-world experiments. While simulations provide a controlled, consistent and flexible environment for training policies, it is difficult to capture the full complexity and unpredictability of real-world conditions. Factors such as sensor noise, hardware imperfections, and dynamic interactions with the open-environment are challenging to accurately replicate in simulations \[[27](#_bookmark81),[28](#_bookmark82)\]. Bridging this gap is critical to ensure the successful deployment of DRL control methods trained in simulated environments

Based on the specific requirements of ground experiments for lunar construction robotic assembly tasks, we propose a sim-to-real pipeline using multi-Aruco visual calibration. This approach simplifies the chal- lenge of deploying policies trained in simulation to our developed lunar construction robot ground prototype, effectively achieving robotic as- sembly ground experiments. We place an array of multiple ArUco markers on a plane fixed relative to the target parts. An RGB-D camera mounted on the robot’s end effector is used to detect and recognize the ArUco markers. Through the ArUco marker recognition and po- sitioning algorithm, the relative pose between the end effector and the ArUco markers is calculated, thereby determining the relative pose relationship between the assembly part and the target part. Inverse kinematics solution is used to calculate the control variable of the end effector, so that the robotic assembly operation can be realized. The calibration results of the multi-Aruco makers during the robotic assembly experiment are shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark48) [23](#_bookmark48).

The data obtained from ArUco marker recognition serves as the real pose of the virtual target point for pre-training of DRL in real experiments, thus effectively eliminating the reality gap. The sim-to- real pipeline we proposed based on multi-Aruco visual calibration can not only reduce the number of experiments for DRL exploration, but also avoid the safety risks caused by the failure of the DRL control, preventing damage to the real robot and experimental toolings.

- 1.  _Results analysis_

We first utilized real data based on multi-Aruco calibration pipeline to pre-train our DRL networks. A total of 100 episodes were conducted, with the results shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark49) [24](#_bookmark49). After about 700 episodes, the reward value stabilizes above the success criterion and pre-training is completed. This approach eliminates the need for manual labeling, enabling autonomous training of the robot.

After pre-training, we conducted 30 experiments using the proposed two-stage DRL control method. Among them, the robot successfully completed the assembly task 24 times, with a success rate of 80%, as shown in [Fig.](#_bookmark50) [25](#_bookmark50).

# Conclusion

Focusing on the lunar construction robotic assembly tasks, this paper proposes a two-stage DRL control method with the hybrid per- ception of visual and force feedback to control the lunar robot to perform assembly operations end-to-end rather than model-base. We designs an assembly task scenarios with an lunar construction robot to analyze the lunar robotic assembly control process and principle more specifically and clearly. In order to address the challenges brought by the lunar open-environment scene and the features of the con- struction tasks, we constructed the Vision-TD3 network and Force-TD3 network to implement two-stage deep reinforcement learning control, and designed a staged reward function based on the visual pose norm and the first-order derivative of the piecewise force. To learn the policy on our lunar construction robot and test the effectiveness of the proposed control method, this paper establishes a 1:1 simulation scenario covering dynamics and kinematics for a large amount of interactive training. A ground real robot prototype was designed to conduct ground experiments for the lunar robotic assembly task, and we provided a sim-to-real pipeline using multi-Aruco visual calibra- tion to eliminate reality gaps. The effectiveness and feasibility of the proposed control method have been verified through simulation exper- iments and ground real-robot experiments. The application of DRL and cross-disciplinary integration helps lunar robotic systems to embody intelligence and perform complex assembly operations autonomously in lunar environments.

In the future work, we hope to further improve robot performance

through the evolution of embodied intelligence and parallel intelli- gence. Further research will focus on systematic methods to build a synchronous verification environment for connection of ground and lunar surface. Moreover, training data will be accumulated through various ways to improve the lunar construction robot performance.
