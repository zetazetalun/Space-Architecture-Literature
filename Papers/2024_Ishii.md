# ABSTRACT

The granular mechanics of lunar and Martian regolith remain inadequately understood, impeding progress in successful exploration, landing, drilling, sampling, and construction activities on extraterrestrial surfaces. This study aims to bridge this knowledge gap by investigating the granular behavior of the lunar and Martian regolith under impact conditions. Impact cratering experiments were conducted for the lunar highlands, lunar mare, Martian regolith simulants (LHS-1, LMS-1, and MGS-1, respectively), and terrestrial silica sand with similar particle sizes as target granular materials, with a sphere projectile dropping at low velocities. A systematic analysis was undertaken to elucidate the influ-ence of parameters, including the fall height of the projectile, impact velocity, kinetic energy of the projectile, porosity, cohesion, and internal friction angle, on the resulting crater depths. Our findings demonstrate that the crater depths of regolith layers of the lunar highlands and Martian surfaces are greater than those of the lunar mare regolith and terrestrial silica sand layers. For example, the crater depth of the lunar highland regolith layer is about two times greater than that of the terrestrial silica sand layer at an impact velocity of 40–70 cm/s. Additionally, our power-law scaling highlights less resistance to crater impact in the lunar and Martian regolith layers than in the terrestrial sand layer. Our study highlights a significant difference in granular behavior between the Earth’s sand layer and the lunar and Martian regolith layers, providing valuable insights for future exploration, coring, drilling, and resource utilization endeavors on the lunar and Martian surfaces.

# INTRODUCTION

26 June 2025 22:17:12

The physical and geomechanical properties of lunar and Martian surfaces remain an open and fundamentally interesting scientific issue. The lunar surface is covered by a layer of loose debris known as lunar regolith, with its average thickness varying from approximately 5 meters on the mare surfaces to around 10 meters on the highland sur-faces. This regolith layer is continually bombarded by micrometeorites, resulting in fragmentation and partial melting, which contributes to forming irregular clusters known as agglutinates, where melted

material mixes with lithic fragments.[<sup>1</sup>](#_bookmark18) On Mars, the regolith layer undergoes intricate geological processes alongside weaker impact and space weathering modifications. The regolith, composed of fragmental and unconsolidated rock material, forms the surface layer nearly every-where, overlaying or covering the bedrock.[<sup>2</sup>](#_bookmark19)

Various missions have conducted exploration, drilling, sampling, and coring projects on the lunar and Martian surfaces, including Luna, Apollo, Chang’E-5, and Curiosity missions. These missions and sam-ples hold immense scientific value, providing insights into the

composition and evolution of the lunar and Martian surfaces. However, the geological and geomechanical uncertainty significantly influences interpretations of exploration data, landing and drilling operations, and building infrastructure on the lunar and Martian sur-faces, emphasizing the necessity of a thorough understanding of rego-lith’s mechanical and physical properties.[<sup>3</sup>](#_bookmark20) Understanding the geomechanical characteristics of regolith, including its strength, stiff-ness, and cohesion, is indispensable for designing successful landing and efficient drilling and coring tools on the lunar and Martian surfa-ces, optimizing the coring process, and enhancing sample collection methods.[<sup>4</sup>](#_bookmark21) It is also essential for evaluating the feasibility and safety of drilling activities on these celestial bodies, aiding in assessing the land-ing and drilling site stability and associated risks. Moreover, the precise comprehension of physical and geomechanical properties, such as par-ticle size distribution, shear strength, and bearing strength, empowers scientists to refine landing, drilling, and sampling techniques, ensuring the successful retrieval of valuable samples from the lunar and Martian surfaces.[<sup>5</sup>](#_bookmark22) Despite advancements, many challenges are still present, particularly in accurately modeling lunar drilling mechanics due to geological complexities and uncertainties.[<sup>6</sup>](#_bookmark23) To facilitate the under-standing of regolith properties, various regolith simulants have been developed and synthesized to approximate the physical, mechanical, and chemical properties of the actual regolith, allowing us to open to study the regolith properties. For example, previous studies affirmed the suitability of Exolith Lab’s simulants lunar highlands simulants (LHS-1) and lunar mare simulants (LMS-1) as lunar regolith analogs, while the fidelity of Martian regolith simulants (MGS-1) simulant was highlighted as Martian basaltic regolith.[<sup>7</sup>](#_bookmark24)<sup>–</sup>[<sup>9</sup>](#_bookmark26) These studies examined various properties, including mineralogy, particle size, density, and shear strength, indicating the importance of simulant fidelity in a broad area of extraterrestrial research.

Studies into the behavior of granular materials under impact conditions have been extensive, offering valuable insights into their response.[<sup>10</sup>](#_bookmark27)<sup>–</sup>[<sup>14</sup>](#_bookmark29) Impact experiments, involving the launch of projec-tiles into target granular materials like regolith particles, provide cru-cial data on crater characteristics such as depth, diameter, and morphology. These features are closely tied to regolith properties like density, cohesion, porosity, and grain size distribution, signifi-cantly influencing the efficiencies of landing, drilling, and boring. Cook and Mortensen[<sup>12</sup>](#_bookmark28) conducted pioneering experiments on

projectile impact in dry sands, highlighting the dependence of crater size on projectile and target material properties. The study of low-velocity impacts in asteroid-level gravity conditions demonstrated that the speed of the eject scales with the projectile to target grain size ratio and the impact speed and that higher impact speeds result in more significant energy loss during collisions with regolith, lead-ing to lower rebound velocities.[<sup>15,</sup>](#_bookmark30)[<sup>16</sup>](#_bookmark31) The scaling of crater size with grain-grain friction coefficient underscores the importance of granu-lar medium properties in crater formation. Higher friction coeffi-cients lead to reduced crater sizes due to increased energy dissipation, while lower coefficients result in larger craters due to lesser energy dissipation.[<sup>17</sup>](#_bookmark32) These findings collectively enhance our understanding of regolith behavior under impact conditions and have practical implications for future space missions. However, most previous studies have conducted impact cratering experiments using terrestrial granular materials and glass beads, hampering a better understanding of the unique behavior of granular regolith.

In this study, we perform the low-velocity impact cratering experiments on the lunar highlands, lunar mare, and Martian regolith surfaces using high-fidelity regolith simulants LHS-1, LMS-1, and MGS-1, respectively, in the Earth’s environment. Our specific focus is on the depth of craters formed by projectile impact onto the granular regolith layers. We integrate experiments, high-resolution 3D topo-graphic mapping, and X-CT measurements. By comparing the results of the impact experiments on the regolith simulants with terrestrial sil-ica sand, we aim to gain insights that will contribute to advancing granular mechanics on the lunar and Martian surfaces. An analysis of shallow crater depths formed by low-velocity falls of spherical projec-tiles provides essential information about the geomechanical properties and mechanical responses of these granular materials to impact, espe-cially the influence of cohesion and porosity. Our study will extract indispensable insights to advance our understanding of granular mechanics in regolith for optimizing future lunar and Martian explorations.

Birch _et al._[<sup>36</sup>](#_bookmark51) investigated the penetration of spherical projectiles into a water-saturated granular medium, observing that the wet medium undergoes plastic deformation with an increased yield strength. Their findings revealed that this yield strength diminishes the mobility of individual grains, resulting in shallower penetration depths. Introducing water into a granular medium introduces a plastic yield stress dependent on grain size, where the strength of individual force chains within a small volume around the impacting sphere predomi-nates energy dissipation. Consequently, a pore fluid alters the scaling from a gravity-dominated regime to one where the material’s yield strength becomes significant.[<sup>36</sup>](#_bookmark51) Pore water, if it exists, cannot be disre-garded in lunar and Martian environments, where icy regolith is expected to be present.[<sup>38</sup>](#_bookmark53)<sup>,</sup>[<sup>39</sup>](#_bookmark54) The pore water in the solid phase due to freezing encourages the rearrangement of regolith particles and alters the total contact area between them.[<sup>25</sup>](#_bookmark40) The freeze-thaw cycling induces decompaction in wet granular materials, resulting in a reduction in cohesion over cycles.[<sup>40</sup>](#_bookmark55) Therefore, the behavior of wet regolith in actual lunar and Martian environments during impacts is presumed to be more complex. Moreover, lunar regolith contains glassy agglutinates [<sup>1</sup>](#_bookmark18) yielding complex morphologies, which could significantly influence the granular behavior of lunar soils. The agglutinates can be chemically reactive, especially in the presence of water or other solvents. The pres-ence of chemically reactive substances in the lunar and Martian soils, such as the formation of iron oxides, can increase soil cohesion.[<sup>41</sup>](#_bookmark56)<sup>,</sup>[<sup>42</sup>](#_bookmark57) The variable abundance of the reactive substances can thus be the fac-tor influencing the scaling laws. The influence of moisture contents, freeze-thaw cycling, and chemically reactive substances as well as vac-uum degree[<sup>30</sup>](#_bookmark45)<sup>,</sup>[<sup>43</sup>](#_bookmark58) on the impact crater depths should be further investi-gated in future studies.

- 1.  Implications for future space exploration missions

Various drilling, sampling, and coring projects have been con-ducted on the lunar and Martian surfaces. Drilling on the lunar surface is not challenging in terms of the absence of abundant water, salts, clay minerals, and a dense atmosphere capable of sustaining liquid water because the lunar is predominantly composed of dry regolith inter-spersed with occasional rocks. Several missions, including Apollo Missions 11–17, Luna Missions 16 and 20, and Chang’e 5 mission, have been successful in drilling down to a couple of meters depth.[<sup>44</sup>](#_bookmark59)<sup>,</sup>[<sup>45</sup>](#_bookmark60) The facts may be consistent with the results in our experiments reveal-ing that the crater depths in lunar regolith simulants are larger than those in terrestrial sand at the given energy upon impact ([Fig. 7](#_bookmark13)). Our results further showed the generated crater depths on the Martian regolith surface can be nearly identical to those on the lunar highlands regolith surface; however, Martian missions have faced more challeng-ing environment for drilling activities, limiting the successful deep dril-ling depths.[<sup>46</sup>](#_bookmark61)<sup>,</sup>[<sup>47</sup>](#_bookmark62)

Our findings will a significant step toward designing stable land-ing and effective drilling systems and tools that can successfully extract valuable resources and conduct scientific investigations on the lunar and Martian surfaces. However, it should be noted that the findings in our work are limited to the uppermost a few centimeters of the surface. While the LMS-1 and MGS-1 layers exhibited low resistance against

impact in our experiments, the results might differ at great depths of the regolith layers. For example, high shear strengths have been dem-onstrated at a depth of 15 cm below the lunar surface.[<sup>48</sup>](#_bookmark63) Furthermore, the high cohesion and fine particle diameter of the regolith are expected to result in high compactability, potentially affecting drilling efficiency. More experiments are necessary to advance our understand-ing of granular mechanics in regolith for optimizing future lunar and Martian surface explorations. Nevertheless, our insights can aid in the design of lunar rovers and Martian landers by optimizing landing strategies to minimize damage and ensure stability on varying and compressible surfaces. The results also enhance sampling strategies by identifying areas where regolith is more compressible or cohesive, guiding the selection of regions for scientific study or resource extrac-tion. Moreover, these findings are crucial for future infrastructure development on extraterrestrial surfaces, such as constructing landing pads or bases. By understanding the compressibility and structural properties of regolith, engineers can better plan for foundation stability and address mechanical challenges during construction.

# CONCLUSIONS

We conducted spherical projectile impact experiments into rego-lith layers under the earth’s gravity in terrestrial atmospheric condi-tions, along with their 3D scan imaging and X-CT measurements, using four different granular materials: lunar regolith simulant LHS-1, lunar mare regolith simulant LMS-1, Martian global regolith simulant MGS-1, and terrestrial silica sand T-8. Our results of the impact experiments revealed significant differences in the crater depths among the studied granular materials upon the impact of a 10-mm alumina ball at a low velocity of 44–70 cm/s. The crater depths for the lowest fall height of 10 mm and the highest fall height of 25 mm were

5.0 6 0.7 mm and 8.3 6 0.5 mm, 3.0 6 0.2 mm, and 4.7 6 0.3 mm,

4.9 6 0.9 mm, and 7.7 6 0.9 mm, and 2.0 6 0.3 mm and 3.8 6 0.1 mm in the LHS-1, LMS-1, MGS-1, and T-8 granular media, respectively. The crater depths in the LHS-1 and MGS-1 layers increased signifi-cantly with impact velocity. In contrast, the LMS-1 and T-8 layers exhibited a more gradual increase in crater depth with impact velocity. These results indicate variations in the response of different regolith and terrestrial sand surfaces to impact events. Our power-law scaling law also indicated that the resistance to crater impact in the lunar and Martian regolith layers is less than that in the terrestrial sand layer. Notably, the crater depth in the LHS-1 layer was greater than that in the LMS-1 layer, suggesting that the typical regolith layer in the lunar mare may present greater resistance to penetration compared to that in the lunar highlands. This highlights the importance of considering the unique granular mechanics of different regolith surfaces when assessing landing and sampling missions and potential challenges for drilling operations on the lunar and Martian surfaces. The lunar high-lands and Martian surface may exhibit reduced resistance to impact due to the higher porosity of the studied LHS-1 and MGS-1 layers, particularly evident in their uppermost surface layers. However, the substantial cohesion inhibiting the penetration into the lunar and Martian surfaces is expected to pose a significant challenge to effective drilling operations. Further investigation into the greater depths of reg-olith below the surface is essential, where the pronounced cohesive properties may exert a more pronounced influence. While the shallow cratering experiment revealed a gradual slope in the scaling law for the LMS-1, we suggest that the scaling law will need modification for larger crater sizes and higher impact velocities. Our findings have

important implications for future exploration missions, particularly in the context of landing, resource utilization, and construction activities on the lunar and Martian surfaces.
