A B S T R A C T

The technology of 3D printing, referred to as additive manufacturing, is widely acknowledged as a trans-formative innovation that has the potential to supplant traditional processing methods in numerous domains. The present study showcases a quantitative assessment of the mechanical properties of moon dust, also known as

Lunar Regolith Simulants (LRS), printed through vat polymerization. In this study, we conduct a thorough investigation and explore the effects of layer height \[L<sub>H</sub>\] (L<sub>H</sub> = 10 μm, 20 μm, 30 μm, 40 μm, 50 μm, 60 μm\]), exposure time \[E<sub>T</sub>\] (E<sub>T</sub> = 3000 ms, 5000 ms, 7000 ms, 11,000 ms), and sintering impact \[1075 <sup>◦</sup>C, 1082 <sup>◦</sup>C, 1083 <sup>◦</sup>C, 1085 <sup>◦</sup>C, 1086 <sup>◦</sup>C, 1087 <sup>◦</sup>C, 1090 <sup>◦</sup>C\] on the mechanical properties of printed structures. Herein, we

utilize a 55 % volume suspension of LRS to print rod and block configurations via digital light printing \[DLP\] that are subsequently consolidated through sintering in ambient air. This 55 % LRS via vat polymerization approach has not been previously reported. The morphology of the simulant powders exhibited irregular and angular

features. Our experimental results show that a 30 um (L<sub>H</sub>) with (E<sub>T</sub>) 11,000 ms exhibits maximum compressive and flexural strength of 330 MPa and 100 MPa at 1085 <sup>◦</sup>C. The sintering atmosphere greatly affects the microstructure, macroscopic features, and mechanical strength of 3D-printed LRS, which reveals diverse

chemical compositions and underlying reaction mechanisms. This sintering process improves particle bonding, resulting in densification and reduced voids within the 3D-printed structure. It is essential to optimize the annealing parameters to achieve the desired strength while avoiding excessive sintering that may cause dimensional distortions or structural defects. This innovative approach opens new possibilities for future space exploration and extraterrestrial construction.

- 1.  _Photocurable slurry formulation_

In our experiments, the slurry is made of acrylic resin, photoinitiator, and CLRS-2 particles. Resin and oligomers are commonly classified into three categories based on the number of reactive functional groups in each molecule: monofunctional resins, bifunctional resins, and trifunc-tional resins. In the case of resins possessing equivalent molecular weights and molar masses, the presence of supplementary functional groups within each molecule leads to an augmentation in the quantity of unsaturated double bonds present in the solution. Consequently, this augmentation serves to expedite the process of curing. The polymeri-zation process results in a polymer network with a high level of compactness, leading to notable improvements in efficiency and me-chanical properties. However, stress concentrations may be induced due to the heat released during the cooling process. Ensuring the appropriate rheological behavior is a crucial aspect of slurry preparation. Adjusting the ratio between bifunctional and trifunctional resins is a common practice to customize the fluidities based on their diverse viscosities. Given the simultaneous consideration of both aspects and the pre-existing research on photocurable ceramic slurry, combining the two resins in equal proportions is preferable. The bifunctional resin mono-mer known as 1,6-hexanediol diacrylate (HDDA), the trifunctional resin monomer referred to as Ethoxylated trimethylolpropane triacrylate (TMP3EOTA) and a photoinitiator, specifically diphenyl (2,4,6-tri-methyl benzoyl) phosphine oxide (TPO) were obtained from Curease Chemical. Ultrasonication is utilized in a light-restricted environment to induce the disintegration of TPO. The inclusion of copolymers with acidic groups (Disperbyk-111, BYK Chemie, Germany) is justified due to their inherent ability to reduce the viscosity of the solution through the utilization of the “steric effect.” The defoamer, Foamex N, manufactured by TEGO in Germany, minimizes the presence of air bubbles in the slurry formulation. The slurry suitable for light curing is formulated following the specifications outlined in _Table S1_ and subsequently subjected to homogenization under high vacuum conditions.

Structural analysis reveals that the as-received LRS has larger

nanoparticles and aggregation before slurry preparation _(Fig. S1)_. The slurry must maintain its reduced particle size to prevent an instrumental failure error during DLP. For this purpose, firstly, we did ball milling by combining 100 g of LRS with 500 g of zirconium oxide grinding balls and

150 g of isopropanol (IPA) in a 500 ml high-density polyethylene (HDPE) bottle. Using a ball mill machine, the mixture was ground into finer powders at a rotational speed of 200 rpm for 2 h _(Fig. S2)_. Sub-sequently, the ground simulants underwent a cleaning process utilizing

an ultrasonic cleaner, followed by a drying period at a temperature of 80 ◦C for 4 h. The regolith simulant slurry was prepared by combining

photocurable resins and ground powders in a volumetric ratio of 55 %.

# Results and discussion

- 1.  _The rheological properties of slurry_

The rheological behaviors of slurry refer to the flow characteristics and properties exhibited by a mixture of solid particles suspended in a liquid medium. The rheological properties of devices must be in accor-dance with the criteria for suitable flow, both during and after squeegee action, to ensure their correct design. The slurry, which consists of the primary component of the light-curing material, exhibits a complex relationship between its rheological properties and its ability to undergo photocuring. This interaction forms the basis for achieving high-quality printability. The presence of suitable rheological properties is of utmost importance in the printing process, as it directly impacts the quality and efficiency of printing \[[65](#_bookmark90)\]. The stability of the slurry plays a crucial role in vat photopolymerization as it determines the material’s suitability in extended print runs \[[66](#_bookmark91)\]. The combined effects of colloidal interactions, Brownian motion, and viscous forces lead to a significant increase in viscosity as the concentration of solid particles in the system rises.

Remarkably, it has been noted that an increased solid content can

unexpectedly result in improved mechanical properties. In order to begin the investigation into the slurry appropriate for vat photo-polymerization, it is crucial first to analyze the solid content of low-titanium LRS. [Fig. 1](#_bookmark8) depicts the rheological characteristics of slurries with solid volume fractions of 55 %. The data clearly shows a direct relationship between the rise in solid content and the subsequent sub-stantial rise in slurry viscosity. This phenomenon arises from the intrinsic propensity of powders to impede their motion by interacting with each other. Throughout this process, a significant portion of the energy employed to facilitate the movement of particles will be dissi-pated as heat \[[67](#_bookmark92)\]. This phenomenon is further confirmed by the noticeable rise in slurry temperature observed during the homogeniza-tion stage. Furthermore, the observed phenomenon of shear-thinning, characterized by a decrease in viscosity under high shear rates, has been recognized as a significant factor contributing to the attainment of seamless printing outcomes when employing a scraper. The addition of Disperbyk-111, a wetting agent, is essential to decrease viscosity by utilizing the “steric effect” \[[68](#_bookmark93)\]. Besides this, it should be noted that the rheological property remains unaffected by both the particle morphology and compositions \[[69](#_bookmark94)\].

# Conclusion

We emphasize the importance of finding the ideal combination of exposure time, layer height, and sintering time to enhance the me-chanical strength of printed structures and comprehensively investigate and analyze the effects of layer height (L<sub>H</sub>) at various levels (10 μm, 20 μm, 30 μm, 40 μm, 50 μm, 60 μm), exposure time (E<sub>T</sub>) at different du-rations (3000 ms, 5000 ms, 7000 ms, 11,000 ms), and sintering impact

at different temperatures (1075 ◦C, 1082 ◦C, 1083 ◦C, 1085 ◦C, 1086 ◦C,

1087 ◦C, 1090 ◦C) on the mechanical properties of printed structures.

Our experiments show that extending the duration of exposure results in various advantages. These include improved curing, enhanced particle fusion, reduced porosity, increased material density, improved layer adhesion, and a decreased risk of delamination. Combining these effects leads to a 3D-printed structure exhibiting enhanced mechanical strength. Based on the findings of our experiment, it can be shown that

there is a positive correlation between the exposure time (E<sub>T</sub> = 11,000

ms) and the comprehensive strength, as well as the flexural strength.

This relationship holds true for a layer thickness of 30 μm and a sintering

temperature of 1085 <sup>◦</sup>C. As evident via structural analysis, the sintering process leads to improved particle bonding, resulting in densification

and reduced voids within the 3D printed structure. Hence, the 3D

printed specimen’s flexural and compression strength increases at 1085◦. The choice of the sintering temperature greatly influences the

final sintered product’s mechanical strength. The entire densification and particle bonding may not be possible at 1050 ◦C, resulting in

reduced mechanical strength than at higher temperatures, such as 1085 ◦C. However, extremely high temperatures can cause over-

sintering, resulting in deformation such as mild inward bending, warping, or even material degradation. In our samples, sintering above 1085 causes a little reduction and compromise in mechanical strength. Thus, optimizing the annealing parameters to achieve the desired strength is essential while avoiding excessive sintering that may cause

dimensional distortions or structural defects (i.e., 1090 ◦C).
