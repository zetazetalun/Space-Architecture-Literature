# Effect of Low-Temperature Fluctuations on Martian Steel Structures: Numerical and Analytical Studies

Yongtao Bai<sup>1</sup>; Jing Li<sup>2</sup>; and Xuhong Zhou<sup>3</sup>

Abstract: To harness a variety of unique resources, humanity is progressively venturing deeper into space. This endeavor necessitates a comprehensive investigation into the formidable challenges presented by hostile environments, particularly the impact of extreme low-tem-perature fluctuations on extraterrestrial engineering and construction. This paper employs numerical simulation methodologies to scrutinize the displacement variations and stress distribution within a full-scale circular arch steel structure subjected to diverse thermal cycles, thereby assessing the influence of severe cryogenic temperature fluctuations on Martian steel infrastructure. The findings of this research contribute to understanding the resilience of such structures within fluctuating low-temperature conditions, thereby offering an empirical foundation for the architectural design and material selection pertinent to the establishment of a Martian base. # Overview of the Martian Environment

Mars, distinguished as the most Earth-analogous celestial body within our solar system, boasts an abundance of material resources, including water ice and a spectrum of minerals. The prospective presence of aqueous resources on Mars affords a unique opportu-nity for the generation of oxygen through solar-powered electroca-talytic oxygen evolution reactions, potentially fostering an oxygenated atmosphere conducive to human habitation. Presently, Mars is deemed the most viable candidate for interplanetary colo-nization ([Zhu et al. 2024](#_bookmark44)). Nonetheless, the environmental condi-tions, external loads, and material responses of extraterrestrial structures diverge radically from Earth-based counterparts. As such, caution is advised when extrapolating terrestrial design codes to Martian architecture.

_Low-Temperature Alternating Environment_

Temperature fluctuations are recognized as an important factor influ-encing structural performance. Although the Moon and Mars both present extreme temperature variations, their differing thermal cy-cling patterns result in distinct fatigue behaviors of materials and structures under extreme conditions. This paper will focus on analyz-ing the low-temperature cycling environment of Mars, which will be applied as a boundary condition to the structures, thereby allowing differentiation from the Moon and other planetary environments. This approach allows for a deeper investigation into the effects of ex-treme low-temperature cycling on structural fatigue on Mars.

Mars’ diurnal cycle closely mirrors that of Earth, with a Martian day—termed a sol—lasting 24 h, 39 min, and 35 s. The axial tilt of Mars is 25.19°, akin to Earth’s 23.44°, endowing Mars with a quar-tet of seasons analogous to those on Earth, although each season is approximately double the duration, given that a Martian year spans 687 Earth days (Table [1](#_bookmark0)) ([Petrov 2004](#_bookmark38)). The Martian atmosphere is notably tenuous, with a density of merely 1% of Earth’s, which

_Structure Form and Force of Martian Architecture_

In the context of extraterrestrial construction, the replication of terrestrial verticalized urban architecture is impractical due to the resultant increased mass of structures and the necessity for deeper foundations. Moreover, the maintenance and replacement of structural components in such configurations could prove to be laborious and time-intensive, particularly within a Martian environment. Consequently, a preference emerges for simplistic, autonomous, single-level edifices characterized by elementary ge-ometries and junctures. On Earth, gravitational forces dictate archi-tectural design; therefore, parabolic arches are favored over semicircular ones due to their efficiency in minimizing internal stress and potential damage. Conversely, under conditions of re-duced gravitational influence, semicircular arches are advantageous for the effective transmission of loads to the foundation while mit-igating the development of internal moments ([Benaroya 2006](#_bookmark23); [Ruess et al. 2006](#_bookmark39)). This discourse references the structural concep-tual module depicted in Fig. [2](#_bookmark2) ([Benaroya 2006](#_bookmark23)), which outlines the design of a rudimentary single-story habitat. This habitat comprises a semicircular arch steel member coupled with a flat floor member, possessing a structural radius of 5 m, a material thickness of 50 mm, and a longitudinal span of 10 m. The design fulfills NASA’s habitat specifications for supporting a quartet of astronauts over a terrestrial annum \[93 m<sup>2</sup> ([NASA and Bradley University 2019](#_bookmark37))\], as illustrated in Fig. [3](#_bookmark3).

Internal Pressure

Internal pressure is the most challenging force in Martian structural design. To preserve the health of the crew members and provide a human-friendly space in Martian structures, the internal pressure should be equal to the atmospheric pressure on Earth (101.325 kPa) ([Soureshjani et al. 2023](#_bookmark42)). Similarly, the International Space Station (ISS) has an internal pressure of 101.3 kPa (14.7 psi) (Earth equivalent). It should be noted that the Martian structures proposed in this study were designed in accordance with 1 atm of internal pressure.

External Pressure

The internal pressure of the structures will create substantial tensile loads on the structure. The overlying regolith, utilized as a shield-ing material, is expected to mitigate the tensile loads at the apex of the structure. Additionally, this regolith layer offers resistance to the deleterious effects of space radiation and micrometeoroid im-pacts. However, due to the reduced Martian gravity, the pressure caused by its weight will not be greater than the internal pressure. Assume that the regolith shield is 3 m high, gravity is 3.73 m/s<sup>2</sup>, and the density of regolith is 1.67 g/cm<sup>3</sup> ([Kalapodis et al. 2020](#_bookmark29)). The pressure created by the regolith will be 18.687 kPa, which is much less than the minimum required internal pressure. To inves-tigate the thermal fatigue performance of the structure’s surface under the most extreme conditions and to simplify the calculations, this study treats the weight generated by the weathering layer as a uniform pressure applied to the exterior of the structure, without considering factors such as the conductivity of the weathering layer.

# Conclusion and Discussion

Using ABAQUS finite-element numerical simulation software, a full-scale steel circular arch model suitable for Mars with high in-ternal pressure as the dominant environment was established. The temperature changes, stress distribution, and displacement changes of the structure under different Mars low-temperature alternating periods were simulated.

1.  The structure was subjected to 1–10 Martian years of tempera-ture cycling without structural damage or cracking, indicating that this 50-mm-thick steel structure consisting of a semicircular arch member and a flat floor member is capable of meeting the Martian environment dominated by high internal pressure plus subjected to temperature cycling.
2.  According to the stress distribution of the structure under low-temperature fluctuations, it was obtained that after the structure has experienced the low-temperature cycles, the stress is con-centrated in the floor member, and the maximum value of the stress occurs at the connection between the bottom of the arch member and the bottom floor member. It is recommended that the structural design of the Mars construction should thicken the thickness of the floor member to improve the bearing capac-ity of the floor member, and optimize the connection between the round arch member and the floor member, to reduce the stress concentration of the structure and improve the durability of the structure.
3.  Upon subjecting the structure to thermal cycling, the vault’s dis-placement exhibits a cyclical alteration pattern. The low-temperature fluctuation cycles unequivocally precipitate an in-cremental accrual of material fatigue damage. However, it is noteworthy that the vault’s maximal displacement did not ex-hibit a monotonic escalation with the proliferation of thermal cycles. Intriguingly, at the 2,061st cycle, the maximal displace-ment value conspicuously diminished in comparison with that observed at the 1,374th cycle.

Research on structural temperature fatigue in the extreme envi-ronment of Mars is currently limited. In this paper, numerical sim-ulations are employed to model the performance and displacement variations of steel structures under low-temperature fluctuations on Mars. The structural stress forms in the Martian environment, which differ from those on Earth (with structural internal pressure being predominant), were systematically analyzed. Low-temperature alternating loads were applied to the structures, and their stability and durability were characterized through metrics such as stress distribution and displacement variations. The boun-dary condition setting methods and numerical simulation results presented in this study aim to provide theoretical references for fu-ture numerical analyses and structural designs in the Martian envi-ronment. Looking ahead, experimental and numerical simulation studies on different structural forms under Martian conditions are planned. It is hoped that these findings will offer scientific insights for enhancing the safety and development of sustainable buildings on Mars.
